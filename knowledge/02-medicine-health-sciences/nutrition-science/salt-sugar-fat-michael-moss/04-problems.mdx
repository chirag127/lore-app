## Problems Addressed

### Key Problem

The core problem Salt Sugar Fat addresses is the systemic, deliberate engineering of addiction into the American food supply. This is not the conventional problem of "people eat too much junk food" — that framing implies individual moral failure. Moss's framing is structural: the processed food industry has spent decades and billions of dollars learning precisely how to override human biology's natural satiety mechanisms, creating products that are scientifically optimized to cause overconsumption. The result is an obesity epidemic that has driven rates of diabetes, heart disease, hypertension, and certain cancers to historically unprecedented levels, with the greatest burden falling on low-income communities with the least access to whole foods.

The problem has three dimensions that Moss documents in sequence: biological (humans are hardwired to seek salt, sugar, fat, and the industry exploits this), corporate (companies knowingly design addictive products and suppress internal research on health consequences), and political (regulatory agencies are captured by the industries they oversee, agricultural subsidies cheapen the very ingredients that cause harm, and voluntary industry reform initiatives are systematically inadequate).

### Proposed Solution

Moss does not offer a single, coherent solution but rather a constellation of responses at different levels. At the individual level, he advocates for consumer awareness: read labels, cook from scratch, understand the bliss point, recognize marketing manipulation. At the industry level, he calls for reformulation — reducing salt, sugar, and fat levels across product categories. At the policy level, he endorses sugar taxes, marketing restrictions to children, clearer labeling requirements, and reforms to agricultural subsidies.

The epilogue makes clear, however, that Moss is skeptical of voluntary industry reform. He notes that every previous wave of industry self-regulation — from salt reduction pledges in the 1980s to fat reduction commitments in the 2000s — has produced minimal results because the economic incentives to keep salt, sugar, and fat levels high are overwhelming. His most concrete policy recommendation is taxation of sugary beverages, which had shown promising results in early studies at the time of his writing and has since been implemented in multiple jurisdictions with measurable consumption declines.

### Evidence and Argumentation

Moss builds his case through triangulation of three evidentiary streams. The first is scientific: he cites peer-reviewed research on taste perception, dopamine receptor activation, satiety signaling, and the neuroscience of craving. The second is documentary: internal industry memos, strategy documents, and marketing plans that reveal conscious decision-making about addiction engineering. The third is testimonial: on-the-record interviews with executives, scientists, and whistleblowers who confirm practices that industry public relations departments would deny.

The most powerful evidence is the word-for-word accounts of industry insiders acknowledging what they did and why. A Kraft executive tells Moss that the company once had an internal program called "Project Weight Loss" that aimed to create healthy products — but it was shut down because it wasn't profitable enough. A Philip Morris food division veteran describes how the company applied cigarette marketing strategies to Kraft cheese products. These testimonies are not scientifically rigorous in the epidemiological sense, but they are devastating as journalism.

### Unresolved Questions

The book leaves several important questions open. First, what should the specific regulatory framework look like? Moss endorses sugar taxes but does not develop a comprehensive regulatory agenda that would address salt and fat with similar specificity. Second, how should the tension between consumer freedom and public health regulation be resolved? Moss acknowledges the libertarian argument against government intervention but does not fully engage with it. Third, what is the role of the healthcare system in addressing diet-related disease? Moss does not discuss whether insurers and providers should play a role in prevention.

The most significant unresolved question is whether reformulation can ever be sufficient. If the fundamental problem is that processed foods are engineered for overconsumption, reducing salt, sugar, and fat levels may improve the nutritional profile of these products without addressing the underlying architecture of addiction. A product with 20% less sugar is still a product designed to be craved.

### Criticisms

Named criticisms of the book center on three issues. Bee Wilson in *The Guardian* argued that Moss's conflation of natural saturated fat with industrially processed trans fats undermines his credibility on the fat section of the book. The Weston A. Price Foundation published a detailed critique arguing that Moss's demonization of salt and saturated fat ignores the research showing that these nutrients are not harmful in the context of whole, unprocessed foods — and that his focus on these three ingredients distracts from more harmful additives like MSG and industrial seed oils.

Other critics note the absence of class and race analysis. The book does not adequately address why low-income communities and communities of color bear a disproportionate burden of both food industry targeting and obesity-related disease. While Moss mentions that food deserts and poverty are factors, he does not integrate this structural inequality into his analysis. The implicit assumption throughout the book is that the problem affects all Americans equally, which evidence flatly contradicts.

### Alternative Solutions

Other thinkers have approached the same problem from different angles. Marion Nestle in *Food Politics* focuses on lobbying, campaign finance, and regulatory capture as the primary mechanisms that need reform — a more explicitly political solution than Moss offers. David Kessler in *The End of Overeating* emphasizes the neuroscience of conditioned hypereating and argues for a clinical, medicalized approach to food addiction treatment. Mark Schatzker in *The Dorito Effect* argues that the real problem is not the three ingredients but the flavor industry's chemical engineering, which creates artificially intense taste experiences that natural foods cannot compete with.

The most radical alternative is Michael Pollan's argument in *In Defense of Food* and *Cooked*: the solution is not better regulation or better reformulation but a cultural shift back to cooking whole foods from scratch. Pollan's approach is simultaneously less demanding in terms of policy (it requires no legislation) and more demanding in terms of individual behavior (it requires a complete reorientation of how people relate to food). The evidence from public health research suggests that all of these approaches — regulation, clinical treatment, and cultural change — are probably necessary and that no single strategy is sufficient.

### Practical Applications

The book's practical value lies primarily in its consciousness-raising function. Readers who finish Salt Sugar Fat will never look at a supermarket aisle, a nutrition label, or a food advertisement the same way again. The specific practical applications include: recognizing that "low-fat" products often compensate with added sugar and vice versa; understanding that products marketed as "natural" are not necessarily healthier; knowing that the ingredient list length and the presence of chemical-sounding names are proxies for degree of processing; and being aware that the cheapest calories in the grocery store are almost always the most highly processed.

For individuals, the single most practical takeaway is to cook from scratch using whole ingredients. For advocates, the book provides ammunition for campaigns around sugar taxes, marketing restrictions, and labeling reform. For policy makers, it supplies a detailed case study of why voluntary industry regulation has failed and why mandatory standards may be necessary.
