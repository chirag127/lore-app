_The Emperor of All Maladies: A Biography of Cancer_ by Siddhartha Mukherjee
is a six-hundred-page meditation on the oldest adversary of the human body
and its most stubbornly unsolved scientific problem. It begins with a name
written in a papyrus three thousand years ago and ends, for now, in a
genomics laboratory where researchers are sequencing cancer's DNA in real time
and tracing its mutations like detectives reading a suspect's code.

The book is structured in seven parts, each drawing its title from a movement
of Beethoven's Eroica symphony — Da Capo, Andante con moto, Scherzo — giving
the narrative the shape and emotional register of a classical arc.

The story opens in the ancient world. The Edwin Smith Papyrus, dated to around
1600 BCE, describes a breast tumour with disarming precision: a "bulging
mass" spreading across the surface of the body, to be treated by cauterisation
with a fire drill, and — crucially — diagnosed as having no cure. The physician
of 1600 BCE and the oncologist of today agree on the fundamental problem.
What they do not agree on is whether human ingenuity can eventually change
that answer.

Hippocrates, the Greek physician working around 400 BCE, named the disease
_karkinos_ — crab — after the way the veins around a tumour seem to splay
outward like the legs of a crustacean across the seabed. Galen extended this
understanding into a full pathological theory: cancer was caused by an
accumulation of black bile in a specific organ, producing a hardened mass, and
it was generally incurable. Unless it was a cancer of the uterus, in which
case falling menstruation might, in theory, carry it away. This theory — that
cancer was caused by a humoral imbalance, and that its cure required a systemic
shift in the body's chemistry — went largely unchallenged for almost fifteen
hundred years.

The real rupture came in the late 19th century, from two simultaneous
advances: systematic cadaveric anatomy and surgical antisepsis. Once surgeons
could map the body reliably and operate without killing patients from infection
within weeks, the breast became the surgical theatre for cancer medicine.
William Stewart Halsted at Johns Hopkins built the radical mastectomy: the
removal of the entire breast, the pectoral muscles beneath it, and the underarm
lymph nodes. His reasoning was compelling. If cancer began locally and spread
stepwise outward, then cutting further would save more lives.

For a generation, this proved true. Halsted's five-year survival rate went
from the low single digits to something approaching forty percent in a
carefully selected patient population. He became a medical celebrity. His
logic became dogma. And here is the warning that runs through the entire book:
dogma in medicine is rarely benign. Surgeons began performing radical
operations on cancers in the liver, the lung, the pancreas — cancers that
had, in many cases, already escaped long before they were felt. The good
statistics from Halsted's era partly reflected earlier diagnosis in the
forum of the surgeon's hand before the disease had matured; they partly
reflected the natural history of less-aggressive cancers. The dogma that more
surgery meant more survival was not wrong in principle, but it was wrong as
a universal claim, and it produced vast, avoidable human suffering.

Radiation entered oncology within weeks of Roentgen's discovery of X-rays in
1896. By the early 20th century, radiation oncologists were achieving
remissions in cervical cancers and lymphomas that surgery could not reach.
But radiation was both a weapon and a risk: the same energy that killed
cancer cells could damage healthy tissue and, over decades, cause new cancers
in the very field it had treated. Oncologists had to learn a new kind of
judgement: the art of knowing how much radiation is enough, and when more
is deadly.

The chemotherapy identity arrived from chemical warfare. During World War II,
Yale pharmacologists Alfred Gilman and Louis Goodman received reports that
survivors of the 1943 Bari air raid — where an American ship loaded with
mustard gas had been bombed — had been left with drastically depleted white
blood cell counts. They reasoned: if mustard gas destroys rapidly dividing
white blood cells, perhaps it can be used against any rapidly dividing cell —
including the leukemic blast cells killing children in hospital wards. In 1942,
they began treating lymphoma patients with nitrogen mustard. Tumours melted.
Patients left hospital in apparent remission. Weeks or months later, they
relapsed and died.

This first wave of chemotherapy established three things that would shape
oncology for the next seventy years. One: cancer could be attacked systemically
— by an agent in the bloodstream, not just by a scalpel or by external
radiation. Two: toxicity was profound and universal; every dividing cell in
the body was collateral damage. Three: the remissions were temporary. The
disease came back, and when it returned, it often returned more aggressively.

Sidney Farber, a pathologist in Boston, took the clinical principle further.
In 1948, he began giving aminopterin to children with acute lymphoblastic
leukaemia — children who would otherwise die within months. Blood counts
recovered. Remissions were maintained for months or years. Some children left
the hospital and went back to school. It was the first proof-of-concept for
systemic chemotherapy, and it made Sidney Farber the most visible oncologist
in the country — and eventually the founder of the Dana–Farber Cancer
Institute, the architect of the national campaign for the National Cancer Act.

That Act was signed by President Richard Nixon in 1971. It declared war on
cancer. It poured newly committed federal funds into the National Cancer
Institute and created the infrastructure of comprehensive cancer centres and
cooperative treatment groups that still underpins clinical cancer research
today. The results — best captured in the work of Gordon Zubrod and the
early cooperative groups — were genuine and substantial. Childhood acute
lymphoblastic leukaemia went from an invariably fatal disease to a disease
with cure rates above eighty percent. Hodgkin lymphoma, metastatic testicular
cancer, and several childhood cancers followed. For adults with the most
common solid tumours, the gains came more slowly and more unevenly. By the
time Mukherjee wrote the book, overall cancer mortality in the United States
had been falling — but only gradually, and only for specific cancer types.

The molecular revolution entered oncology with the discovery, in 1981, that
cancer can be driven by mutations in specific genes identical to their normal
counterparts — proto-oncogenes — transformed into active drivers by single
base changes. The first human oncogene identified by molecular cloning was
_v-src_. It was followed by _ras_, _myc_, _erb-b_, and eventually a catalogue
of genes that now runs into the hundreds. The conceptual framework was
profoundly Darwinian: cancer is not a foreign invader; it is the patient's
own cells, evolving under selective pressure within the body, acquiring
mutations that confer survival advantages — faster growth, resistance to cell
death, the ability to recruit new blood supply, the capacity to invade other
organs.

Harold Varmus and J. Michael Bishop, who shared the Nobel Prize in 1989 for
this work, had cerebral origins far from medicine:bishop was a literature
scholar before he became a virologist. Their story is a reminder that the
conceptual revolution in oncology was driven not only by clinicians with
patients at their bedside but by basic scientists asking questions that
medicine had not thought to ask.

The evolutionary logic of cancer — a genetically unstable population within a
body, adapting to hostile environments including chemotherapy, evolving
resistance through mutation and selection — is a theme that recurs. It is the
reason chemotherapy alone rarely cures metastatic disease: the selective
pressure of treating one clone enables a pre-existing resistant minority to
take over. And it is the conceptual bridge to what comes next.

That next was Gleevec — imatinib, a drug that inhibits the specific tyrosine
kinase produced by the BCR-ABL fusion gene created by the Philadelphia
chromosome translocation in chronic myeloid leukaemia. In a 1999 phase one
trial, patients who had exhausted every medical alternative entered complete
cytogenetic remission on a pill taken by mouth, with remarkably little side
effect. In 2001, the FDA approved imatinib in record time. Over the next
decade, CML transformed from a disease with a median survival of three to
five years into one in which most patients live normal lifespans on an ongoing
dose. Gleevec's story became oncology's founding myth of targeted therapy:
find the mutation driving a cancer, build a drug against it, watch the
disease respond.

But Gleevec proved the rule in two senses. Most cancers are not like CML.
Solid tumours do not depend on a single driver oncogene. They are
evolutionarily heterogeneous ecosystems with redundant survival pathways.
The majority of patients with non-CML cancers have not yet benefited from
the precision-oncology paradigm in clinically meaningful ways.

The immunotherapy revolution — the checkpoint inhibitors blocking PD-1 and
PD-L1, the CAR-T cell therapies, the bispecific antibodies — was beginning
to appear when the book was published. It has since produced genuine, durable
responses in melanoma, lung cancer, Hodgkin lymphoma, and other previously
hard-to-treat cancers, though the mechanism is not molecular precision but
immune-system release. Mukherjee acknowledges the trend but the data were
still thin when he wrote, and the full importance of these therapies is
something he anticipated with characteristic modesty rather than certainty.

The final section of the book turns from treatment to survivorship and
prevention — the areas where oncology has been slowest to move. For every
patient cured of their disease, there is a future of surveillance, anxiety,
chronic treatment-related morbidity, and sometimes secondary malignancies from
the therapies that saved them. For every patient with incurable cancer, there
is a need for palliation, emotional and social support, and honest
communication — all areas underdeveloped in oncology's professional culture.

Mukherjee, who is himself an oncologist, writes this from direct experience.
He writes the history as someone who has also lived it: prescribing the same
alkylating agents he describes in the chapters on chemotherapy; watching
patients the age of his own children enter remission and then relapse; feeling
the particular weight of a conversation in which the chief, the only, and
perhaps the most honest word one can offer is: we do not yet know.

The last passages have a quiet, elegiac quality. He describes the experience
of a family whose daughter has died — not of a failure of medicine, but in
spite of every available therapeutic option — and the doctor left holding a
remedy that does not remedy. This is where the biography metaphor becomes most
honest. Cancer is not a character that will be vanquished in a climactic scene.
It is an environment that living cells have inhabited for as long as cells
have evolved, and in which humans are temporarily interlopers. The story
continues. The emperor has not abdicated.
