_The Man Who Mistook His Wife for a Hat and Other Clinical Tales_ is Oliver Sacks's most famous work and a landmark of 20th-century medical writing. Published in 1985, it collects twenty-four case studies drawn from Sacks's decades of practice as a neurologist. The patients described here suffer from conditions that seem almost unbelievable: a man who cannot recognize his own wife's face and mistakes her head for his hat; a sailor trapped in 1945, unable to form a single new memory; a woman who loses all sensation of her own body and must learn to control it by sight alone; twins who cannot do basic arithmetic but can generate six-digit prime numbers for fun.

But Sacks is not interested in mere clinical oddity. He approaches each patient as a whole person — a human being living inside a strange and often terrifying neurological condition, finding ways to adapt, compensate, and sometimes even flourish. The book is divided into four parts: "Losses" (conditions involving deficits of normal function), "Excesses" (conditions involving surpluses or distortions), "Transports" (altered states and involuntary recollections), and "The World of the Simple" (the unexpected gifts of intellectually disabled patients). Each section reveals Sacks's central conviction: that the brain's mysteries cannot be understood through scans and tests alone, but require the deep, patient, compassionate attention that he calls "the art of the clinical tale."
