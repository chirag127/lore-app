## Chapter 1: Introduction

Craig opens with a brief history of robotics, tracing the term to Karel Capek's 1920 play *Rossum's Universal Robots* and noting the first industrial robot — the Unimate — installed at a General Motors plant in 1961. He defines a robot as "a reprogrammable, multifunctional manipulator designed to move materials, parts, tools, or specialized devices through variable programmed motions for the performance of a variety of tasks." The chapter distinguishes between manipulators (fixed-base arms), mobile robots, and other categories, but the book focuses entirely on serial-link manipulators. Craig introduces the standard notation used throughout: frames are denoted {A}, vectors are written with a leading superscript indicating the reference frame (e.g., ^AP), and rotation matrices are 3x3 orthonormal matrices with determinant +1. He outlines the structure of the book: Chapters 2-8 cover kinematics, dynamics, and mechanical design (the "mechanics" half); Chapters 9-11 cover control theory; Chapters 12-13 cover programming and simulation. The chapter closes with an overview of robot anatomy: links, joints (revolute R and prismatic P), degrees of freedom, workspace, and a classification of common robot geometries (articulated, SCARA, Cartesian, cylindrical, spherical) with their respective advantages and applications. Precision and accuracy are distinguished: repeatability (the ability to return to a taught position) versus absolute accuracy (the ability to reach a commanded position). Craig notes that industrial robots typically achieve 0.1 mm repeatability but may have 1-2 mm absolute accuracy, the gap bridged by calibration.

## Chapter 2: Spatial Descriptions and Transformations

This foundational chapter introduces the mathematical language used throughout the book. A **position** is represented as a 3x1 vector; an **orientation** is represented as a 3x3 rotation matrix whose columns are the unit axes of the rotated frame expressed in the reference frame. A **frame** combines position and orientation into a 4x4 homogeneous transformation matrix of the form [R p; 0 1]. Craig presents three interpretations of these matrices: a description (representing the pose of one frame relative to another), a mapping (changing the description of a vector from one frame to another), and an operator (moving a vector within the same frame). He covers the fundamental rotation matrices R_x(theta), R_y(theta), R_z(theta) and shows how to compose them. The chapter introduces:
- X-Y-Z fixed-angle representation (roll-pitch-yaw)
- Z-Y-X Euler angles
- The equivalent angle-axis representation (Rodrigues' formula)
- Quaternions are mentioned briefly in the 4th edition as an alternative for orientation representation
Transformation arithmetic is covered in detail: composition of transformations corresponds to matrix multiplication, and the inverse of a transformation [R p; 0 1] is [R^T -R^T p; 0 1]. Transform equations of the form {A}T = {B}T * {C}T are solved by pre- and post-multiplying. The chapter closes with computational considerations, noting that because rotation matrices are orthonormal, their inversion reduces to a transpose operation — saving significant computation.

## Chapter 3: Forward Kinematics

Forward kinematics answers: given the joint angles of a manipulator, what is the pose of the end-effector? Craig introduces the **Denavit-Hartenberg (DH) convention** — the standard method for assigning coordinate frames to each link of a serial manipulator. The four DH parameters are: a_i (link length, distance along X_i between Z_{i-1} and Z_i), alpha_i (link twist, angle from Z_{i-1} to Z_i about X_i), d_i (link offset, distance along Z_{i-1} to the common normal), and theta_i (joint angle, rotation about Z_{i-1}). The individual link transformation matrix A_i is the product of four elementary transformations: Rot_z(theta_i), Trans_z(d_i), Trans_x(a_i), Rot_x(alpha_i). The overall forward kinematic equation is ^0T_n = A_1 A_2 ... A_n. Craig walks through the PUMA 560 manipulator as a complete example — a 6-axis articulated arm with revolute joints — computing symbolic expressions for each A_i matrix and combining them. He also treats simple 2-link and 3-link planar arms to build intuition. The chapter emphasizes that forward kinematics is unique: given joint angles, there is exactly one end-effector pose.

## Chapter 4: Inverse Kinematics

Inverse kinematics asks the reverse question: given a desired end-effector pose, what joint angles achieve it? This is harder — there may be zero, one, multiple, or infinitely many solutions. Craig classifies solutions as analytical (closed-form) or numerical. Closed-form solutions exist for manipulators satisfying sufficient conditions (e.g., three consecutive axes intersect at a point, or three consecutive axes are parallel). The PUMA 560 is again the running example: because the wrist axes intersect at a point (the "spherical wrist"), the inverse kinematics decouples into position and orientation subproblems. First, the wrist center position is computed from the desired pose. Then the first three joints (arm) are solved for the wrist position using geometric or algebraic methods. Finally, the last three joints (wrist) are solved for the desired orientation. Craig presents both **Pieper's method** (for manipulators with any three consecutive intersecting axes) and general algebraic approaches using trigonometric identities and the Atan2 function. Multiple solutions arise from elbow-up vs elbow-down configurations, wrist-flip options, and should-left vs should-right. The chapter discusses solution selection criteria: joint limits, obstacle avoidance, and smoothness of motion. Numerical methods (Newton-Raphson) are covered for manipulators lacking closed-form solutions.

## Chapter 5: Velocities, Static Forces, and Jacobians

The Jacobian matrix J maps joint velocities (q-dot) to end-effector velocities: v = J(q) * q-dot, where v is a 6-vector of linear and angular velocities. Craig derives the Jacobian by expressing each joint's contribution to the end-effector velocity. For a revolute joint i, the contribution to angular velocity is z_{i-1} * q-dot_i and to linear velocity is z_{i-1} x (p - p_{i-1}) * q-dot_i, where p is the end-effector position. The Jacobian is then built column-by-column, giving the "geometric Jacobian." Craig presents two methods for computing the Jacobian: direct differentiation of the forward kinematics equations, and the cross-product construction. The Jacobian is a function of configuration, and when it loses rank the manipulator is in a **singular configuration** — the end-effector cannot move in certain directions. Singularities are classified as workspace-boundary (at the edge of reachable space) or workspace-interior (within the reachable volume, such as when the wrist center lies on the shoulder axis). Craig also introduces the **Jacobian transpose** for force/torque mapping: static forces at the end-effector are related to joint torques by tau = J^T * F. The statics relationship is derived from the principle of virtual work. Examples include computing the joint torques needed to support a given load at the wrist.

## Chapter 6: Dynamics

Dynamics relates joint torques/forces to the resulting motion. Craig presents both the **Newton-Euler** and **Lagrangian** formulations. The Newton-Euler method is recursive: an outward iteration computes velocities and accelerations of each link (from base to end-effector), then an inward iteration computes forces and torques (from end-effector to base). This approach is computationally efficient — O(n) — and is typically used for real-time control. The Lagrangian method derives the equations of motion from kinetic and potential energy: L = K - P, and the Euler-Lagrange equation d/dt(dL/dq-dot) - dL/dq = tau. For an n-link manipulator this yields n coupled, second-order differential equations of the form M(q)q-ddot + C(q,q-dot)q-dot + G(q) + F(q-dot) = tau, where M is the inertia matrix (symmetric positive definite), C contains Coriolis and centrifugal terms, G is gravity, and F is friction. Craig works through a 2-link planar arm completely, deriving the inertia matrix, Coriolis matrix, and gravity vector term by term. He discusses the **composite rigid-body algorithm** for computing M efficiently and the **recursive Newton-Euler algorithm** (RNE) for computing all dynamic terms simultaneously. The chapter closes with a note on friction modeling (Coulomb and viscous) and the identification of dynamic parameters through offline least-squares estimation from motion data. Craig emphasizes that accurate dynamics models are essential for computed-torque control and that parameter identification is a practical prerequisite for high-performance applications.

## Chapter 7: Trajectory Planning

Trajectory planning determines the time history of joint positions, velocities, and accelerations as the robot moves from one configuration to another. Craig distinguishes between **joint-space** and **Cartesian-space** planning. Joint-space planning computes motion in each joint independently: given initial and final joint angles, a smooth trajectory is generated using polynomials. A cubic polynomial (4 coefficients) satisfies endpoint position and velocity constraints; a quintic polynomial (6 coefficients) additionally constrains endpoint accelerations, eliminating jerk at the start and end. For multi-segment trajectories through intermediate points, Craig presents cubic splines and the blend approach (linear segments with parabolic blends, LSPB). Cartesian-space planning computes the end-effector path in workspace coordinates, then uses inverse kinematics at each timestep. This is more computationally expensive but produces straight-line motion in workspace — important for tasks like welding or adhesive dispensing. Cartesian trajectories must account for orientation interpolation as well. Craig discusses **spline interpolation** using cubic and quintic splines with continuous velocity and acceleration at knot points. He covers both point-to-point motions and continuous-path motions, and notes the importance of checking kinematic singularities and joint limits during trajectory generation.

## Chapter 8: Mechanical Design of Robots

This chapter covers the mechanical engineering aspects of robot design. Craig discusses actuator types: electric (DC servo motors, brushless motors, stepper motors), hydraulic, and pneumatic. Electric actuators dominate modern industrial robots due to their controllability and cleanliness. Transmission systems are covered: gears (harmonic drives are highlighted for their zero-backlash property), belts, chains, and ball screws. Harmonic drives — consisting of a wave generator, flexspline, and circular spline — achieve high reduction ratios (50:1 to 300:1) in a compact package. Craig explains the effect of gearing on reflected inertia: the motor sees the load inertia divided by the square of the gear ratio. Sensors covered include: resolvers and encoders (absolute and incremental) for joint position measurement, tachometers for velocity, and force/torque sensors for the wrist. Structural design considerations include stiffness, natural frequency, and minimizing deflection. Craig discusses the concept of **stiffness** (both joint and link) and its effect on positioning accuracy and bandwidth. The chapter also covers gears and gear trains, preloading to eliminate backlash, and the tradeoffs between different transmission types. A short section on robot wrist design shows how three actuators (often routed through concentric hollow shafts) drive three intersecting roll-pitch-yaw axes.

## Chapter 9: Linear Control

This chapter introduces linear control theory as applied to robot manipulators. Craig presents the block diagram approach starting from the second-order dynamic equation. He covers **PID control** (proportional-integral-derivative) in detail: the PD controller provides stiffness and damping, while the integral term eliminates steady-state error due to gravity or friction. The closed-loop system for a single joint (decoupled approximation) is a second-order system characterized by natural frequency omega_n and damping ratio zeta. Craig shows how to select PID gains to achieve desired transient response: zeta = 1 (critically damped) for fastest response without overshoot, or zeta < 1 for faster response with some overshoot. The **independent joint control** approach treats each joint as a separate SISO system, ignoring coupling effects — valid when the robot moves slowly. Craig covers:
- Proportional control and steady-state error
- PD control for improved damping
- PID control for eliminating steady-state error
- Feedforward control to compensate for known disturbances
The chapter discusses actuator dynamics (the voltage-to-torque relationship in DC motors) and how the electrical time constant is usually negligible compared to the mechanical time constant. Craig introduces the concept of **joint flexibility** (due to harmonic drives) and its effect on control — a fourth-order system with two resonant modes. The Nyquist stability criterion and root locus methods are used for controller design. Craig discusses gain tuning as a practical skill: Ziegler-Nichols rules for initial PID gains, then iterative refinement based on observed response. He notes that in practice, robot controllers use lower gains than theory allows because of unmodeled flexibility, sensor noise, and actuator limits.

## Chapter 10: Non-Linear Control

Recognizing that robot dynamics are inherently nonlinear and coupled, Craig presents nonlinear control strategies. **Computed-torque control** (also called "feedback linearization") is the key idea: cancel the nonlinear dynamics by choosing control tau = M-hat(q)[q-ddot_d + K_v e-dot + K_p e] + C-hat + G-hat, where M-hat, C-hat, G-hat are the estimated model terms. If the model is perfect, this reduces the system to the linear error dynamics e-ddot + K_v e-dot + K_p e = 0. Craig discusses robustness to modeling errors and the tradeoffs between model accuracy and computational cost. He covers **adaptive control** — when the robot's parameters (mass, inertia, friction) are unknown or vary during operation, an adaptation law estimates them online. The **Lyapunov-based adaptive controller** uses a parameter update law derived from a Lyapunov function to guarantee stability. A simpler approach is **model reference adaptive control (MRAC)**. Craig also presents **resolved motion rate control** (first formulated by Whitney in 1969): using the Jacobian inverse or pseudo-inverse to map desired end-effector velocities to joint velocities. He discusses the **singular value decomposition (SVD)** for analyzing the Jacobian and handling singularities — when the Jacobian is singular, a damped least-squares solution (using the pseudo-inverse) provides a feasible approximation. Nonlinear control becomes essential at high speeds, where coupling effects (Coriolis forces at 40%+ of maximum torque) cannot be ignored.

## Chapter 11: Force Control

Force control is needed when the robot makes contact with the environment — grinding, deburring, assembly, polishing — where pure position control would generate excessive forces. Craig distinguishes **passive** (mechanical compliance) and **active** (computer-controlled) force control. The key concept is that in contact, the environment imposes kinematic constraints: the robot can only move in certain directions (the **natural constraints**), while in other directions forces are regulated. Craig presents **Mason's taxonomy**: natural position constraints occur when the environment prevents motion, natural force constraints occur when the environment cannot sustain a force. **Artificial constraints** are the user-specified position/force setpoints consistent with natural constraints. The **hybrid position/force control** framework (proposed by Raibert and Craig, 1981) uses a selection matrix S to split the task space into position-controlled and force-controlled directions. Position control is used in unconstrained directions; force control in constrained directions. For force control, Craig presents:
- Simple proportional force control (force error drives torque)
- Force control with inner velocity loop
- Implicit force control (stiffness control) — treat the robot as a programmable spring
- Explicit force control with integral action for zero steady-state error
The chapter also covers **impedance control** (Hogan, 1985), which regulates the dynamic relationship between force and motion rather than controlling force directly. Impedance control is especially useful for tasks where contact geometry is uncertain. The stability of force control is analyzed: contact with a stiff environment makes force control prone to instability, requiring lower gains or additional sensors.

## Chapter 12: Programming Languages and Systems

This chapter surveys robot programming approaches. **Lead-through programming** (manually guiding the robot through motions) and **textual programming** (writing code) are distinguished. Early robot languages include VAL (Unimation), AML (IBM), and AL (Stanford). Craig describes the **VAL language** in some detail: it includes motion commands (MOVE, APPROACH, DEPART), tool and frame definitions, signal I/O, and branching. He then discusses task-level programming, where the programmer specifies what to do rather than how to do it — the system automatically plans motions, grasps, and assembly sequences. Craig presents the **Stanford AI Lab's "shakey"** project and the development of **automatic planning** systems (STRIPS). He covers **sensor integration** in programming: how to use vision, force, and proximity sensors to adapt robot behavior to varying conditions. The chapter discusses **robot operating systems** — not ROS (which did not exist when the 3rd edition was published), but rather the hierarchical control architecture model: task planner, trajectory planner, servo controller. Craig notes that robot languages have evolved from assembly-language-like motion primitives to high-level object-oriented systems. The 4th edition adds references to modern approaches including the Robot Operating System (ROS), though the treatment is brief.

## Chapter 13: Simulation and Off-Line Programming

The final chapter covers simulation tools used to develop and validate robot programs without taking production robots offline. Craig discusses the **computational geometry** needed for collision detection: modeling robots and their environments as convex polyhedra, computing distances between objects, and detecting intersections. Cell-decomposition and hierarchical bounding-volume (sphere, axis-aligned bounding box, oriented bounding box) approaches are introduced. **Kinematic simulation** (computing forward and inverse kinematics offline), **dynamic simulation** (integrating the equations of motion forward in time), and **graphical simulation** (rendering the robot and its environment) are distinguished. Craig covers the **Newton-Euler dynamics simulation**: at each timestep, compute joint accelerations from tau = M q-ddot + ... using the RNE algorithm, then integrate to get velocities and positions. Numerical integration methods (Euler, Runge-Kutta 4th order, predictor-corrector) are discussed with their stability and accuracy tradeoffs. The chapter closes with off-line programming systems that generate robot programs automatically from CAD models of parts and workcells. These systems reduce programming time and allow path optimization and cycle-time estimation before the physical robot is installed. Craig discusses sensor-based simulation (modeling noisy sensor readings and uncertainty) and the role of calibrated virtual cells to bridge the gap between simulated and real robot behavior — a gap that remains one of the central challenges of off-line programming.

## Reading Guide

**Sufficiency:** Craig's book provides a complete, self-contained introduction to the theory of serial-link robotic manipulators. All necessary mathematics (linear algebra, rigid-body transformations, differential equations) is developed within the text. For the intended audience — senior undergraduates and first-year graduate students with basic engineering mechanics background — no other textbook is needed. However, the book does not cover mobile robotics, computer vision, or machine learning for robotics.

**Recommended path:** Chapters 2-3 are essential for anyone — they establish the fundamental mathematical language. Read Chapters 4-6 selectively: Chapter 4 (inverse kinematics) is important for applications; Chapter 5 (Jacobians) is essential for understanding control; Chapter 6 (dynamics) can be skimmed on first reading if the primary interest is kinematics. Chapters 9-11 are the control sequence and should be read together. Chapters 1, 8, 12, and 13 can be read independently or assigned as supplementary reading.

**Chapters to focus on:** Chapter 2 (spatial transformations) is the most foundational. Chapter 5 (Jacobians) is the bridge between kinematics and control. Chapter 10 (nonlinear control) contains the most important modern control material.

**Chapters to skip on first reading:** Chapter 7 (trajectory planning) can be skipped if the focus is on servo-level control. Chapter 8 (mechanical design) is more relevant to mechanical engineers. Chapters 12-13 (programming, simulation) are overview chapters that can be read quickly.
