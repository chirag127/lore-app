## The Key Problem

The central problem *The Richest Man in Babylon* addresses is financial poverty despite adequate earning power. Bansir the chariot builder and Kobbi the musician represent a specific kind of poverty: not the poverty of unemployment or disability, but the poverty that persists even when a capable person works steadily. The problem is not low income but a missing framework for money management.

Clason identifies the root cause as ignorance of financial principles, specifically the failure to distinguish between earning and keeping. Most people believe that increasing income will solve their financial problems, but Arkad's teaching is that income without a savings discipline is useless—a bigger paycheck merely leads to bigger expenses. The real problem is behavioral: people do not know how to manage what they earn because no one has taught them.

---

## Proposed Solution

Clason proposes a seven-part solution (the Seven Cures) structured as a progressive system:

**First — Save 10%:** Pay yourself first by setting aside a tenth of all earnings before spending on anything else. This is not optional; it is the foundational habit without which no wealth can be built.

**Second — Budget:** Distinguish between necessities and desires. Create a written budget that limits total spending to nine-tenths of income. The remaining tenth must be treated as non-negotiable.

**Third — Invest:** The saved tenth must earn returns. Lend it, invest it, or use it to acquire assets that generate income. Hoarded gold does no one any good.

**Fourth — Protect:** Guard against loss by investing cautiously, checking security, and seeking expert advice. The first duty of capital is preservation.

**Fifth — Own Assets:** Use savings to acquire productive assets, starting with a home. Ownership reduces ongoing costs and builds equity.

**Sixth — Plan Long-Term:** Prepare for retirement and family protection through insurance or dedicated savings. Wealth building has a multi-decade horizon.

**Seventh — Invest in Yourself:** Continuously develop skills and knowledge to increase earning capacity. The most important asset is your own ability to earn.

## Evidence and Argumentation

Clason's argument rests entirely on anecdotal and narrative evidence, not on empirical data. Each parable functions as a controlled experiment: a character applies the principles and succeeds, or violates them and fails. Nomasir's report on the Five Laws of Gold is the clearest example—he explicitly states that following the laws produced wealth and breaking them produced losses.

The book's argumentation is rhetorical rather than analytical. Clason uses repetition, memorable phrasing, and narrative identification to persuade. When Arkad says "For every ten coins thou placest within thy purse take out for use but nine," the reader does not need a statistical analysis—the rule is so clear and simple that it feels self-evidently correct.

The limitations of this approach are significant. The book offers no data on savings rates, no analysis of alternative strategies, no discussion of when the principles might not apply. It assumes universal applicability based on the authority of the fictional Arkad rather than on evidence.

---

## Unresolved Questions

- What about people earning so little that saving 10% would leave them unable to meet basic needs? The book does not address the poverty trap.
- How should a person evaluate investment options in a modern economy? "Lend to those who repay" offers no framework for comparing stocks, bonds, real estate, ETFs, or alternative assets.
- What is the right amount of insurance, and what types are appropriate? The book recommends insurance but offers no specific guidance.
- How do inflation, taxes, and market cycles affect the advice? These are not mentioned.
- Is homeownership always the right move? The book assumes yes, but modern analysis shows it depends on market conditions, mobility needs, and alternative investment returns.
- What about systemic barriers—discrimination, disability, medical debt, student loans—that can prevent even disciplined people from building wealth? The book's assumption that anyone who follows the principles will succeed ignores these realities.

## Criticisms

**Carles Carrera (Medium, 2023):** Found the book "quite boring" and noted his 14-year-old son could not finish it. The archaic language and repetitive structure are genuine barriers to modern readers.

**Gender and representation critics:** The book's exclusively male framing is a significant limitation. Women appear only as wives. The advice assumes a male breadwinner in a traditional household structure, reducing its relevance for modern readers of all genders.

**Financial analysts:** Many financial professionals consider the book too simplistic to be useful as anything more than a motivational starting point. It provides no framework for evaluating investments, no guidance on risk assessment, and no discussion of modern financial products.

**Housing market skeptics:** The book's universal advocacy of homeownership has been criticized by analysts who note that in many markets, renting and investing the difference produces better outcomes than buying.

## Alternative Solutions

| Approach | Author | Comparison to *Richest Man in Babylon* |
|---|---|---|
| The Total Money Makeover | Dave Ramsey | Modern expansion of similar principles with explicit debt snowball; more detailed but less philosophical |
| The Simple Path to Wealth | JL Collins | Focuses on index fund investing; provides specific investment guidance that Clason lacks |
| Your Money or Your Life | Vicki Robin | Examines the relationship between money and life satisfaction more deeply; compatible with Clason's savings emphasis |
| I Will Teach You to Be Rich | Ramit Sethi | Contemporary approach emphasizing automation and conscious spending; more practical for modern financial systems |
| Die With Zero | Bill Perkins | Contrarian view: argues for optimizing lifetime experiences over maximizing wealth; directly challenges Clason's accumulation focus |

## Practical Applications

Despite its limitations, *The Richest Man in Babylon* offers techniques that remain genuinely useful:

1. **The 10% savings rule** — Set up automatic transfers of 10% of every paycheck into a separate savings or investment account before you see the money. Behavioral economics confirms this works better than trying to save whatever is left at month-end.
2. **The written budget** — Track every expense for a month, then create a budget that limits total spending to 90% of income. The act of writing forces awareness.
3. **The "is this necessary?" test** — Before any non-essential purchase, ask: is this a genuine need or an inflated desire? The question alone reduces impulse spending.
4. **Seeking expert advice** — For investment decisions beyond your expertise, consult a fee-only fiduciary advisor rather than acting on tips or hunches.
5. **Debt elimination plan from the Clay Tablets** — Communicate honestly with creditors, create a written repayment plan, and allocate a specific percentage of income to debt reduction until the debt is zero.
6. **Skill investment** — Dedicate time and money each year to developing skills that increase earning capacity. The highest-yielding investment is often in yourself.
