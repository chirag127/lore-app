# Interpersonal Skills & Emotional Intelligence

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Emotional intelligence (EQ), popularized by Daniel Goleman in 1995, refers to the ability to recognize, understand, manage, and reason with emotions in oneself and others. Goleman's framework — self-awareness, self-regulation, empathy, social skills, and motivation — provided a vocabulary for a set of skills that were previously unnamed. Research has since shown that EQ predicts relationship satisfaction, job performance, leadership effectiveness, and mental health better than IQ in many contexts.

This sub-subcategory exists because interpersonal skills are taught nowhere and expected everywhere. Schools teach reading, writing, and arithmetic but not how to listen, how to resolve conflict, or how to express emotions constructively. Yet these skills determine the quality of relationships, the trajectory of careers, and the texture of daily life. This space collects the best evidence-based communication frameworks — Nonviolent Communication, Crucial Conversations, active listening, conflict resolution — and presents them as a learnable skill set.

## What Belongs Here

- Emotional intelligence frameworks (Goleman, Bar-On)
- Active listening: paraphrasing, validation, open questions
- Nonviolent Communication (Rosenberg): observations, feelings, needs, requests
- Conflict resolution: interest-based negotiation, de-escalation
- Empathy: cognitive and emotional dimensions
- Social awareness: reading social cues, group dynamics
- Assertive communication (see also Assertiveness Training)
- Giving and receiving feedback effectively
- Difficult conversations and holding multiple perspectives

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- Assertiveness training specifically (belongs in its own sub-subcategory)
- Friendship formation and maintenance (belongs in Building Deep Friendships)
- Romantic relationship dynamics (belongs in Romantic Relationships & Attachment)
- Personality typology systems (MBTI, Enneagram — low validity; belongs in Personality Psychology)
- Manipulation or social engineering (belongs in Ethics or not at all)

## Essential Books

1. **Emotional Intelligence: Why It Can Matter More Than IQ** — Daniel Goleman (1995) — The book that introduced EQ to the mainstream, with a comprehensive framework and research overview.
2. **Nonviolent Communication: A Language of Life** — Marshall B. Rosenberg (1999) — Communication framework organized around observations, feelings, needs, and requests.
3. **Crucial Conversations: Tools for Talking When Stakes Are High** — Kerry Patterson, Joseph Grenny, Ron McMillan, and Al Switzler (2002) — Practical tools for handling high-stakes, emotionally charged conversations.
4. **Difficult Conversations: How to Discuss What Matters Most** — Douglas Stone, Bruce Patton, and Sheila Heen (1999) — Harvard Negotiation Project's guide to the structure of hard conversations.
5. **How to Win Friends and Influence People** — Dale Carnegie (1936) — Timeless principles of interpersonal effectiveness: genuine interest, appreciation, remembering names, listening.
6. **Radical Honesty: How to Transform Your Life by Telling the Truth** — Brad Blanton (1994) — Provocative argument for complete honesty as the foundation of authentic relationships.
7. **Never Split the Difference: Negotiating as If Your Life Depended on It** — Chris Voss (2016) — FBI hostage negotiator's techniques for high-stakes communication, applicable to everyday conflicts.
8. **Thanks for the Feedback: The Science and Art of Receiving Feedback Well** — Douglas Stone and Sheila Heen (2014) — Focused on the often-neglected skill of receiving — rather than giving — feedback.

## Key Concepts

- Goleman's EQ domains: self-awareness, self-regulation, empathy, social skill, motivation
- The four components of NVC: observation, feeling, need, request
- Active listening: paraphrase, validate, inquire
- Interest-based vs. position-based negotiation
- Ladder of inference and mental models
- Empathy vs. sympathy distinction
- Social radar and situational awareness
- The Feedback Equation
