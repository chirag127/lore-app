# Romantic Relationships & Attachment

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Romantic relationships are the most intensely personal and consequential domain of adult life — and one of the least systematically taught. Attachment theory, developed by John Bowlby and Mary Ainsworth and extended to adult relationships by Cindy Hazan, Phillip Shaver, and Amir Levine, provides the most empirically grounded framework for understanding romantic dynamics. People develop attachment styles (secure, anxious, avoidant, fearful-avoidant) based on early caregiving experiences, and these styles shape how they approach intimacy, conflict, and commitment.

This sub-subcategory exists to bring research-based understanding to a domain dominated by folk wisdom, gender stereotypes, and commercial dating advice. It covers attachment theory, communication patterns in couples (John Gottman's research on what predicts divorce), the stages of relationships, love languages (Gary Chapman's popular but weakly supported framework), and practical skills for partnership. It is distinct from general social skills because romantic relationships involve unique dynamics of physical intimacy, long-term commitment, cohabitation, and merging of lives.

## What Belongs Here

- Attachment theory: secure, anxious, avoidant, fearful-avoidant styles
- Gottman's research: the Four Horsemen, bids for connection, conflict patterns
- Love languages (with caveats about limited evidence)
- Sternberg's triangular theory of love: intimacy, passion, commitment
- Relationship stages: limerence, power struggle, conscious partnership
- Communication in couples: conflict resolution, repair attempts
- Dating skills: initiating, reading interest, rejection
- Long-term relationship maintenance: rituals, novelty, friendship
- Breakups, divorce, and relationship endings

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- General friendship building (belongs in Building Deep Friendships)
- General interpersonal skills (belongs in Interpersonal Skills & EQ)
- Sex therapy or clinical sexuality (belongs in Sexology or Clinical Psychology)
- Family therapy or parenting (belongs in Family Psychology)
- Dating apps or pickup artistry (partially related but distinct)
- Marriage counseling as a clinical practice (belongs in Clinical Psychology)

## Essential Books

1. **Attached: The New Science of Adult Attachment and How It Can Help You Find — and Keep — Love** — Amir Levine and Rachel Heller (2010) — Accessible introduction to adult attachment theory and its practical implications for romantic relationships.
2. **The Seven Principles for Making Marriage Work: A Practical Guide from the Country's Foremost Relationship Expert** — John M. Gottman and Nan Silver (1999) — Based on Gottman's 40+ years of research, with specific, evidence-based interventions for couples.
3. **Hold Me Tight: Seven Conversations for a Lifetime of Love** — Sue Johnson (2008) — Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT) for couples, based on attachment theory, presented as a self-help program.
4. **All About Love: New Visions** — bell hooks (2000) — Cultural critique and redefinition of love as an act of will and action, not just feeling.
5. **Mating in Captivity: Unlocking Erotic Intelligence** — Esther Perel (2006) — Exploring the tension between domestic security and erotic desire in long-term relationships.
6. **The Relationship Cure: A 5 Step Guide to Strengthening Your Marriage, Family, and Friendships** — John M. Gottman and Joan DeClaire (2001) — Focused on the concept of "bids for connection" and emotional banking.
7. **Getting the Love You Want: A Guide for Couples** — Harville Hendrix (1988) — Imago Relationship Therapy framework, combining attachment theory with depth psychology.
8. **Love Sense: The Revolutionary New Science of Romantic Relationships** — Sue Johnson (2013) — Summarizes the research base for EFT and attachment-based couple therapy.

## Key Concepts

- Attachment styles: secure, anxious, avoidant, fearful-avoidant
- The Four Horsemen: criticism, contempt, defensiveness, stonewalling
- Bids for connection and turning toward vs. turning away
- Gottman's 5:1 positivity ratio
- Sternberg's triangular theory: intimacy, passion, commitment
- Limerence vs. mature love
- The pursuer-distancer dynamic
- Repair attempts and emotional banking
