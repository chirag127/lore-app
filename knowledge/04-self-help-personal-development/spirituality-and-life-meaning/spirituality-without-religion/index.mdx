# Spirituality Without Religion

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

A growing portion of the population — particularly in Western societies — identifies as spiritual but not religious (SBNR). These individuals seek transcendent experience, a sense of connection to something larger than themselves, and practices that cultivate inner peace and perspective — but without adherence to institutional religion or theological doctrines. This is not a rejection of spirituality but a redefinition of it for a secular age.

This sub-subcategory exists to curate the practices and frameworks that serve this need. It draws on contemplative neuroscience (the study of meditation's effects on the brain), the psychology of awe (Dacher Keltner's research on the "self-diminishing" emotion), Stoic philosophy (practical wisdom without gods), mindfulness traditions adapted from Buddhism but separated from Buddhist theology, and the growing literature on nature connection as a spiritual practice. It is distinct from both religion (no dogma, no institution) and standard self-help (it addresses the transcendent dimension, not just psychological adjustment).

## What Belongs Here

- Meditation and mindfulness as secular practices
- The psychology of awe and wonder (Keltner)
- Flow states as spiritual experience
- Nature connection and ecological spirituality
- Psychedelic-assisted spiritual experience (research context)
- Mysterianism: embracing not-knowing as a spiritual stance
- Ritual and ceremony without religion
- Sacred reading of secular texts
- The science of contemplative practice

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- Organized religion and theology (belongs in Religion & Spirituality)
- Finding purpose and life meaning (belongs in Finding Purpose & Meaning)
- Clinical applications of mindfulness in therapy (belongs in Clinical Psychology)
- New Age pseudoscience and unsubstantiated claims (critically examined but not promoted)
- Conversion or evangelism of any kind

## Essential Books

1. **Waking Up: A Guide to Spirituality Without Religion** — Sam Harris (2014) — Neuroscientist and philosopher's secular guide to meditation, mindfulness, and spiritual experience grounded in rational inquiry.
2. **The Varieties of Religious Experience** — William James (1902) — The classic psychological study of mystical and religious experience, still relevant for understanding non-religious spirituality.
3. **Awe: The New Science of Everyday Wonder and How It Can Transform Your Life** — Dacher Keltner (2023) — Leading researcher's exploration of awe as a fundamental human emotion with profound spiritual and health implications.
4. **Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind** — Yuval Noah Harari (2011) — While not a self-help book, its exploration of humans as meaning-making animals provides context for the spiritual impulse.
5. **Secular Spirituality: The Next Step Forward** — Phil Zuckerman (2022) — Sociological exploration of how non-religious people find meaning, connection, and transcendence.
6. **The Headspace Guide to Mindfulness and Meditation** — Andy Puddicombe (2011) — Practical, secular introduction to meditation practice, stripped of Buddhist religious context.
7. **The Book of Joy: Lasting Happiness in a Changing World** — Dalai Lama, Desmond Tutu, and Douglas Abrams (2016) — Conversation between two spiritual leaders that works for secular and religious readers alike.
8. **Why Buddhism Is True: The Science and Philosophy of Meditation and Enlightenment** — Robert Wright (2017) — Evolution of the Buddhist analysis of the mind through a secular, scientific lens.

## Key Concepts

- Mysterianism: the spiritual value of not knowing
- Ritual: creating meaning through intentional practice
