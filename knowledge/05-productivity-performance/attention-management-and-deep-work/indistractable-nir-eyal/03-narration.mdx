# Narration

## The Moment That Changed Everything

Picture this: you sit down at your desk. You have an important task —
a report, a proposal, a difficult conversation you've been putting off.
You open your laptop. Three minutes later, you're watching a video of a
dog surfing.

Sound familiar?

Nir Eyal opens his book with this exact scenario, and here is the thing
that will stay with you: it's not your fault. Not in the way you think,
anyway. You are not weak. You are not undisciplined. What you are is
human — and humans run from discomfort with remarkable speed.

Eyal's argument is simple and subversive: the phone is not the problem.
The notification is not the problem. The problem is the feeling you are
trying to escape when you reach for that phone.

---

## Traction and Distraction: The Master Distinction

Before you fix anything, you need a map. Eyal gives you one.

**Traction** is any action that moves you toward what you *want* to do.
**Distraction** is any action that moves you away from what you *want*
to do.

Notice the nuance: both involve doing something. Scrolling Instagram and
writing a quarterly report are both actions. One pulls you away from your
intent; the other serves it. The difference is not the activity — it is
whether that activity lines up with a choice you made in advance.

This distinction matters more than you think. Most productivity advice
treats everything that is not work as bad. Eyal says: no. Playing with
your kids is traction. Calling your friend is traction. Even watching a
show after a long day — if you *chose* it — is traction. The enemy is
not the activity. The enemy is the pull. The feeling that something is
happening *to* you rather than *by* you.

---

## The Uncomfortable Truth

Here is the part most people resist: distraction starts inside.

Eyal spent years designing apps that keep you scrolling. He built
Hooked, the book that teaches companies how to create habit-forming
products. He knows the tricks. And he will tell you: none of them work
unless you are already uncomfortable.

Boredom. Uncertainty. Fatigue. Anxiety. Loneliness. These are the real
triggers. When you feel the pull to check your phone, the phone is not
the cause — it is the symptom. You are trying to escape a feeling.

This is what separates Indistractable from every other book on the shelf.
Most tell you to put the phone down. Eyal tells you to look at the
feeling that made you pick it up. And then, instead of fighting it, you
surf it.

The technique comes from therapy — acceptance and commitment therapy,
or ACT. When the urge hits, don't battle it. Don't obey it. Get curious
about it. Where do you feel it in your body? Is it tight in your chest?
Restless in your legs? Watch it. Breathe with it. Most urges pass in
under ten minutes if you stop feeding them.

## Timeboxing: The Non-Negotiable

Step two of Eyal's model is where most people expect meditation or
mindfulness. Instead they get something that sounds almost too simple to
work: timeboxing.

Schedule every minute of your day.

Not just work. Everything. Meals, breaks, exercise, time with your
partner, time with your kids, and — this is the radical part — time
deliberately set aside for distraction.

Eyal's point is sharp: if you haven't decided what to do with your time,
external forces will decide for you. A notification decides. A colleague
decides. An algorithm decides. Timeboxing is taking back that decision.

The irony many people miss is that when you schedule your scrolling, it
stops being guilt. You are not "procrastinating" at 2 PM if you planned
to do exactly that at 2 PM. You are executing. Guilt belongs to
unintentional distraction. Intentional leisure has no guilt at all.

## Hacking Back the World Around You

Step three is where Eyal the product designer re-emerges. Once you have
mastered internal triggers and designed your time, now you fix your
environment.

External triggers are everything outside you that tugs at your attention:
notifications, pings, popups, even the tap on your shoulder from a
colleague. Eyal's rule: turn everything off by default. Then whitelist
only what genuinely serves you.

For most people, the whitelist is tiny. Phone calls from family.
Calendar alerts. That is about it.

He walks through specific targets: email (check on a schedule, not on
demand), chat (do-not-disturb during deep work), apps (delete and use
web versions instead of keeping the scrollable version on your phone),
and even people (schedule office hours).

The three-step audit is the actionable core here. Track every external
trigger for a week. Analyze which serve you and which don't. Eliminate
the rest. Most people are shocked at how much of their environment was
designed by other people.

## Pacts: Locking In Your Future Self

Step four is precommitment — making it harder to betray your future self
when your present self is tired, hungry, and tempted.

Eyal describes three types:

**Effort pact** — make the distraction harder to reach. Delete the app.
Keep your phone in another room. Use a site blocker with a password only
your future self knows.

**Price pact** — make distraction costly. Bet a friend money. Use an
app like StickK that charges you when you fail. Loss aversion is real —
we hate losing money far more than we enjoy keeping it.

**Identity pact** — the deepest one. Make your distraction inconsistent
with who you believe you are. "I'm not the kind of person who checks
their phone at dinner." This is not self-deception. It is self-definition.
Once a value is internalized, violating it feels like betrayal — and
that feeling is its own punishment.

## The Distraction Triangle

Eyal introduces the distraction triangle to map where triggers come from:

The three vertices are: internal triggers (your feelings), external
triggers (your environment), and your planned activities (your calendar).
Distraction emerges when an internal or external trigger meets an
unscheduled moment.

The fix works on all three sides. Master the internal trigger through
ACT techniques. Design the external environment through audits and
hacks. Fill the calendar through timeboxing so there are no empty spaces
for distraction to fill.

## Culture: The Invisible Force

One section of the book that does not get enough attention is the
chapter on organizational culture. Eyal argues that distraction is not
just an individual problem — it is a cultural symptom.

A manager who answers emails at 10 PM signals that this is expected.
A company with open floor plans and constant interruptions designs
distraction into the architecture. A policy of responding to Slack
within minutes trains everyone to live in reactive mode.

The fixes are concrete: designated focus hours, async-first
communication norms, predictable meeting schedules, and leaders who
model behavior rather than just mandating it.

This section matters because even people with perfect individual systems
break down when their culture rewards reactivity. The four-step model
works best when culture does not undermine it.

## Why Willpower Is Not Enough

Every chapter of this book is, in a sense, an argument against
willpower. Eyal does not say willpower does not exist. He says it is
unreliable, intermittent, and depleted by use.

His alternative is structure: decide in advance. Design the environment
for the version of you that will be tired, hungry, and tempted. The late
you who wants to scroll is real. Build the system for that person —
not for the energized morning version who swears they will focus today.

This is the same logic behind locking your credit card at home when you
are on a diet or putting your alarm across the room when you need to
wake up early. You know you will fail. You plan for the failure.

## Technology: Use It, Don't Reject It

A theme that runs through the book and defies both Silicon Valley hype
and digital minimalism orthodoxy: technology is not the enemy.

Eyal is not a Luddite. He is a former product designer who understands
exactly how these tools capture attention. His argument is that
abstinence does not teach you how to live with technology — it just
temporarily removes the symptom.

The alternative is mindful use. Decide what technology serves your
values. Use it intentionally. Increase the friction of thoughtless use
without rejecting the tool itself.

This is harder than it sounds. It requires constant vigilance. But it is
also more honest than either extreme: the techno-utopian claim that
apps are neutral, or the moral panic that treats every notification as
poison.

## The Paradoxical Theory of Distraction

One of the most quietly powerful ideas in the book is what Eyal calls
the paradoxical theory of distraction: the more you try to suppress a
thought or urge, the stronger it becomes.

The classic example: "Do not think of a pink elephant." What are you
thinking of?

Eyal applies this to the attention problem. When you force yourself to
ignore your phone, you paradoxically increase its psychological pull.
The urge grows. When you acknowledge it, observe it, and let it pass
without feeding it — the urge weakens.

This reframing underlies the entire Step 1. Surfing the urge does not
mean enabling it. It means removing the shame and the fight from the
equation, which is what gives the urge its power in the first place.

## Bringing It Home

So what do you actually do Monday morning?

Start with the internal triggers. Every time you feel the pull to
distract yourself this week, write down what you are feeling. One word:
bored, anxious, tired, uncertain. Look for the pattern.

Then open your calendar. Schedule every block from when you wake up to
when you go to sleep — work blocks, breaks, meals, family time, even
distraction time. Treat it like a contract with yourself.

Perform a notification audit. Every app on your phone that sends a
ping — ask: does this serve me? If the honest answer is no, turn it off.

Design one pact. Pick the distraction that costs you the most time.
Make it harder, costlier, or less consistent with who you are.

And the next time you feel the urge to check your phone mid-task — try
the ten-minute rule. Tell yourself you can look in ten minutes. In most
cases, the urge passes. In the cases where it does not, at least you
chose.

That is the book. Not a transformation — a practice. Not a finish line,
but a direction.
