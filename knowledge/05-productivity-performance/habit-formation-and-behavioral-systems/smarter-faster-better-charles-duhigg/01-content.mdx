## Motivation

Duhigg opens with the story of a woman who survived a plane crash by mentally preparing for emergencies during routine flights. The key to motivation, he argues, is creating a sense of control. When we feel we have chosen a task, we engage with it. When we feel forced, we disengage. The simplest technique is transforming a chore into a choice: "I choose to finish this report" rather than "I have to finish this report."

Locus of control — the belief that you control your own destiny — is the single strongest predictor of motivation. People with an internal locus of control are more motivated, more resilient, and more successful. The good news: locus of control can be trained. Duhigg cites experiments where teaching prisoners to see their actions as choices reduced recidivism.

## Teams

The best teams are not the ones with the highest collective IQ or the most experienced members. Duhigg presents Google's Project Aristotle, which analyzed hundreds of teams to identify what made them effective. The single most important factor was psychological safety — the belief that you can take risks without being penalized. Teams with high psychological safety performed better on every metric. The second factor was conversational turn-taking: the best teams had meetings where everyone spoke roughly equally.

## Focus

Focus is not about eliminating distractions. It is about building mental models that predict what will happen next. Duhigg tells the story of Air France Flight 447, which crashed because the pilots lacked a shared mental model of what was happening to the aircraft. When we build mental models — narratives of cause and effect — we can recognize when reality deviates from expectation and adjust. Productivity systems that externalize mental models (checklists, schedules) improve focus because they free cognitive resources.

## Goal-Setting

Duhigg distinguishes between stretch goals (ambitious, long-term objectives) and SMART goals (specific, measurable, achievable, realistic, time-bound). Both are necessary, but they serve different functions. Stretch goals provide direction and inspiration. SMART goals provide the execution pathway. The most effective approach is to set a stretch goal and then reverse-engineer the SMART goals needed to achieve it. Duhigg uses the story of the Gates Foundation's effort to eradicate malaria to illustrate how ambitious goals require disciplined sub-goaling.

## Managing Others

The key to managing others is not command and control but fostering autonomy. Duhigg describes how the CEO of a manufacturing plant reversed its decline by giving workers decision-making authority over their tasks. When people feel ownership of their work, they engage more deeply and perform better. Micromanagement is the enemy of productivity.

## Decision-Making

The best decision-makers are not those who gather the most data but those who know how to think probabilistically. Duhigg tells the story of a poker champion who made better decisions than opponents who had access to the same information because he thought in terms of probabilities and expected value. The technique: for every decision, ask "what would have to be true for this option to be the right choice?"

## Innovation

Innovation comes from combining existing ideas in new ways. Duhigg presents research on creative teams showing that the most innovative breakthroughs come from people who have broad, diverse networks — not deep expertise in a single domain. The concept of "creative brokering" involves connecting ideas from different fields. Duhigg uses the story of Disney's Frozen to show how the creative team synthesized existing concepts into something new.

## Absorbing Data

The final chapter addresses information overload. Duhigg argues that the problem is not too much data but the wrong kind of data processing. He presents the concept of "information exposure" vs. "information immersion" — rather than trying to consume everything, create systems that expose you to the most important information and let the rest go.
