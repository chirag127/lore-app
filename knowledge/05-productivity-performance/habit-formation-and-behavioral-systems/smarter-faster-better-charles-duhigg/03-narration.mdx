## Writing Style & Voice

Duhigg's writing in Smarter Faster Better is essentially the same as in The Power of Habit — confident, narrative-driven, and meticulously reported. The voice is slightly more expansive, reflecting his increased confidence as an author. The sentences retain the same journalistic precision, but the chapters are longer and the case studies more detailed. If The Power of Habit reads like a series of New Yorker articles adapted for a book, Smarter Faster Better reads like the book Duhigg always intended to write.

## Narrative Structure

The eight chapters are independent — they can be read in any order. Each follows the same structure: opening story, conceptual explanation, supporting research, and practical implications. The independence is both a strength and a weakness — readers can jump to relevant chapters, but the book lacks the cumulative momentum of The Power of Habit. The chapters on motivation and teams are the strongest; they most closely follow the method that made the first book successful.

## Rhetorical Techniques

Duhigg continues to rely on **ethos** (Pulitzer Prize, New York Times) and **pathos** (emotionally compelling case studies). The most effective device is the **contrast narrative** — showing how a person or organization changed from ineffective to effective by applying one of the eight concepts. The Air France Flight 447 story is the book's most memorable: a deadly crash caused by the failure to build a shared mental model illustrates the concept of focus more vividly than any abstract explanation could.

## Readability & Accessibility

Slightly more demanding than The Power of Habit. The concepts are more abstract (mental models, probabilistic thinking) and require more cognitive engagement. The reading level is appropriate for college-educated readers. The book assumes comfort with business and technology contexts in most examples.

## Comparative Context

Among Duhigg's own works, Smarter Faster Better is less culturally influential than The Power of Habit but represents a more ambitious intellectual project. Within the productivity genre, it occupies the high end — it is more intellectually serious than Getting Things Done or The 4-Hour Workweek but less systematic. For readers who found The Power of Habit transformative, Smarter Faster Better offers a satisfying extension of Duhigg's thinking.
