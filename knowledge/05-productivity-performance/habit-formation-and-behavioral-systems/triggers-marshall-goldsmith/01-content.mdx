## Why We Do What We Do

Goldsmith opens with a provocative claim: the environment is more powerful than willpower. We believe we have control over our behavior, but our surroundings trigger responses that override our intentions. A trigger is any stimulus that reshapes our thoughts and actions — people, events, emotions, locations. The book's premise is that lasting behavior change requires managing triggers, not suppressing them.

## The Gap Between Knowing and Doing

The central problem: we know what we should do (exercise, listen more, eat better) but we do not do it. Knowledge is not the issue — everyone knows they should exercise. The issue is that triggers in the moment overwhelm our good intentions. Goldsmith introduces the concept of "the gap" — the space between our intentions and our behavior. This gap is filled with triggers.

## Active Questions

Goldsmith's signature technique is the daily active question process. Instead of asking "how am I doing?" (a passive question), ask "did I do my best to...?" Active questions create daily accountability. They force you to evaluate your behavior against your intentions while the evaluation is still immediate enough to matter.

Goldsmith recommends choosing six to eight active questions covering different life domains (health, relationships, work, personal growth). Each evening, rate yourself on each question on a 1-10 scale. The process works because it creates a daily feedback loop that passive reflection cannot match.

## The Four Factors of Triggering

Goldsmith identifies four categories of triggers:

- **People triggers**: Others can trigger our worst behaviors. Difficult colleagues, demanding bosses, and critical family members are powerful triggers.
- **Situation triggers**: Where we are and what is happening — meetings, traffic, deadlines — create predictable behavioral responses.
- **Emotion triggers**: Fear, anger, excitement, and exhaustion all lower our behavioral standards.
- **Environment triggers**: Physical surroundings — the phone, the TV, the layout of a room — cue behaviors automatically.

## The Structure of Effective Questions

Not all active questions are equal. Effective questions are:

1. **Specific**: "Did I do my best to listen when others were speaking?" rather than "Was I a good listener?"
2. **Measurable**: Each question must be answerable with a 1-10 rating.
3. **Actionable**: The question should point to a behavior you can actually choose.
4. **Non-judgmental**: The purpose is awareness, not self-criticism.

## The Payoff

Goldsmith presents evidence from executive coaching clients who used the active questions process. The payoff is threefold: increased self-awareness (you learn your trigger patterns), increased accountability (you cannot hide from your daily ratings), and increased control (knowing your triggers gives you the power to choose different responses).

## Sustainability

The book concludes with strategies for maintaining behavior change over the long term. Goldsmith emphasizes that there is no finish line — behavior change requires ongoing maintenance. The active questions process is not a temporary fix but a lifelong practice.
