## Book Context & Background

Triggers was published in May 2015, building on the success of Goldsmith's earlier bestseller What Got You Here Won't Get You There (2007). The habit and behavior change market in 2015 was dominated by Duhigg's explanatory framework and Rubin's personality-based approach. Goldsmith carved a distinct niche by focusing on the executive coaching perspective — his book addresses the specific challenge of sustained behavior change for successful, busy people who already know what to do but cannot make it stick.

## About the Author

Marshall Goldsmith is one of the world's most influential executive coaches and leadership thinkers. A PhD in organizational behavior from UCLA, he has been ranked the number one leadership thinker by Thinkers50 and has worked with over 200 major CEOs. His previous book What Got You Here Won't Get You There (2007) is a classic of leadership literature. Goldsmith's credentials give Triggers unusual authority — he is not a journalist writing about behavior change but a practitioner who has spent decades actually changing executive behavior. The limitation of this background is that the book is heavily oriented toward high-achieving professionals and may not resonate with general readers.

## Core Thesis & Argument

The central claim: the gap between what we know we should do and what we actually do is caused by triggers — environmental, emotional, and social stimuli that override our intentions. The solution is not more knowledge or willpower but a structured daily accountability system. The book's core argument rests on the distinction between passive reflection ("How am I doing?") and active questioning ("Did I do my best to...?").

## Thematic Analysis

The theme of **self-deception** runs throughout. Goldsmith argues that we systematically overestimate our discipline and underestimate the power of our environment. This is supported by research on self-serving bias and fundamental attribution error — we attribute our own failures to circumstances and others' failures to character.

The theme of **daily structure** is the practical backbone. Goldsmith does not believe in occasional reflection. Change requires daily, structured attention. This is more demanding than the advice in most habit books, which acknowledge the need for consistency but rarely require the reader to commit to a daily self-assessment.

The theme of **individual responsibility** is central but carefully qualified. Goldsmith acknowledges environmental triggers but ultimately places the responsibility for managing them on the individual. This distinguishes Triggers from books that emphasize systemic change.

## Argumentation & Evidence

Goldsmith's evidence is primarily anecdotal and drawn from his executive coaching practice. The research citations are present but secondary to the case studies from client work. The active questions methodology has not been validated by independent research; its effectiveness is supported by Goldsmith's reputation and client testimonials. For the academic reader, this is a significant limitation. For the practical reader, the framework's intuitive appeal and Goldsmith's authority may be sufficient.

## Strengths

1. **The active questions process is a genuinely novel technique**: No other major habit book proposes this specific daily structure.
2. **Executive-level credibility**: Goldsmith's experience gives the book an authority that journalists and self-help authors cannot match.
3. **Honest about difficulty**: Triggers does not promise easy change — it demands consistent effort, which respects the reader's intelligence.
4. **Targeted at the knowing-doing gap**: This is a real problem that many habit books assume away.
5. **Focus on triggers**: The concept of triggers as the bridge between environment and behavior is more nuanced than simple cue-routine-reward.

## Criticisms & Weaknesses

**1. Narrow audience.** A Kirkus review noted that the book is "clearly aimed at executives" and the examples and language reflect that orientation, making it less accessible to general readers.

**2. Anecdotal evidence base.** The active questions methodology lacks independent validation, and Goldsmith relies heavily on his own coaching case studies.

**3. The daily process is demanding.** While this is also a strength, reviewers note that the 1-10 daily rating process requires a level of discipline that the target audience may lack — creating a chicken-and-egg problem.

**4. Repetitive structure.** Publishers Weekly noted that the book "repeats its core message more often than necessary," with the first half of the book feeling stretched.

**5. Limited attention to systemic barriers.** The individual-responsibility focus means Triggers does not address structural factors (poverty, discrimination, health issues) that create triggers beyond individual control.

## Comparative Analysis

Triggers is the most demanding and least mass-market book in this collection. Compared to Atomic Habits, it requires more daily discipline but offers more targeted results for high performers. Compared to Switch, it is more individually focused and less concerned with organizational dynamics. The closest analog is What Got You Here Won't Get You There, which covers similar ground for professional behavior change. Triggers extends that work into a daily practice framework.

## Impact & Legacy

Triggers has sold over 500,000 copies and is widely used in executive coaching programs. The active questions methodology has been adopted by many Fortune 500 companies as a leadership development tool. The book has not achieved the cultural penetration of Atomic Habits or The Power of Habit, but it occupies a respected niche in the leadership development space.

## Summary Sufficiency

Accuracy rating: 6/10 — Practitioner wisdom rather than validated science; claims should be evaluated with that context. Completeness rating: 7/10 — Framework and process are well-captured; the coaching examples add depth.
