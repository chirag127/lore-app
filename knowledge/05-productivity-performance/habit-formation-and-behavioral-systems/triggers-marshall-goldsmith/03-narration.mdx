## Writing Style & Voice

Marshall Goldsmith writes with the directness and clarity of a seasoned executive coach. His voice is authoritative, occasionally blunt, and never condescending. He addresses the reader as a capable professional who has simply not yet built the right systems. The tone is collaborative rather than prescriptive — Goldsmith shares his own failures (struggling with patience, getting defensive) to create rapport. The prose is clean and efficient, with short paragraphs and frequent bullet lists that make the content digestible.

The most distinctive stylistic feature is the **active voice** — every verb is an action verb, every sentence points toward a specific practice. This mirrors the book's emphasis on active over passive questioning.

## Narrative Structure

Triggers follows a three-act structure: the problem (why we fail to follow through), the solution (active questions), and the application (sustaining change). The first half diagnoses the trigger problem; the second half prescribes the solution. The pivot occurs at the introduction of the active questions process, which changes the book from analytical to prescriptive. The structure creates a satisfying intellectual arc from problem recognition to solution implementation.

## Rhetorical Techniques

Goldsmith's primary technique is **practitioner authority**. Unlike journalists or self-help authors who report on research, Goldsmith speaks from decades of direct experience changing executive behavior. This gives his claims a weight that anecdotal evidence alone would not carry.

The **active/passive distinction** is rhetorically brilliant. By framing passive questions as the problem and active questions as the solution, Goldsmith creates a clear binary that readers can immediately implement. The binary is somewhat artificial — passive reflection has its place — but it is an effective teaching device.

Goldsmith also uses **contrast** effectively: showing the before-and-after of executive coaching clients, letting their transformations demonstrate the method's power.

## Readability & Accessibility

The book is accessible to any adult reader but is clearly oriented toward professionals. The vocabulary is straightforward, but the contexts (corporate meetings, leadership challenges, performance reviews) assume business familiarity. The daily rating system is simple to implement but requires consistent effort. The self-assessment format is the book's most reader-friendly feature.

## Comparative Context

Goldsmith writes with more authority and less narrative flair than Duhigg, more specificity and less warmth than Rubin, and more rigor and less humor than the Heath brothers. Triggers sits at the practical, professional end of the habit spectrum. Within Goldsmith's own body of work, Triggers represents a deepening of his earlier themes — moving from identifying the problem (interpersonal behavior in What Got You Here) to building the daily practice (in Triggers).
