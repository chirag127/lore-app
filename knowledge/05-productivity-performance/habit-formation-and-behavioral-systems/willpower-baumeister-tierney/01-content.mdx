## Introduction: Is Willpower More Important Than IQ?

Baumeister and Tierney open with the marshmallow test and Walter Mischel's decades of follow-up research. Children who could delay gratification at age four had significantly better life outcomes — higher SAT scores, less substance abuse, lower divorce rates. The headline: self-control predicted academic performance more robustly than IQ. This finding sets up the book's central argument: willpower is the greatest human strength, but we have neglected it.

## Chapter 1: The Biology of Self-Control

The authors present the ego depletion model: willpower is a finite resource that gets depleted with use. Baumeister's radish-and-cookie experiment demonstrated this: participants who resisted cookies and ate radishes gave up faster on a subsequent puzzle than those who ate the cookies. The brain uses glucose as fuel for self-control — when blood sugar drops, willpower drops. This has practical implications: make important decisions when your energy is highest, and keep your blood sugar stable.

## Chapter 2: Where Does Willpower Come From?

Self-control draws on a single global resource, not separate reserves for different domains. People who exert self-control in one area (quitting smoking) show depletion in unrelated areas (losing their temper, making poor financial decisions). This "domain-general" model means that conserving willpower across all activities matters.

## Chapter 3: The Value of a Good Name

The authors explore the social dimension of self-control. Reputation — your good name — is a form of social currency built on self-control. People who keep their promises, show up on time, and control their impulses earn trust. The chapter traces historical attitudes toward self-control, arguing that we abandoned a virtue that our ancestors understood.

## Chapter 4: The Power of Planning

Implementation intentions — specific if-then plans — dramatically improve follow-through. Baumeister and Tierney cite Peter Gollwitzer's research showing that specifying when, where, and how you will act makes follow-through nearly automatic. The authors recommend making decisions about when you will perform a behavior, not just what behavior you want to perform.

## Chapter 5: The Marshmallow Test Revisited

This chapter examines Walter Mischel's research in depth. The ability to delay gratification depends on attention control — children who could distract themselves (look away from the marshmallow, imagine it as a cloud) succeeded. The lesson: willpower is not about brute resistance but about strategic attention management.

## Chapter 6: The Judgment of Paris

The authors explore decision fatigue — the phenomenon where the quality of decisions degrades as willpower depletes. The famous study of parole judges showed that the same judge gave drastically different sentences depending on how much decision-making they had already done (morning vs. afternoon). The solution: limit the number of decisions you make each day.

## Chapter 7: The Pursuit of Happiness

Self-control paradoxically enables happiness by making it possible to pursue long-term goals, maintain relationships, and avoid behaviors that create regret. The authors argue that instant gratification creates short-term pleasure but long-term misery.

## Chapter 8: A User's Guide to Self-Control

The concluding chapter provides practical recommendations: monitor your behavior (tracking alone improves control), set specific goals, prioritize sleep and nutrition (which affect glucose levels), and build habits that automate good behavior.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment

This summary captures the major research findings but cannot replicate the depth of the original studies or the historical narrative.

### Recommended Reading Path

| Reader Type | Time | What to Read |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | ~15 min | This summary |
| Interested | ~4 hr | Chapters 1-4 + Chapter 8 |
| Scholar | ~12 hr | Full book + cited studies |

### Chapters to Read in Full

- **Chapter 1** — The ego depletion model and its evidence
- **Chapter 6** — Decision fatigue (the most practical chapter)
- **Chapter 8** — Practical recommendations

### Chapters to Skim or Skip

- **Chapter 3** — The historical chapter is interesting but less actionable

### What You'll Miss

The replication crisis context — the book presents ego depletion as settled science, which subsequent research has complicated considerably.
