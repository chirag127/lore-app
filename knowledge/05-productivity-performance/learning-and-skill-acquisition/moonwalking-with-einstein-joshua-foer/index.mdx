Can an average person with an ordinary memory train to become a national memory champion? Joshua Foer, a science journalist covering the US Memory Championship, set out to answer that question—and ended up winning the competition the following year. *Moonwalking with Einstein* is the story of that journey, interwoven with a fascinating history of memory from ancient Greece to the present.

Foer's transformation came through ancient memory techniques, particularly the method of loci—also known as the memory palace. By associating information with vivid, spatial images placed in familiar locations, he learned to memorize decks of cards, long sequences of numbers, and lists of hundreds of random words. Along the way, he explores why these techniques work, how memory champions think, and what modern neuroscience reveals about the brain's remarkable plasticity.

But the book is about more than winning competitions. Foer argues that memory is not just a party trick—it is the foundation of deep learning, creativity, and understanding. In a world where we outsource remembering to Google and smartphones, *Moonwalking with Einstein* is a compelling case for reclaiming the lost art of memory.
