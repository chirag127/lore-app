What makes the Beatles the greatest band in history? Why do so many elite hockey players share the same birth month? How did Bill Gates's high school computer club create one of the wealthiest men on earth? In *Outliers*, Malcolm Gladwell argues that we ask the wrong questions about success. We focus too much on individual characteristics—intelligence, ambition, personality—and too little on the context that makes success possible.

Gladwell's central claim is that outliers—people who achieve extraordinary success—are not self-made individuals who rose from nothing through sheer force of will. Rather, they are beneficiaries of hidden advantages: the year they were born, the families they grew up in, the cultural traditions they inherited, and the extraordinary opportunities they were given. The 10,000-hour rule—the idea that true expertise requires about ten thousand hours of practice—became the book's most famous concept, though Gladwell drew it from Anders Ericsson's research and arguably oversimplified it.

Written in Gladwell's signature narrative style, *Outliers* weaves together case studies, historical analysis, and social science research to tell a compelling story about the structural nature of success. The book does not deny that talent and hard work matter, but it insists they are never sufficient. The question it poses—and that readers must answer—is whether our celebration of individual achievement blinds us to the systemic inequalities that shape who gets to succeed.
