What separates world-class violinists from competent ones? What makes a chess grandmaster see moves that others miss? For decades, the answer was assumed to be innate talent—a gift bestowed on a lucky few. Anders Ericsson, the psychologist who spent thirty years dismantling this assumption, offers a radically different explanation in *Peak*.

The book introduces **deliberate practice**, a systematic approach to skill development that is fundamentally different from the way most people practice. Unlike simple repetition or casual play, deliberate practice requires specific goals, immediate feedback, and constant pushing beyond one's current capabilities. Ericsson shows that with the right methods, almost anyone can achieve extraordinary performance, whether in music, sports, chess, medicine, or business.

Co-authored with science writer Robert Pool, *Peak* translates decades of rigorous research into an accessible framework. It corrects popular misconceptions—most notably Malcolm Gladwell's oversimplified 10,000-hour rule from *Outliers*—and provides a practical roadmap for anyone serious about mastering a skill. The book's central message is both empowering and demanding: expertise is not a gift but a process, one that requires sustained effort, intelligent design, and a willingness to embrace discomfort.
