What if everything you believe about talent is wrong? Geoff Colvin, senior editor at *Fortune* magazine, makes a compelling case that the qualities we call "talent" are largely a myth. Drawing on decades of research into expert performance—from Tiger Woods to Winston Churchill to Warren Buffett—Colvin argues that deliberate practice, not innate ability, is the real differentiator between average and world-class performers.

The book builds on the groundbreaking research of psychologist Anders Ericsson, who demonstrated that elite performers in fields as diverse as violin, chess, and sports all share one thing: thousands of hours of highly structured, deliberately challenging practice. Colvin translates this research into practical insights for anyone who wants to improve, whether in business, sports, music, or any other domain.

*Talent Is Overrated* is not a how-to guide in the traditional sense. It is a thought-provoking examination of what excellence really requires—and a challenge to the comfortable belief that some people are simply born with gifts the rest of us lack. The message is empowering but demanding: great performance is available to almost anyone, but only through sustained, intelligent effort.
