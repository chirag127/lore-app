Josh Waitzkin became famous as a child chess prodigy, the subject of the book and film *Searching for Bobby Fischer*. But his story did not end there. As an adult, Waitzkin walked away from competitive chess and, applying the same learning principles, became a world champion in Tai Chi Push Hands—a martial art he had never practiced before. *The Art of Learning* is his account of how he did it.

The book is not a memoir, though it contains compelling personal stories. It is a manual on the meta-skill of learning itself. Waitzkin argues that true mastery is not about domain-specific knowledge but about understanding how to learn, adapt, and perform under pressure. He introduces principles like "investment in loss" (embracing short-term failure for long-term growth), "the downward spiral" (managing emotional states during competition), and "making smaller circles" (condensing complex skills into their essence).

What makes *The Art of Learning* unique among expertise books is its first-person perspective. Waitzkin is not a researcher reporting on others—he is a practitioner who has tested these principles at the highest levels of two completely different disciplines. His insights into the psychology of competition, the nature of flow, and the art of recovery from defeat are hard-won and deeply felt.
