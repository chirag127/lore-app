What is the secret behind the world's greatest talent hotbeds—the tiny tennis club in Russia that produced more top-twenty women players than the entire United States, or the music school in upstate New York that sends prodigies to Carnegie Hall? Daniel Coyle, a *New York Times* bestselling journalist, traveled to nine such hotbeds to find out, and discovered a common neurological mechanism: myelin.

Myelin is a fatty substance that wraps around nerve fibers, insulating them and dramatically increasing the speed and accuracy of signal transmission. Every time we practice a skill, we fire a specific neural circuit—and each firing stimulates more myelin to wrap around that circuit. The more myelin, the faster and more precise the skill. The implications are revolutionary: talent is not a gift but a process of building myelin through the right kind of practice.

Coyle identifies three essential elements of talent development: deep practice (struggling at the edge of your ability), ignition (the motivation that drives hours of practice), and master coaching (guidance that optimizes the learning process). *The Talent Code* weaves together neuroscience, on-the-ground observation, and practical advice into a compelling story about how greatness is built, not born.
