## Chapter 1: Questions and Answers

Eagleton opens by examining why the question "What is the meaning of life?" has been discredited in analytic philosophy. Logical positivists like A.J. Ayer dismissed it as meaningless — a pseudo-question generated by the misuse of language. Wittgensteinians argued that meaning is a matter of language games and that the question confuses different kinds of meaning.

Eagleton argues that the dismissal of the question is itself a philosophical position, not a neutral observation. The question may be poorly formulated, but it gestures toward something real: the human need for significance, purpose, and value. He defends the question's legitimacy while acknowledging that it cannot be answered in the way we answer factual questions.

---

## Chapter 2: The Question of Meaning

This chapter explores different ways of understanding the concept of meaning. Eagleton distinguishes between linguistic meaning (what a word or sentence means), existential meaning (what makes life worth living), and cosmic meaning (whether the universe as a whole has a purpose). The question of life's meaning, he argues, primarily concerns existential meaning.

He examines various answers that have been proposed: happiness, pleasure, self-realization, service to others, creative work, religious devotion. Each has something to be said for it, but none is fully satisfactory. The search for meaning, Eagleton suggests, is inseparable from the search for love and solidarity.

---

## Chapter 3: The Meaning of Life, East and West

Eagleton surveys how different cultural and philosophical traditions have approached the question. He examines ancient Greek philosophy (Aristotle's eudaimonia), Christianity (love of God and neighbor), existentialism (Sartre's radical freedom, Camus's absurd hero), and Eastern traditions (Buddhist rejection of the self as the source of suffering).

The chapter is too brief to do justice to any single tradition, but it demonstrates the range of possible answers and shows that the question is universal across cultures. Eagleton's own sympathy is with a broadly Aristotelian-Christian-humanist tradition that locates meaning in the practice of virtue and the quality of our relationships.

---

## Chapter 4: Death and Meaning

This chapter confronts the problem that death seems to threaten meaning. If we all die, and eventually all our achievements are forgotten, what meaning can life have? Eagleton examines several responses: the religious (immortality gives meaning), the existential (death gives life urgency and authenticity), and the humanist (meaning is found in the quality of the life lived, not its duration).

Eagleton rejects both the view that death makes life meaningless and the view that only immortality can give meaning. He argues that death and meaning are not opposed but intertwined — our finitude is what makes our commitments serious and our love precious.

---

## Chapter 5: Love and Meaning

The penultimate chapter argues that love is the closest we come to a concrete answer to the question of life's meaning. Eagleton draws on Aristotle (friendship as the highest good), Christianity (God is love), and psychoanalysis (the capacity to love as a sign of psychic health) to develop an account of love as the fundamental human good.

Love, for Eagleton, is not primarily a feeling but a practice — a way of attending to the world and to others that affirms their value. The capacity to love, and to be loved in return, is what makes life feel meaningful even in the face of suffering and death.

---

## Chapter 6: Conclusion — The Meaning of Life

The final chapter synthesizes the book's arguments. The meaning of life, Eagleton concludes, is not a proposition to be discovered but a quality of life to be achieved. It is found in the practice of love, friendship, and solidarity; in creative work that engages our full capacities; in the struggle for a just society; and in the capacity to face mortality without despair.

Eagleton ends with a characteristically political turn: a society that systematically prevents people from finding meaning — through inequality, exploitation, and the commodification of all human relationships — is not just unjust but inhuman. The search for meaning is inseparable from the struggle for a better world.

---

## Reading Guide

This book can be read in an afternoon but deserves to be lingered over. Each chapter is dense with argument and allusion. The book pairs well with Eagleton's *After Theory* (2003), which pursues similar themes in a more polemical mode, and with Viktor Frankl's *Man's Search for Meaning* (1946), which offers a complementary account from a radically different perspective.
