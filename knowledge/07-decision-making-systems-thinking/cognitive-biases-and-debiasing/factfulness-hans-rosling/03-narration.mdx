Factfulness: Ten Reasons We're Wrong About the World -- and Why Things Are
Better Than You Think, by Hans Rosling, with Ola Rosling and Anna Rosling
Rönnlund.

This is a book about the gap between how the world really is and how we
think it is. And that gap is enormous. When Hans Rosling asked thousands
of people across the world basic questions about global trends, they
systematically got the answers wrong. Not just a little wrong.
Consistently, dramatically wrong -- worse than random chance. A
chimpanzee choosing answers at random would score about 33 percent on a
three-option multiple choice question. Human experts, including Nobel
laureates and medical researchers, scored between 10 and 20 percent. We
are actively, reliably, and predictably ignorant about the state of our
world.

The reason, Rosling argues, is not that we are stupid. It is that we are
equipped with a set of psychological instincts that evolved to help our
ancestors survive on the savanna. These instincts served us well when
the biggest threat was a predator in the tall grass. But they are
catastrophically ill-suited to understanding global data in the 21st
century. The book identifies ten such instincts, devotes a chapter to
each, and offers a practical antidote for every one.

The first and most fundamental is the Gap Instinct. This is our tendency
to divide the world into two opposing groups and see a gap between them.
We talk about "developed" and "developing" countries, "the West and the
rest," "rich and poor." But this binary is a lie. Rosling replaces it
with his most important contribution: the four income levels. Level one
is extreme poverty -- less than two dollars per day. About one billion
people live here. Level two is two to eight dollars per day. About three
billion people live here. Level three is eight to thirty-two dollars per
day. About two billion people live here. Level four is more than
thirty-two dollars per day. About one billion people live here. The
world is not divided into two camps. It is a continuous spectrum, and
most people are in the middle. The antidote to the Gap Instinct is
simple: whenever you see two groups, look for the distribution. Look for
the overlap. Look for the people in between.

The second instinct is the Negativity Instinct. We notice bad news more
than good news. This is not a conspiracy. It is the nature of media and
the nature of human attention. A plane landing safely is not news. A
plane crashing is front page. A million children vaccinated is not
reported. A single child dying from a rare disease creates headlines. As
a result, we systematically overestimate the prevalence of bad things and
underestimate the prevalence of good things. Rosling offers a powerful
antidote: when you see bad news, ask whether equally positive news would
have reached you. If the answer is no, your perception is being skewed.
Most things are improving. But improvement is slow, silent, and
undramatic.

The third instinct is the Straight Line Instinct. We assume that current
trends will continue in a straight line into the future. They rarely do.
Lines can curve. They can be S-curves, where growth accelerates then
flattens. They can be humps, where things rise then fall. They can be
doubling curves, where growth accelerates. Rosling gives the example of
population. Many people fear that population will continue growing in a
straight line until the planet is overwhelmed. In reality, the number of
children per woman has already fallen from 5 to 2.5 globally, and
population is projected to level off around 10 to 11 billion. The
antidote: remember that lines can curve. Ask what shape the trend has
historically followed, and why it might change.

The fourth instinct is the Fear Instinct. Our brains prioritize dramatic,
rare threats over common, mundane ones. We fear terrorism more than
diabetes, plane crashes more than car accidents, shark attacks more than
falls. Rosling shows that deaths from natural disasters have fallen by 75
percent over the past century, but our fear of them has only increased.
The antidote: before you panic, calculate the actual risk. Ask: how many
people does this actually kill per year? Compare it to something familiar.
Fear and danger are not the same thing.

The fifth instinct is the Size Instinct. When we see a big number, we
react emotionally without putting it in context. Four million babies die
in their first month of life every year. That is an enormous, tragic
number. But in 1950, it was fourteen million. In 1800, it was more than
40 percent. The size of the problem is shrinking dramatically, but we
feel only the absolute number. The antidote: always put numbers in
perspective. Compare them to something. Compare per capita. Compare over
time. A number alone is meaningless.

The sixth instinct is the Generalization Instinct. We categorize people
and things, then assume every member of the category is the same. We talk
about "African culture" as if fifty-four countries with a billion people
share one culture. We talk about "Muslims" as a monolith. We use the term
"developing world" as if it describes a single thing. The antidote:
question every category. Look for variation within groups. Ask whether
the average is representative or whether the spread around it tells the
real story. Rosling shows that the majority of "developing" countries
have higher life expectancy than the United States had in 1950. The
category itself is a relic.

The seventh instinct is the Destiny Instinct. We assume that slow change
means no change. We think countries will always be poor, cultures will
always be traditional, values will always be the same. Rosling shows that
this is false. The same values we associate with "Western" culture today
existed in Sweden sixty years ago -- patriarchal, religious,
authoritarian. As economies develop, values change. The number of
children per woman drops. Girls stay in school longer. Democracy becomes
more likely. The change is slow -- a few percentage points per decade --
but over a lifetime it is transformative. The antidote: track gradual
improvements. Update your mental map. Remind yourself that cultures
evolve across generations, not centuries.

The eighth instinct is the Single Perspective Instinct. We fall in love
with one idea, one solution, one framework, and apply it to every
problem. Doctors see medical solutions. Economists see market solutions.
Activists see political solutions. All of them miss the full picture.
Rosling gives the example of the US healthcare debate, where both
market advocates and single-payer advocates behave as if their one
solution will fix everything. In reality, every successful healthcare
system in the world is a mix. The antidote: be humble about the limits
of your own expertise. Collect diverse tools. Test your favorite idea
against reality. If you only have a hammer, everything looks like a nail.

The ninth instinct is the Blame Instinct. When something goes wrong,
we look for a villain. We need someone to blame. This is emotionally
satisfying but practically useless. When you blame an individual, you
stop looking at the system that allowed them to act as they did. A
corrupt politician exists because of a system that permits corruption.
A company that pollutes does so because regulations or incentives allow
it. The same applies to good outcomes. When a child is vaccinated, it is
not because of a heroic doctor -- it is because of a functioning health
system. The antidote: resist pointing fingers. Look for causes in the
system. Give credit to the process.

The tenth instinct is the Urgency Instinct. When someone declares a
crisis, our first instinct is to act immediately. Politicians, activists,
and marketers exploit this instinct to bypass our rational thinking.
Rosling offers a framework: when someone says "act now or it's too late,"
ask whether the opportunity will ripen or rot with time. Most problems
that are presented as urgent have been developing for years, even decades.
They will benefit from measured analysis. The few true emergencies are
better handled by prepared systems than by panicked reactions. The
antidote: take a breath. Demand data. Beware of action bias.

Throughout the book, Rosling weaves in stories from his own life: his
childhood in Uppsala with an outdoor privy, his medical work in
Mozambique where he discovered the paralytic disease konzo, his
encounters with world leaders including Fidel Castro, his sword-swallowing
finale to TED talks. These stories ground the data in lived experience.
They remind us that statistics are not abstract numbers -- they are
descriptions of real human lives.

The book also introduces Dollar Street, Anna Rosling Rönnlund's
photographic project. Over 264 homes in more than 50 countries were
photographed, with consistent images of the same objects: the family's
toothbrushes, their stove, their bed, their shoes. When ordered by
income rather than by country, the patterns are striking. A Level 2
family in Bangladesh and a Level 2 family in Nigeria have nearly
identical possessions. A Level 4 family in the US and a Level 4 family
in South Africa look similar. Income explains far more than culture.

Rosling's message is not that the world is perfect. It is that the world
is better than we think, and that understanding this progress is the
prerequisite for making even more. He calls this stance "possibilism" --
the ability to hold two ideas simultaneously: that things are getting
better, and that serious problems remain. This is not optimism. It is
not pessimism. It is accuracy.

The four income levels are the book's most practical tool. Once you
internalize them, the old binary of developed versus developing becomes
unusable. You see the world as it is: a continuous distribution where
most people are in Levels 2 and 3, moving upward over time. The extreme
poverty of Level 1 is shrinking. The affluence of Level 4 is expanding.
The vast middle is rising slowly but steadily.

The book closes with a plea and a challenge. The plea: that we adopt a
fact-based worldview, not because facts are more comfortable (they are
often uncomfortable) but because they are more useful. The challenge:
that we take responsibility for updating our own understanding, rather
than outsourcing it to media, politicians, or pundits.
