## The Good Judgment Project

In 2011, the US intelligence community, stung by failures on Iraq WMD, the Arab Spring, and other events, launched a four-year forecasting tournament through IARPA called ACE (Aggregative Contingent Estimation). Five academic teams competed to see whose methods could produce the most accurate geopolitical forecasts. Tetlock's team — the Good Judgment Project (GJP) — entered and won decisively.

The GJP recruited roughly 20,000 volunteers through online ads, enrolled them in a one-hour training tutorial on probabilistic reasoning, and set them to work forecasting hundreds of geopolitics questions: Would Assad still be in power in six months? Would Greece leave the Eurozone? Would North Korea test another nuclear device? Each volunteer assigned a numerical probability to each question, and every forecast was scored using the Brier score — the mean squared error between predicted probability and actual outcome. A score of 0 is perfect; 0.25 is roughly chance; 0.5 means you are worse than random.

The finding that made the project famous: a small fraction of volunteers — roughly 2% — consistently produced Brier scores below 0.20, beating not just the other volunteers but also intelligence analysts with access to classified data. These were the "superforecasters."

## Fox vs Hedgehog

Tetlock's earlier work, *Expert Political Judgment* (2005), analyzed 284 experts over 20 years and found that cognitive style — not domain expertise, credentials, or ideology — was the primary predictor of forecasting accuracy. He drew on Isaiah Berlin's famous distinction between the hedgehog, who knows one big thing and filters every problem through that single lens, and the fox, who knows many small things and draws on diverse perspectives.

Hedgehogs make more confident, more categorical predictions — and are less accurate. Foxes express uncertainty in probabilities, update incrementally, and sustain their accuracy across time horizons. The most striking finding: hedgehogs' worst forecasts were on their own topics of expertise. The more they knew, the more wrong they were.

## What Makes Superforecasters Different

Through personality testing and behavioral observation, Tetlock identified the traits that distinguish superforecasters: active open-mindedness (genuinely considering opposing views), cognitive humility (acknowledging the limits of one's knowledge), numeracy (comfort with numbers and probabilities), curiosity (wanting to understand how things work), a growth mindset (believing forecasting skill can improve), perspective-taking (adopting others' viewpoints), disciplined attention (focusing without drift), and detachment from ego (caring about accuracy, not being right).

Notably absent: domain expertise, IQ beyond a modest threshold, and ideological commitment. Superforecasters come from all backgrounds — a retired librarian, a Silicon Valley entrepreneur, a professional bridge player. They are not elites. They simply practice better cognitive habits.

## Thinking in Base Rates and Bayesian Updating

The single most powerful practical technique in the book is the distinction between the inside view and the outside view, drawn from Kahneman and Tversky. The inside view starts with the specific case: its details, actors, and unique circumstances. The outside view starts with the reference class: what happens in similar situations? Everyone defaults to the inside view. Superforecasters deliberately invert the order.

Asked whether a new authoritarian regime will fall within two years, the inside view examines the regime's specific vulnerabilities and opposition strength. The outside view asks: what percentage of authoritarian regimes fall within two years of taking power? The base rate is roughly 15%. That becomes the anchor, adjusted up or down based on case-specific factors. This one technique does more to improve accuracy than any other.

Superforecasters think like Bayesians — even without using the formula. They start with a prior probability (often derived from the base rate), evaluate each new piece of evidence for its diagnosticity, and update in proportion. The key habit: they update in small increments, not dramatic reversals. A shift from 50% to 55% in response to a single data point is more rational than a leap to 80%. The GJP found that superforecasters updated nearly twice as often as average forecasters.

## The Dragonfly-Eye Perspective

Superforecasters consider problems from multiple angles simultaneously — like a dragonfly's compound eye, which contains thousands of individual lenses. They integrate viewpoints from different disciplines, ideologies, and methodologies before reaching a judgment. The technique is deliberately contrarian: before settling on an estimate, they actively seek the strongest argument against their current position — not to refute it, but to genuinely understand it and update accordingly.

## Granular Probabilities

Superforecasters avoid categorical language entirely. They do not say "likely" or "unlikely." They use the full 0-100% probability spectrum. This forces calibration: you cannot say "85%" without thinking about what 85% actually means. It enables scoring: only numerical probabilities can be evaluated with proper scoring rules. And it resists overconfidence: translating "I'm pretty sure" into "70%" is sobering.

The Brier score measures forecasting accuracy as the mean squared error between predicted probabilities and outcomes. Superforecasters consistently achieved Brier scores below 0.20, meaning their 70% forecasts happened about 70% of the time — excellent calibration.

## Breaking Problems into Component Parts

Superforecasters decompose. They break seemingly intractable questions into tractable sub-problems — a Fermi-style approach. Instead of asking "Will Country X default on its debt?" they ask: what is Country X's debt-to-GDP ratio? What is the base rate for sovereign default at that ratio? What is the political leadership's willingness to repay? What are the external pressures? Each sub-question gets its own probability, and the overall forecast emerges from the synthesis.

## Team Forecasting and Prediction Markets

The GJP found that groups consistently outperform individuals — but only when structured correctly. The key conditions: independent judgment first (members estimate before discussing, preventing groupthink), diverse perspectives (fox-like teams outperform hedgehog teams), accountability (members see their tracked scores), and psychological safety (norms encouraging disagreement).

The extremizing algorithm amplified team wisdom: when members independently converged on similar estimates, the aggregate was pushed further in that direction. If the average was 70% and agreement was high, the extremized estimate might be 80%. If estimates were scattered, the average stayed near 50%. This mathematically exploits the signal in consensus while damping noise.

## The Hedgehog and the Fox Revisited

The fox-hedgehog distinction runs through the entire book as its organizing insight. Hedgehogs build grand theories and filter evidence to fit. Foxes gather what works, update what does not, and accept that the world is too complex for any single framework. The book's most radical implication is that cognitive style is not fixed — it can be cultivated. Superforecasters are not born; they are made through deliberate practice of specific mental habits: seeking base rates, thinking probabilistically, updating frequently, embracing humility, and treating every wrong forecast as data for learning.
