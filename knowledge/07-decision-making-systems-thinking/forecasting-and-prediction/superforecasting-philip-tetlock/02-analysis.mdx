## Why Superforecasters Beat CIA Analysts

The most striking finding of the Good Judgment Project was not that trained volunteers could produce accurate forecasts — it was that they outperformed professional intelligence analysts who had access to classified information. This challenges a deeply held assumption: that secret data and domain expertise are the keys to prediction. The GJP results suggest the opposite: cognitive habits matter more than information advantage.

Intelligence analysts operate in an environment that rewards confidence and penalizes uncertainty. Expressing a forecast as "65% likely" can look indecisive. Superforecasters, operating outside bureaucratic pressure, could freely express calibrated uncertainty. The training they received — base rates, probabilistic thinking, frequent updating — directly addressed the cognitive biases that plague all human judgment. The implication is uncomfortable but clear: classified data does not compensate for poor reasoning.

## Strengths

The book's greatest strength is its empirical foundation. Tetlock's claims rest on the largest forecasting experiment ever conducted — millions of forecasts over four years, scored against objective outcomes. The honesty about uncertainty is refreshing: the book never claims forecasting is clairvoyance. The techniques are immediately actionable: the inside/outside view, base rate reasoning, and Bayesian updating can be applied by anyone starting tomorrow.

The profiles of individual superforecasters make the research accessible. These are not elite geniuses but ordinary people who cultivated extraordinary habits — a message that is both humbling and empowering. Forecasting is presented as a trainable skill, not an innate gift.

## Critiques and the Taleb Challenge

The most persistent criticism comes from Nassim Taleb: the GJP tested forecasters on questions someone else chose, about events that could happen. This misses the black swan problem — the most consequential events are those nobody predicted because they were not on anyone's radar. Superforecasters are good at answering questions within a defined frame, but the hardest part of real-world forecasting is deciding what to forecast.

Tetlock acknowledges this. The first of the Ten Commandments is "triage": do not forecast what cannot be forecast. But triage itself is a skill the GJP never tested. The ACE tournament questions were deliberately chosen to be scorable — which biases toward incremental rather than transformational events. Whether superforecasters would have anticipated the 2008 financial crisis, the rise of ISIS, or a global pandemic is an open question.

A second limitation: the GJP participants were self-selected volunteers, likely more numerate and intellectually curious than average. The selection effect was larger than the training effect. The top 2% were discovered, not created — though training did improve performance by roughly 10%.

## Challenging Expertise Culture

The book's most uncomfortable implication is that expertise does not imply predictive skill. This threatens the foundation of punditry, consulting, and much of professional analysis. Tetlock is careful not to overstate: expertise is valuable for explanation and diagnosis. But for prediction, cognitive habits matter more. This finding has slowly penetrated intelligence agencies and corporate strategy, but the gap between evidence and practice remains wide.

## Replication and Extension

Since the GJP, the superforecasting approach has been replicated and extended. Prediction markets incorporating superforecasting methods have shown consistent gains. The IARPA tournament was followed by the Hybrid Forecasting Competition (HFC), which confirmed the GJP's findings and extended them to new domains. Organizations including the CIA's In-Q-Tel and various hedge funds have attempted to apply superforecasting principles.

## Can Forecasting Be Taught at Scale?

The GJP proved that a one-hour tutorial improves accuracy by about 10%. But producing superforecasters — the top 2% — required selection, not just training. The teachable skills (probabilistic thinking, base rates, Bayesian updating) are accessible to anyone. The cognitive style (active open-mindedness, intellectual humility) is harder to instill. The most honest answer: basic forecasting competence can be taught at scale. Elite forecasting requires finding people with the right disposition and then giving them the right tools.
