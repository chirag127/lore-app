## The Collaboration

*Superforecasting* is the product of a rare and effective collaboration between an academic and a journalist. Philip Tetlock, a psychologist at the University of Pennsylvania, had spent decades studying political judgment — first diagnosing the problem in *Expert Political Judgment* (2005), then building the solution through the Good Judgment Project. Dan Gardner, a journalist and author of *Risk: The Science and Politics of Fear*, brought narrative skill and accessibility. Together they produced a book that is rigorous enough for academics and readable enough for a general audience.

## Structure and Narrative

The book unfolds in three movements. The first establishes the problem: experts are terrible forecasters, and the GJP proved that ordinary people can do better. The second introduces the superforecasters themselves — following specific individuals as characters, showing how they think and work. The third distills the methods into a practical system — the Ten Commandments, the inside/outside view, Bayesian updating, team forecasting.

This structure — problem, people, method — gives the book a compelling arc. The narrative device of following individual superforecasters is particularly effective. Rather than presenting abstract principles, Tetlock and Gardner show real people struggling with uncertainty, updating their beliefs, and sometimes getting it wrong. The reader learns alongside them.

## Tone

The tone is humble and intellectually serious without being dry. Tetlock and Gardner never claim that forecasting is easy or that superforecasters are infallible. The book is full of admissions of failure, uncertainty, and the limits of prediction. This intellectual honesty is part of what makes it credible. The authors are not selling certainty — they are selling better calibration.

## Reception

The book earned praise from both academics and practitioners. Daniel Kahneman called it "a landmark in the scientific study of judgment." Nate Silver described it as "a brilliant and accessible guide." It was named one of the best books of the year by *The Economist* and became a *New York Times* bestseller. Its influence has extended beyond forecasting to fields as diverse as intelligence analysis, corporate strategy, investing, and public policy.
