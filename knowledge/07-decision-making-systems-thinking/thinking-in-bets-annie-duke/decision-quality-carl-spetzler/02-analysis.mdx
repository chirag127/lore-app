## Book Context & Background

Published in 2016, *Decision Quality* emerged from over 35 years of consulting practice at SDG. While the decision analysis literature had long existed in academic journals, Spetzler's contribution was to package it for organizational adoption. The book reflects the accumulated wisdom of thousands of consulting engagements.

## About the Authors

Carl Spetzler founded SDG in 1981 and chaired it for over three decades. Hannah Winter is a senior consultant at SDG specializing in organizational decision processes. Jennifer Meyer is a decision quality practitioner and educator at SDG. The trio combine deep analytic expertise with practical organizational experience.

## Core Thesis

Organizational decision quality requires a systematic process that is analytically rigorous and organizationally inclusive. The Dialogue Decision Process bridges the gap between analysis and action by involving decision-makers directly in the reasoning process.

## Strengths

The book addresses an important and neglected question: how to scale good decision-making beyond the individual. The six-requirement framework is comprehensive and well-integrated. The Dialogue Decision Process is a genuinely innovative response to the organizational challenge. The treatment of framing — the most important phase — is excellent.

## Criticisms

The book has been criticized as a consulting pitch. The reliance on SDG's proprietary methods (including their trademarked Dialogue Decision Process) can feel like advertising. Academic reviews have noted that the book lacks new empirical evidence. The book also assumes a level of organizational sophistication that many companies do not have. Strategy+business review noted that the framework works best in organizations with strong analytic cultures — precisely the ones that need it least.

## Comparative Analysis

*Decision Quality* extends *Smart Choices* from individual to organizational decisions. It shares the analytical DNA of the ProACT framework but adds the organizational process dimension. Compared to Kahneman, Sibony, and Sunstein's *Noise*, it is more prescriptive and less critical — it assumes organizations can improve rather than cataloging their failures.
