## Book Context & Background

Published in 2020, *How to Decide* arrived as the natural follow-up to *Thinking in Bets* (2018). The decision-science market had grown crowded with theoretical books but lacked practical workbooks. Duke positioned this book as the actionable counterpart — a handbook rather than a manifesto. Its publication during the COVID-19 pandemic, a period of heightened uncertainty and consequential decision-making, gave it unusual timeliness.

## About the Author

Annie Duke built her reputation as a World Series of Poker champion with a PhD in cognitive psychology. By 2020 she had established herself as a corporate speaker and consultant. *How to Decide* represents her shift from establishing credibility to providing scalable tools. The book reflects her experience coaching executives at Google, Bridgewater, and other organizations.

## Core Thesis & Argument

Knowing *that* you should think in bets is insufficient; you need structured practice to rewire cognitive habits. Duke argues that decision-making is a skill that improves with deliberate practice, and the book provides the practice regime. The thesis is that consistent use of simple tools — journals, checklists, pre-mortems — will produce better decisions than raw intelligence or experience alone.

## Thematic Analysis

**Practice Over Theory**: Duke emphasizes that reading about cognitive biases does not reduce them. Only structured repetition of counter-bias behaviors — like writing down predictions before outcomes — actually changes behavior.

**Emotional Regulation**: The book gives unusual attention to the emotional dimension of decision-making. Duke's "tilt detector" acknowledges that rationality requires emotional stability first.

**Scalable Simplicity**: The tools are designed to be simple enough for daily use. Duke explicitly warns against complex systems that people abandon after two weeks.

## Strengths

The structured templates are genuinely useful and battle-tested. The decision journal format is specific enough to guide beginners but flexible enough for advanced users. The group decision protocols fill a genuine gap in the literature — most decision books focus on individual choices.

## Criticisms

The book is essentially an extended workbook, which some readers find repetitive. The Journal of Behavioral Decision Making noted that while the tools are plausible, Duke provides no systematic evidence of their effectiveness beyond testimonials. The overlap with *Thinking in Bets* is substantial; readers who own both may feel they paid twice for the same content.

## Impact & Legacy

*How to Decide* solidified Duke's position as a decision-making coach and consultant. The templates have been adopted by several Fortune 500 companies. The book's influence is visible in the subsequent proliferation of decision-journaling apps. It has not, however, had the cultural impact of *Thinking in Bets*.
