## Writing Style & Voice

Duke adopts a more instructional, less narrative tone than in *Thinking in Bets*. Sentences are imperative: "Write this down." "Do this exercise." "Check your bias." The style is closer to a training manual than a trade book. Vocabulary remains accessible, and poker metaphors are reduced in favor of business and life examples.

## Narrative Structure

The book is structured as a progression through decision-making skills, each chapter building on the previous one. Unlike the narrative arc of *Thinking in Bets*, this book is modular — readers can jump to specific chapters for specific tools. This makes it more useful as a reference but less engaging as a read-through.

## Rhetorical Techniques

Duke relies on authority (her poker career and consulting work) and on the logical appeal of structured practice. She uses repetition intentionally: key concepts reappear across chapters in slightly different forms, reinforcing learning through spacing. The tone is encouraging rather than scolding.

## Readability & Accessibility

The book targets the same audience as *Thinking in Bets* but requires more active participation. Reading level is approximately 8th grade. The workbook format means it reads faster but demands more from the reader.

## Comparative Context

Among decision workbooks, *How to Decide* is distinguished by its author's credibility and the quality of its templates. It is more structured than Russo and Schoemaker's *Decision Traps* and more accessible than Hammond, Keeney, and Raiffa's *Smart Choices*.
