## Writing Style & Voice

The dual-author voice is remarkably consistent. Thaler provides the behavioral economics expertise and Sunstein the legal/philosophical framework. The writing is clear, engaging, and frequently witty. The tone is persuasive but not preachy — the authors respect the intelligence of their audience.

## Narrative Structure

The book moves from theory (biases, defaults) through application (savings, health, education) to principles (what nudges are and are not). The structure is logical and effective. Each application chapter follows a similar pattern: problem, behavioral diagnosis, nudge solution.

## Rhetorical Techniques

The authors rely on behavioral research, vivid examples, and the inherent appeal of the libertarian paternalism concept ("both pro-choice and pro-government intervention"). The framing of the book as "third way" policy — neither laissez-faire nor command-and-control — gives it broad appeal.

## Readability

Reading level is approximately 10th grade. The book is accessible to a general audience. Behavioral economics concepts are explained clearly. The policy sections require more concentration.

## Comparative Context

*Nudge* is the most policy-oriented book in this category. It is best read alongside *Predictably Irrational* (the behavioral science that nudges rely on) and *Thinking in Bets* (the individual-skill complement to the policy approach). Together, these books cover the full spectrum from individual decision skills to policy design.
