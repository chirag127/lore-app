## Chapter 1: The Truth About Relativity

Why everything is relative. Ariely introduces the decoy effect: people choose differently when a decoy (clearly inferior) option is present. The Economist subscription experiment demonstrates how decoys shift preference.

## Chapter 2: The Fallacy of Supply and Demand

How arbitrary coherence — the tendency to anchor on initial prices — shapes what we are willing to pay. Ariely shows that exposing people to random numbers influences their willingness to pay.

## Chapter 3: The Cost of Free

Zero price is uniquely attractive. Free items reduce the perceived risk to zero and short-circuit rational cost-benefit analysis. Free shipping, free samples — all leverage this principle.

## Chapter 4: Social vs. Market Norms

Social norms (doing favors, helping) and market norms (payment, exchange) operate in different domains. Introducing money into a social relationship destroys it. Ariely's Thanksgiving experiment shows that offering to pay for dinner changes the entire dynamic.

## Chapter 5: The Power of Arousal

Hot states (emotional arousal) change behavior dramatically. People underestimate how much emotions will influence their decisions — the hot-cold empathy gap. Ariely's experiment with sexually aroused male students demonstrates the gap vividly.

## Chapter 6: Procrastination and Self-Control

Humans systematically procrastinate. Ariely shows that self-imposed deadlines can improve performance, and that people recognize their self-control problems and will voluntarily adopt commitment devices.

## Chapter 7: The High Price of Ownership

The endowment effect: people value what they own more than identical items they do not own. Three quirks: we love what we have, we focus on what we might lose rather than gain, and we assume others see value the same way we do.
