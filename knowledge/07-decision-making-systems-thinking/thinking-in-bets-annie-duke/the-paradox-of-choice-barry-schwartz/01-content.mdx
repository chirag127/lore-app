## Chapter 1: When We Choose

Schwartz introduces the central paradox: we believe more choice is always better, but evidence shows that too much choice leads to paralysis, regret, and unhappiness. He cites the famous jam study (where shoppers were more likely to buy when presented with 6 options than 24).

## Chapter 2: How We Choose

The distinction between maximizing (seeking the best) and satisficing (settling for good enough). Maximizers are more likely to be depressed, anxious, and dissatisfied. Satisficers are happier, even though their objective outcomes may be slightly worse.

## Chapter 3: Deciding and Choosing

The decision process: how we evaluate options, the role of preferences, and how context shapes choice. Schwartz shows that preferences are often constructed rather than discovered.

## Chapter 4: When Only the Best Will Do

Maximizers suffer from higher expectations, which means they are less likely to be satisfied with any given outcome. The gap between expectation and reality is larger for maximizers.

## Chapter 5: Choice and Happiness

The relationship between choice and well-being is not linear. Beyond a threshold, additional choice reduces happiness. Schwartz reviews the evidence from positive psychology and behavioral economics.

## Chapter 6: Regret and Disappointment

Regret is more likely when options are abundant because counterfactuals (what might have been) are more salient. Anticipated regret can paralyze decision-making.

## Chapter 7: When the Self Confronts Choice

How choice affects identity and self-esteem. Poor choices are more damaging to self-image when options were abundant — there are fewer excuses.

## Chapter 8: What to Do

Schwartz offers practical guidance: choose satisficing, limit options, lower expectations, embrace constraints, practice gratitude, regret less, and stay committed to decisions.
