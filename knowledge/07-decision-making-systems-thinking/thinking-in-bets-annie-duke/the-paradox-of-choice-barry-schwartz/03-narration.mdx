## Writing Style & Voice

Schwartz writes in a clear, engaging, conversational style. The voice is that of a knowledgeable professor explaining ideas to an intelligent non-specialist. He uses humor, personal anecdotes, and vivid examples. The tone is persuasive but not polemical.

## Narrative Structure

The book moves from problem (choice explosion) through mechanism (maximizing, regret) to solution (satisficing strategies). The structure is linear and easy to follow. Each chapter builds on the previous one. The final chapter's recommendations are concrete and actionable.

## Rhetorical Techniques

Schwartz relies on argument from research (experimental studies), argument from logic (the paradox itself), and argument from shared experience (we have all felt overwhelmed by choice). The jam study serves as a central anchor — a memorable demonstration of the thesis.

## Readability

Reading level is approximately 9th-10th grade. The book is highly accessible, with minimal jargon. Technical concepts (opportunity cost, counterfactual thinking) are explained in plain language.

## Comparative Context

*The Paradox of Choice* is the most psychologically-oriented of the decision books in this category. It focuses on the consumer experience of choice rather than on decision processes. It is closer to behavioral economics (Ariely, Thaler) than to decision analysis (Hammond, Spetzler).
