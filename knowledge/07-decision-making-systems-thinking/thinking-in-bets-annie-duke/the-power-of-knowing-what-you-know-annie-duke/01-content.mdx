## Introduction: The Quitting Problem

Duke opens with a puzzle: why do smart, successful people persist in failing ventures? She traces the problem to three sources: sunk cost bias (the more we invest, the harder it is to leave), ego (quitting feels like admitting failure), and a culture that celebrates persistence indiscriminately.

## Part I: The Psychology of Persistence

This section explores the cognitive biases that make quitting so difficult. Sunk cost bias is the most powerful: Duke shows how people throw good money after bad, stay in miserable jobs, and continue failing relationships because of past investment. She draws on Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory to explain why losses hurt more than equivalent gains, making us cling to losing positions to avoid realizing losses.

**Ego and Identity**: Duke argues that persistence becomes entangled with identity. "I'm not a quitter" becomes a reason to persist in anything, regardless of its merits. This is especially damaging for high achievers who have been rewarded for persistence throughout their careers.

**Social Pressure**: The fear of others' judgment — "they'll think I failed" — is a powerful driver of irrational persistence. Duke provides strategies for separating public perception from decision quality.

## Part II: The Quit Decision Framework

Duke's core contribution is a structured framework for making quit decisions:

**Pre-Commitment**: Set explicit "kill criteria" before starting any project. These should be specific, measurable conditions that, if met, trigger a quit decision automatically.

**Expected Value Calculation**: Evaluate the expected value of continuing versus quitting. Duke provides a simple template: estimate the probability of success, the value of success, and the remaining cost.

**The Opportunity Cost Lens**: Every hour spent on a losing project is an hour not spent on a better one. Quitting frees resources for higher-value uses.

## Part III: Quitting in Practice

The final section applies the framework to specific domains: careers, investments, relationships, and creative projects. Duke provides case studies of successful quitters — entrepreneurs who pivoted, investors who cut losses, artists who abandoned unproductive directions. She also addresses the emotional aftermath of quitting: the regret, doubt, and social awkwardness.
