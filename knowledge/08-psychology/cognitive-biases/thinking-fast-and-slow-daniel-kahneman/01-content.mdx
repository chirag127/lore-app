## System 1 and System 2

Kahneman's central organizing metaphor divides mental life into two fictional characters. **System 1** operates automatically and quickly, with little or no effort and no sense of voluntary control. It generates impressions, feelings, and inclinations. **System 2** allocates attention to effortful mental activities — complex computations, logical reasoning, self-control. Its operations feel deliberate and require concentration.

The critical insight: System 1 runs the show most of the time. It continuously generates suggestions — judgments, intuitions, intentions — for System 2. System 2 typically endorses these suggestions with little modification because it is lazy, defaulting to the path of least cognitive effort. Only when System 1 runs into difficulty — an unexpected event, a violation of expectations, a problem requiring deliberate calculation — does System 2 fully engage.

### Cognitive Ease and the Associative Machine

System 1 operates on **cognitive ease**: when things feel familiar, fluent, and easy to process, System 1 is more likely to accept them as true. Repeated exposure to a statement increases its perceived truth — the **mere-exposure effect** — even when the repetition comes without conscious awareness.

Kahneman describes System 1 as an **associative machine** that continuously activates ideas, memories, and emotions in a spreading network of associations. A single word can prime an entire cascade of related concepts: seeing the word "eat" makes you complete "so_p" as "soup" rather than "soap." This **priming** effect operates below consciousness and can influence behavior — people who were primed with words related to old age walked more slowly down a hallway, a phenomenon called the **ideomotor effect**.

### The Law of Small Numbers and Overconfidence

System 1 is wildly overconfident in small samples. Kahneman demonstrates this through the **law of small numbers**: people treat small samples as if they were large, drawing strong conclusions from flimsy data. A hospital that reports three excellent surgical outcomes in a row is not necessarily the best — it may just be lucky. But System 1 constructs a coherent story and System 2 rarely objects.

This overconfidence extends to expert prediction. Kahneman shows that financial advisors, political pundits, and clinical psychologists all exhibit **the illusion of validity** — the conviction that their judgments are accurate despite evidence to the contrary. When a finance professor predicts stock movements with 90% confidence but is correct only 55% of the time, the gap between confidence and accuracy is the fingerprint of System 1.

## Heuristics and Biases

### Anchoring

**Anchoring** is the tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information encountered (the "anchor") when making decisions. If someone asks whether the Mississippi River is longer or shorter than 2,000 miles and then asks for an exact estimate, the answer will be pulled toward 2,000 — even if the anchor is clearly arbitrary. Kahneman demonstrates that anchoring works even when the anchor is obviously random, such as a number generated by spinning a wheel of fortune.

### Availability Heuristic

The **availability heuristic** is the tendency to judge the frequency of an event by how easily examples come to mind. People overestimate the frequency of dramatic, vivid events (plane crashes, shark attacks, terrorist attacks) because they are memorable, while underestimating more common but less vivid causes of death (asthma, diabetes, stroke). The ease with which examples are retrieved — the fluency — serves as a misleading proxy for actual frequency.

### Representativeness Heuristic

The **representativeness heuristic** causes people to judge the probability of an event by how similar it is to a prototype, ignoring base rates. Kahneman's classic **Linda problem** illustrates this: participants are told Linda is single, outspoken, and deeply concerned with social justice. When asked whether Linda is more likely to be a bank teller or a bank teller active in the feminist movement, most choose the latter — violating probability theory because the conjunction of two conditions cannot be more likely than either alone. This **conjunction fallacy** reveals that System 1 judges by resemblance, not logic.

## Prospect Theory and Choices

Kahneman and Amos Tversky's **prospect theory**, for which Kahneman won the 2002 Nobel Prize in Economics, fundamentally challenged expected utility theory. It contains three core ideas:

**Loss aversion**: losses hurt about twice as much as equivalent gains feel good. Losing $100 feels roughly as bad as gaining $200 feels good. This asymmetry explains why people hold losing stocks too long (hoping to break even), reject favorable gambles, and demand much more to give up something they own than they would pay to acquire it (the **endowment effect**).

**The fourfold pattern of risk attitudes**: people's risk preferences depend on the probability and the valence of outcomes. For high-probability gains, people are risk-averse (prefer a certain $900 over a 90% chance of $1,000). For high-probability losses, they are risk-seeking (prefer a 90% chance of losing $1,000 over a certain $900 loss). For low-probability gains, they are risk-seeking (buy lottery tickets). For low-probability losses, they are risk-averse (buy insurance).

**Framing effects**: the same choice presented differently produces opposite preferences. The **Asian disease problem** illustrates this: when told a disease will kill 600 people, people prefer a program that "saves 200 people" (gain frame) over one with a "one-third probability that 600 people will be saved and two-thirds probability that no one will be saved" — but they reverse their preference when the same outcomes are framed as "400 people will die" versus "one-third probability that no one will die."

## The Two Selves

### The Experiencing Self vs. the Remembering Self

Kahneman distinguishes between two conceptions of well-being. The **experiencing self** lives in the moment — it feels pleasure and pain second by second. The **remembering self** constructs stories about our lives and makes decisions. These two selves often disagree.

### The Peak-End Rule and Duration Neglect

The **peak-end rule** states that our memory of an experience is dominated by the most intense moment (the peak) and the final moment (the end), not by the total duration. In a classic experiment, participants held one hand in painfully cold water for 60 seconds, then repeated the experience with an additional 30 seconds of slightly warmer water. When asked which trial they preferred, most chose the longer one — because the improvement at the end made the memory less unpleasant. This is **duration neglect**: adding more pain to an experience, as long as it reduces the peak or improves the ending, can make the *memory* of the experience better even though the *experience* itself was worse.

### The Focusing Illusion

The **focusing illusion** — "nothing in life is as important as you think it is while you are thinking about it" — explains why people overestimate the impact of life changes on their happiness. Californians are not significantly happier than Midwesterners, and paraplegics adapt more than healthy people expect. When a specific factor (weather, income, disability) is in focus, it dominates judgment; when attention shifts to other aspects of life, its importance fades.

## Key Experiments

| Experiment | Concept Demonstrated |
|---|---|
| **Linda Problem** | Conjunction fallacy — judging by representativeness rather than probability |
| **Asian Disease Problem** | Framing effects — preferences reverse under gain vs. loss frames |
| **Cold Water Hand Immersion** | Peak-end rule — memory dominated by peak and end, not duration |
| **Wheel of Fortune** | Anchoring — arbitrary numbers influence subsequent estimates |
| **Old Age Priming** | Ideomotor effect — primes influence behavior automatically |
| **Tom W.'s Specialty** | Base-rate neglect — ignoring statistical base rates for representativeness |
