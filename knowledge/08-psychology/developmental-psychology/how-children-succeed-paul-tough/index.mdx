How Children Succeed, published in 2012, emerged from Paul Tough's reporting for the New York Times Magazine on education, poverty, and child development. The book synthesizes research from economics, psychology, and neuroscience into a single compelling argument: the qualities that matter most for children's success are not cognitive skills but character strengths — grit, curiosity, self-control, optimism, and conscientiousness.

Tough argues that conventional wisdom about education has been wrong: the cognitive focus (test scores, IQ, academic skills) has come at the expense of character development, which is both more predictive of life success and more responsive to intervention. The book weaves together stories of researchers, educators, and children to show how stress damages the developing brain, how character can be built, and what schools and families can do differently. A sequel, Helping Children Succeed (2016), provided a more practical follow-up.
