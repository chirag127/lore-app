## Chapter 1: The Inverse Power of Praise

The most famous chapter in the book. Carol Dweck's research on fixed vs. growth mindset is presented through a compelling narrative: when children are praised for being smart ("You're so intelligent"), they avoid challenge for fear of losing that label. When praised for effort ("You worked so hard"), they seek challenge and persist longer. The effect is large and well-replicated. Bronson and Merryman reveal that most parents and teachers believe they should praise intelligence — and are doing exactly the wrong thing.

## Chapter 2: The Lost Hour

Sleep deprivation in children is epidemic and largely invisible. A child who gets one hour less sleep than needed does not act sleepy — they act hyperactive, inattentive, and emotionally volatile — symptoms that are often misdiagnosed as ADHD. The chapter presents research showing that later school start times dramatically improve academic performance, reduce car accidents among teen drivers, and decrease depression.

## Chapter 3: Why White Parents Don't Talk About Race

Despite believing they are raising colorblind children, white parents who avoid discussing race actually raise children with more bias. The chapter presents research showing that children notice race by preschool and develop implicit racial attitudes from the absence of discussion as much as from explicit prejudice. Silence about race does not produce colorblindness; it produces confusion and default bias.

## Chapter 4: Why Kids Lie

Lying is a developmental milestone that reflects cognitive sophistication: the ability to understand others' minds (theory of mind), inhibit the truth, and manage a false narrative. Young children lie more as they get older not because they become more dishonest but because their cognitive abilities develop. The chapter explores what parents can do to encourage honesty.

## Chapter 5: The Search for Intelligent Life in Kindergarten

Gifted-and-talented programs and kindergarten readiness tests are largely ineffective at identifying truly gifted children, especially from disadvantaged backgrounds. The chapter follows the story of a child who missed the cutoff for a prestigious program by a few points and the researchers who argue that intelligence screening at early ages is more about predicting socioeconomic status than cognitive potential.

## Chapter 6: The Sibling Effect

Siblings are more influential than parents in shaping children's development, particularly in domains like peer relationships, conflict resolution, and social skills. The chapter challenges the emphasis on parent-child attachment in developmental psychology and argues that sibling dynamics deserve more attention.

## Chapter 7: The Science of Teen Rebellion

Teens do not rebel against their parents' values per se — they rebel against the perception that their parents are hypocritical. When parents' behavior does not match their stated values, teens are more likely to reject those values entirely. The chapter argues that parental hypocrisy — not authority — is the main driver of teen rebellion.

## Chapter 8: Can Self-Control Be Taught?

The chapter reviews the research on self-control training programs, including the famous Tools of the Mind curriculum. The findings are mixed: self-control can be improved in the short term, but long-term effects and generalizability are uncertain.

## Chapter 9: Plays Nice With Others

The research on social skills training and bullying prevention programs. Many popular programs have no effect or make things worse. The chapter discusses what actually works.

## Chapter 10: The Myth of the Superior Sibling

The birth order literature is re-examined: the belief that firstborns are more responsible, later-borns more creative is largely unsupported by research when controlling for family size and socioeconomic status. Birth order effects, when they exist, are small.
