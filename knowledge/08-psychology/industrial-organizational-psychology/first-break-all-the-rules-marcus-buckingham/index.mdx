What if everything you were taught about management was wrong? In 1999, Marcus Buckingham and Curt Coffman — researchers at the Gallup Organization — published the explosive findings of a 25-year study involving 80,000 managers and over a million employees. Their conclusion: the most effective managers systematically ignore the conventional wisdom of their field.

Great managers do not try to fix people's weaknesses. They do not treat everyone the same. They do not believe that anyone can achieve anything with enough effort. Instead, they select for talent over experience, define outcomes rather than steps, focus relentlessly on strengths, and find the right fit for each person rather than pushing everyone up the same career ladder.

The book introduced the Gallup Q12 — twelve survey questions that predict team performance more accurately than any other known instrument. These questions became the global standard for measuring employee engagement, administered to over 57 million employees across 212 countries. Time magazine named *First, Break All the Rules* one of the 25 Most Influential Business Management Books, and it spent 93 weeks on the New York Times bestseller list.

This book is not a theory. It is the distillation of the largest study of managerial effectiveness ever conducted. Its insights remain as counterintuitive and actionable today as they were on publication.
