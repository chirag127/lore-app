Sacks's narrative voice is the book's most distinctive feature — and its most controversial. He writes as a participant-observer, present in the room with his patients, reacting with wonder, compassion, and intellectual excitement. Where a conventional neurologist might strive for clinical detachment, Sacks leans into his own emotional response. He is moved by Jimmie's predicament, delighted by the twins' numerical gifts, humbled by Rebecca's emotional depth. The reader experiences each case through Sacks's eyes, and Sacks's eyes are not those of a detached scientist but of a curious, humane witness.

The debt to A.R. Luria is explicit throughout. Luria's "romantic science" — the phrase appears in the book's preface — was Sacks's model: a form of inquiry that treats each patient as a unique human being whose condition cannot be understood apart from their life story. Luria believed that the 19th-century tradition of the clinical case study, which combined precise observation with narrative empathy, had been lost in the rush to make neurology "scientific." Sacks set out to revive it.

Each case in the book reads like a short story. Sacks introduces a character, establishes a normal world, then reveals the neurological disruption that changes everything. There is a moment of discovery — often dramatic, sometimes comic — followed by reflection. The structure mirrors the classic medical tale format that Sacks admired, but his prose elevates it. His sentences are measured, clear, and freighted with implication. He does not need to tell you that something is profound; he trusts the story to carry that weight.

The ethical dimension of this narrative approach is delicate. Sacks risked exploiting his patients for literary effect, turning their suffering into entertainment. He was aware of this tension and addressed it directly: he sought consent, changed names, and wrote with evident respect. But the 2025 revelations about fabricated details have reopened these questions. If Sacks changed facts to improve a story, did he betray the trust of the patients who shared their struggles with him? The question haunts the book's legacy.

Yet at its best, Sacks's narrative method achieves something rare: it makes the reader care about neurological function as a dimension of human experience. After reading about Dr. P., you will never take face recognition for granted again. After Christina, you will notice the quiet miracle of knowing where your body is without looking. Sacks's gift was to make the invisible infrastructure of consciousness visible through the stories of those who lost it.
