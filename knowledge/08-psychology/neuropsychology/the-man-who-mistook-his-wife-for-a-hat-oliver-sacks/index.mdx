Twenty-four clinical tales from Oliver Sacks's career as a neurologist, each a window into the strange workings of the human brain. The book's title case — a musician who mistakes his wife's head for his hat — exemplifies Sacks's gift for finding profound lessons in the most disorienting neurological failures.

Sacks writes in the tradition of A.R. Luria's "romantic science," treating each patient not as a collection of symptoms but as a person whose condition reveals something essential about how the brain constructs our experience of reality. The cases range from memory loss so absolute that a man lives perpetually in 1945, to twins who communicate in six-digit prime numbers, to a woman who feels her own body as an alien object.
