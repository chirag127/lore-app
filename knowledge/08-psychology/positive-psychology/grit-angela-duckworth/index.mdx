Why do some people accomplish more than others of equal intelligence? Angela
Duckworth — a MacArthur Fellow, former McKinsey consultant, and University of
Pennsylvania psychology professor — spent over a decade investigating this
question. Her answer, supported by studies at West Point, the National Spelling
Bee, and Fortune 500 companies, is that the crucial differentiator is not innate
talent but **grit**: a combination of passion and perseverance for long-term
goals.

Published in 2016, *Grit* became an instant *New York Times* bestseller,
sparked a global conversation about character and achievement, and ignited
intense debate among researchers about how we measure — and whether we can
teach — the non-cognitive skills that drive success.

## Overview

*Grit* is organized into three parts. Part I defines grit, distinguishes it from
talent, and introduces Duckworth's central formula: effort counts twice. Part II
explores the four psychological assets that gritty people cultivate from within
— interest, practice, purpose, and hope. Part III examines how parents, coaches,
teachers, and organizations can foster grit from the outside in.

The book weaves together personal narrative (Duckworth's own immigrant-family
story), original research (the Grit Scale, validated across thousands of
subjects), and profiles of "grit paragons" ranging from Seattle Seahawks coach
Pete Carroll to *New Yorker* puzzle editor Will Shortz. It is both a manifesto
for effort over talent and a practical guide for cultivating resilience.

*Grit* sold over 5 million copies worldwide, was translated into 40+ languages,
and cemented Duckworth's status as one of the most visible psychologists of her
generation. Yet it also drew sharp criticism: the construct overlaps heavily with
conscientiousness, its predictive power beyond established traits is modest, and
educators have raised concerns about using grit scores to evaluate students.
The debate continues — but the book's influence on how we think about success
is undeniable.
