**Emotional Intelligence** arrived in 1995 at a cultural moment when America was obsessed with IQ, standardized testing, and the question of what makes people succeed. Daniel Goleman, a Harvard-trained psychologist and _New York Times_ science journalist, offered a provocative answer: not IQ, but something he called emotional intelligence — the ability to recognize, understand, and manage our own emotions and those of others.

The book became an international phenomenon, spending a year and a half on the _New York Times_ bestseller list and eventually selling over 5 million copies in 40 languages. TIME magazine named it one of the 25 most influential business management books. But the book was never just a business book — it wove together emerging neuroscience on the amygdala and prefrontal cortex, longitudinal psychology studies like the Marshmallow Test, and cross-cultural observations about emotional expression.

What made the book revolutionary was its core claim: that emotional competencies — self-awareness, impulse control, persistence, empathy, and social deftness — can matter more than raw intellect for success in relationships, at work, and for physical health. Even more radical was Goleman's insistence that these skills are not fixed at birth but can be learned, because the brain remains plastic throughout life. This message of hope and teachability resonated with millions and launched an entire industry of EQ assessments, training programs, and school curricula.

At its heart, the book argues that humans have two minds — the rational (thinking) mind and the emotional (feeling) mind — and that emotional intelligence is the skill of getting these two minds to work in harmony. When passions overwhelm reason, Goleman called it an "amygdala hijack" — the emotional brain seizes control before the rational brain can intervene. The emotionally intelligent person is not someone who suppresses emotion, but someone who manages the interplay between thought and feeling wisely.
