subtitle: Podcast Script — The Righteous Mind, a Liberal and a Conservative in Conversation
---

**[Host]**: Welcome to Common Ground. I'm your host. Today we're doing something
a little different. We're discussing Jonathan Haidt's *The Righteous Mind* —
a book that claims to explain why good people are divided by politics and
religion. And to do that, I've invited two people who live that division every
day. On my left, Sarah, a progressive activist and community organizer. On my
right, Mark, a conservative communications strategist. Both have read the book.
Both have strong opinions about it. Sarah, let me start with you: what did you
think?

**[Sarah]**: I'll be honest — I came to this book wanting to hate it. I'd heard
the buzz: "Haidt says conservatives are more moral." And I thought, great,
another book telling progressives we're the problem. But... I didn't hate it.
I was angry at parts of it. I still think some of it is wrong. But it made me
think in ways I wasn't expecting.

**[Mark]**: And I came to it feeling like someone was finally explaining my life.
I grew up in a conservative household, went to a liberal university, and spent
four years feeling like my moral intuitions were treated as pathologies. When
Haidt says liberals only use three of the six moral taste buds — Care, Fairness,
and Liberty — and conservatives use all six... I felt seen.

**[Sarah]**: And that's exactly what I find frustrating. You felt seen. But did
you feel *challenged*? Because I read the same data and I see a theory that
lets conservatives off the hook. "Oh, we're not prejudiced — we just have a
different moral foundation called Loyalty and Authority." Can't Loyalty to the
ingroup justify racism? Can't Authority justify oppression?

**[Mark]**: It can. But the point is that those aren't necessarily the same
thing. Haidt's whole argument is that the other side isn't *evil* — they're
responding to different moral intuitions. When I say I value loyalty, I'm not
saying I hate outsiders. I'm saying I feel a genuine moral obligation to my
community. That's a real thing. Liberals dismiss it as tribalism and then
wonder why they can't persuade anyone outside their bubble.

**[Host]**: Let's get into the mechanism. Haidt's central metaphor is the rider
and the elephant. The rider is conscious reasoning; the elephant is gut
intuition. The rider thinks it's in charge, but it's mostly a press secretary
for decisions the elephant already made. Sarah, does that ring true for you?

**[Sarah]**: Honestly? Yes. And that's the part that made me uncomfortable.
I see it in myself. I have instant gut reactions to political news — disgust
at a Trump rally, rage at a corporate tax cut — and then I find evidence to
support what I already feel. I'm guilty of the same post-hoc rationalization
I criticize in conservatives.

**[Mark]**: Right. And I see the same thing on my side. My tribe gets outraged
at a protestor burning a flag, and we rush to justify that outrage with
arguments about respect for the military. But the outrage came first. The
arguments came after. Haidt's point — and I think this is his deepest insight —
is that reason evolved to *win arguments*, not to find truth. We're all
press secretaries.

**[Host]**: And that's where Moral Foundations Theory comes in. The six taste
buds — Care, Fairness, Loyalty, Authority, Sanctity, Liberty. Haidt says
liberals build their moral cuisine on Care and Fairness; conservatives use
all six. Mark, does that describe your experience?

**[Mark]**: Almost perfectly. When I talk to liberal friends about immigration,
they talk about compassion for refugees. That's Care. When I talk about border
security, I'm activating Loyalty (protecting my country) and Authority
(respecting the rule of law). We're talking past each other because we're
speaking different moral languages. Haidt gave me the vocabulary to understand
that.

**[Sarah]**: But here's my problem with that framework. When you call it a
"moral foundation," you're implying it's equally valid. Is the Loyalty
foundation that makes someone fly a Confederate flag really *moral*? Or is it
just a fancy name for ingroup bias? Haidt dances on the line between describing
why people feel what they feel and *justifying* those feelings.

**[Mark]**: That's a fair critique. I think Haidt would say: understanding is
not endorsing. You can understand why a coal miner in West Virginia votes
against his economic interests — because his Loyalty and Sanctity foundations
are triggered by the Democrat who wants to shut down his way of life. You don't
have to agree with his vote. But if you call him stupid or racist, you've just
triggered his Loyalty foundation harder, and you've lost any chance of
persuasion.

**[Sarah]**: And that part I agree with. I've been that person. I've called
people stupid for voting Republican. And it didn't change anyone's mind. But
I worry about the political implications of Haidt's argument. He says
conservatives have a "moral advantage" because they appeal to more foundations.
That reads to me as: "liberals, you're losing because your morality is
narrower. Be more like conservatives." That's a normative claim dressed up
as science.

**[Mark]**: Or maybe it's a descriptive claim with strategic implications.
If you want to win elections, you need to understand why people vote the way
they do. Haidt is saying: conservatives win because they speak to the full
range of human moral intuitions. If liberals want to compete, they need to
learn to speak Loyalty and Sanctity — not abandon their own values, but
translate them.

**[Sarah]**: But should we have to? What if the Loyalty and Sanctity
foundations are inherently conservative? What if trying to engage them means
compromising on progressive values?

**[Mark]**: Then you accept that you'll never persuade a majority. And that's
a choice. But don't blame conservatives for winning when you refuse to learn
their language.

**[Host]**: This brings us to Part 3 of the book. Haidt argues that humans
evolved for group selection — we're 90% chimp (selfish individual) and 10%
bee (hive-oriented group member). Morality binds us into teams and blinds us
to other perspectives. Sarah, how does that land with you?

**[Sarah]**: I actually found this the most compelling part. The idea that
religion evolved as a "hive switch" — a way to bind strangers into cooperative
groups — reframed how I think about faith. I'm an atheist, and I used to see
religion as pure delusion. Haidt helped me see it as an adaptive technology
for cooperation. I still don't believe, but I understand the function.

**[Mark]**: And for me as a Christian, that chapter was surprisingly
affirming. Haidt isn't saying religious beliefs are true. But he's saying
they're *functional* — they solved a real evolutionary problem. And that's a
lot more respectful than the New Atheist position that religious people are
just stupid.

**[Sarah]**: See, this is the bind I'm in with this book. Every time I want
to dismiss it, Haidt says something that makes me think. But every time I
want to embrace it, my progressive friends would say I'm giving cover to the
other side. Can we be honest about something?

**[Mark]**: Go ahead.

**[Sarah]**: I think the biggest barrier to cross-partisan understanding is
not that we don't understand each other's foundations. It's that we don't
*trust* each other. I can understand that your Loyalty foundation makes you
support a strong military. But I also know that "loyalty" has been used to
justify wars I consider immoral. You can understand that my Care foundation
makes me support universal healthcare. But you also know that "compassion"
has been used to justify welfare systems you consider destructive. We can
map each other's foundations all day. Trust is a different thing.

**[Mark]**: That's actually the most honest thing I've heard in this
conversation. And I think Haidt would agree. His goal isn't to make everyone
agree. It's to make the disagreement less toxic. If I understand that you're
not *evil* — you just weight Care higher than Loyalty — I can disagree with
you without demonizing you. And that's the precondition for trust.

**[Sarah]**: So here's what I'm taking away. MFT is a useful map. But a map
is not the territory. It explains some things, it oversimplifies others.
The danger is using it as a weapon — "see, my side is more moral because we
use more foundations." The value is using it as a lens — "oh, that's why
we're talking past each other."

**[Mark]**: And I'd add: the book's deepest lesson is not about the other
side. It's about yourself. Which of your own moral foundations are you blind
to? Which intuitions do you treat as self-evident truths when they're really
just the configuration of your own elephant? That's humbling. And humility
is what we need more of.

**[Host]**: So where do we go from here? If Haidt is broadly right —
intuitions come first, morality is multi-foundational, and we're wired for
groupishness — what do we actually *do*?

**[Sarah]**: I think the practical takeaway is: before you attack, translate.
Whatever your position, ask yourself: can I express this in terms that honor
the other side's foundations? For me as a progressive, that means learning
to talk about climate change not just as "save the planet" (Care) but as
"protect God's creation" (Sanctity) and "patriotic duty to preserve our
country" (Loyalty). Those frames feel uncomfortable to me. But they reach
people who don't share my Care-first morality.

**[Mark]**: And for me as a conservative, it means recognizing that when a
liberal argues for universal healthcare, they're not trying to destroy
personal responsibility. They're acting from a genuine Care intuition that
is as morally serious as my Loyalty and Authority intuitions. I don't have
to agree with their policy. But I should stop treating their motives as
suspect.

**[Host]**: Final question. One sentence each. Did this book make you
understand the other side better?

**[Sarah]**: Yes. It made me realize that conservatives aren't morally
deficient — they're morally *different*. And that difference deserves respect,
not contempt.

**[Mark]**: And it made me realize that liberals aren't naive — they're
responding to real moral foundations that I undervalue. We need both
perspectives. That's the point of a pluralistic society.

**[Host]**: *The Righteous Mind* by Jonathan Haidt. Whether you agree with
every claim or not — and neither of our guests did — it's a book that
changes how you argue. And in a world that's more polarized than ever,
that's no small thing. Thanks to Sarah and Mark for a real conversation.
Thanks for listening. This is Common Ground.
