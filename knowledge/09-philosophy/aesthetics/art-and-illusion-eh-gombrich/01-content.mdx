## Introduction: Psychology and the Riddle of Style

Gombrich poses the central question of the book: why does art have a history? If representation were simply a matter of copying nature, styles should converge on a single standard of accuracy. But they do not. The answer, Gombrich argues, lies in the psychology of perception and the role of convention in image-making.

He introduces the concepts that will organize the book: schema (the learned formula that artists use as a starting point), making and matching (the process of adjusting the schema to fit the observed world), and the beholder's share (the active contribution of the viewer to the experience of illusion).

## Part 1: The Limits of Likeness

### Chapter 1: From Light into Paint

Gombrich examines the problem of representing light and color. Artists cannot literally reproduce the brightness of the sun or the subtlety of atmospheric light; they must find equivalents in the medium of paint. The history of painting is in part a history of discovering such equivalences.

John Constable claimed that "painting is a science" — a systematic investigation of the natural world. Gombrich takes Constable's claim seriously and shows how the attempt to represent the effects of light drove the development of new pictorial techniques.

### Chapter 2: Truth and Stereotype

A picture cannot be true or false in the way a statement can; the caption of a picture can be judged for truth, but the picture itself is a construction. Artists begin not with observation but with a schema — a first approximate category that is gradually tightened to fit the form to be reproduced.

Gombrich introduces his famous example: a Chinese artist painting a landscape uses a different set of conventions than a European artist. Neither is simply "seeing" nature; both are applying learned schemata.

## Part 2: Function and Form

### Chapter 3: Pygmalion's Power

This chapter explores the ancient belief that images have magical power. The earliest images were not made to be admired but to be effective — to commemorate, to protect, to worship. The aesthetic attitude toward images is a late development.

Gombrich argues that the magical function of images explains why early art is conceptual rather than perceptual: the artist represents what he knows to exist, not what he sees from a particular viewpoint.

### Chapter 4: Reflections on the Greek Revolution

The Greek discovery of naturalistic representation around the fifth century BCE was not simply a leap forward in skill but a transformation of the purpose of art. The Greeks invented the schema for representing the body in action, for creating the illusion of three-dimensional space, and for depicting facial expression.

Gombrich argues that this revolution was made possible by a shift in the function of art from the magical to the mimetic. The schema was not abandoned but refined to serve new purposes.

### Chapter 5: Formula and Experience

The medieval artist, like the Greek artist, worked with formulas. But where the Greeks used their formulas to create the illusion of natural appearances, medieval artists used formulas to convey spiritual meanings. The difference is not of skill but of intention.

Gombrich shows how the recovery of naturalistic representation in the Renaissance was a recovery of ancient schemata, not a purely empirical discovery. Artists learned from ancient sculpture before they learned from nature.

## Part 3: The Beholder's Share

### Chapter 6: The Image in the Clouds

Gombrich introduces the concept of projection: the tendency to see meaningful forms in ambiguous patterns, such as clouds or inkblots. Artists exploit this tendency by providing just enough cues to trigger the viewer's projective capacity.

The beholder's share is the active contribution the viewer makes to the experience of the image. Without this contribution, the marks on the canvas are merely paint.

### Chapter 7: Conditions of Illusion

Illusion requires the right conditions. The viewer must be sufficiently engaged to project meaning onto the image, but not so engaged that the illusion collapses. Gombrich discusses the "guided projection" that characterizes the perception of pictorial art.

He introduces the important concept of "testing" — the viewer constantly tests hypotheses about what the image represents, and the successful image confirms enough of these hypotheses to sustain the illusion.

### Chapter 8: Ambiguities of the Third Dimension

The representation of three-dimensional space on a flat surface is inherently ambiguous. Gombrich shows how artists have exploited this ambiguity to create depth while also acknowledging the flatness of the surface.

He draws on the psychology of perception, particularly the work of the Gestalt psychologists, to explain how the mind resolves spatial ambiguities.

## Part 4: Invention and Discovery

### Chapter 9: The Analysis of Vision in Art

Gombrich examines the claim that impressionism and post-impressionism represent a more accurate analysis of vision than traditional perspective. He argues that this claim is misleading: what the impressionists discovered was not a truer representation of visual experience but a new set of schemata.

### Chapter 10: The Experiment of Caricature

Caricature is not a distortion of accurate representation but a refinement of it. The caricaturist must first know how to produce a faithful likeness before he can exaggerate it selectively. Gombrich uses caricature to demonstrate the relationship between schema and deviation.

### Chapter 11: From Representation to Expression

The final chapter traces the transition from representation as the primary goal of art to expression as an independent artistic value. Gombrich argues that expression is not the opposite of representation but a development of it — the expressive power of art depends on the same schematic processes as representational accuracy.
