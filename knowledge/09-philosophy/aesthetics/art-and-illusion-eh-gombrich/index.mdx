E.H. Gombrich's *Art and Illusion: A Study in the Psychology of Pictorial Representation* (1960) is one of the most influential works of art theory of the twentieth century. Derived from the A.W. Mellon Lectures in the Fine Arts delivered at the National Gallery of Art in Washington in 1956, the book brings together art history, perceptual psychology, and philosophy to answer a fundamental question: why do different ages and cultures represent the visible world in such different ways?

<BookHeader />

Born in Vienna in 1909, Ernst Gombrich came to London in 1936 to work at the Warburg Institute, which he directed from 1959 until 1976. His *The Story of Art* (1950) is the best-selling art book ever written. Deeply influenced by the psychology of Karl Popper (a close friend) and the art history of Aby Warburg, Gombrich brought to art history a rigorous commitment to falsifiability and a suspicion of Hegelian grand narratives.
