John Dewey's *Art as Experience* (1934) is the most important work of American pragmatist aesthetics. Based on the first William James Lectures at Harvard in 1932, the book dismantles the barriers between art and life, arguing that aesthetic experience is not a rarefied museum category but the consummatory phase of every fulfilled experience. Dewey draws on biology, psychology, and the history of art to show how perception, emotion, and intelligence cooperate in both making and appreciating art.

The book had a profound influence on art education, the Barnes Foundation's pedagogy, and later thinkers such as Richard Shusterman and Arnold Berleant. Though marginalized by the analytic revolution in mid-century aesthetics, *Art as Experience* has enjoyed a major revival since the 1990s as pragmatist and naturalist approaches to aesthetics have regained prominence.

<BookHeader />

Born in Burlington, Vermont in 1859, John Dewey was the leading figure of American pragmatism alongside Charles Sanders Peirce and William James. A prolific philosopher, psychologist, and educational reformer, his work spanned logic, ethics, epistemology, and social theory. *Art as Experience* represents the culmination of his lifelong effort to overcome the dualisms — mind/body, theory/practice, art/life — that he believed had fragmented modern thought. At 355 pages, it remains the most ambitious statement of a naturalist, experience-centered philosophy of art.
