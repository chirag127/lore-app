David Byrne's *How Music Works* (2012) is a magisterial, genre-defying book that is part memoir, part music theory, part industry exposé, and part evolutionary anthropology. The Talking Heads co-founder argues against the romantic notion of the solitary genius and instead shows how context — the shape of a room, the limitations of recording tape, the economics of a record deal — fundamentally determines what music gets made. The book was a *New York Times* bestseller and earned praise from *The Guardian*, *The A.V. Club*, *The Washington Post*, and others, while also drawing a famously dismissive review from Dwight Garner in the *Times*.

<BookHeader />

Born in Scotland in 1952 and raised in Baltimore, David Byrne co-founded Talking Heads in 1975, a band that would become one of the most critically acclaimed and influential groups of the rock era. Beyond the band, Byrne has collaborated with Brian Eno, Twyla Tharp, Robert Wilson, and Fatboy Slim; scored films (winning an Oscar for *The Last Emperor*); created Broadway productions (*American Utopia*, *Here Lies Love*); and authored several books including *Bicycle Diaries*. His intellectual style combines a journalist's curiosity with an artist's practical knowledge, and he has described himself as having traits associated with Asperger's syndrome, which may contribute to his characteristically analytical, detached perspective.
