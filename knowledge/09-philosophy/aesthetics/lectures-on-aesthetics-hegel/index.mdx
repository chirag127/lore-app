G. W. F. Hegel's *Lectures on Aesthetics* (1835) is the most ambitious philosophy of art ever written. Compiled posthumously from his lecture notes and student transcripts by Heinrich Gustav Hotho, the work presents a systematic account of art's nature, history, and ultimate significance. Hegel defines art as "the sensuous manifestation of the Idea" and traces its development through three world-historical stages: Symbolic (ancient Eastern art, where form and content fail to match), Classical (Greek art, where they achieve perfect unity), and Romantic (Christian and modern art, where spirit transcends sensuous embodiment).

The lectures are famous for Hegel's controversial thesis that art, in its highest vocation, is for us a thing of the past — the claim now known as the "end of art" thesis. This does not mean art stops being made but that it ceases to be the supreme vehicle for human self-understanding.

<BookHeader />

Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel (1770-1831) was the culminating figure of German Idealism. He taught at the University of Berlin from 1818 until his death in 1831, delivering the lectures on aesthetics multiple times. His system attempted to comprehend all of reality — nature, history, art, religion, philosophy — as the self-unfolding of Absolute Spirit through dialectical development.
