Herbert Marcuse's *The Aesthetic Dimension: Toward a Critique of Marxist Aesthetics* (1977) is the final major work by one of the most influential thinkers of the Frankfurt School. Written in response to orthodox Marxist theories that demanded art directly reflect class struggle and economic realities, Marcuse argues for the emancipatory power of art's formal autonomy. Art resists the repressive apparatus of advanced industrial society not by carrying political messages but by creating a world of form that transcends the given reality and evokes the promise of liberation.

<BookHeader />

Born in Berlin in 1898, Herbert Marcuse fled Nazi Germany in 1933 and settled in the United States, where he became a leading figure of the Frankfurt School. His works *Eros and Civilization* (1955) and *One-Dimensional Man* (1964) made him a intellectual hero of the New Left. *The Aesthetic Dimension* synthesizes his lifelong engagement with aesthetics, psychoanalysis, and Marxist theory into a compact, densely argued defense of art as a site of resistance.
