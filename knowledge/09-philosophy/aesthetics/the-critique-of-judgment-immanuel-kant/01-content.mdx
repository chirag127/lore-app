## Introduction: The Need for a Critique of Judgment

Kant opens by explaining why judgment requires its own critique. The understanding legislates for nature (theoretical reason) and reason legislates for freedom (practical reason), but there is a gulf between the two. Judgment is the mediating faculty that allows us to move from nature to freedom. Its a priori principle is **purposiveness** (Zweckmassigkeit): the idea that nature can be judged as if it were designed for our cognitive faculties.

Kant distinguishes **determining judgment** (subsuming particulars under universal laws already given by the understanding) from **reflective judgment** (finding a universal for a particular, requiring a principle of its own). Reflective judgment is the subject of the third Critique.

## Part I: Critique of Aesthetic Judgment

### First Book: Analytic of the Beautiful (SS1-22)

Kant analyzes judgments of taste through four "moments" corresponding to the logical categories of quality, quantity, relation, and modality.

**First Moment: Quality (SS1-5).** The judgment of taste is **disinterested** — it involves no interest in the real existence of the object. This distinguishes the beautiful from the agreeable (which gratifies the senses) and the good (which commands respect). Beauty pleases without reference to need or desire.

**Second Moment: Quantity (SS6-9).** The judgment of taste claims **universal validity** without being based on concepts. We speak of beauty as if it were a property of the object, but it is really rooted in the subject's feeling. This is possible because the mental state of free play between imagination and understanding is universally communicable.

**Third Moment: Relation (SS10-17).** Beauty is **purposiveness without purpose**. The object appears designed (purposive) but without any specific end or concept telling us what it ought to be. Kant distinguishes free beauty (Pulchritudo vaga) — which presupposes no concept of what the object should be, like natural beauty or abstract design — from dependent beauty (Pulchritudo adhaerens) — which does presuppose a concept, like the beauty of a church or a horse.

**Fourth Moment: Modality (SS18-22).** The judgment of beauty carries **necessity** — not logical or practical necessity, but exemplary necessity. We expect everyone ought to agree with our judgment, grounded in the idea of a **common sense** (sensus communis) that all humans share.

### Second Book: Analytic of the Sublime (SS23-29)

Kant distinguishes the sublime from the beautiful. The beautiful concerns form and boundedness; the sublime concerns the formless and unbounded. There are two kinds:

**The Mathematically Sublime (SS25-26).** Encountered when we confront something absolutely large — the vastness of the starry sky, the scale of the pyramids. Our imagination fails to comprehend such magnitude, and this failure reveals the superiority of reason, which can think the infinite even though the senses cannot grasp it.

**The Dynamically Sublime (SS28-29).** Experienced when we face overwhelming natural power — a storm at sea, a volcanic eruption — from a position of safety. The experience is of our physical vulnerability combined with our spiritual independence from nature. Fear yields to a feeling of our rational vocation.

### Deduction of Pure Aesthetic Judgments (SS30-38)

Kant argues that judgments of taste can legitimately claim universal validity because the free play of imagination and understanding that grounds them is a condition of cognition as such. The deduction shows that the pleasure in beauty is not merely personal but rests on the subjective condition of the possibility of knowledge.

### Dialectic of Aesthetic Judgment (SS39-44)

The antinomy of taste: the thesis that judgments of taste are not based on concepts (else they could be proven), the antithesis that they are based on concepts (else we could not dispute them). Kant resolves the antinomy by distinguishing between determinate concepts of the understanding (which cannot ground taste) and the indeterminate concept of the supersensible substrate of appearances (which can).

### Art, Genius, and Aesthetic Ideas (SS45-54)

Fine art is distinguished from nature by being a conscious production, but it must appear natural — free from artifice. **Genius** is the innate talent through which nature gives the rule to art. Genius has four characteristics: originality, exemplarity (serving as a model), the inability to describe scientifically how it produces its works, and being limited to fine art.

Genius works through **aesthetic ideas** — representations of the imagination that occasion much thought without any definite concept being adequate to them. Aesthetic ideas are the counterpart of rational ideas; they expand the mind by setting the imagination into free play.

## Part II: Critique of Teleological Judgment

### Analytic of Teleological Judgment (SS60-68)

Kant analyzes the concept of a **natural end** (Naturzweck): an organized being in which the parts are both means and ends for each other and for the whole. Organisms cannot be fully explained by mechanical laws; we must judge them as if they were purposely designed.

### Dialectic of Teleological Judgment (SS69-78)

The antinomy of teleological judgment: mechanism and teleology appear to conflict. Kant resolves this by arguing that they are regulative principles, not constitutive ones. We must pursue mechanical explanation as far as possible but supplement it with teleological concepts when mechanism proves insufficient. The ultimate ground of the unity of mechanism and teleology lies in the supersensible, which we cannot know.

### Methodology of Teleological Judgment (SS79-91)

Kant explores the implications of teleology for natural science and theology. Teleological judgment leads us to think of nature as a system of ends, culminating in the human being as the ultimate end of creation — not for biological reasons but because humans can set ends through reason and culture.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment
This summary covers the major sections of both parts of the Critique. It cannot capture the density of Kant's arguments or the intricacies of his distinctions.

### Recommended Reading Path
| Reader Type | Time | What to Read |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | ~15 min | This summary |
| Interested | ~6-8 hr | Introduction, Analytic of the Beautiful, Analytic of the Sublime |
| Scholar/Practitioner | ~18-20 hr | Full text with commentary |

### Chapters to Read in Full
- **SS1-22: Analytic of the Beautiful** — The heart of Kant's aesthetic theory
- **SS23-29: Analytic of the Sublime** — The theory of sublimity
- **SS46-49: Genius and Aesthetic Ideas** — Kant's theory of artistic creation
- **The Two Introductions** — Essential for understanding the system

### What You'll Miss by Not Reading the Full Book
- The intricate architectonic of Kant's system
- The detailed resolution of the antinomies
- The connection between aesthetics and morality
- Kant's specific examples of artworks and natural phenomena
