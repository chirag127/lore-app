Immanuel Kant's *Critique of Judgment* (1790) is the third of his three great Critiques, completing the critical project begun in the *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781) and *Critique of Practical Reason* (1788). It addresses the faculty of judgment, which Kant saw as the bridge between theoretical understanding (nature, necessity) and practical reason (freedom, morality). The work contains two major parts: the "Critique of Aesthetic Judgment," covering the beautiful and the sublime, and the "Critique of Teleological Judgment," covering the concept of natural purposes.

The *Critique of Judgment* transformed Western aesthetics. Its concepts — disinterested pleasure, purposiveness without purpose, the sublime, genius, free play of the faculties — became permanent fixtures in art theory. Kant's attempt to show that aesthetic experience is both subjective and universally valid remains one of the most elegant and contested arguments in the history of philosophy.

<BookHeader />

Immanuel Kant (1724-1804) lived his entire life in Konigsberg, East Prussia. The author of the three Critiques, he revolutionized Western philosophy by arguing that the mind actively structures experience rather than passively receiving it. The *Critique of Judgment* was his final major work, published when he was 66, and it represents the culmination of his attempt to unify the domains of nature and freedom within a single philosophical system.
