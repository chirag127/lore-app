George Santayana's *The Sense of Beauty* (1896) is a foundational text of American aesthetics. Written — as Santayana himself wryly admitted — to secure his tenure at Harvard, it has outlasted its institutional origins to become a classic. The book defines beauty as "pleasure objectified": not a property of objects but a human value projected onto them. This naturalistic approach grounds aesthetics in psychology and biology rather than in transcendent metaphysics.

The book is divided into four parts: The Nature of Beauty, The Materials of Beauty, Form, and Expression. Santayana's prose is elegant and epigrammatic, far removed from the technical density of German aesthetics.

<BookHeader />

George Santayana (1863-1952) was a Spanish-born American philosopher, poet, and novelist. He taught at Harvard from 1889 to 1912, where his students included T. S. Eliot, Wallace Stevens, and Walter Lippmann. He is best known for his maxim "Those who cannot remember the past are condemned to repeat it" and for his systematic works *The Life of Reason* (1905-1906) and *The Realms of Being* (1927-1940).
