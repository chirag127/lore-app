## Chapter 1: Works of Art and Mere Real Things

Danto opens with the problem that organizes the entire book: how can something be a work of art when it looks exactly like an ordinary thing? He introduces his famous thought experiment — the gallery of perceptually indiscernible red canvases, each with a different interpretation and status (one is a painting of red, another is Kierkegaard's mood, another is a minimalist artwork). The example establishes that art cannot be identified by perceptual criteria alone.

The difference between an artwork and a mere thing is not visible; it is a matter of interpretation, history, and theory. Danto calls this the "transfiguration" of the commonplace — the transformation of ordinary objects into art through the application of an interpretative framework.

## Chapter 2: Content and Causation

Danto argues that artworks are about something: they have content. But content alone is not enough — a scientific diagram has content but is not usually art. The content must be caused by the artist's intentions and embodied in the material of the work in a way that is essential to its meaning.

He introduces the concept of "aboutness" as the defining feature of representations. Artworks are representations, but not all representations are artworks. The difference lies in how the representation is related to its content and to the intentions of its maker.

## Chapter 3: Philosophy and Art

This chapter locates Danto's project in the history of philosophy. He argues that the relationship between art and philosophy has been one of progressive clarification: art has gradually revealed its own nature, culminating in the readymades and pop art that force the question "What is art?" explicitly.

Danto draws on Hegel's philosophy of history to argue that art has become self-conscious. The moment when art becomes philosophical is also the moment when philosophy can finally understand what art is. This is the famous "end of art" thesis: not the end of art-making but the end of art's progressive historical narrative.

## Chapter 4: Aesthetics and the Work of Art

Danto makes a crucial distinction between aesthetics (the philosophy of beauty) and the philosophy of art. Aesthetics concerns perceptual pleasure and the experience of beauty; the philosophy of art concerns the nature and conditions of artworks. The confusion between the two has led to bad theories of art.

Artworks need not be beautiful, and beautiful objects need not be artworks. The aesthetic properties of a work are independent of its status as art. Danto argues that this distinction is essential for understanding contemporary art, which often deliberately rejects beauty.

## Chapter 5: Interpretation and Identification

Interpretation is not an external commentary on the artwork; it is constitutive of the artwork. Danto introduces the concept of the "is of artistic identification" — the special sense in which we say "this patch of red is Icarus" when looking at a painting. This is different from the ordinary "is" of identity.

Interpretation determines what the work is about and how it is to be understood. Different interpretations produce different artworks, even when the physical object remains the same. This is why the Brillo Box is art and the supermarket carton is not: the artworld interpretation transfigures the object.

## Chapter 6: Works of Art and Mere Representations

Danto distinguishes artworks from other kinds of representations. Mere representations (maps, diagrams, photographs) refer to their subjects but do not embody meaning in the way art does. Artworks are representations that present their content through a medium in a way that is inseparable from the meaning.

He introduces the concept of "metaphorical representation": art represents not by copying but by metaphorical transfiguration. The work is not just about its subject; it presents its subject through a medium that transforms our understanding of it.

## Chapter 7: Metaphor, Expression, and Style

Danto develops his account of how art means. Metaphor is central: art works by saying one thing and meaning another, by presenting the ordinary in a transfigured form. Expression is the way the work's content is colored by the artist's attitude and the work's internal organization.

Style is the pervasive quality of the work that results from the artist's choices. Style is not decoration but the very way meaning is embodied. Danto distinguishes individual style (the unique way an artist works) from period style (the conventions of an era) and argues that genuine style is the embodiment of the artist's way of seeing the world.

## Chapter 8: Conclusion: Philosophy and the Transfiguration

Danto concludes by reflecting on the philosophical significance of the transfiguration of the commonplace. Art, by making ordinary things strange, reveals the power of interpretation to transform reality. The transfiguration is not an illusion; it is a real change in the status of the object, effected by the artworld and sustained by interpretation.

The book ends by affirming that the philosophy of art is possible and necessary. The transfiguration of the commonplace is not a mystery but a philosophical problem that can be solved — and Danto believes he has solved it.
