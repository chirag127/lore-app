## Book Context & Background

*The Transfiguration of the Commonplace* was published in 1981, at a moment when analytic aesthetics was consolidating as a distinct subdiscipline. Monroe Beardsley's *Aesthetics* (1958) and Nelson Goodman's *Languages of Art* (1968) had established the terms of debate, and the institutional theory of George Dickie was the dominant approach to defining art.

Danto's book was a response to the crisis created by the readymades of Marcel Duchamp and the pop art of Andy Warhol. These works had shown that anything could be art, raising the question of whether art had any essence at all. The anti-essentialist position, derived from Wittgenstein's concept of family resemblance (Morris Weitz, 1956), held that art cannot be defined. Danto set out to prove the anti-essentialists wrong.

## About the Author

Arthur C. Danto (1924-2013) was Johnsonian Professor of Philosophy at Columbia University and art critic for *The Nation*. Before *The Transfiguration of the Commonplace*, he had established his reputation with works in the philosophy of history (*Analytical Philosophy of History*, 1965) and the philosophy of action (*Analytical Philosophy of Action*, 1973).

Danto's intellectual formation combined the rigor of analytic philosophy with the historical sweep of Continental thought. He was deeply influenced by Hegel, Nietzsche, and Sartre, and he brought to analytic aesthetics a historical sensibility that was unusual in the tradition. His intellectual biases include a commitment to intentional explanation, a historicist understanding of art's development, and a belief in the power of conceptual analysis to solve philosophical problems.

## Core Thesis & Argument

Danto's central claim is that artworks differ from ordinary objects in kind, not merely in degree, and that this difference is constituted by interpretation. An artwork is not a physical object with special properties; it is a physical object transfigured by an interpretation that gives it meaning and structure.

The argument proceeds through a series of distinctions: between art and reality, between aesthetics and art theory, between representation and expression, between interpretation and identification. Danto's method is to show that the key concepts of art theory — representation, expression, style, metaphor — can be given precise philosophical definitions that together constitute a real definition of art.

## Thematic Analysis

**Indiscernibility.** The most important theme. The fact that artworks can be indistinguishable from ordinary things reveals that art's essence is not perceptual but conceptual. This is Danto's fundamental insight.

**Interpretation as Constitution.** Interpretation does not discover the artwork; it creates it. The artwork is the object under an interpretation. This does not mean that anything can be art (the interpretation must be appropriate to the artworld context), but it does mean that the artwork's identity is not fixed by its physical properties.

**The Artworld.** Danto's famous concept, introduced in 1964, refers to the historical and theoretical context that makes interpretation possible. The artworld is not a social institution but an epistemic field — a space of reasons and meanings.

**Metaphor and Transfiguration.** Art transfigures the ordinary by treating it metaphorically. The Brillo Box is not just a representation of a Brillo box; it is a metaphor for something — the nature of art, the relationship between commerce and culture, the conditions of representation itself.

## Argumentation & Evidence

Danto's method is conceptual analysis supported by thought experiments and historical examples. His evidence consists of carefully constructed cases of perceptual indiscernibility, readings of specific artworks (Warhol's Brillo Box, Duchamp's Fountain, Pollock's drips), and philosophical arguments drawn from the history of aesthetics.

The argument is strongest when Danto develops his positive theory and weakest when he makes historical claims about the end of art or the necessary development of art history. The book's dialectical engagement with other philosophers (Goodman, Dickie, Wollheim) is one of its greatest strengths.

## Strengths

**Systematic ambition.** The book is the most systematic attempt in analytic aesthetics to provide a comprehensive definition of art.

**Conceptual innovation.** The concepts of indiscernibility, the "is of artistic identification," and transfiguration are original and productive.

**Historical-philosophical integration.** Danto successfully integrates the history of art with philosophical analysis, showing that the history of art illuminates art's essence.

**Engagement with contemporary art.** The book takes seriously the radical innovations of twentieth-century art and shows that philosophy can explain them.

**Clarity and wit.** Despite its difficulty, the book is written with Danto's characteristic clarity and humor.

## Criticisms & Weaknesses

**Marcia Muelder Eaton (1982)** in her *Journal of Aesthetics and Art Criticism* review argued that Danto's theory is too intellectualist, ignoring the role of perception and emotional response in art. Eaton contended that Danto's emphasis on interpretation and theory underestimates the aesthetic dimension of art.

**Noel Carroll (1993)** argued that Danto's definition is insufficient because it cannot distinguish art from non-art in the absence of an artworld context. Carroll developed an alternative "narrative" theory of art that emphasizes how artworks are embedded in art-historical narratives.

**Crispin Sartwell (1988)** in *Aesthetic Dualism and the Transfiguration of the Commonplace* argued that Danto's theory reproduces a problematic dualism between the physical object and its interpretation. Sartwell contended that this dualism is incompatible with the embodied character of aesthetic experience.

**George Dickie (1984)** argued that Danto's theory is an unwitting version of the institutional theory. Dickie claimed that Danto's "artworld" functions exactly like the social institution Dickie had described, and that Danto's differences with the institutional theory are more apparent than real.

**Joseph Margolis (1981)** argued that Danto's theory cannot account for the ontological variety of artworks. Margolis contended that Danto's model, based on the paradigm of the painting or sculpture, does not apply to performance art, conceptual art, or other temporal forms.

## Comparative Analysis

*The Transfiguration of the Commonplace* stands in direct relation to Nelson Goodman's *Languages of Art* (1968), which similarly analyzes art in terms of symbolic systems. Danto differs from Goodman in emphasizing the role of interpretation and the artworld.

The book engages with the institutional theory of George Dickie (*Art and the Aesthetic*, 1974), rejecting Dickie's sociological functionalism in favor of a more intentionalist and historical account.

Danto's Hegelianism distinguishes him from most analytic aestheticians. He shares with Hegel the view that art has a history that reveals its essence, and that the end of this history is art's self-consciousness.

In relation to the other books in this directory, Danto's work is the most systematically philosophical and the most closely tied to the analytic tradition. It stands in implicit opposition to Dewey's naturalism and Berger's Marxism.

## Impact & Legacy

*The Transfiguration of the Commonplace* is widely regarded as the most important work of analytic aesthetics of its generation. It established Danto as the leading philosopher of art in the English-speaking world and set the terms for subsequent debates about the definition of art.

The book influenced a generation of aestheticians and art critics, and its concepts — the artworld, indiscernibility, the end of art, embodied meaning — have become standard tools in discussions of contemporary art.

Despite its influence, the book has also been the target of sustained criticism. The institutional theory, the narrative theory (Carroll), and various forms of naturalism and pragmatism have all been developed in response to Danto's positions. The debate continues.
