Arthur Danto's *The Transfiguration of the Commonplace: A Philosophy of Art* (1981) is the most important work of analytic aesthetics of the late twentieth century. Taking as its starting point the philosophical scandal of artworks that cannot be visually distinguished from ordinary things — Marcel Duchamp's readymades, Andy Warhol's Brillo Boxes — the book develops a systematic theory of art organized around the concepts of representation, interpretation, metaphor, expression, and style.

<BookHeader />

Arthur C. Danto (1924-2013) was the preeminent American philosopher of art of his generation. Before turning to aesthetics, he had established his reputation in the philosophy of history and the philosophy of action. *The Transfiguration of the Commonplace* represents the application of Danto's analytical method to the problem that had haunted him since his encounter with Warhol's Brillo Box in 1964 at the Stable Gallery in New York.
