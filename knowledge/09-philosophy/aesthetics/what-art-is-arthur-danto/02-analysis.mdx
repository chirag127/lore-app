## Book Context & Background

*What Art Is* was published in 2013, the year of Danto's death, as the culmination of more than five decades of thinking about the nature of art. The book appears at a moment when the definition of art had become one of the central topics of analytic aesthetics, with competing theories (institutional, historical, functional) vying for acceptance.

Danto's own work had helped create this situation. His 1964 article "The Artworld" and his 1981 book *The Transfiguration of the Commonplace* had established the terms of debate for a generation of aestheticians. *What Art Is* is his final attempt to settle the question he had posed fifty years earlier.

## About the Author

Arthur Coleman Danto (1924-2013) was the most influential American aesthetician of the late twentieth century. He studied art at Wayne State University before turning to philosophy at Columbia, where he spent his entire career. He was also a prolific art critic for *The Nation* from 1984 to 2009.

Danto was deeply influenced by Hegel's philosophy of history, Nietzsche's critique of metaphysics, and the analytic tradition's commitment to clarity and argument. His intellectual biases include a strong historicism (art must be understood in its historical context), a commitment to intentional explanation (artworks express the intentions of their makers), and a suspicion of naturalistic or biological approaches to art.

## Core Thesis & Argument

Danto's central claim is that art has a real definition: a work of art is an embodied meaning. This definition has two necessary conditions: (1) aboutness (the work has content) and (2) embodiment (the content is presented through a material medium). The definition is intended to be both necessary and sufficient — it applies to all art, from the cave paintings of Lascaux to the readymades of Duchamp.

The book argues against three alternative views: that art cannot be defined (the anti-essentialist position), that art is defined by its aesthetic properties (the formalist position), and that art is defined by institutional status (the institutional theory).

## Thematic Analysis

**Indiscernibility.** The Brillo Box example establishes that art cannot be defined perceptually. Since an artwork can be perceptually indistinguishable from a non-art object, the difference must be conceptual.

**Embodied Meaning.** Danto's positive definition emphasizes that art is not mere representation but representation that embodies its content in a way that is essential to its meaning. A painting of a crucifixion does not just depict a crucifixion; it presents the meaning of the crucifixion through its formal and material choices.

**The End of Art.** Danto's Hegelian thesis that art has reached the end of its historical mission. Art no longer develops progressively; it has entered a post-historical phase where everything is permitted.

**The Artworld.** The theoretical context that enables something to be seen as art. The artworld is not a social institution but an epistemic framework — a way of understanding that transforms mere things into bearers of meaning.

## Argumentation & Evidence

Danto's method is philosophical rather than empirical. He does not survey artistic practices or conduct psychological experiments. His evidence consists of thought experiments (the Brillo Box), examples from art history (Duchamp, Warhol, Pollock), and conceptual analysis.

The argument is strongest when Danto shows how his definition handles problematic cases and weakest when he makes sweeping historical claims without detailed evidence. The end of art thesis, in particular, rests on a selective reading of art history.

## Strengths

**Clarity.** Danto states his definition with admirable precision. The distinction between aboutness and embodiment is clear and operational.

**Explanatory power.** The embodied meaning definition accounts for traditional art (which has both content and form) and for conceptual art (which has content but often minimal perceptual embodiment).

**Historical sensitivity.** Danto's definition acknowledges the historical character of art without reducing it to mere convention.

**Critical engagement.** The book engages seriously with alternative views and offers genuine arguments against them.

**Accessibility.** Danto writes with remarkable clarity and wit, making complex philosophical arguments accessible to non-specialists.

## Criticisms & Weaknesses

**Noel Carroll (1994)** argued that Danto's definition is both too broad and too narrow. Too broad: many things embody meaning without being art (traffic signs, diagrams, advertising). Too narrow: some artworks may not be "about" anything in the sense Danto requires (pure abstraction, some minimalist works).

**Cynthia Freeland (2001)** argued that Danto's definition privileges the Western tradition of art-for-art's-sake and cannot account for the diverse functions of art in non-Western cultures. Freeland contended that Danto's artworld is implicitly Eurocentric.

**Marcia Muelder Eaton (1981)** in her review of *The Transfiguration of the Commonplace* anticipated the criticism that Danto's definition ignores the cultural context of art's production. Eaton argued that any adequate definition of art must attend to the social practices in which art is embedded, not just to the intentional structure of the artwork.

**George Dickie (1984)** from the perspective of the institutional theory argued that Danto's definition covertly depends on the very institutional structures it claims to supersede. Dickie contended that "embodied meaning" cannot explain why some embodied meanings are art and others are not without invoking the artworld as an institution.

**Richard Shusterman (1997)** from a pragmatist perspective argued that Danto's definition is overly intellectualist. Shusterman claimed that Danto's emphasis on meaning and theory ignores the embodied, somatic dimensions of aesthetic experience that Dewey had identified.

## Comparative Analysis

Danto's position is closest to Hegel's aesthetics, which defines art as the sensuous presentation of the Idea. Danto secularizes Hegel: art is the embodied presentation of meaning, not of the Absolute Spirit.

The book is in explicit dialogue with the institutional theory of George Dickie, which defines art as whatever the artworld says is art. Danto rejects Dickie's functionalism while accepting the importance of the artworld.

Danto's commitment to definition distinguishes him from the Wittgensteinian anti-essentialism of Morris Weitz, who argued that art is an open concept that cannot be defined. Danto's response is that the history of art itself has made definition possible by revealing art's essence.

## Impact & Legacy

*What Art Is* is Danto's final word on the topic he had spent his career exploring. As a definitive statement, it has been widely discussed and critiqued. The embodied meaning definition has been taken up by many subsequent philosophers of art.

The book's influence extends beyond academic philosophy to art criticism and curatorial practice. Danto's concept of the end of art and his definition of art as embodied meaning continue to shape how contemporary art is understood and evaluated.

However, the book has also been criticized as a consolidation of Danto's earlier positions rather than a genuine advance. Some critics argue that *The Transfiguration of the Commonplace* remains the more original and philosophically substantial work.
