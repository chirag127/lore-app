# Chinese Philosophy

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Chinese philosophy represents one of the world's oldest continuous philosophical traditions, with roots in the Spring and Autumn period (c. 771-476 BCE) and the Warring States period (c. 475-221 BCE), when the Hundred Schools of Thought flourished. Unlike Western philosophy, which often prioritizes abstract metaphysical system-building, Chinese philosophy has historically emphasized practical ethics, social harmony, political order, and the cultivation of the self within a cosmic and social context. The major schools — Confucianism, Daoism, Mohism, and Legalism — developed distinct answers to questions about human nature, the basis of social order, and the relationship between humanity and the natural world.

This sub-subcategory is necessary because Chinese philosophy constitutes a largely independent philosophical lineage with its own conceptual vocabulary, textual canon, and internal debates. Terms like *ren* (benevolence/humaneness), *dao* (the Way), *wu wei* (non-action), *qi* (vital force), and *li* (ritual propriety) have no exact Western equivalents. Chinese philosophy also developed distinctive approaches to metaphysics, such as the yin-yang cosmology and the Neo-Confucian synthesis of the 11th-17th centuries, which integrated Buddhist and Daoist insights into a Confucian framework. Modern Chinese philosophy, from the May Fourth Movement onward, grapples with the encounter between Chinese traditions and Western modernity.

## What Belongs Here

- **Confucianism (Rujia)**: The Analects, Mencius, Xunzi; concepts of ren, li, xiao (filial piety), junzi (exemplary person), the rectification of names, the Mandate of Heaven
- **Daoism (Daojia)**: Laozi's Tao Te Ching, Zhuangzi; concepts of dao, de (virtue), wu wei, pu (uncarved block), ziran (spontaneity/naturalness)
- **Legalism (Fajia)**: Han Feizi, Shang Yang; legal and administrative philosophy, power, statecraft, human nature as self-interested
- **Mohism (Mojia)**: Mozi; universal love, impartial caring, consequentialist ethics, opposition to offensive warfare
- **School of Names (Mingjia)**: Hui Shi, Gongsun Long; logic, semantics, paradoxes
- **Yin-Yang cosmology** and the Five Phases (wuxing)
- **Neo-Confucianism (Lixue)**: Zhu Xi, Wang Yangming; principle (li) and material force (qi), the investigation of things, the unity of knowledge and action
- **Buddhist influence on Chinese philosophy**: Chan (Zen), Tiantai, Huayan schools
- **Modern Chinese philosophy**: Maoism, New Confucianism (Mou Zongsan, Tu Weiming), the May Fourth Movement's critique of tradition

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- **Pure religious practice** (burning incense, temple worship, folk religion) — belongs in Religion & Spirituality
- **Zen Buddhism as practiced in Japan** — belongs in Japanese Philosophy
- **Chinese political history** apart from philosophical ideas — belongs in History
- **TCM (Traditional Chinese Medicine) clinical practice** — belongs in Medicine, though its philosophical foundations connect here
- **Feng shui as a practice** — belongs in Cultural Studies, though its cosmological assumptions connect to Chinese philosophy

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Adjacent Sub-subcategories

Chinese philosophy is not merged with **Japanese Philosophy** because despite shared roots in Chinese texts (especially Confucianism and Buddhism), Japanese philosophy developed its own distinct traditions — Zen as a practice-oriented school, the Kyoto School's engagement with Western philosophy, Bushido as a warrior ethic, and Shinto-inspired thought. The political and cultural contexts produced divergent philosophical emphases: Chinese philosophy focused on social harmony and cosmic order, while Japanese philosophy often foregrounded aesthetic experience, impermanence, and the relationship between self and nothingness.

Chinese philosophy is not merged with **Indian Philosophy** because, despite the influence of Indian Buddhism on Chinese thought, the two traditions emerged from fundamentally different cultural and intellectual contexts. Indian philosophy organized itself around the Vedic framework of dharma, karma, and moksha, with sophisticated schools of logic (Nyaya), metaphysics (Vedanta), and epistemology (Buddhist pramana theory). Chinese philosophy, by contrast, was from its inception more focused on this-worldly concerns of ethics, governance, and social harmony.

## Essential Books

1. **The Analects** by Confucius — The foundational text of Confucianism; records the Master's sayings on virtue, learning, ritual, and governance in short, aphoristic passages.
2. **Tao Te Ching** by Laozi — The foundational text of Daoism; 81 short chapters of poetry and paradox on the Way, non-action, and wisdom.
3. **Zhuangzi** by Zhuang Zhou — A masterpiece of Daoist philosophy; brilliantly entertaining stories and dialogues that undermine conventional distinctions and celebrate spontaneity.
4. **Mencius** by Mencius — Develops Confucian thought around the innate goodness of human nature and the importance of benevolent governance.
5. **Xunzi** by Xun Kuang — Argues that human nature is evil and requires transformation through ritual, education, and law; a major corrective to Mencius.
6. **Han Feizi** by Han Fei — Synthesizes Legalist thought; argues for rule by law, state power, and pragmatic governance over moral cultivation.
7. **Mozi** by Mozi — The central text of Mohism; defends universal love, meritocracy, and frugality against Confucian ritualism.
8. **Reflections on Things at Hand** by Zhu Xi — The foundational anthology of Neo-Confucianism; systematizes the relationship between principle (li) and material force (qi).
9. **Instructions for Practical Living** by Wang Yangming — Develops the school of mind; argues that knowledge and action are one and that moral principle is innate.
10. **The Spirit of Chinese Philosophy** by Fung Yu-lan — A modern survey that introduced Chinese philosophy to the Western academy; translates key concepts into comparative framework.

## Key Concepts

- Ren (仁) — benevolence, humaneness, the cardinal Confucian virtue
- Li (禮) — ritual propriety, the patterns of social interaction
- Dao (道) — the Way, the ultimate principle of reality
- Wu wei (無爲) — non-action, action in harmony with the natural order
- Qi (氣) — vital force, psychophysical energy
- Li (理) — principle, pattern, the rational structure of reality (Neo-Confucian)
- Yin and Yang — complementary cosmic forces
- Rectification of names (正名) — the doctrine that language must correspond to reality
- Filial piety (孝) — respect for parents and ancestors
- The Mandate of Heaven (天命) — the justification for political authority

## Connections to Other Subcategories

Chinese philosophy connects to **Ethics & Moral Philosophy** through Confucian virtue ethics and the debates over human nature (Mencius vs. Xunzi). It connects to **Metaphysics & Ontology** through the Daoist conception of the Way and Neo-Confucian theories of principle and material force. **Buddhist Philosophy** in its Chinese transformation (Chan, Tiantai, Huayan) represents a major bridge between Indian and Chinese thought. **Political Philosophy** engages Chinese theories of legitimate authority, from the Mandate of Heaven to Legalist statecraft. **Japanese Philosophy** inherits and transforms Chinese traditions, particularly Zen Buddhism and Neo-Confucianism.
