# Nature of Knowledge and JTB

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

The nature of knowledge — what knowledge is, what distinguishes it from mere true belief, and what conditions must be satisfied for a person to know something — is the foundational question of epistemology. For most of the 20th century, the standard analysis was the JTB (justified true belief) account: a subject S knows that p if and only if (1) p is true, (2) S believes that p, and (3) S is justified in believing that p. This tripartite analysis originated with Plato's Theaetetus and was refined by generations of analytic philosophers.

In 1963, Edmund Gettier published a three-page paper ("Is Justified True Belief Knowledge?") that demolished the JTB analysis with counterexamples showing that one could have justified true belief without knowledge. The Gettier problem — the challenge of identifying what fourth condition transforms JTB into knowledge — has generated an enormous literature exploring alternatives: causal theories, reliabilism, no-defeater conditions, virtue epistemology, and knowledge-first epistemology. This sub-subcategory houses the fundamental debates about what knowledge is, what epistemic value consists in, and whether knowledge should be the central concept of epistemology.

## What Belongs Here

- **The JTB analysis**: The tripartite definition, its origins in Plato, its development in 20th-century analytic philosophy
- **The Gettier problem**: Gettier's original counterexamples, the structure of the problem, post-Gettier responses
- **Internalism vs. externalism about justification**: Whether justification depends solely on factors accessible to the subject's awareness
- **Causal theories of knowledge** (Goldman): Knowledge requires an appropriate causal connection between belief and fact
- **Reliabilism**: Knowledge is belief produced by a reliable cognitive process
- **No-defeater conditions**: Adding a condition that there are no overriding or undermining defeaters
- **Virtue epistemology**: Knowledge as true belief arising from intellectual virtue (Sosa, Zagzebski, Greco)
- **Knowledge-first epistemology** (Williamson): Treating knowledge as primitive and analyzing belief and justification in terms of knowledge
- **The value problem**: Why is knowledge more valuable than mere true belief?
- **Radical skepticism**: The challenge that we cannot have knowledge at all (the problem of the external world, underdetermination)

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Adjacent Sub-subcategories

The nature of knowledge and the JTB analysis is not merged with **Rationalism vs. Empiricism** because the latter concerns the *sources* and *limits* of knowledge (reason vs. experience), while the former concerns the *definition* and *structure* of knowledge itself. One could accept the JTB analysis while being either a rationalist or an empiricist. The Gettier problem is orthogonal to the rationalism-empiricism debate: both sides must confront it.

This sub-subcategory is not merged with **Philosophy of Science** because philosophy of science focuses on the epistemology of scientific inquiry specifically — induction, confirmation, explanation, scientific change — while the nature of knowledge sub-subcategory addresses universal questions about the concept of knowledge that apply to all domains, not just science.

## Essential Books

1. **Theaetetus** by Plato — The earliest systematic treatment of the definition of knowledge; explores and rejects the JTB analysis.
2. **"Is Justified True Belief Knowledge?"** by Edmund Gettier (1963) — The three-page paper that revolutionized epistemology.
3. **Epistemology and Cognition** by Alvin Goldman — Develops reliabilist theory of knowledge and justification.
4. **Knowledge and Its Limits** by Timothy Williamson — The definitive statement of knowledge-first epistemology.
5. **Epistemic Justification** by Laurence BonJour — A defense of internalism and the coherence theory of justification.
6. **Virtues of the Mind** by Linda Zagzebski — Develops virtue epistemology; argues that knowledge is true belief arising from intellectual virtue.
7. **A Virtue Epistemology** by Ernest Sosa — Presents a sophisticated virtue-theoretic account of knowledge and epistemic normativity.
8. **The Structure of Empirical Knowledge** by Laurence BonJour — Defends coherentism against foundationalism.
9. **Knowledge in Perspective** by Ernest Sosa — Collected essays developing the distinction between animal and reflective knowledge.
10. **Problems of Knowledge** by Michael Williams — A sophisticated introduction that situates the JTB debate within broader epistemological questions.

## Connections to Other Subcategories

The nature of knowledge connects to **Rationalism vs. Empiricism** (different views on the sources of justification affect JTB). It connects to **Philosophy of Science** through scientific knowledge as a special case of propositional knowledge. **Epistemology of Disagreement** applies the concept of justification to cases of peer conflict. **Philosophy of Mind** engages the relationship between mental states and epistemic justification. **Formal Logic** provides tools for analyzing the structure of epistemic arguments.
