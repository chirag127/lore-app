# Philosophy of Science and Scientific Explanation

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

The philosophy of science examines the foundations, methods, and implications of science. It asks: what distinguishes science from non-science (the demarcation problem)? What constitutes a scientific explanation? How do theories relate to evidence? Are scientific theories true representations of an unobservable reality, or merely useful instruments for prediction? These questions are distinct from the practice of science itself — a physicist uses the scientific method; a philosopher of science examines what the scientific method is and why it works.

The sub-subcategory also addresses the nature of scientific explanation. The deductive-nomological (DN) model (Hempel) holds that explanation is subsumption under general laws. Alternatives include statistical relevance models, causal-mechanical models, and the pragmatic theory of explanation (van Fraassen). The historical turn in philosophy of science — particularly Kuhn's *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* — transformed the field by emphasizing the social and historical dimensions of scientific change, paradigm shifts, and incommensurability. Contemporary philosophy of science engages scientific practice across disciplines, from fundamental physics to biology, neuroscience, and the social sciences.

## What Belongs Here

- **The demarcation problem**: What distinguishes science from pseudoscience? Popper's falsificationism, Kuhn's puzzle-solving, Laudan's criterion
- **The problem of induction**: Hume's challenge, Goodman's new riddle of induction, the grue paradox
- **Confirmation and evidence**: Bayesian confirmation theory, hypothetico-deductivism, the ravens paradox
- **Scientific explanation**: Deductive-nomological model (Hempel), inductive-statistical, causal-mechanical (Salmon), unificationist (Kitcher), pragmatic (van Fraassen)
- **Scientific realism vs. anti-realism**: The no-miracles argument (Putnam), the pessimistic meta-induction (Laudan), constructive empiricism (van Fraassen), entity realism (Hacking)
- **Theory change and scientific revolutions**: Kuhn's paradigms and normal science, incommensurability, Lakatos's research programmes, Feyerabend's methodological anarchism
- **Reductionism and emergence**: Intertheoretic reduction, supervenience, emergence in physics, biology, and consciousness studies
- **Philosophy of specific sciences**: Philosophy of physics (space, time, quantum mechanics), philosophy of biology (evolution, natural selection, fitness), philosophy of social science
- **Values in science**: The role of non-epistemic values, feminist philosophy of science, science and policy
- **Models and idealization**: The nature of scientific models, idealization, abstraction, computer simulation

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- **Doing science itself** (conducting experiments, collecting data) — belongs in the relevant scientific categories
- **History of science** as a purely descriptive enterprise — belongs in History of Science
- **Sociology of scientific knowledge** (SSK) as a purely empirical project — belongs in Sociology of Science, though the philosophical implications connect here
- **Formal logic and Bayesian statistics as technical disciplines** — belongs in Mathematics or Formal Logic
- **Pseudoscience advocacy** (homeopathy, creationism, astrology) — does not belong anywhere in the taxonomy as positive knowledge

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Adjacent Sub-subcategories

Philosophy of science is not merged with **Nature of Knowledge and JTB** because it addresses specifically scientific knowledge, with its characteristic features — theory-laden observation, the role of mathematics, experimentation, peer review — that raise questions not present in epistemology generally. The demarcation problem, the structure of theories, and scientific explanation are distinct from general questions about justified true belief.

Philosophy of science is not merged with **Rationalism vs. Empiricism** because, while the rationalism-empiricism debate informs philosophy of science, the latter has its own specific problematics: induction, confirmation, theory choice, scientific change, and realism. The development of Bayesian epistemology and formal learning theory has largely superseded the classic rationalism-empiricism framework within philosophy of science.

## Essential Books

1. **The Logic of Scientific Discovery** by Karl Popper — The classic defense of falsificationism as the demarcation criterion for science.
2. **The Structure of Scientific Revolutions** by Thomas Kuhn — The most influential work in 20th-century philosophy of science; introduced paradigms, normal science, and incommensurability.
3. **Aspects of Scientific Explanation** by Carl Hempel — The definitive statement of the deductive-nomological model of explanation.
4. **The Scientific Image** by Bas van Fraassen — The classic defense of constructive empiricism against scientific realism.
5. **The Aim and Structure of Physical Theory** by Pierre Duhem — A foundational work on holism, underdetermination, and scientific theory structure.
6. **Representing and Intervening** by Ian Hacking — Defends entity realism; argues that experiment has a life of its own independent of theory.
7. **Against Method** by Paul Feyerabend — Argues for epistemological anarchism; "anything goes" in science.
8. **Fact, Fiction, and Forecast** by Nelson Goodman — Introduces the new riddle of induction (grue) and examines counterfactual reasoning.
9. **The Methodology of Scientific Research Programmes** by Imre Lakatos — Develops a sophisticated falsificationism based on competing research programmes.
10. **Science, Truth, and Democracy** by Philip Kitcher — Examines the relationship between scientific authority, values, and democratic decision-making.

## Connections to Other Subcategories

Philosophy of science connects to **Nature of Knowledge and JTB** through issues of confirmation and the structure of scientific justification. It connects to **Rationalism vs. Empiricism** through the debate about the role of reason vs. experience in scientific theory construction. **Formal Logic** provides Bayesian and probabilistic tools for confirmation theory. **Metaphysics** engages the ontological commitments of scientific theories (realism debates, reduction and emergence). **Philosophy of Consciousness** intersects with philosophy of neuroscience and the mind-body problem in science. **Political Philosophy** engages the role of values in science and science policy.
