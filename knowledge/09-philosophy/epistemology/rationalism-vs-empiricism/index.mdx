# Rationalism vs Empiricism

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

The rationalism vs. empiricism debate is the central drama of early modern epistemology, framed by the scientific revolution and the collapse of Aristotelian scholasticism. Rationalists (Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz) held that genuine knowledge is achieved through reason independently of sensory experience — that the mind possesses innate ideas or capacities that allow it to grasp necessary truths. Empiricists (Locke, Berkeley, Hume) countered that all knowledge derives from sensory experience — that the mind at birth is a *tabula rasa* (blank slate) and that even abstract ideas are constructed from sensory impressions.

This debate is not merely historical. It frames enduring questions: Are there synthetic a priori truths? Can reason alone disclose the structure of reality? Is all knowledge ultimately empirical? Kant's "Copernican revolution" attempted a synthesis: the mind actively structures experience through a priori categories, making synthetic a priori knowledge possible. Contemporary philosophy continues to grapple with these questions in the context of cognitive science, linguistics (Chomsky's innate universal grammar), and mathematics (the nature of mathematical knowledge). The debate also informs the epistemology of modality (how we know what is possible and necessary) and the foundations of logic.

## What Belongs Here

- **Continental rationalism**: Descartes (innate ideas, the Cogito, clear and distinct perception), Spinoza (geometric method, substance monism), Leibniz (monads, pre-established harmony, innate principles)
- **British empiricism**: Locke (tabula rasa, primary and secondary qualities, simple and complex ideas), Berkeley (idealism, esse est percipi), Hume (impressions and ideas, causation as constant conjunction, the problem of induction)
- **Kant's transcendental idealism**: The synthetic a priori, the categories of understanding, the Copernican revolution, phenomena vs. noumena
- **The analytic-synthetic distinction**: Kant's original formulation, Quine's "Two Dogmas of Empiricism" and its critique
- **Innate ideas and nativism**: The rationalist doctrine of innate ideas, Chomsky's linguistic nativism, contemporary cognitive science on innate knowledge
- **A priori knowledge**: The nature and possibility of knowledge independent of experience; Kripke's a priori contingent truths
- **The epistemology of modality**: How we know what is possible and necessary; rationalism vs. empiricism about modal knowledge
- **Conceptual empiricism vs. conceptual nativism**: Are all concepts derived from experience, or are some innate?
- **The given and the myth of the given**: Sellars's critique of the "Myth of the Given" in empiricism

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- **The JTB analysis and Gettier problem** — belongs in Nature of Knowledge and JTB

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Adjacent Sub-subcategories

Rationalism vs. empiricism is not merged with **Nature of Knowledge and JTB** because it addresses different questions. The JTB analysis asks "what is knowledge?" while the rationalism-empiricism debate asks "where does knowledge come from?" and "what are its limits?" One can accept the same JTB analysis while being either a rationalist or an empiricist.

This sub-subcategory is not merged with **Philosophy of Science** because, while it informs the epistemology of science, its focus is broader — encompassing mathematics, metaphysics, logic, and morality — and its methodology is more concerned with the general sources of knowledge than with specifically scientific explanation, confirmation, and theory change.

## Essential Books

1. **Meditations on First Philosophy** by René Descartes — The founding text of modern rationalism; the methodological skepticism, the Cogito, and the proof of God.
2. **An Essay Concerning Human Understanding** by John Locke — The founding text of modern empiricism; the blank slate, simple and complex ideas, the limits of human understanding.
3. **A Treatise of Human Nature** by David Hume — The most radical expression of empiricism; demolishes causation, personal identity, and induction.
4. **An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding** by David Hume — A more accessible version of the Treatise's epistemology.
5. **Critique of Pure Reason** by Immanuel Kant — The most important work of modern epistemology; synthesizes rationalism and empiricism through transcendental idealism.
6. **Ethics** by Baruch Spinoza — Demonstrates a complete metaphysical system through the geometric method; the pinnacle of rationalist systematic philosophy.
7. **New Essays on Human Understanding** by G.W. Leibniz — A point-by-point rationalist response to Locke's Essay; defends innate ideas and necessary truths.
8. **Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous** by George Berkeley — A defense of idealism; argues that matter does not exist independently of perception.
9. **"Two Dogmas of Empiricism"** by W.V.O. Quine — The most influential 20th-century critique of the analytic-synthetic distinction and logical empiricism.
10. **Naming and Necessity** by Saul Kripke — Revolutionized the epistemology of modality; argues for a priori contingent truths and necessary a posteriori truths.

## Connections to Other Subcategories

Rationalism vs. empiricism connects to **Nature of Knowledge and JTB** through questions about the sources of justification. It connects to **Philosophy of Science** through the debate about the role of reason vs. experiment in scientific method. **Philosophy of Language** engages the relationship between analytic truth and meaning. **Philosophy of Mind** connects through nativism (Chomsky) and the modularity of mind. **Metaphysics** is deeply engaged because rationalists and empiricists draw different conclusions about the nature of reality from their epistemological premises.
