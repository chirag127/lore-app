# Problems Addressed and Unresolved Questions

## Key Problem: The Nature of Scientific Change

The central problem Kuhn addresses is the nature of scientific change. How does scientific knowledge actually develop? The traditional answer — that science progresses through the steady accumulation of facts and the progressive refinement of theories — is, Kuhn argues, historically false. But if the cumulative picture is wrong, what replaces it? This is the problem that the book confronts: to give an empirically adequate, historically informed account of how scientific knowledge develops that does justice to both the successes of science and the discontinuities of its history.

## Proposed Solution: The Cyclical Model

Kuhn's solution is the cyclical model of normal science → crisis → revolution → new normal science. Scientific change is not a smooth accumulation but a punctuated equilibrium: long periods of stable, tradition-bound research (normal science) interrupted by relatively brief episodes of revolutionary transformation. The key innovation is to see the periods of stability as essential to the process — without the detailed puzzle-solving of normal science, anomalies would not be recognized, and without anomalies, there would be no revolutions.

The model solves several problems simultaneously. It explains why scientists resist new theories (they are invested in the paradigm that makes their work possible). It explains why science appears to progress (it does — normal science is cumulatively effective within a paradigm). It explains why scientific change is discontinuous (because paradigms are incommensurable). It explains why science is special (its community structure enforces a degree of consensus unknown in other fields).

## Evidence and Argumentation

Kuhn's evidence is historical. He presents extended case studies of the Copernican revolution (astronomy), the chemical revolution (Lavoisier's oxygen theory), the Newtonian revolution (mechanics), and the Einsteinian revolution (relativity), plus shorter discussions of the discovery of X-rays, oxygen, and Uranus. The evidence is qualitative and interpretive: Kuhn is not counting frequencies or testing hypotheses statistically; he is interpreting historical episodes through the lens of his model.

The argumentative strategy is abductive: the pattern of normal science → crisis → revolution → new normal science is the best explanation of the historical record. Where logical empiricism had to explain away discontinuities (as mere revisions within a continuous tradition) and Popper had to explain away long periods of what looked like normal science (as failures to be properly Popperian), Kuhn's model accepts the historical phenomena at face value and incorporates them into the theory.

The argument for incommensurability is based on specific cases: the meaning of "mass" in Newton and Einstein, "element" in Lavoisier and Dalton, "planet" in Ptolemy and Copernicus, "motion" in Aristotle and Galileo. In each case, Kuhn argues that the term does not simply refer to the same thing in a different way — it refers to a different thing, because the conceptual framework within which it operates has been transformed.

## Unresolved Questions

**The problem of paradigm choice**: If paradigms are incommensurable, how does the scientific community choose between them? Kuhn's answer — persuasion and conversion — has been seen as insufficient by critics who want a rational account of theory choice. Kuhn insisted that paradigm choice is rational without being rule-governed, but the precise nature of this rationality remains contested.

**The scope of the model**: Does Kuhn's model apply to all sciences? Kuhn focused on physics and chemistry. The extension to biology, geology, medicine, and the social sciences is controversial. Evolutionary biology, for example, has not experienced a Kuhnian revolution since Darwin — does that mean it is in a long period of normal science, or that the model does not apply?

**Teleology without truth**: Kuhn's rejection of scientific progress toward truth left him with the problem of explaining what scientific progress means. His evolutionary analogy (science evolves like a species, becoming more adapted to its environment) is suggestive but underdeveloped. If science does not aim at truth, what does it aim at?

**The social dimensions**: Kuhn opened the door to the sociology of scientific knowledge but did not walk through it himself. How much of scientific change is driven by genuinely cognitive factors (evidence, argument, logic) and how much by social factors (authority, resources, politics)? Later sociologists of science pushed this question far beyond Kuhn's comfort zone.

**Revolutionary science and normal science**: The boundary between them is unclear. Lakatos, Feyerabend, and Toulmin each argued that most science is a mix of normal and revolutionary elements. Can the distinction be maintained, or does it dissolve on closer inspection?

**Incommensurability and communication**: If paradigms are incommensurable, how do scientists in different paradigms communicate? How does conversion even happen? Kuhn's account of translation and partial understanding is plausible but vague. The problem of cross-paradigm communication remains a live research question in philosophy of language and cognitive science.

**The role of values**: Kuhn acknowledged that values (simplicity, consistency, fruitfulness, scope) play a role in theory choice. But values are ambiguous and can be interpreted differently by different scientists. The question of how values function in scientific reasoning — whether they are rationally binding or merely psychologically persuasive — remains unresolved.

## Criticisms

**From Karl Popper**: Popper's critique is the most influential. He argued that Kuhn's normal scientist is engaged in something closer to theology than science — the uncritical defense of a dogma rather than the critical testing of hypotheses. Popper's ideal scientist is a revolutionary, constantly trying to falsify theories; Kuhn's normal scientist is a puzzle-solver who never questions fundamentals. For Popper, Kuhn had described the pathology of science, not its healthy state. In *Criticism and the Growth of Knowledge* (1970), Popper wrote: "In my view the 'normal' scientist, as Kuhn describes him, is a person one ought to be sorry for."

**From Imre Lakatos**: Lakatos offered the most sophisticated attempt to reconcile Popper's rationalism with Kuhn's historicism. His methodology of scientific research programmes aimed to preserve the rationality of scientific change while acknowledging the historical continuities that Kuhn identified. Lakatos charged Kuhn with making paradigm change a matter of irrational "mob psychology." In the same volume, Lakatos argued that his own framework could explain the same historical episodes that Kuhn cited while preserving a rational account of scientific progress.

**From Paul Feyerabend**: Feyerabend's critique was that Kuhn did not go far enough. If incommensurability is real, Feyerabend argued, then the whole idea of scientific method is a myth. *Against Method* (1975) pushed Kuhn's insights to a radical conclusion: there is no single scientific method, no universal standard of rationality, and the best rule for scientists is "anything goes." Feyerabend also charged that Kuhn's distinction between normal and revolutionary science was artificial — normal science is not as consensual as Kuhn claimed, and revolutions are not as sudden.

**From Larry Laudan**: Laudan's *Progress and Its Problems* (1977) accepted Kuhn's critique of logical empiricism but rejected the incommensurability thesis and the revolutionary model. Laudan's "research traditions" model treated scientific change as more continuous and rational than Kuhn allowed. He argued that scientists can and do compare rival traditions on multiple dimensions, and that incommensurability is a pseudo-problem generated by an overly rigid conception of meaning.

**From the Strong Programme (David Bloor, Barry Barnes)**: The Edinburgh School's "strong programme" in the sociology of scientific knowledge took Kuhn's insights in a direction he did not endorse. Bloor and Barnes argued that scientific knowledge is a social construction to be explained by the same sociological principles as any other belief system. They rejected Kuhn's residual commitment to the special epistemic status of science. Kuhn was uncomfortable with this extension, insisting that his project was philosophical, not sociological.

**From Scientific Realists (Hilary Putnam, Richard Boyd, Philip Kitcher)**: Realists argued that Kuhn's incommensurability thesis is exaggerated. Putnam developed a causal theory of reference that allowed "mass" to refer to the same entity across paradigm change. Kitcher ("the division of linguistic labor") argued that the apparent conceptual discontinuities can be resolved by distinguishing between the reference and the sense of theoretical terms.

**From Dudley Shapere**: Shapere agreed with Kuhn's critique of logical empiricism but argued that the concept of paradigm is too vague to do the work required. In a series of papers throughout the 1960s and 1970s, Shapere developed a "contextualist" alternative that treats scientific reasoning as historically situated but still rational and objective.

**From the Frankfurt School (Jürgen Habermas)**: Habermas argued that Kuhn's account of scientific change was insufficiently attentive to the broader social and political context. While Kuhn acknowledged external factors, his model remained internalist. Habermas's theory of communicative action sought to integrate the analysis of scientific knowledge with a broader theory of society.

## Alternative Solutions

The problem that Kuhn addressed — the nature of scientific change — has been approached from several other directions.

**Popper's falsificationism** offers a simpler, more rationalistic account: science progresses by conjecture and refutation. Theories are proposed, tested empirically, and rejected when falsified. The model preserves the rationality of science but is historically inaccurate — scientists do not abandon theories at the first sign of falsification, as Lakatos and Kuhn both pointed out.

**Lakatos's methodology of scientific research programmes** attempts to preserve Popper's rationalism while accommodating Kuhn's historical insights. Research programmes have identifiable structures (hard core, protective belt, positive heuristic) and can be evaluated as progressive or degenerating. This preserves a rational account of theory choice while acknowledging historical continuity.

**Laudan's reticulated model** treats scientific rationality as a process of mutual adjustment among theories, methods, and aims. Scientific change is neither as revolutionary as Kuhn claimed nor as rational as Popper hoped. It is a process of piecemeal adjustment in which all elements can change, but not all at once and not without reason.

**The semantic approach to scientific theories** (Patrick Suppes, Bas van Fraassen, Ronald Giere) sidesteps the historical questions by focusing on the structure of scientific theories rather than their historical development. Theories are analyzed as families of models, and theory change is understood as model evolution.

**Social epistemology and the sociology of scientific knowledge** (Bloor, Barnes, Latour, Collins, Shapin) pursue the social dimensions that Kuhn identified but did not develop. These approaches treat scientific knowledge as a social product to be explained by social causes, sometimes rejecting Kuhn's residual commitment to the rationality of science.

**The evolutionary epistemology tradition** (Donald Campbell, Karl Popper, Stephen Toulmin) treats the growth of knowledge as analogous to biological evolution — variation, selection, and retention — without the revolutionary punctuations that Kuhn emphasized.

**Bayesian epistemology** offers a formal account of theory change as rational belief revision. Bayesians argue that the problems Kuhn identified (underdetermination, theory-ladenness, meaning change) can be handled within a probabilistic framework, though the question of whether Bayesianism captures the historical dynamics Kuhn described remains contested.

## Practical Applications

Kuhn's work has found applications far beyond philosophy. In **science education**, Kuhn's analysis of how paradigms are transmitted through exemplars has informed debates about inquiry-based learning and the role of textbooks. In **organizational theory**, the concept of paradigm shift has been used to understand innovation and resistance to change in firms and institutions. In **policy analysis**, Kuhn's account of how communities become attached to frameworks has informed thinking about how policy paradigms change.

Kuhn's most profound practical implication may be for the **self-understanding of scientists**. By showing that science is a human enterprise — shaped by tradition, community, and the limits of perception — Kuhn invited scientists to reflect on their own practice in ways that the official ideology of science (cumulative, objective, value-free) discouraged. This has been liberating for some scientists and threatening to others.

The book has also been used in **science communication and public engagement**. Understanding the social dynamics of scientific change — why scientists resist new ideas, how consensus forms, what makes evidence persuasive — is essential for communicating science in a skeptical public sphere. Kuhn's work provides a vocabulary for these conversations, even as it complicates the simple story of science as straightforward discovery of truth.
