Few academic books have escaped their disciplinary boundaries to become cultural phenomena. *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* is one of them. When Thomas Kuhn, a physicist-turned-historian, published this slender monograph in 1962 as part of Otto Neurath's International Encyclopedia of Unified Science, he could not have predicted that it would sell over a million copies, be translated into 24 languages, and become one of the most cited academic works of the twentieth century.

Kuhn's central argument is deceptively simple: science does not progress through the steady accumulation of facts, but through periodic revolutions that shatter old frameworks and establish new ones. He called these frameworks "paradigms," and the transitions between them "scientific revolutions" or — the phrase that entered everyday language — "paradigm shifts." Normal science, Kuhn argued, is a conservative, puzzle-solving activity conducted within a paradigm. Only when anomalies overwhelm the paradigm does a crisis occur, leading to the revolutionary adoption of a new way of seeing.

This book belongs in epistemology because it fundamentally reorients the question of how scientific knowledge grows. Rather than a story of ever-closer approximation to truth, Kuhn offers a picture of knowledge as a social, historical, and psychological phenomenon — shaped by communities, constrained by tradition, and transformed by revolutions that change not just what we know, but what counts as knowledge at all.
