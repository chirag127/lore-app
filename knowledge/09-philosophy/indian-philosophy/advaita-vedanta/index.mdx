# Advaita Vedanta

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Advaita Vedanta, systematized by Shankara (c. 8th century CE), is arguably the most influential school of Indian philosophy and one of the world's great metaphysical systems. Its core thesis is radical non-dualism: Brahman, the ultimate reality, is one, unchanging, pure consciousness, and the individual self (Atman) is ultimately identical with Brahman. The appearance of a plural world of distinct objects and separate selves is the result of maya (cosmic illusion) — not sheer non-existence, but a superimposition (adhyasa) that obscures the true nature of reality.

Shankara developed a sophisticated epistemology to support his metaphysics. He distinguished between two levels of truth: vyavaharika (empirical/pragmatic reality, which is conventionally real) and paramarthika (ultimate/transcendental reality, which is absolutely real). This distinction allowed him to accept the reality of everyday experience for practical purposes while denying its ultimate status. His commentaries on the Brahma-Sutras, the Upanishads, and the Bhagavad Gita established Advaita as the dominant interpretation of Vedanta for centuries. The school continues to influence modern Indian thought through figures like Swami Vivekananda and Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan.

## What Belongs Here

- **Shankara's Advaita**: The brahman-atman identity, maya and avidya (ignorance), adhyasa (superimposition), the two levels of truth
- **The concept of maya**: How the world appears without being ultimately real; the rope-snake analogy, the distinction between maya as illusion vs. maya as creative power
- **The pramana system**: How Advaita adapts the Nyaya pramanas; the role of shruti (scripture) as a pramana for transcendent reality
- **The three states of consciousness**: Waking (jagrat), dreaming (svapna), and deep sleep (sushupti); the fourth (turiya) as pure consciousness
- **Post-Shankara Advaita**: Padmapada, Sureshvara, Vachaspati Mishra; the debate between the "reflection" (bimba-pratibimba) and "limitation" (avaccheda) theories of the individual self
- **Vivarana vs. Bhamati schools**: The two major interpretive traditions in post-Shankara Advaita
- **Adhyasa-bhashya**: Shankara's introduction to the Brahma-Sutras; the definitive statement of the Advaita position on superimposition
- **Advaita critiques of Buddhist philosophy**: The distinctive features of Advaita vs. Buddhist emptiness (shunyata)
- **Contemporary Advaita**: Modern interpretations and defenses of non-dualism

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- **Dvaita Vedanta** (Madhva's dualism) — belongs in its own sub-subcategory
- **Vishishtadvaita Vedanta** (Ramanuja's qualified non-dualism) — belongs in its own sub-subcategory
- **Buddhist Madhyamaka emptiness** (superficially similar but fundamentally different) — belongs in Buddhist Philosophy
- **Contemporary "neo-Advaita" or "pop Advaita"** (simplified non-dualism marketed as self-help) — belongs in Self-Help or Spirituality

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Adjacent Sub-subcategories

Advaita Vedanta is not merged with **Dvaita & Vishishtadvaita** because the differences are fundamental. Advaita holds that Brahman is qualityless (nirguna) and the world is ultimately illusory; Dvaita holds that God, souls, and matter are eternally distinct; Vishishtadvaita holds that Brahman has attributes (saguna) and the world is real as Brahman's body. These are genuinely incompatible metaphysical positions, and each requires separate exposition.

Advaita Vedanta is not merged with **Buddhist Philosophy** because, despite superficial similarities between maya and emptiness (shunyata), Shankara explicitly and extensively critiques Buddhist positions. For Advaita, Brahman is positive (sacchidananda — being, consciousness, bliss) while for Madhyamaka Buddhism, ultimate reality is emptiness, which is not a positive reality. Shankara's philosophy requires accepting the authority of the Upanishads, which Buddhism rejects.

## Essential Books

1. **The Brahma-Sutra-Bhashya** by Shankara — The foundational commentary establishing Advaita Vedanta; Shankara's systematic exposition of Vedantic metaphysics.
2. **Upadeshasahasri (A Thousand Teachings)** by Shankara — Shankara's own independent philosophical work; the most accessible introduction to his system.
3. **Vivekacudamani (The Crest Jewel of Discrimination)** attributed to Shankara — A popular manual of Advaita philosophy and practice.
4. **The Mandukya Upanishad with Gaudapada's Karika and Shankara's Commentary** — Gaudapada's Karika (pre-Shankara) is the earliest systematic Advaita text; Shankara's commentary develops the tradition.
5. **The Bhamati** by Vachaspati Mishra — The most influential sub-commentary on Shankara's Brahma-Sutra-Bhashya; founds the Bhamati school.
6. **The Panchapadika** by Padmapada — The foundation of the Vivarana school of Advaita interpretation.
7. **Advaita Vedanta: A Philosophical Reconstruction** by Eliot Deutsch — A clear modern exposition of Advaita philosophy.
8. **The Encyclopedia of Indian Philosophies: Volume 3, Advaita Vedanta up to Shankara and His Pupils** edited by Karl Potter — The definitive scholarly reference.
9. **Shankara and Indian Philosophy** by Natalia Isayeva — A scholarly study of Shankara's philosophy in its historical context.
10. **A History of Advaita Vedanta** by A.K. Chatterjee — Traces the development of Advaita from the Upanishads through the 20th century.

## Key Concepts

- Advaita — non-dualism, "not two"
- Brahman — ultimate reality, one without a second
- Atman — the inner self, identical with Brahman
- Maya — cosmic illusion, the principle of appearance
- Avidya — ignorance of the true nature of reality
- Adhyasa — superimposition (mistaking the non-self for the self)
- Vyavaharika — empirical/pragmatic level of reality
- Paramarthika — ultimate/transcendental level of reality
- Pratyaksha — perception; anubhava (direct experience) as authoritative
- Turiya — the fourth state of consciousness, beyond waking, dreaming, and deep sleep
- Nirguna Brahman — Brahman without qualities
- Saguna Brahman — Brahman with qualities (Ishvara)
- Jivanmukti — liberation while living
- Sacchidananda — being-consciousness-bliss (the nature of Brahman)

## Connections to Other Subcategories

Advaita Vedanta connects to **Dvaita & Vishishtadvaita** as the three major Vedanta schools that argued with each other. It connects to **Buddhist Philosophy** through the debate between Advaita's Brahman and Buddhist emptiness, which shaped both traditions. **Six Orthodox Schools** provides the larger framework in which Advaita operates. **Heterodox Schools** (Carvaka) were targets of Shankara's critiques. **Indian Logic: Navya-Nyaya** provided the logical tools that later Advaita thinkers used. **Modern Indian Philosophy** engages Advaita through Vivekananda, Radhakrishnan, and others.
