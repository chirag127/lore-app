# Buddhist Philosophy

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Buddhist philosophy is one of the world's great philosophical traditions, with a continuous history of over 2,500 years. Originating in India with the Buddha's teachings, it developed into a remarkably sophisticated intellectual tradition with its own epistemology, metaphysics, logic, ethics, and philosophy of mind. Unlike many religious traditions, Buddhism placed rigorous philosophical analysis at the center of its path to liberation: the Buddha himself encouraged critical inquiry, and subsequent thinkers developed elaborate systems of argumentation and analysis.

The major traditions of Buddhist philosophy — Abhidharma (analysis of ultimate realities), Madhyamaka (the middle way of emptiness), Yogacara (the consciousness-only school), and Buddhist logic and epistemology (Dignaga and Dharmakirti) — constitute some of the most technically sophisticated philosophical systems in any tradition. Buddhist philosophy has become increasingly influential in contemporary Western philosophy, particularly in metaphysics (the no-self doctrine), philosophy of mind (consciousness without a self), and ethics (compassion as a normative foundation).

## What Belongs Here

- **The Buddha's philosophical teachings**: The Four Noble Truths, the Eightfold Path, dependent origination (pratityasamutpada), anatman (no-self), impermanence (anitya)
- **Abhidharma philosophy**: The analysis of dharmas (ultimate constituents of reality), the two truths, the theory of momentariness
- **Madhyamaka**: Nagarjuna's emptiness (shunyata), the middle way between eternalism and nihilism, the two truths, the critique of svabhava (inherent existence), the Tetralemma
- **Yogacara (Vijnanavada)**: Consciousness-only (vijapti-matrata), the three natures (trisvabhava), the storehouse consciousness (alaya-vijnana)
- **Buddhist logic and epistemology (Pramana)**: Dignaga and Dharmakirti; perception and inference, apoha (exclusion) theory of meaning, the trairupya (three conditions of a logical reason)
- **Tibetan Buddhist philosophy**: The Prasangika-Svatantrika distinction, Tsongkhapa's interpretation of Madhyamaka, the great completeness (dzogchen) tradition
- **Buddhist ethics**: Compassion (karuna) and loving-kindness (maitri), karma theory, the bodhisattva path, Buddhist meta-ethics
- **Buddhist philosophy of mind**: The nature of consciousness, the no-self doctrine, mindfulness (sati), the relationship between mind and world

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- **Zen/Chan as a living tradition** (koan practice, meditation) — belongs in Eastern Philosophy or Religion
- **Pure Land Buddhism** as a devotional practice — belongs in Religion & Spirituality
- **Buddhist meditation** as a technique (mindfulness, Vipassana) — belongs in Psychology or Religion
- **Buddhist ethics as applied** (engaged Buddhism, social justice) — belongs in Buddhist Studies
- **Jain philosophy** (uses similar terms but different system) — belongs in its own sub-subcategory

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Adjacent Sub-subcategories

Buddhist philosophy is not merged with **Six Orthodox Schools** because Buddhism rejects the authority of the Vedas and the existence of a permanent self (atman), placing it in fundamental opposition to orthodox Brahmanical schools. Its philosophical vocabulary (emptiness, no-self, dependent origination) developed in conscious opposition to Upanishadic metaphysics. However, it is also not merged with **Heterodox Schools** because Buddhist philosophy developed a comprehensive, systematic philosophical tradition of its own — with vast textual corpora, institutional structures, and internal debates — that goes far beyond the simpler heterodox positions of Carvaka and Ajivika.

Buddhist philosophy is not merged with **Eastern Philosophy** because, while Buddhism spread throughout East Asia and was transformed there, its philosophical foundations were laid in India. The Indian Buddhist tradition — Abhidharma, Madhyamaka, Yogacara, Buddhist logic — is a distinct Indian philosophical tradition that engaged with Nyaya, Vedanta, and other Indian schools.

## Essential Books

1. **The Fundamental Wisdom of the Middle Way** by Nagarjuna (translated by Jay Garfield) — The foundational text of Madhyamaka; one of the most important works in Buddhist philosophy.
2. **The Dhammapada** — A collection of the Buddha's sayings; the most accessible introduction to Buddhist philosophical and ethical principles.
3. **Abhidharmakosha** by Vasubandhu — The definitive systematic work of Abhidharma philosophy; analyzes all phenomena into ultimate constituents.
4. **The Thirty Verses on Consciousness-Only** by Vasubandhu — A concise statement of Yogacara philosophy; consciousness-only and the three natures.
5. **Seven Works of Dharmakirti** — The foundation of Buddhist logic and epistemology; the theory of perception, inference, and the apoha theory of meaning.
6. **The Bodhisattvacharyavatara (Guide to the Bodhisattva's Way of Life)** by Shantideva — A masterpiece of Buddhist ethics and philosophy; integrates Madhyamaka with the bodhisattva path.
7. **The Great Treatise on the Stages of the Path to Enlightenment (Lamrim Chenmo)** by Tsongkhapa — The definitive synthesis of Tibetan Buddhist philosophy; integrates Madhyamaka with yogic practice.
8. **Buddhist Philosophy: Essential Readings** edited by William Edelglass and Jay Garfield — A comprehensive anthology covering the full range of Buddhist philosophical traditions.
9. **The Selfless Mind** by Peter Harvey — A careful study of the Buddhist doctrine of no-self and its implications.
10. **Empty Words: Buddhist Philosophy and Cross-Cultural Interpretation** by Jay Garfield — Engages Madhyamaka with contemporary Western philosophy, including Wittgenstein and Derrida.

## Key Concepts

- Anatman — no-self, the denial of permanent self
- Pratityasamutpada — dependent origination
- Shunyata — emptiness (of inherent existence)
- Dharmas — ultimate constituents of reality
- Samsara — the cycle of rebirth
- Nirvana — the cessation of suffering
- Karma — the law of moral causation
- The two truths — conventional and ultimate
- Alaya-vijnana — storehouse consciousness (Yogacara)
- Trairupya — the three conditions of a valid logical reason (Buddhist logic)
- Apoha — exclusion as the mechanism of meaning
- Bodhicitta — the mind of awakening, the aspiration to liberate all beings
- Upaya — skillful means

## Connections to Other Subcategories

Buddhist philosophy connects to **Six Orthodox Schools** and **Advaita Vedanta** through centuries of debate between Buddhist and Brahmanical thinkers. It connects to **Jain Philosophy** as a fellow heterodox Indian tradition. **Chinese Philosophy** was profoundly shaped by the transmission of Buddhism, leading to Chan/Zen. **Japanese Philosophy** developed Zen, the Kyoto School, and other Buddhist-inspired traditions. **Philosophy of Consciousness** finds Buddhist resources for understanding consciousness without a self. **Metaphysics & Ontology** engages Buddhist emptiness theory as a distinctive approach to the nature of reality.
