# Dvaita and Vishishtadvaita

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Not all Vedanta is non-dualist. Ramanuja (c. 11th-12th century) and Madhva (c. 13th century) developed sophisticated alternatives to Shankara's Advaita that preserved the reality of the world and the distinction between God, individual souls, and matter. These schools represent the theistic heart of classical Indian philosophy, grounding devotional (bhakti) practice in rigorous metaphysical and epistemological systems.

Vishishtadvaita (qualified non-dualism), founded by Ramanuja, holds that Brahman (identified with Vishnu-Narayana) possesses attributes and that the world and individual souls are real parts of Brahman's nature — like the body is part of the self, or like attributes are part of a substance. Reality is one, but it includes real distinctions. Dvaita (dualism), founded by Madhva, holds that God, souls, and matter are eternally and irreducibly distinct. There are five fundamental and eternal differences. Madhva's system is one of the few thoroughgoing dualisms in world philosophy and includes distinctive positions on the nature of knowledge (saxaprama — direct, immediate knowledge) and the eternal hierarchy of souls.

## What Belongs Here

- **Ramanuja's Vishishtadvaita**: Saguna Brahman vs. nirguna Brahman, the body-soul analogy for God-world relation, pranayama (self-luminous knowledge), the critique of maya
- **The Vishishtadvaita theory of reality**: Substance-attribute distinction, the reality of the physical world, the individual self as a real part of Brahman
- **Ramanuja's commentary on the Brahma-Sutras (Sri Bhashya)**: The definitive Vishishtadvaita interpretation
- **Madhva's Dvaita**: The five eternal differences (God-matter, God-soul, soul-soul, soul-matter, matter-matter)
- **Dvaita epistemology**: Saxaprama (direct knowledge), the role of perception, the proof of God's existence
- **Dvaita metaphysics**: The eternal hierarchy of souls (taratamya), some souls eternally destined for liberation, others for damnation
- **The critique of Advaita maya**: Both Ramanuja and Madhva argue that Advaita's doctrine of maya is incoherent and undermines religious practice
- **The role of bhakti**: How devotional practice is justified by the metaphysics of difference
- **The historical development**: The Srivaishnava tradition, the Gaudiya Vaishnava tradition (Chaitanya), the Haridasa movement

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- **Advaita Vedanta** — belongs in its own sub-subcategory
- **Bhakti as religious practice** (temple worship, ritual) — belongs in Religion & Spirituality
- **Pure theism without philosophical system** (simple belief in God) — belongs in Religion
- **Vaishnavism as a sectarian tradition** — belongs in Religion & Spirituality, though its philosophical foundations connect here

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Adjacent Sub-subcategories

Dvaita and Vishishtadvaita are not merged with **Advaita Vedanta** because the differences are fundamental and irreconcilable. Ramanuja and Madhva devoted enormous energy to refuting Advaita. The three schools disagree on the nature of Brahman (with or without qualities), the reality of the world (real vs. illusory), the nature of the individual self (identical with Brahman vs. eternally distinct), and the path to liberation (knowledge vs. devotion). Merging them would obscure the most significant debate in classical Indian philosophy.

Dvaita and Vishishtadvaita are combined because both are theistic Vedanta schools that emphasize the reality of distinction and the importance of bhakti, in contrast to Advaita's radical non-dualism and emphasis on knowledge (jnana). However, their differences are also significant — qualified non-dualism vs. full dualism — and this is reflected in their separate treatment within this sub-subcategory.

## Essential Books

1. **Sri Bhashya** by Ramanuja — The definitive commentary on the Brahma-Sutras from the Vishishtadvaita perspective; the most important text in the tradition.
2. **Vedartha Sangraha** by Ramanuja — A concise summary of Vishishtadvaita philosophy; more accessible than the Sri Bhashya.
3. **Gita Bhashya** by Ramanuja — Ramanuja's commentary on the Bhagavad Gita; applies Vishishtadvaita to the Gita's synthesis of knowledge, action, and devotion.
4. **Anu Bhashya** by Madhva — A concise commentary on the Brahma-Sutras presenting the Dvaita interpretation.
5. **Madhva's Commentary on the Bhagavad Gita** — Applies Dvaita metaphysics to the Gita; emphasizes the difference between the individual soul and God.
6. **Tattva-viveka** by Madhva — A systematic treatise on the categories of reality from the Dvaita perspective.
7. **The Nyaya Sudha** by Jayatirtha — The most important commentary on Madhva's works; the definitive systematization of Dvaita philosophy.
8. **Ramanuja on the Gita** by J.A.B. van Buitenen — A scholarly translation and study of Ramanuja's Gita commentary.
9. **The Philosophy of Ramanuja** by S.M.S. Chari — A comprehensive modern study of Vishishtadvaita.
10. **The Philosophy of Madhva** by B.N.K. Sharma — The definitive modern exposition of Dvaita philosophy in English.

## Key Concepts

### Vishishtadvaita
- Vishishtadvaita — qualified non-dualism
- Saguna Brahman — Brahman with attributes
- Aprithak-siddhi — inseparable relation (like substance-attribute)
- Sarira-sariri-bhava — body-soul relationship between world and God
- Pranayama — self-luminous knowledge
- Bhakti — loving devotion as the path to liberation
- Karma-jnana-bhakti — the synthesis of action, knowledge, and devotion

### Dvaita
- The five eternal differences (pancha bheda)
- Saxaprama — direct, immediate knowledge
- Taratamya — the eternal hierarchy of souls
- Svabhava — intrinsic nature
- Paratantra — dependence on God
- Nitya-samsarins — souls eternally bound to samsara
- Nitya-muktas — souls eternally liberated

## Connections to Other Subcategories

Both schools connect to **Advaita Vedanta** through their shared Vedanta framework and their critiques of each other. They connect to **Buddhist Philosophy** and **Six Orthodox Schools** as participants in the broader Indian philosophical conversation. **Modern Indian Philosophy** engages these traditions through figures like Radhakrishnan who sought to synthesize them. They connect to **Political Philosophy** indirectly through their influence on Indian social hierarchy and devotional movements.
