# Indian Logic: Navya-Nyaya

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Indian logic represents one of the world's great logical traditions, developing independently of Greek and Western logic for over two millennia. The Nyaya school, founded by Gautama (c. 2nd century CE), established the framework: a theory of inference (anumana) with a five-membered syllogism, a classification of pramanas (means of knowledge), and an elaborate theory of debate and fallacies. The Navya-Nyaya (New Logic) revolution, beginning with Gangesha Upadhyaya's *Tattva-Chintamani* (c. 14th century), transformed Indian logic into a technical discipline of extraordinary precision, with a sophisticated formal language for analyzing relations, properties, and negation.

Navya-Nyaya developed technical concepts — abstractive cognition (samanya-lakshana), the property-limit (avacchedaka), the cognition-limit (prakara), the theory of negation (abhava) — that are comparable in sophistication to Fregean predicate logic, though developed within a completely different framework. The tradition also engaged in the epistemology of testimony (shabda pramana), the analysis of language and meaning, and the theory of debate. Navya-Nyaya continued as a living tradition into the 19th century and has recently attracted interest from comparative logicians and analytic philosophers.

## What Belongs Here

- **Classical Nyaya logic**: The five-membered syllogism (pratijna, hetu, udaharana, upanaya, nigamana), the theory of inference (anumana), the three conditions of a valid reason (trairupya)
- **The pramana system**: Perception (pratyaksha), inference (anumana), analogy (upamana), testimony (shabda) — as developed by Nyaya
- **The theory of debate (katha)**: The classification of debates, legitimate and illegitimate rejoinders, the theory of fallacies (hetvabhasa)
- **Navya-Nyaya technical language**: The logic of relations, abstractive cognition, the theory of negation, property-limit and cognition-limit
- **Gangesha Upadhyaya's Tattva-Chintamani**: The foundational text of Navya-Nyaya; analyzes inference, perception, testimony, and the nature of knowledge
- **Raghunatha Siromani**: The most technical Navya-Nyaya thinker; developed the logic of relations and the theory of properties
- **The theory of meaning**: The Nyaya theory of reference, the relationship between word and object, the theory of universal terms
- **Jain and Buddhist logic**: Alternative logical traditions within Indian philosophy
- **Comparison with Western logic**: The relationship between Indian logic and Aristotelian, Stoic, and modern symbolic logic

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- **Western formal logic** (propositional and predicate calculus) — belongs in Formal Logic under Logic & Critical Thinking
- **Aristotelian syllogistic** — belongs in Western Philosophy by Era (Ancient) or Formal Logic
- **General epistemology** (JTB analysis, Gettier problems) — belongs in Nature of Knowledge and JTB
- **The philosophical content of the Nyaya school** (atomism, theology, metaphysics) — belongs in Six Orthodox Schools
- **Buddhist logic** (Dignaga, Dharmakirti) — belongs in Buddhist Philosophy, though comparative treatment is appropriate here

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Adjacent Sub-subcategories

Indian logic is not merged with **Formal Logic: Propositional and Predicate** because it represents an independent logical tradition with its own technical vocabulary, problematics, and historical development. Western symbolic logic (Frege, Russell, Tarski) and Indian logic (Gangesha, Raghunatha) approached logical problems from different directions: Western logic focused on propositional structure and quantifiers, while Indian logic focused on inference patterns, relation analysis, and the epistemology of reasoning. Merging them would obscure the distinctive contributions of each.

Indian logic is not merged with **Six Orthodox Schools** because, while Nyaya is one of the six orthodox schools, the logical tradition — especially Navya-Nyaya — developed a technical apparatus that became independent of any particular metaphysical school. Jain and Buddhist thinkers also contributed to and debated within the logical tradition.

## Essential Books

1. **Tattva-Chintamani** by Gangesha Upadhyaya — The foundational text of Navya-Nyaya; the most important work in Indian logic after the Nyaya-Sutras.
2. **The Nyaya Sutras of Gautama** — The foundational text of the Nyaya school; establishes the framework of inference, pramanas, and debate.
3. **The Nyaya Kusumanjali** by Udayana — A defense of the existence of God using Nyaya logical methods; demonstrates the application of logical analysis to theological questions.
4. **The Tarka-Sangraha** by Annambhatta — A concise manual of Nyaya-Vaisheshika logic and metaphysics; the standard introductory text used in Indian pedagogy.
5. **The Dinakari** by Mahadeva Punatamkara — A major commentary on the Tattva-Chintamani; develops key aspects of Navya-Nyaya theory.
6. **Indian Logic: A Reader** edited by Jonardon Ganeri — An accessible anthology of primary texts and secondary essays covering the full tradition.
7. **Philosophy in Classical India** by Jonardon Ganeri — Situates Indian logic within the broader context of classical Indian philosophy.
8. **The Structure of the World in Udayana's Time** by Dr. N.S. Ramanuja Tatacharya — A major study of Navya-Nyaya ontology and logic.
9. **Navya-Nyaya System of Logic** by D.C. Guha — A systematic exposition of Navya-Nyaya technical concepts.
10. **Logic in India** edited by Bimal Krishna Matilal — A collection of essays covering the full range of Indian logical traditions.

## Key Concepts

- Anumana — inference
- Trairupya — the three conditions of a valid reason
- Hetu — the logical reason (middle term)
- Vyapti — invariable concomitance (the relation of pervasion)
- Paksata — the property of being the subject of inference
- Avacchedaka — property-limit (a Navya-Nyaya technical term)
- Prakara — cognition-limit
- Abhava — negation (a real category in Nyaya)
- Samanya-lakshana — abstractive cognition, the faculty that apprehends universals
- Samsaya — doubt, the starting point of inquiry
- Hetvabhasa — fallacy, the semblance of a reason
- Jalpa — disputation (debate aimed at victory)
- Vitanda — destructive criticism (debate aimed only at refutation)
- Tarka — hypothetical reasoning, dialectical reasoning

## Connections to Other Subcategories

Indian logic connects to **Formal Logic: Propositional and Predicate** through comparative logic studies. It connects to **Six Orthodox Schools** through the Nyaya school's place in the orthodox framework. **Buddhist Philosophy** and **Jain Philosophy** developed competing logical traditions. **Nature of Knowledge and JTB** shares the epistemological concerns with perception, inference, and testimony. **Philosophy of Language** connects through the Nyaya theory of reference and meaning. **Advaita Vedanta** and **Dvaita & Vishishtadvaita** used Navya-Nyaya tools in their philosophical arguments.
