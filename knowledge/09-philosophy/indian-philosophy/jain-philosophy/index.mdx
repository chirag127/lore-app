# Jain Philosophy

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Jain philosophy represents one of the most distinctive and sophisticated traditions within Indian philosophy. Its core commitments — non-violence (ahimsa) as the supreme ethical principle, the doctrine of manifold aspects (anekantavada), and the theory of conditioned predication (syadvada) — make it unique among the world's philosophical systems. The Jains developed a rigorous pluralistic metaphysics: reality is complex and multi-faceted, no single viewpoint captures the whole truth, and all judgments are true "in a certain respect" (syat).

The Jain tradition also produced sophisticated theories of karma (understood as a material substance that binds to the soul), an elaborate cosmology, and a systematic account of the means of knowledge (pramana). Jain epistemology emphasizes the limitations of human knowledge and the need for intellectual humility — a position with contemporary resonance. Jain ethics, centered on ahimsa, has profoundly influenced Indian culture (Gandhi's non-violence was directly inspired by Jain teachings) and continues to inform contemporary environmental and animal ethics.

## What Belongs Here

- **Anekantavada**: The doctrine of non-absolutism or many-sidedness; reality is complex and cannot be captured by any single perspective
- **Syadvada (the theory of conditioned predication)**: The seven-fold predication (saptabhanginaya); every proposition is true only from a certain standpoint
- **Jain epistemology**: The theory of pramanas (perception, inference, testimony); the distinction between direct and indirect knowledge; the limitations of empirical knowledge
- **Jain metaphysics**: The distinction between jiva (soul) and ajiva (non-soul); the categories of reality (dravya, guna, paryaya)
- **Karma theory**: Karma as a material substance that binds to the soul; the eight types of karma; the mechanism of bondage and liberation
- **Jain ethics**: Ahimsa as the supreme duty, the five great vows (mahavratas), the distinction between householder and ascetic ethics
- **Jain cosmology**: The structure of the universe, the three worlds, the cycle of time
- **Jain logic**: The theory of naya (standpoints or viewpoints), the seven-fold scheme of predication
- **Major Jain philosophers**: Mahavira (the historical founder), Kundakunda, Umasvati, Akalanka, Hemachandra, Haribhadra

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- **Jain religious practice** (temple worship, rituals, festivals) — belongs in Religion & Spirituality
- **Jain social history** (caste, community, institutions) — belongs in History or Sociology
- **The historical Buddha or Buddhist philosophy** — belongs in Buddhist Philosophy
- **The Carvaka school** — belongs in Heterodox Schools
- **Contemporary Jainism as a living religion** — belongs in Religion & Spirituality, though its philosophical foundations are here

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Adjacent Sub-subcategories

Jain philosophy is not merged with **Buddhist Philosophy** despite being similarly heterodox (rejecting Vedic authority). The differences are fundamental: Jains affirm the existence of a permanent self (jiva) with the same ontological status as matter, while Buddhists deny any permanent self. Jain anekantavada is a form of pluralism (reality has many aspects), while Buddhist Madhyamaka is a form of emptiness (reality has no inherent nature). Jain karma is a material substance, while Buddhist karma is a mental-intentional process.

Jain philosophy is not merged with **Heterodox Schools** because, despite its nastika classification, Jain philosophy developed a comprehensive, systematic tradition with a vast textual corpus comparable to the orthodox schools. Reducing it to "heterodox" obscures its positive contributions to logic, epistemology, and pluralistic metaphysics.

## Essential Books

1. **Tattvartha Sutra** by Umasvati — The foundational text of Jain philosophy; systematically presents the seven categories of truth (tattvas) that constitute reality.
2. **The Samayasara** by Kundakunda — A profound exploration of the nature of the self and the distinction between the conventional and ultimate perspectives.
3. **The Pravachanasara** by Kundakunda — A systematic work on Jain epistemology and the means to liberation.
4. **The Syadvada Manjari** by Mallishena — A classic text on the theory of conditioned predication; defends Jain non-absolutism.
5. **The Yoga of Delight: The Yogas of the Jainas** by Hemachandra — A Jain philosophical manual that parallels the Buddhist and Hindu yoga traditions.
6. **Anekantavada and Syadvada** by Satkari Mookerjee — A scholarly analysis of Jain logic and epistemology.
7. **Jaina Theory of Knowledge** by Y.J. Padmarajiah — A comprehensive modern study of Jain epistemology.
8. **Outlines of Jain Philosophy** by Sohan Raj Tater — An accessible introduction to the major principles of Jain philosophy.
9. **The Jaina Path of Purification** by Padmanabh Jaini — A comprehensive study of Jain philosophy, ethics, and soteriology.
10. **The Jaina Philosophy of Reality** by Dalsukh Malvania — A study of Jain metaphysics and its relationship to other Indian schools.

## Key Concepts

- Ahimsa — non-violence, the supreme ethical principle
- Anekantavada — the doctrine of many-sidedness
- Syadvada — the theory of conditioned predication
- Saptabhanginaya — the seven-fold scheme of predication
- Jiva — soul/conscious substance
- Ajiva — non-soul matter
- Karma (as matter) — subtle matter that binds to the soul
- Dravya — substance (the six eternal substances)
- Guna — quality (inseparable from substance)
- Paryaya — mode/modification (changing states of substance)
- Kevala jnana — omniscience, the highest state of knowledge
- Mahavratas — the five great vows (non-violence, truth, non-stealing, celibacy, non-attachment)
- Naya — standpoint, a partial perspective on reality
- Moksha — liberation of the soul from karma

## Connections to Other Subcategories

Jain philosophy connects to **Buddhist Philosophy** as a fellow heterodox Indian tradition with which it shares concepts (karma, rebirth, liberation) but interprets them differently. It connects to **Six Orthodox Schools** through shared engagement with Nyaya logic and epistemology. **Heterodox Schools** provides the larger classification context. **Indian Logic: Navya-Nyaya** and Jain logic influenced each other. **Modern Indian Philosophy** engages Jain themes through Gandhi's non-violence and contemporary pluralism. **Ethics & Moral Philosophy** finds in Jain ahimsa one of the most thoroughgoing ethical systems in world philosophy. **Political Philosophy** engages Jain contributions to pluralism and tolerance.
