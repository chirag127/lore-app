# Modern Indian Philosophy

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Modern Indian philosophy emerged from the encounter between traditional Indian thought and Western modernity — colonialism, science, democracy, and Christianity. From the 19th century onward, Indian thinkers confronted questions that their classical predecessors had not faced: How should Indian philosophy respond to Western science and rationalism? Can traditional Indian concepts like dharma and karma be reconciled with democracy and human rights? What is the relationship between Indian national identity and its diverse philosophical and religious traditions?

This period produced a remarkable flowering of philosophical creativity. Swami Vivekananda synthesized Advaita Vedanta with a activist, socially-engaged spirituality that inspired both Indian nationalism and global religious pluralism. Rabindranath Tagore developed a distinctive humanistic philosophy rooted in Upanishadic ideals but oriented toward internationalism and creative expression. Sri Aurobindo created an evolutionary metaphysics integrating Vedanta with Western evolutionary theory. Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan presented Indian philosophy as a world philosophy with universal significance. B.R. Ambedkar, drawing on Buddhism, developed a radical critique of caste and a vision of social justice. Muhammad Iqbal articulated a dynamic Islamic philosophy that combined Sufi mysticism with Nietzschean self-assertion.

## What Belongs Here

- **Neo-Vedanta and the Hindu Renaissance**: Ram Mohan Roy, Dayananda Saraswati, the Brahmo Samaj and Arya Samaj
- **Swami Vivekananda**: Practical Vedanta, the harmony of religions, the divinity of the self, karma yoga
- **Rabindranath Tagore**: The philosophy of creative expression, universal humanism, the critique of nationalism
- **Sri Aurobindo and The Mother**: Integral Yoga, evolutionary metaphysics, the supramental consciousness, The Life Divine
- **Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan**: Indian philosophy as world philosophy, the idealist interpretation of Advaita, comparative philosophy
- **B.R. Ambedkar**: Buddhist conversion, the philosophy of Navayana (New Buddhism), the critique of caste and Brahmanical ideology
- **Muhammad Iqbal**: The reconstruction of religious thought in Islam, the self (khudi), dynamic creativity
- **Mahatma Gandhi**: Satyagraha, ahimsa as a political philosophy, swaraj, the critique of industrial civilization, Hind Swaraj
- **Jawaharlal Nehru**: Scientific humanism, the synthesis of democracy, socialism, and Indian tradition
- **K.C. Bhattacharyya**: The concept of the Absolute, the philosophy of freedom, swaraj in ideas
- **Contemporary Indian philosophy**: The continuing engagement with Western philosophy, Indian philosophy in the diaspora

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- **Classical Indian philosophy** (the six orthodox schools) — belongs in its own sub-subcategories
- **Indian religious practice** (temple worship, puja, festivals) — belongs in Religion & Spirituality
- **Indian history** (political history of the independence movement) — belongs in History
- **Indian literature** (novels, poetry without philosophical content) — belongs in Literature
- **Postcolonial theory** (Said, Spivak, Bhabha as academic discourse) — belongs in Critical Theory or Cultural Studies

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Adjacent Sub-subcategories

Modern Indian philosophy is not merged with **Advaita Vedanta** or **Six Orthodox Schools** because it represents a different intellectual project: the critical engagement between Indian traditions and Western modernity. Modern Indian thinkers were not simply extending classical debates; they were responding to new challenges — colonialism, science, democracy — that required new frameworks. Vivekananda's "practical Vedanta" is not the same project as Shankara's commentary on the Brahma-Sutras, even though it draws on Advaita concepts.

This sub-subcategory is not merged with **Western Modern Philosophy** because, despite engagement with Western ideas, these thinkers remained rooted in Indian philosophical categories. Their questions were shaped by the colonial encounter, the struggle for independence, and the project of articulating an Indian modernity.

## Essential Books

1. **The Complete Works of Swami Vivekananda** (9 volumes) — The foundational texts of modern Hindu philosophy; Vedanta applied to social reform, science, and interfaith dialogue.
2. **The Life Divine** by Sri Aurobindo — A monumental work of evolutionary metaphysics; integrates Vedanta, evolution, and the vision of a transformed humanity.
3. **Sadhana: The Realisation of Life** by Rabindranath Tagore — Tagore's philosophical essays; the harmony of the individual with the universe through creative expression.
4. **Indian Philosophy, Volumes 1 and 2** by Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan — The most influential modern survey of Indian philosophy; presents Indian thought as a world philosophy.
5. **The Buddha and His Dhamma** by B.R. Ambedkar — The foundational text of Navayana Buddhism; a radical reinterpretation of Buddhism as social justice philosophy.
6. **The Reconstruction of Religious Thought in Islam** by Muhammad Iqbal — A series of lectures reinterpreting Islamic philosophy in light of modern science and philosophy.
7. **Hind Swaraj** by Mahatma Gandhi — Gandhi's most important philosophical work; the critique of modern civilization and the vision of swaraj.
8. **The Concept of the Absolute in Indian Philosophy** by K.C. Bhattacharyya — A sophisticated engagement between classical Indian philosophy and Western Idealism.
9. **The Philosophy of Sri Aurobindo** edited by Haridas Chaudhuri — A comprehensive examination of Aurobindo's philosophical system.
10. **An Introduction to Indian Philosophy** by K. Satchidananda Murty — A modern introduction that represents the Radhakrishnan tradition of neo-Vedanta.

## Key Concepts

- Practical Vedanta (Vivekananda)
- Harmony of religions
- Integral Yoga / supramental consciousness (Aurobindo)
- The universal man / visva-manava (Tagore)
- Swaraj — self-rule (Gandhi)
- Satyagraha — truth-force
- Khudi — the self (Iqbal)
- Navayana — New Buddhism (Ambedkar)
- Annihilation of caste
- Swaraj in ideas (K.C. Bhattacharyya)
- Scientific humanism (Nehru)
- Comparative philosophy
- Indian Renaissance

## Connections to Other Subcategories

Modern Indian philosophy connects to **Advaita Vedanta** (Vivekananda, Radhakrishnan), **Buddhist Philosophy** (Ambedkar), **Dvaita & Vishishtadvaita** (the bhakti tradition that informs modern religious thought), and **Six Orthodox Schools** generally. It connects to **Political Philosophy** through Gandhi and Ambedkar. **Eastern Philosophy** and **Chinese Philosophy** engage comparative questions. **Ethics & Moral Philosophy** engages Gandhian non-violence and Ambedkarite social justice. **Western Philosophy by Era** connects through the engagement with modern Western thinkers (Hegel, Nietzsche, Bergson, James).
