# Six Orthodox Schools

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Indian philosophy has traditionally been classified into orthodox (astika) and heterodox (nastika) schools based on their acceptance or rejection of Vedic authority. The six orthodox schools — Nyaya (logic and epistemology), Vaisheshika (atomistic metaphysics), Samkhya (enumeration of reality), Yoga (meditative practice and metaphysics), Purva Mimamsa (ritual exegesis), and Uttara Mimamsa or Vedanta (the end of the Vedas) — each developed systematic philosophical positions over more than two millennia. While they accepted the authority of the Vedas in principle, they argued vigorously with each other and with heterodox schools, producing a remarkably sophisticated philosophical literature.

This sub-subcategory serves as the umbrella for understanding the six-school framework, while individual schools (particularly the three major Vedanta traditions) receive their own sub-subcategories due to their depth and influence. The orthodox-orthodox interactions — Nyaya's epistemology used by all schools, Samkhya metaphysics influencing Yoga and Vedanta, Mimamsa's influence on Hindu jurisprudence — are a rich topic of study. Understanding the six schools is essential for grasping the structure of classical Indian philosophy as a whole.

## What Belongs Here

- **The astika-nastika classification**: What counts as "accepting Vedic authority" and the boundaries of orthodoxy
- **Nyaya**: The school of logic and epistemology; four pramanas (means of knowledge), the Nyaya syllogism, the theory of inference, Nyaya realism
- **Vaisheshika**: Atomistic metaphysics; the seven categories of reality (padarthas), atomism, causation as asatkaryavada
- **Samkhya**: Dualism of purusha (consciousness) and prakriti (matter); the 25 tattvas, the three gunas, the evolution of the manifest world
- **Yoga**: Patanjali's Yoga Sutras; the eight limbs of yoga, the modification of the mind, kaivalya (liberation), the relationship between Samkhya and Yoga
- **Purva Mimamsa**: The school of Vedic exegesis; the authority of the Vedas, the nature of dharma, the theory of language (sphota), apurva
- **Vedanta (Uttara Mimamsa)**: The school of the Upanishads; Brahma-sutras, the three major sub-schools (Advaita, Dvaita, Vishishtadvaita)
- **The relationship and debates between the schools**: How Nyaya epistemology was adopted by others, Samkhya critiques of Vedanta, Mimamsa vs. Vedanta on the nature of Brahman
- **Historical development**: From the sutra period through the medieval scholastic tradition

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- **Advaita Vedanta as a detailed system** — belongs in its own sub-subcategory
- **Dvaita and Vishishtadvaita Vedanta** — belong in their own sub-subcategory
- **Buddhist philosophy** — belongs in its own sub-subcategory under Indian Philosophy
- **Carvaka materialism** — belongs in Heterodox Schools
- **Navya-Nyaya (new logic)** — belongs in Indian Logic: Navya-Nyaya

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Adjacent Sub-subcategories

The six orthodox schools are not merged into a single "Classical Indian Philosophy" category because each school has sufficient depth and internal sophistication to warrant separate treatment. The divergence between Advaita, Dvaita, and Vishishtadvaita Vedanta alone represents one of the most sophisticated metaphysical debates in world philosophy, comparable to the dispute between monism and pluralism in the West. The six-school framework provides an organizing structure, but the detail required to do justice to each tradition demands specific sub-subcategories.

This sub-subcategory is not merged with **Indian Logic: Navya-Nyaya** because Navya-Nyaya represents a specific later development (c. 13th-17th centuries) of the Nyaya tradition that introduced technical innovations in logic and epistemology that merit separate treatment.

## Essential Books

1. **The Six Systems of Indian Philosophy** by Max Muller — A classic late-19th-century survey that established the six-school framework for Western scholarship.
2. **Indian Philosophy, Volume I and II** by Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan — The most comprehensive modern survey; covers all six schools in depth.
3. **A History of Indian Philosophy, Volumes 1-5** by Surendranath Dasgupta — The definitive scholarly treatment of the entire tradition.
4. **The Nyaya Sutras** by Gautama (translated by Ganganatha Jha) — The foundational text of the Nyaya school of logic and epistemology.
5. **The Vaisheshika Sutras** by Kanada — The foundational text of the atomistic Vaisheshika school.
6. **The Samkhya Karika** by Ishvara Krishna — The foundational text of the Samkhya school; presents the 25 tattvas in concise verses.
7. **The Yoga Sutras of Patanjali** — The foundational text of the Yoga school; the eight limbs, the modifications of the mind, the path to kaivalya.
8. **The Purva Mimamsa Sutras** by Jaimini — The foundational text of the Mimamsa school; the nature of dharma and Vedic exegesis.
9. **An Introduction to Indian Philosophy** by Satischandra Chatterjee — An accessible introduction to all six schools with clear exposition of their positions.
10. **Classical Indian Philosophy** by Peter Adamson and Jonardon Ganeri — A recent, accessible overview that situates the six schools in their historical and intellectual context.

## Key Concepts

- Astika and nastika — orthodox and heterodox
- Pramana — means of valid knowledge
- Padartha — category of reality
- Purusha and Prakriti — consciousness and matter (Samkhya)
- The three gunas — sattva, rajas, tamas
- Asatkaryavada vs. satkaryavada — theories of causation
- The Nyaya syllogism (five-membered inference)
- Atomic theory (paramanu)
- The eight limbs of yoga
- Dharma and adhikara
- Apurva — the unseen potency (Mimamsa)
- Brahman and Atman (Vedanta)

## Connections to Other Subcategories

The six orthodox schools connect to **Advaita Vedanta**, **Dvaita & Vishishtadvaita**, **Buddhist Philosophy**, **Jain Philosophy**, and **Heterodox Schools** as co-existing traditions that debated with each other. **Indian Logic: Navya-Nyaya** develops the Nyaya school's technical apparatus in a later period. **Eastern Philosophy** (particularly Chinese Buddhism) was influenced by Indian Buddhist philosophy that emerged from the same intellectual milieu. The schools connect to **Metaphysics & Ontology** and **Epistemology** as parallel traditions with their own sophisticated treatments of these questions.
