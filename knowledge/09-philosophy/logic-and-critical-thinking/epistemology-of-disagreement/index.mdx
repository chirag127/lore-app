# Epistemology of Disagreement

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

The epistemology of disagreement addresses a question that is both theoretically pressing and practically urgent: what should you do when someone you regard as your epistemic equal — as intelligent, well-informed, and honest as yourself — disagrees with you? Should you reduce confidence in your belief? Should you hold firm? The answer has implications for the rationality of religious belief, political conviction, scientific consensus, and everyday judgment.

The contemporary debate began with the work of Thomas Kelly and David Christensen in the early 2000s and has since become one of the most active areas of epistemology. Conciliationists argue that discovering peer disagreement should lead both parties to reduce confidence, because people of equal intelligence and evidence are equally likely to be mistaken. Steadfasters argue that you can rationally maintain your belief even in the face of disagreement if you have good reasons for thinking you are right and the other person is wrong. The debate connects to broader issues about the nature of evidence, the role of higher-order evidence, the epistemology of group belief, and the rationality of polarization in contemporary society.

## What Belongs Here

- **The problem of peer disagreement**: Defining epistemic peers, the significance of disagreement between peers
- **Conciliationism**: The equal weight view (Elga), the total evidence view (Christensen), the requirement to reduce confidence
- **Steadfastness**: The reasons view (Kelly), the asymmetry strategy, the right-reasons response
- **The independence thesis**: The requirement that you should not rely on the reasoning that led to your own view when evaluating the epistemic significance of disagreement
- **Higher-order evidence**: Evidence about the reliability of your own reasoning; the problem of epistemic self-doubt
- **The significance of extreme disagreement**: When peers disagree about fundamental epistemic principles or values
- **Disagreement and belief polarization**: Why belief polarization persists despite shared evidence; the role of motivated reasoning
- **Group epistemology and disagreement**: How groups should respond to internal disagreement; the epistemology of committees, juries, and scientific communities
- **Disagreement and deep epistemic diversity**: Cross-cultural disagreement, incommensurable worldviews, the limits of conciliationism
- **Applied epistemology of disagreement**: Implications for political polarization, scientific consensus, religious pluralism, and public policy

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- **Informal fallacies** (ad hominem, straw man) — belongs in Informal Logic & Argumentation Theory
- **Social epistemology in general** (testimony, epistemic networks) — belongs in Social Epistemology
- **Psychology of belief polarization** (confirmation bias, motivated cognition) — belongs in Cognitive Psychology or Social Psychology
- **Peer disagreement in formal epistemology** (Bayesian models of disagreement) — belongs in Formal Epistemology
- **The sociology of scientific disagreement** — belongs in Sociology of Science
- **General norms of argumentation** — belongs in Informal Logic & Argumentation Theory

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Adjacent Sub-subcategories

The epistemology of disagreement is not merged with **Informal Logic & Argumentation Theory** because it focuses on a narrower, more specific question: the rational response to peer disagreement. It is an epistemological question about belief revision and the normative significance of disagreement, rather than a question about the structure and evaluation of arguments in general. The epistemology of disagreement draws on formal epistemology, Bayesian reasoning, and the theory of higher-order evidence in ways that informal logic typically does not.

This sub-subcategory is not merged with **Nature of Knowledge and JTB** because, while it concerns epistemic justification, it addresses a specific problem that arises only in a social context: the discovery that others share your evidence but reach different conclusions. The general JTB analysis does not address this problem, and the disagreement literature has generated distinctive positions and arguments that go beyond general epistemology.

## Essential Books

1. **Epistemology of Disagreement: New Essays** edited by David Christensen and Jennifer Lackey — The essential collection that launched the contemporary debate; features both conciliationist and steadfast positions.
2. **The Epistemology of Disagreement** by Jonathan Matheson — A book-length defense of conciliationism; develops the equal weight view systematically.
3. **Disagreement in the Philosophy of Religion** edited by Daniel Howard-Snyder — Applies the epistemology of disagreement to religious belief and religious pluralism.
4. **Disagreement** by Bryan Frances — An accessible introduction to the main positions and arguments.
5. **Social Epistemology: Essential Readings** edited by Alvin Goldman and Dennis Whitcomb — Contains key essays on peer disagreement within the broader social epistemology context.
6. **The Significance of Philosophical Disagreement** — A symposium issue of the journal Episteme (2005) featuring early formulations by Kelly, Christensen, Elga, and Feldman.
7. **The Rationality of Religious Disagreement** by Richard Feldman — Applies the epistemology of disagreement to the rationality of religious belief.
8. **Knowledge in an Uncertain World** by Jeremy Fantl and Matthew McGrath — Places peer disagreement within a broader pragmatic account of knowledge.
9. **Democratic Authority** by David Estlund — Connects the epistemology of disagreement to political philosophy and democratic theory.
10. **The Bright and the Good: The Connection between Intellectual and Moral Virtues** edited by Adam Carter — Connects the epistemology of disagreement to virtue epistemology and intellectual character.

## Connections to Other Subcategories

The epistemology of disagreement connects to **Nature of Knowledge and JTB** through the role of higher-order evidence in epistemic justification. It connects to **Informal Logic & Argumentation Theory** through norms of argumentative engagement. **Political Philosophy** engages disagreement in democratic deliberation, the role of pluralism, and the justification of political authority in the face of persistent disagreement. **Philosophy of Religion** engages the problem of religious diversity and peer disagreement about religious beliefs. **Ethics & Moral Philosophy** connects through moral disagreement and meta-ethical relativism. **Philosophy of Science** engages the role of disagreement in scientific progress and the epistemology of consensus.
