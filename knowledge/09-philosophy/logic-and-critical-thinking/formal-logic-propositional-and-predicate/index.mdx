# Formal Logic: Propositional and Predicate

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Formal logic is the systematic study of the forms of valid inference. It abstracts away from the content of arguments to analyze their structure, using artificial symbolic languages to represent logical relations with mathematical precision. Propositional (or sentential) logic studies the behavior of logical connectives — "and," "or," "not," "if...then" — and the validity of inferences that depend only on how propositions are combined. Predicate (or quantifier) logic extends this to analyze the internal structure of propositions — subjects and predicates, objects and relations, and the all-important quantifiers "all" and "some."

The development of modern formal logic from Frege through Russell and Whitehead, Godel, Tarski, and others represents one of the greatest intellectual achievements of the 20th century, transforming not only philosophy but also mathematics, computer science, and linguistics. Formal logic provides the tools for analyzing the validity of arguments, the foundations of mathematics, the semantics of natural language, and the structure of computation. Key metalogical results — Godel's incompleteness theorems, Tarski's undefinability theorem, Church's undecidability theorem — reveal both the power and the limits of formal systems.

## What Belongs Here

- **Propositional (sentential) logic**: Connectives (negation, conjunction, disjunction, conditional, biconditional), truth tables, tautologies and contradictions, logical equivalence, natural deduction (rules of inference and replacement)
- **Predicate (first-order) logic**: Quantifiers (universal and existential), predicates and relations, variable binding, free and bound variables, identity, natural deduction for predicate logic
- **Metalogic**: Soundness, completeness, consistency, decidability; Godel's completeness theorem for first-order logic; Godel's incompleteness theorems for arithmetic
- **Model theory**: Interpretations, models, satisfaction, logical consequence, compactness, the Lowenheim-Skolem theorems
- **Proof theory**: Formal systems, axiomatic systems, natural deduction, sequent calculus, the Curry-Howard correspondence
- **Higher-order logics**: Second-order logic, type theory, the lambda calculus
- **Non-classical logics**: Modal logic (necessity and possibility), intuitionistic logic, many-valued logic, relevance logic, paraconsistent logic
- **Philosophical implications**: The nature of logical truth, logical consequence, the relationship between logic and ontology, the normativity of logic
- **History of formal logic**: Aristotle's syllogistic, the Stoic tradition, the medieval theory of supposition, Frege's revolution, Russell's theory of descriptions

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- **Aristotelian syllogistic as a historical topic** — belongs in Ancient Western Philosophy, though its formal analysis can be included here
- **Informal fallacies** — belongs in Informal Logic & Argumentation Theory
- **Bayesian probability and statistics** — belongs in Mathematics or Decision Theory
- **The philosophy of mathematics** (the nature of numbers, mathematical realism vs. nominalism) — belongs in Philosophy of Mathematics
- **Computer programming** as an applied skill — belongs in Computer Science
- **Cognitive biases in reasoning** — belongs in Cognitive Psychology

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Adjacent Sub-subcategories

Formal logic is not merged with **Informal Logic & Argumentation Theory** because they address fundamentally different aspects of reasoning. Formal logic studies validity in terms of logical form using artificial languages, while informal logic studies the norms of reasoning in natural language, including fallacies, argument schemes, and dialectical structure. The two sub-subcategories complement each other: formal logic provides precise tools for analyzing certain types of validity, while informal logic addresses the messier reality of everyday argumentation.

Formal logic is not merged with **Mathematics** because, while logic and mathematics overlap significantly (especially in proof theory and model theory), philosophy of logic addresses philosophical questions about the nature of logical truth, the normativity of logic, and the relationship between logic and thought that go beyond mathematics.

## Essential Books

1. **Principia Mathematica** by Alfred North Whitehead and Bertrand Russell — The monumental attempt to derive all mathematics from logical axioms; the founding text of modern logic.
2. **Begriffsschrift** by Gottlob Frege — The birth of modern logic; Frege's invention of quantifier notation and the formalization of logical inference.
3. **Introduction to Mathematical Logic** by Alonzo Church — The classic textbook that systematically develops first-order logic through the incompleteness theorems.
4. **A Mathematical Introduction to Logic** by Herbert Enderton — The standard graduate textbook; covers propositional and predicate logic, completeness, and incompleteness.
5. **Computability and Logic** by George Boolos, John Burgess, and Richard Jeffrey — A comprehensive introduction connecting logic to computability and the limits of formal systems.
6. **The Logic of Decision** by Richard Jeffrey — Applies formal logic and probability to decision theory.
7. **Modal Logic** by Patrick Blackburn, Maarten de Rijke, and Yde Venema — A comprehensive introduction to modal logic and its applications.
8. **Proofs and Types** by Jean-Yves Girard — Explores the Curry-Howard correspondence between proofs and programs.
9. **Philosophical Logic** edited by Lou Goble — A comprehensive anthology covering the major topics in philosophical logic.
10. **Godel's Proof** by Ernest Nagel and James R. Newman — The most accessible introduction to Godel's incompleteness theorems for non-specialists.

- Logical equivalence and entailment
- Quantifiers (universal, existential)
- First-order vs. higher-order logic

- The Church-Turing thesis

- Natural deduction
- Sequent calculus
- Curry-Howard correspondence

## Connections to Other Subcategories

Formal logic connects to **Informal Logic & Argumentation Theory** as the formal complement to natural-language reasoning. It connects to **Nature of Knowledge and JTB** through the analysis of deductive inference as a source of knowledge. **Epistemology of Disagreement** uses formal models of peer disagreement. **Philosophy of Language** engages the semantics of formal languages and their relationship to natural language. **Philosophy of Science** uses formal logic in the analysis of confirmation and explanation. **Metaphysics** engages the ontological commitment of logical theories.
