# Informal Logic and Argumentation Theory

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Not all reasoning can be captured by formal logic. Everyday arguments — in law, politics, ethics, and ordinary conversation — rely on patterns of reasoning that resist formalization in propositional or predicate calculus. Informal logic emerged in the mid-20th century as a response to the limitations of formal logic in analyzing real arguments. It studies the norms of good reasoning in natural language, identifies common fallacies (ad hominem, straw man, false dilemma, slippery slope), and develops methods for argument analysis, reconstruction, and evaluation.

Argumentation theory extends this project by studying argumentation as a social and dialectical process. The Pragma-dialectical approach (van Eemeren and Grootendorst) analyzes argumentation as a critical discussion governed by procedural rules. The Toulmin model breaks arguments into claims, grounds, warrants, backings, qualifiers, and rebuttals — a framework widely used in law, education, and artificial intelligence. Contemporary informal logic also addresses visual argumentation, argument mapping, and the analysis of arguments in new media.

## What Belongs Here

- **The nature of informal logic**: The distinction between formal and informal logic; the critique of formal logic as the sole norm of reasoning
- **Fallacies**: Classification and analysis of informal fallacies (ad hominem, ad populum, straw man, false dilemma, slippery slope, begging the question, appeal to authority, correlation vs. causation)
- **The Toulmin model**: Claims, grounds, warrants, backings, qualifiers, rebuttals; the application of the model in law, education, and argument analysis
- **Pragma-dialectics**: Van Eemeren and Grootendorst's theory of argumentation as critical discussion; the ten rules for rational debate
- **Argument schemes**: Walton's classification of argument patterns (argument from expert opinion, argument from analogy, argument from consequences)
- **Argument mapping**: Visual representation of argument structure; premises, conclusions, objections, and rebuttals
- **Critical thinking pedagogy**: Teaching informal logic and argument analysis in education
- **Visual and multimodal argumentation**: Arguments in images, film, diagrams, and multimedia
- **The ethics of argumentation**: Norms of intellectual honesty, the duty to engage opposing views, intellectual humility
- **The history of informal logic**: Aristotle's Sophistical Refutations, the medieval tradition, Whately's Elements of Logic, the 20th-century revival

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- **Formal logic** (propositional and predicate calculus) — belongs in its own sub-subcategory
- **Cognitive biases** (confirmation bias, availability heuristic) — belongs in Cognitive Psychology
- **Rhetoric as persuasion** (Aristotle's ethos, pathos, logos as persuasive techniques) — belongs in Rhetoric or Communication
- **Debate as competitive performance** (high school debate, Lincoln-Douglas) — belongs in Speech and Debate
- **The epistemology of disagreement** — belongs in its own sub-subcategory
- **Propaganda analysis** — belongs in Media Studies or Political Science

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Adjacent Sub-subcategories

Informal logic is not merged with **Formal Logic** because they employ different methods and address different domains. Formal logic analyzes deductive validity using symbolic languages; informal logic analyzes inductive, presumptive, and conductive reasoning in natural language. Formal logic can tell you that "if P then Q, P, therefore Q" is valid but cannot tell you whether the straw man fallacy is present in a political debate. The two sub-subcategories are complementary partners in the broader study of reasoning.

Informal logic is not merged with **Epistemology of Disagreement** because it addresses a broader range of reasoning phenomena. Epistemology of disagreement focuses on the narrow question of how to respond to peer disagreement, while informal logic encompasses all aspects of natural-language argumentation, including fallacies, argument structure, and dialectical norms.

## Essential Books

1. **The Uses of Argument** by Stephen Toulmin — The foundational text of informal logic; introduces the Toulmin model and challenges the sufficiency of formal logic for analyzing real arguments.
2. **Argumentation: Analysis, Evaluation, Presentation** by Frans van Eemeren, Rob Grootendorst, and Francisca Snoeck Henkemans — The definitive presentation of Pragma-dialectics.
3. **Fundamentals of Critical Argumentation** by Douglas Walton — An accessible introduction to argument schemes, fallacies, and dialogue theory.
4. **A Practical Study of Argument** by Trudy Govier — The standard textbook for informal logic and critical thinking courses.
5. **Fallacies: Selected Papers 1972-1982** edited by Charles Hamblin — The modern revival of fallacy theory; Hamblin's "dialectical" approach to fallacies.
6. **A Rulebook for Arguments** by Anthony Weston — A concise, practical guide to constructing and evaluating arguments.
7. **The New Rhetoric: A Treatise on Argumentation** by Chaim Perelman and Lucie Olbrechts-Tyteca — A classic work that reconnects argumentation theory with rhetoric.
8. **Logic and Contemporary Rhetoric** by Howard Kahane — Connects informal logic to real-world examples from politics, advertising, and media.
9. **Critical Thinking: An Introduction to Reasoning** by Francis Watanabe Dauer — A comprehensive textbook integrating formal and informal approaches.
10. **Understanding Arguments** by Walter Sinnott-Armstrong and Robert Fogelin — A widely-used textbook that bridges formal and informal logic.

## Key Concepts

- Informal fallacy
- Ad hominem (circumstantial, abusive, tu quoque)
- Straw man
- False dilemma / false dichotomy
- Slippery slope
- Begging the question (petitio principii)
- Appeal to authority (argumentum ad verecundiam)
- Toulmin model (claim, ground, warrant, backing, qualifier, rebuttal)
- Pragma-dialectics
- Argument schemes
- Burden of proof
- Argument mapping
- Conductive argument (argument from analogy or precedent)
- Causal fallacy (post hoc ergo propter hoc)
- Red herring

## Connections to Other Subcategories

Informal logic connects to **Formal Logic** as the complement to formal methods. It connects to **Epistemology of Disagreement** through norms of argumentative engagement. **Political Philosophy** engages the role of rhetoric and argument in democratic deliberation. **Philosophy of Language** connects through speech act theory (Austin, Searle) and the pragmatics of argumentation. **Ethics & Moral Philosophy** connects through the ethics of belief and argumentation. **Law and Legal Reasoning** is one of the primary applied domains of argumentation theory.
