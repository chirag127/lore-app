# Content Summary: Division-by-Division

## Introduction: The Question of the Meaning of Being (sections 1–8)

Heidegger opens with a striking claim: the question of the meaning of Being (*Seinsfrage*) has been forgotten. He argues that although "Being" is the most universal concept, it is not a genus and cannot be defined in the traditional way. Every understanding of entities presupposes an understanding of Being, yet the Western tradition — from Plato through Aristotle, Medieval scholasticism, Descartes, Kant, and Hegel — has systematically obscured the question by treating Being either as the emptiest abstraction or as something "self-evident" that requires no investigation.

The Introduction establishes the method: hermeneutic phenomenology, drawn from Husserl but radically transformed. Where Husserl bracketed the natural attitude to study the structures of consciousness, Heidegger argues that phenomenology must let "what shows itself be seen from itself." The phenomenon to be investigated is not consciousness but Dasein — the entity whose very Being is an issue for it. The method is hermeneutic because our access to Being is always mediated by interpretation, circular in structure. The famous "hermeneutic circle" is not a vicious circle but the very structure of understanding.

Heidegger announces his strategy: first, a preparatory fundamental analysis of Dasein (Division One), followed by an interpretation of Dasein in terms of temporality (Division Two), which will disclose the horizon for raising and answering the question of Being in general. A third division — a "destructuring" of the history of ontology — was planned but never written.

## Division One: Preparatory Fundamental Analysis of Dasein (sections 9–44)

### Chapter 1: The Existential Analytic of Dasein (sections 9–11)

Heidegger introduces Dasein's two defining characteristics: (1) its essence lies in its existence — what Dasein is is always a matter of how it is; and (2) Dasein is in each case mine — it is a being that relates to its own Being as a possibility. The mode of Being of Dasein is called *existence*, and its structures are *existentialia* (as opposed to the *categories* that apply to entities not of Dasein's kind).

The analytic must begin from Dasein's "average everydayness" — its most ordinary, undifferentiated mode — because that is where Dasein mostly finds itself. Heidegger warns against applying traditional categories (subject, object, consciousness, soul) that distort the phenomena.

### Chapter 2: Being-in-the-World in General (sections 12–13)

Heidegger introduces the foundational concept of the book: *Being-in-the-world* (*In-der-Welt-sein*). This is a unitary phenomenon that must be grasped as a whole before being analyzed into its constituent moments. "Being-in" is not spatial containment (like water in a glass) but *dwelling* — Heidegger draws on the German word *Wohnen* to evoke a familiar, engaged inhabiting of a world.

He rejects Descartes' picture of a worldless subject who must somehow "bridge" the gap to the external world. For Heidegger, skepticism about the external world is a pseudo-problem — Dasein is always already in-the-world. The "problem of the external world" arises only when we misinterpret Dasein as a subject thing and the world as an object thing.

### Chapter 3: The Worldhood of the World (sections 14–24)

This is one of the most famous and influential chapters. Heidegger distinguishes four senses of "world": (1) the optical concept of the totality of entities; (2) the ontological concept of the Being of those entities; (3) the regional sense (e.g., "the world of the mathematician"); and (4) the existential sense of worldhood as a structure of Dasein. His focus is on (4).

He arrives at worldhood through an analysis of *equipment* (*Zeug*). In our everyday dealings, we do not encounter objects as bare things with properties. We encounter *equipment* — hammers, nails, doors, writing implements — that are ready-to-hand (*zuhanden*). Equipment always belongs to an equipmental context; a hammer refers to nails, wood, a workbench, a workshop, the world. The mode of Being of equipment is *readiness-to-hand* (*Zuhandenheit*). A hammer is genuinely encountered *as* a hammer when we are using it unreflectively — when it "withdraws" into the task at hand.

When equipment breaks or malfunctions, it becomes *unready-to-hand* (*unzuhanden*) — it obtrudes as an obstacle. When we step back and contemplate an object theoretically, stripping it of its equipmental context, it becomes *present-at-hand* (*vorhanden*) — a bare thing with properties. Heidegger argues that presence-at-hand is a *deficient mode* of encountering entities, not the primary mode. The scientific attitude, which treats the world as an array of objects with measurable properties, is derivative from the primordial engagement of practical coping.

Heidegger then analyzes spatiality as a structure of Being-in-the-world. Dasein's space is not the homogeneous space of physics but a lived space organized by *deseverance* (*Ent-fernung* — literally "de-distancing"), *directionality*, and *regions*. The things that matter to us are "near" in an existential sense, regardless of objective distance.

### Chapter 4: Being-with and Being-one's-Self. The "They" (sections 25–27)

Heidegger argues that Dasein is essentially *Being-with* (*Mitsein*) others. Solipsism is not an epistemological problem to be solved but a privation of an originary sociality. Others are encountered not by inference from analogies but directly in the shared world. Our everyday mode of Being-with is *solicitude* (*Fürsorge*), which can take the deficient mode of leaping in and dominating, or the positive mode of leaping ahead and liberating.

But in everydayness, Dasein is not itself. It is dominated by *das Man* — the "they" or the "one" — the anonymous social norm that determines what "one" does, what "one" says, what "one" considers proper. The they is not a specific person or group but a neutral, impersonal force: "The they prescribes one's state-of-mind, and determines what one sees and how one sees." Dasein's default mode is *fallenness*: absorption in the world, captivity to the they, and flight from its own possibilities. This is *inauthenticity* (*Uneigentlichkeit*) — not a moral failing but an existential structure.

### Chapter 5: Being-in as Such (sections 28–38)

Heidegger analyzes the three equiprimordial existential structures of Being-in: *state-of-mind* (*Befindlichkeit*), *understanding* (*Verstehen*), and *discourse* (*Rede*).

State-of-mind is Dasein's always-already-being-in-a-mood. Moods are not subjective feelings projected onto an objective world but ways in which the world *matters* to us. The most fundamental mood is *anxiety* (*Angst*), which is radically different from fear. Fear is always fear *of* something specific — a threat, a danger. Anxiety has no object; it is anxious *about* nothing in particular, and this nothing is the world as such. Anxiety individualizes Dasein, tearing it away from the familiar they-self and disclosing its uncanny (*unheimlich*) character — the feeling of not-being-at-home.

Understanding is Dasein's projective capacity — its ability to project itself onto possibilities. Understanding is always interpretive: it operates through the "as-structure," grasping something *as* something. Interpretation (*Auslegung*) works by means of a fore-structure: every interpretation has a fore-having (a prior grasp of the whole), a fore-sight (a perspective), and a fore-conception (a conceptual articulation). This is the hermeneutic circle at work.

Discourse (*Rede*) is the articulation of intelligibility. Language is derivative — discourse is the existential-ontological foundation of language. Listening and keeping silent are modes of discourse as fundamental as speaking.

Heidegger also analyzes *falling* (*Verfallen*) — Dasein's absorption in the they and the world. The temptations of curiosity (*Neugier* — seeing for the sake of seeing), ambiguity (*Zweideutigkeit* — where nothing is genuinely understood but everything is talked about), and idle talk (*Gerede* — groundless chatter) characterize fallenness. These are not accidental features of modern life but existential structures.

### Chapter 6: Care as the Being of Dasein (sections 39–44)

Heidegger synthesizes the preparatory analysis by identifying *care* (*Sorge*) as the Being of Dasein. Care has a tripartite structure: existence (projection onto future possibilities), facticity (thrownness into a world not of our choosing), and fallenness (absorption in the present world). The unity of care is temporal: Dasein is ahead-of-itself (future), already-in (past), and along-with (present).

Heidegger defines Dasein's Being as: "ahead-of-itself-Being-already-in-(the-world) as Being-alongside (entities encountered within-the-world)." This formal structure captures the temporal depth of existence: Dasein is always projecting forward, grounded in what it has been, and engaged with the present.

The chapter concludes with a demonstration that care underlies truth. Truth is not primarily correspondence between propositions and things; it is *disclosedness* (*Erschlossenheit*) — the uncovering of entities. Propositional truth is derivative from this more fundamental openness of Dasein. The traditional definition of truth as *adaequatio intellectus et rei* (adequation of intellect and thing) presupposes a more primordial disclosedness of Being.

## Division Two: Dasein and Temporality (sections 45–83)

### Chapter 1: Dasein's Possibility of Being-a-Whole, and Being-towards-Death (sections 45–53)

Division Two deepens the analysis by showing that the meaning of care is temporality. But to access authentic temporality, Heidegger must first confront the problem of Dasein's wholeness: as long as Dasein exists, it is incomplete (outstanding possibilities remain); at death, it becomes complete but ceases to exist. How can Dasein be grasped as a whole?

Heidegger's answer is *Being-towards-death* (*Sein-zum-Tode*). Death is not an event that happens at the end of life; it is a way of being that pervades Dasein's entire existence. Death is (1) Dasein's ownmost possibility (no one can die for us); (2) non-relational (it cuts off all relations to others); (3) not to be outstripped (certain but indefinite); and (4) certain yet indeterminate as to when.

The they covers over death's existential character: "one dies" is the they's way of neutralizing death into an anonymous event that happens to everyone but not to anyone in particular. Authentic Being-towards-death involves *anticipation* (*Vorlaufen*) — running forward into death existentially, not as a morbid fixation but as a way of individualizing Dasein and wrenching it from the they's grip.

### Chapter 2: The Attestation of Authentic Existence: Conscience and Resoluteness (sections 54–60)

How does Dasein know it can be authentic? The attestation comes from the *call of conscience* (*Gewissenruf*). Conscience is not a moral faculty in the Kantian sense but a mode of discourse in which Dasein calls itself back from the they to its ownmost potentiality-for-Being. The call has no content — it says nothing specific; it simply summons Dasein to its own Being-guilty.

*Guilt* (*Schuld*) for Heidegger is not a moral category but an existential one: Dasein is guilty because, as thrown, it is the *ground of a nullity*. Dasein never fully masters its own thrownness; it is always "not" the ground of its own Being. And in projecting onto possibilities, it always chooses one possibility at the expense of others — a "not" that pervades existence.

Heidegger's response to the call is *resoluteness* (*Entschlossenheit* — literally "un-closedness"), the disclosedness of Dasein in which it takes over its thrownness and projects itself authentically onto its possibilities. Resoluteness is not a once-and-for-all decision but a constant mode of Being.

### Chapter 3: The Authentic Potentiality-for-Being-a-Whole and Temporality as the Ontological Meaning of Care (sections 61–66)

This is the pivotal chapter. Heidegger argues that the ontological meaning of care is *temporality* (*Zeitlichkeit*). The tripartite structure of care corresponds to the three *ecstases* of temporality:
- The future (*Zukunft*): the "coming-towards-oneself" in projection — the primary ecstasis of authentic existence
- The having-been (*Gewesenheit*): taking over thrownness — the primary ecstasis of facticity
- The present (*Gegenwart*): making-present — the primary ecstasis of fallenness

In authentic temporality, the future is primary: Dasein "comes back" to its thrownness from its projected possibilities, and makes the present present in a way that is genuinely *in the moment*. This "moment of vision" (*Augenblick*), which Heidegger borrows from Kierkegaard, is the authentic present — not the drifting present of curiosity but the resolute engagement with the situation.

### Chapter 4: Temporality and Everydayness (sections 67–71)

Heidegger reinterprets the structures of everyday Dasein — understanding, state-of-mind, falling, discourse — in terms of temporality. Understanding is grounded in the future; state-of-mind in having-been; falling in making-present. The inauthentic temporality of the they is characterized by *awaiting* (not anticipating), *forgetting* (not repeating), and *making-present* (not the moment of vision). In inauthenticity, the present dominates, and past and future are flattened.

He also analyzes the temporality of concernful dealing with equipment. The "in-order-to" structure of equipment is a "awaiting which retains" — we hold onto our past skills and await the desired outcome, making present the equipment in the task.

### Chapter 5: Temporality and Historicality (sections 72–77)

Dasein's temporality is the ground of its *historicality* (*Geschichtlichkeit*). Heidegger rejects both the objectivist view of history (events in a neutral time-line) and the subjectivist view (history as mental representation). History is grounded in Dasein's temporal stretching-along between birth and death.

Authentic historicality involves *repetition* (*Wiederholung*) — the resolute retrieval of inherited possibilities. This is not a return to the past but a creative appropriation of past possibilities in light of Dasein's own finite future. "The repetition makes a *reciprocative rejoinder* to the possibility of that existence which has-been-there."

Heidegger discusses Dilthey's influence and contrasts his own view with Hegel's philosophy of history. The chapter grounds the hermeneutic character of historical understanding — the idea that all interpretation is situated in a living tradition.

### Chapter 6: Temporality and Within-Time-ness as the Origin of the Vulgar Concept of Time (sections 78–83)

The final chapter traces how the ordinary understanding of time (as a sequence of "nows") arises from a leveling of primordial temporality. "Within-time-ness" (*Innerzeitigkeit*) — the everyday experience of time as dated, spanned, and public — is not wrong but derivative. Dasein's concernful dealings "take time" and "date" events in terms of its projects ("after the meal," "before the lecture"). The "now" of ordinary time is a fallen mode of the present.

Heidegger argues that the "vulgar" concept of time — infinite, irreversible, homogeneous — originates when Dasein forgets the originary temporality that makes it possible. This is not an error but an existential tendency of fallenness. Aristotle's definition of time as "the number of motion" and Hegel's concept of time as "negativity" are both derivative from a more originary temporality that they cannot account for.

The book ends — famously and frustratingly — with Heidegger breaking off the investigation. "Is there a way leading from primordial time to the meaning of Being? Does time itself manifest itself as the horizon of Being?" These questions are left unanswered; the promised Division Three was never written.

## Reading Guide

*Being and Time* is best approached in stages. First-time readers should begin with the Introduction and Division One (sections 1–44), which contain the most accessible and influential material — the analysis of equipment, the they, anxiety, and care. Division Two (sections 45–83) is more abstract and builds systematically on Division One. Sections 72–83 on historicality and the vulgar concept of time can be read as a conclusion to the project, though they remain unfinished.

The sections on temporality (63–66) are the conceptual heart of the work but are notoriously difficult. Readers who find them impenetrable on a first pass should focus instead on the existential analysis of Division One and return to temporality later.

For those short on time: sections 1–7 (Introduction), 12–18 (worldhood), 25–27 (the they), 29–30 (anxiety), 39–40 (care), 46–53 (Being-towards-death), and 54–60 (conscience and resoluteness) cover the essential argument. Sections 72–83 on historicality and time can be skimmed without losing the core of the project.

The book rewards multiple readings; most scholars agree that *Being and Time* cannot be understood on a single pass. Heidegger's claim that the hermeneutic circle is not a vicious circle but the structure of understanding applies to the reading of his own text.
