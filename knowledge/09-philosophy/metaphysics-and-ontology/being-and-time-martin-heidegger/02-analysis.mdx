# Analysis

## 1. Historical Context

*Being and Time* emerged from the crisis of early twentieth-century German philosophy. Neo-Kantianism, which dominated German universities, had reduced philosophy to epistemology and philosophy of science, abandoning the great metaphysical questions. At the same time, the natural sciences were claiming authority over all domains of knowledge — the "scientific worldview" of the Vienna Circle threatened to dismiss philosophy's traditional questions as meaningless. Heidegger's teacher Edmund Husserl had developed phenomenology — "back to the things themselves" — as a rigorous method for philosophy. But Husserl's phenomenology remained idealist and centered on consciousness. Heidegger radicalized the project, arguing that the fundamental question was not about consciousness but about Being. His 1927 masterwork, written at white heat in his mid-thirties, was published in Husserl's journal and immediately marked him as the leading philosopher of his generation. The broader cultural context — Weimar Germany's social fragmentation, the loss of tradition, the rise of mass society — pervades the book's anxious tone and its critique of "the they."

## 2. Author's Background and Biases

Martin Heidegger (1889–1976) was trained as a Catholic theologian and studied medieval philosophy (Duns Scotus, Aquinas, Eckhart, Luther) alongside Aristotle and Husserl. His early lecture courses at Freiburg and Marburg — now published as the *Gesamtausgabe* — reveal a scholar of extraordinary depth, methodically working through the history of philosophy to retrieve its primordial experiences. But his biases shaped the project in problematic ways. His deep anti-modernism, agrarian romanticism, and hostility to democracy, technology, and mass culture saturate *Being and Time*'s imagery. The book's social ontology — the they, fallenness, idle talk — reads at times as a philosopher's contempt for ordinary social life. The political catastrophe of his Nazi engagement (membership in the Party, rectorate speech of 1933, subsequent silence about the Holocaust) has prompted endless debate about whether these biases are merely biographical stains or reflect deep problems in the philosophy itself. Richard Wolin (1990, *The Politics of Being*) argues that the book's critique of the they and its rhetoric of "resoluteness" prepared the ground for authoritarian decisionism. Heidegger himself never repudiated *Being and Time*, though his later thought substantially revised its approach.

## 3. Core Argument and Structure

The book's argument moves on two levels simultaneously: an existential analysis of human existence (Division One) and a temporal interpretation of that existence (Division Two). The core thesis is that Dasein's Being is care, and the meaning of care is temporality. Because Dasein exists temporally — projecting into the future, taking over a past, making present — it has an understanding of Being at all. Being is not a property of entities but that which is disclosed through Dasein's temporal ecstases. The argument thus progresses from the concrete (tools, moods, the they) to the abstract (temporality, historicality, the horizon of Being). The planned third division, which would have made the transition from Dasein's temporality to the meaning of Being as such, was never written. Many readers consider this incompleteness a structural flaw; others argue that the later work implicitly completes the project, but in a different idiom.

## 4. Methodology — Hermeneutic Phenomenology

Heidegger's method is the book's most original contribution and its most criticized feature. He proposes that phenomenology — letting what shows itself be seen from itself — must be *hermeneutic* because the phenomenon of Being always already involves interpretation. There is no neutral, presuppositionless access to Being. The method is therefore circular: we must understand Being to inquire about Being, but the inquiry is what discloses Being. The hermeneutic circle is not a failure of method but the very structure of understanding. This move has been enormously influential — it grounds Gadamer's philosophical hermeneutics and, through him, much of contemporary interpretive theory. But critics ask: if all understanding is circular, what distinguishes good from bad interpretation? How do we avoid relativism? Heidegger's answer — that we must (circularly) begin from the right fore-structures — is powerful but vulnerable to the charge of dogmatism.

## 5. Key Concepts

**Dasein** — The entity for whom Being is an issue; not "human being" in the anthropological sense but the mode of existence that characterizes the kind of beings we are. Dasein is not a subject, not consciousness, not a person — it is the "there" (*Da*) where Being is disclosed.

**Being-in-the-world** — The unitary phenomenon that collapses the Cartesian subject-object distinction. We are not first subjects who somehow connect to an objective world; we are always already in-the-world, shaped by its practical and social contexts.

**Readiness-to-hand / Presence-at-hand** — The distinction between equipment (ready-to-hand) as encountered in practical use, and objects (present-at-hand) as encountered in theoretical contemplation. Readiness-to-hand is ontologically prior.

**The They (*das Man*)** — The anonymous social norm that governs everyday existence. The they is not a specific person or group but a mode of Being in which Dasein is dispersed into the public world, relieved of the burden of choosing.

**Care (*Sorge*)** — The Being of Dasein, with a tripartite structure: existence (future projection), facticity (thrownness into a past), and fallenness (absorption in the present).

**Being-towards-death** — Death as a mode of existence, not a future event. Anticipation of death individualizes Dasein and makes authentic existence possible.

**Temporality** — The ontological meaning of care. The three ecstases (future, having-been, present) correspond to the three structures of care. In authentic temporality, the future is primary.

**Resoluteness (*Entschlossenheit*)** — The authentic disclosedness of Dasein, in which it takes over its thrownness and projects onto its ownmost possibilities.

## 6. Critical Reception — Named Scholars

**Theodor Adorno (1903–1969)**: In *The Jargon of Authenticity* (1964), Adorno launched a devastating critique of Heidegger's language and project. He argued that the "jargon of authenticity" — words like "resoluteness," "authenticity," "the call of Being" — masquerades as a critique of reified modern society while remaining empty and ideology-laden. Heidegger's concepts, Adorno charged, had no ethical content: "authenticity" was presented as a value-free existential structure, but its rhetoric of decision and commitment could accommodate any political content — including Nazism. Adorno's critique is the most influential Marxist-humanist attack on Heidegger, though it has been accused of misreading Heidegger's technical terms as mere jargon.

**Bertrand Russell (1872–1970)**: In *A History of Western Philosophy* (1945) and elsewhere, Russell dismissed Heidegger's philosophy with characteristic bluntness. "He is an extremely obscure philosopher, and one cannot help suspecting that language is here running riot." Russell's positivist critique charges Heidegger with generating pseudo-problems through linguistic confusion — a position shared by members of the Vienna Circle.

**A. J. Ayer (1910–1989)**: In *Language, Truth and Logic* (1936) and later works, Ayer outright called Heidegger a charlatan. For Ayer, Heidegger's claims about Nothing, Being, and Angst were literal nonsense — violations of the verification principle that could not be empirically verified and therefore had no cognitive meaning. This positivist dismissal shaped the analytic tradition's reception of Heidegger for decades.

**Emmanuel Levinas (1906–1995)**: In *Totality and Infinity* (1961) and "Is Ontology Fundamental?" (1951), Levinas offered the most philosophically penetrating critique from within the continental tradition. He argued that Heidegger's ontology prioritizes Being over the Good — that by making Dasein's understanding of Being the fundamental question, Heidegger subordinates the ethical relation to the Other to an impersonal ontology. For Levinas, the face of the Other — not Dasein's Being — is the first philosophy. The critique has been enormously influential, shaping subsequent ethical and political readings of Heidegger.

**Jürgen Habermas (b. 1929)**: In *The Philosophical Discourse of Modernity* (1985) and earlier essays, Habermas both appropriates and criticizes Heidegger. He acknowledges the power of Heidegger's critique of the philosophy of the subject (the Cartesian subject, Kantian consciousness) but argues that Heidegger replaces it with a philosophy of Being that is equally problematic — it eliminates the intersubjective dimension that Habermas's communicative action theory seeks to establish. Habermas also famously remarked in 1989 that "elitist complaints about the 'dictatorship of public opinion' were common currency to the German mandarins of the twenties," situating *Being and Time* in its politically dangerous milieu.

**Hubert Dreyfus (1929–2017)**: Dreyfus's *Being-in-the-World: A Commentary on Division I* (1991) is the most influential English-language commentary on *Being and Time* and the conduit through which Heidegger entered analytic philosophy of mind and cognitive science. Dreyfus read Heidegger as offering a "pragmatist" critique of representationalism — the idea that our primary relation to the world is not mental representation but skilled coping. His interpretation shaped debates about artificial intelligence (he argued that Heidegger's account of skill shows why AI cannot replicate human understanding) and made Heidegger productive for thinkers far beyond the continental tradition.

**Richard Rorty (1931–2007)**: In "Wittgenstein, Heidegger, and the Reification of Language" and other essays, Rorty treated Heidegger as one of the three most important philosophers of the century (alongside Dewey and Wittgenstein). But he insisted that Heidegger's concept of Being is metaphorical — there is no "hidden power" called Being. Rorty's deflationary reading made Heidegger useful for neopragmatism while rejecting the metaphysical ambition that Heidegger himself took to be central.

**Roger Scruton (1944–2020)**: In *Fools, Frauds and Firebrands* (1985, revised 2016), Scruton delivered one of the most trenchant conservative critiques of Heidegger. He argued that *Being and Time* is willfully obscure, that its rhetoric of authenticity and resoluteness substitutes for argument, and that the book's influence on French philosophy has been intellectually disastrous. Scruton's critique represents the analytic-conservative dismissal of continental thought.

**Simon Critchley (b. 1960)**: In a nine-part commentary series for *The Guardian* (2009) and in scholarly works, Critchley argues that *Being and Time* actually offers "no sense of how we might answer the question of being as such" — despite its stated aim. Instead, it provides an answer to the question of what it means to be human. Critchley's reading is sympathetic but deflationary: he sees the book's real value in its existential analysis, not its metaphysical claims.

## 7. Strengths

The book's greatest strength is its revolutionary reconception of what it means to be a human being. Heidegger's critique of the subject-object distinction is one of the most powerful and enduring contributions to philosophy. The analysis of equipment and practical coping transformed philosophy of mind, cognitive science, and technology studies. The concepts of Being-in-the-world, the they, and being-towards-death have entered the broader intellectual culture as permanent resources. The hermeneutic method — the idea that understanding is circular and situated — grounds entire fields of interpretive theory. No serious philosophy of existence, social ontology, or phenomenology can ignore *Being and Time*.

## 8. Weaknesses

The incompleteness of the project is the most obvious weakness. Division Three was never written, and the transition from Dasein's temporality to the meaning of Being in general is never made. The book's obscurity is not merely a difficulty but, some argue, a vice: Heidegger's neologisms can obscure rather than clarify, and the reader must work through a dense terminological apparatus to arrive at claims that could, perhaps, be stated more simply. The political silence — the book says nothing about ethics, justice, or political life — is troubling in light of Heidegger's later Nazism. Some critics, like Adorno and Wolin, see this silence as structural: the very concepts of the book (authenticity, resoluteness, the they) may be incapable of grounding an ethical or political philosophy.

## 9. Enduring Influence

*Being and Time* shaped virtually every major movement in twentieth-century European philosophy: existentialism (Sartre, Merleau-Ponty, Beauvoir), hermeneutics (Gadamer, Ricoeur), deconstruction (Derrida), post-structuralism (Foucault, Deleuze), critical theory (Marcuse, Habermas, to some extent Adorno), and psychoanalysis (Lacan, Boss, Laing). Outside philosophy, it influenced theology (Rahner, Tillich, Bultmann), literary theory (Steiner, Hartman, de Man), psychology (the Daseinsanalysis movement, existential therapy), cognitive science (the enactivist approach, Dreyfus's critique of AI), sociology (Bourdieu's *The Political Ontology of Martin Heidegger*, Giddens's structuration theory), and political theory (Arendt, the later Lefort). It remains the foundational text of the continental tradition in English-speaking philosophy.

## 10. Contemporary Relevance

*Being and Time* has become newly relevant in several domains. In cognitive science, enactivist and embodied cognition approaches (Varela, Thompson, Rosch; Clark; Noë) draw directly on Heidegger's account of Being-in-the-world. The critique of technology — though more prominent in Heidegger's later work — is anticipated in the book's analysis of equipment and readiness-to-hand, and has informed contemporary philosophy of technology (Ihde, Feenberg, Borgmann). The existential analysis of anxiety and authenticity resonates with contemporary mental health debates and therapeutic practice. The critique of "the they" and idle talk has found new resonance in the age of social media. In political philosophy, debates about the nature of public discourse, the dictatorship of public opinion, and the loss of authentic community continue to draw on (and contest) the categories of *Being and Time*.

## 11. Sufficiency of Argument and Evidence

The book's method — hermeneutic phenomenology — makes the question of "evidence" complex. Heidegger does not offer empirical evidence for his claims; he appeals to phenomena that he claims the reader can verify by attending to their own existence. The strategy is to "let what shows itself be seen from itself." This works brilliantly for some analyses (the breakdown of equipment, the experience of anxiety, the character of everyday social life) and less well for others (the derivation of temporality, the transition from Dasein's temporality to Being). The claim that Dasein is "in each case mine" and that authentic existence requires individualization raises questions about whether the analysis commits itself to a particular cultural or historical picture of human existence — an objection that has been pressed by feminist (Irigaray), Marxist (Lukács, Adorno), and postcolonial critics. The argument that the they is an existential (universal) rather than a social-scientific (empirical) concept is crucial for Heidegger but difficult to sustain in light of the obvious cultural specificity of his examples. Despite these problems, the book's central claims — that human existence is fundamentally temporal, that our relation to the world is primarily practical, and that authenticity requires confronting finitude — remain among the most powerful and provocative in the philosophical canon.
