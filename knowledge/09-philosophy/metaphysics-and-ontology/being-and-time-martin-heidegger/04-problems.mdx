# Problems Addressed and Unresolved Questions

## Key Problem: The Forgottenness of Being

The core problem *Being and Time* addresses is the "forgottenness of Being" (*Seinsvergessenheit*) in the Western metaphysical tradition. Heidegger claims that since Plato, philosophy has systematically mistaken *beings* (*Seiendes*) for *Being* (*Sein*). It has asked "what is?" — seeking the fundamental entity (substance, God, the subject, matter) — rather than "what does it mean to be?" This "ontological difference" between Being and beings is the central insight of the book. The crisis of Western civilization — nihilism, rootlessness, technological domination — is, for Heidegger, a direct consequence of this metaphysical forgetting.

## Proposed Solution: Fundamental Ontology Through Dasein

Heidegger's solution is a "fundamental ontology" that investigates the meaning of Being through the analysis of Dasein — the being for whom Being is an issue. Since Dasein already has an implicit, pre-ontological understanding of Being, the task is to make this understanding explicit through phenomenological analysis. The key move is to show that Dasein's understanding of Being is grounded in temporality: we understand Being *as* presence because our own existence is temporally structured, and presence-at-hand (the dominant metaphysical interpretation of Being) is a specific modification of primordial temporality.

## Evidence and Argumentation

Heidegger's evidence is phenomenological rather than empirical. He claims that his descriptions — of equipment use, of anxiety, of the they, of being-towards-death — are phenomenologically evident to anyone who attends to their own experience. The argument for the primacy of readiness-to-hand (practical coping) over presence-at-hand (theoretical contemplation) is the most detailed and compelling. The argument that temporality is the meaning of care is more abstract and relies on the systematic structure built up in Division One. Critics question whether Heidegger's phenomenology genuinely "discovers" these structures or imposes them. The incompleteness of the project — the missing Division Three — means that the central question ("What is the meaning of Being?") is never directly answered.

## Unresolved Questions

Several major questions remain unresolved:

1. **The status of Division Three**: Would Heidegger have succeeded in making the transition from Dasein's temporality to the meaning of Being in general? The later work (especially *Time and Being*, 1962) suggests a different approach — "Being" is not something that can be defined but is an event (*Ereignis*). Was the original project impossible, or merely abandoned?

2. **The normativity of authenticity**: If inauthenticity is not a moral failing but an existential structure, what justifies the preference for authenticity? Heidegger insists his analysis is "value-free," but the language of "lostness," "fallenness," and "flight" clearly carries evaluative weight. This tension is at the heart of Adorno's critique.

3. **The politics of resoluteness**: Resoluteness involves "seizing" possibilities, but the content of those possibilities is left completely undetermined. What distinguishes authentic from inauthentic political action? Heidegger's own biography — his enthusiastic embrace of Nazism as a "resolute" historical decision — suggests that the concept is politically dangerous.

4. **The body**: *Being and Time* is striking for its near-complete silence about the body. Dasein is not embodied in any substantial sense; bodily states are discussed only obliquely through moods and readiness-to-hand. Merleau-Ponty's phenomenology of perception explicitly develops what Heidegger omitted. Is the body a contingent omission or a structural gap?

5. **Others and ethics**: Despite the analysis of Being-with (*Mitsein*), the other plays a subordinate role in *Being and Time* — Dasein is individualized in being-towards-death, not constituted by relation to the Other. Levinas's critique — that Heidegger subordinates ethics to ontology — is the most powerful version of this problem.

6. **Temporality and within-time-ness**: Does Heidegger succeed in deriving the "vulgar" concept of time from primordial temporality, or does his account presuppose the ordinary concept it claims to explain? Critics like Levinas and Derrida have suggested that Derrida's "différance" better captures the temporal oscillation that Heidegger's text enacts but cannot theorize.

## Criticisms

**From the analytic tradition**: Russell and Ayer dismissed Heidegger as generating nonsense through linguistic confusion. More nuanced analytic critics (e.g., Michael Friedman, *A Parting of the Ways*, 2000) trace the divergence between analytic and continental philosophy to the competing projects of Carnap and Heidegger. Carnap's 1932 "The Elimination of Metaphysics" targeted Heidegger's "What Is Metaphysics?" (1929) — which extends *Being and Time*'s analysis of Nothing — as a paradigm of meaningless metaphysics. This debate has been revisited in recent years, with some scholars arguing that Carnap misunderstood Heidegger and that the two projects are not as incompatible as traditionally thought.

**From the Frankfurt School**: Adorno's *The Jargon of Authenticity* (1964) remains the most influential left-wing critique. Adorno argues that Heidegger's existential concepts — emptied of social content — serve as an ideology that mystifies social domination. Marcuse, who studied under Heidegger and was briefly a "Heideggerian Marxist," later broke with his teacher, arguing that Heidegger's philosophy could not accommodate the concrete analysis of social and economic conditions required for critical theory.

**From feminist philosophy**: Luce Irigaray's *The Forgetting of Air in Martin Heidegger* (1983) offers a feminist-deconstructive reading, arguing that Heidegger's analysis of Dasein implicitly depends on a masculine subject and occludes the material, embodied, elemental conditions of existence. Feminist critics also point to the absence of any analysis of gender in the structures of Dasein, raising the question of whether the existentialia are truly universal or covertly masculine.

**From deconstruction**: Derrida's "Ousia and Gramme" (1968) in *Margins of Philosophy* engages *Being and Time*'s reading of Aristotle and Hegel on time, arguing that Heidegger's critique of the metaphysics of presence remains partially captive to the very tradition it criticizes. Derrida's project — deconstruction — is unthinkable without *Being and Time*, but it also pushes beyond it.

## Alternative Solutions

The problem *Being and Time* addresses — the nature and meaning of existence — has been approached differently by other traditions. In analytic metaphysics, the question of Being is pursued through formal ontology, mereology, and the logic of quantification (Quine's "to be is to be the value of a variable"). In pragmatism, the meaning of existence is understood through action and consequences (Dewey's naturalism, Rorty's neopragmatism). In Buddhism, the question of existence is addressed through the analysis of impermanence (*anicca*), non-self (*anatta*), and dependent origination (*paticca-samuppāda*) — a tradition that Heidegger himself was interested in late in his career. The later Wittgenstein dissolves the question through therapy: the "meaning of Being" is a grammatical confusion that disappears when we attend to language-in-use.

## Practical Applications

Despite the remarkable abstraction of *Being and Time*, it has found practical applications in several domains. In **existential therapy** (Irvin Yalom, *Existential Psychotherapy*; Medard Boss, *Daseinsanalysis*), Heidegger's analysis of death, anxiety, guilt, and authenticity provides the theoretical framework for therapeutic practice. In **cognitive science**, the concept of Being-in-the-world has informed the enactive approach (Varela, Thompson, Rosch's *The Embodied Mind*) and critiques of representationalist AI. In **technology studies**, the distinction between readiness-to-hand and presence-at-hand grounds analyses of how technology changes our relation to the world. In **education theory**, the hermeneutic circle has shaped pedagogical approaches to reading and interpretation. In **political theory**, Heidegger's analysis of the they — the anonymous forces of normalization — has been taken up by critical theorists of mass society and the public sphere. The book's most profound practical implication may be its insistence that authentic existence requires confronting mortality — a claim that, despite its metaphysical framework, resonates in therapy, end-of-life care, and everyday reflection.
