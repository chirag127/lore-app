*Being and Time* (German: *Sein und Zeit*, 1927) is the magnum opus of German philosopher Martin Heidegger and one of the most influential, controversial, and difficult works of twentieth-century philosophy. It attempts to revive the question of the meaning of Being (*Seinsfrage*) — a question Heidegger claims the Western tradition has forgotten since Plato. Through a rigorous phenomenological analysis of Dasein ("being-there," the mode of existence characteristic of humans), Heidegger argues that our understanding of Being is fundamentally temporal and that only by confronting our finitude can we achieve authentic existence. The book's stature in intellectual history is routinely compared with works by Kant and Hegel, yet it remains deeply contested: celebrated for its depth, originality, and existential urgency, and criticized for its obscurity, political entanglements, and ethical evasions.

Martin Heidegger (1889–1976) was born in Messkirch, a small town in southwest Germany, into a devout Catholic family. He trained initially for the priesthood before turning to philosophy, studying under Edmund Husserl — the founder of phenomenology — at the University of Freiburg. *Being and Time* was published in Husserl's *Yearbook for Philosophy and Phenomenological Research* in 1927, and Heidegger succeeded Husserl's chair at Freiburg the following year. The book was intended as the first half of a larger project, but the second half was never written — Heidegger's thought took a "turn" (*Kehre*) in the 1930s. His reputation was permanently tarnished by his involvement with the Nazi Party as rector of Freiburg University in 1933–34, a fact that continues to generate intense debate about the relationship between his philosophy and his politics.
