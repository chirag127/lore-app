# Free Will and Determinism

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

The free will problem arises from a tension between two powerful ideas. On one hand, we experience ourselves as agents who make genuine choices and are responsible for our actions. On the other hand, science suggests that every event — including our decisions — may be caused by prior events according to deterministic laws. If determinism is true, can we be said to have free will? If we do not have free will, can we be held morally responsible for our actions? The debate has implications for law, ethics, religion, and our self-conception as human beings.

The major positions are: hard determinism (free will is incompatible with determinism, determinism is true, so we lack free will — d'Holbach, Spinoza); libertarianism (free will is incompatible with determinism, but we have free will, so determinism must be false — Reid, Chisholm, Kane); and compatibilism (free will is compatible with determinism — Hobbes, Hume, Mill, Frankfurt, Dennett). Within each camp, there are sophisticated variations. The debate has been reinvigorated by neuroscience (Libet-style experiments), experimental philosophy, and the quantum mechanics challenge to classical determinism.

## What Belongs Here

- **Determinism**: Causal determinism, logical determinism, theological determinism, biological/genetic determinism, the consequence argument
- **Hard determinism**: Free will is incompatible with determinism, determinism is true, so free will is an illusion
- **Libertarianism (metaphysical)**: Agent causation (Reid, Chisholm, Clarke), event-causal libertarianism (Kane), the requirement that the agent could have done otherwise
- **Compatibilism**: Classical compatibilism (Hobbes, Hume), hierarchical theories (Frankfurt), reasons-responsive theories (Fischer and Ravizza)
- **The consequence argument** (van Inwagen): If determinism is true, our actions are the consequence of laws of nature and events in the remote past, over which we have no control
- **The mind argument**: The objection that indeterminism is not sufficient for control; the problem of random events
- **Moral responsibility**: The relationship between free will and moral responsibility; Strawson's reactive attitudes, the possibility of moral responsibility without free will
- **The neuroscience of free will**: Libet's experiments and their interpretation, the readiness potential, recent challenges
- **Free will and the law**: Legal responsibility, the insanity defense, criminal punishment
- **Free will and theism**: Theological determinism, predestination, the problem of evil
- **Experimental philosophy of free will**: Do ordinary people believe in free will? The impact of experimental data on the philosophical debate

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- **Political liberty** (freedom as a political concept) — belongs in Political Philosophy
- **The nature of consciousness** — belongs in Philosophy of Consciousness
- **Personal identity** — belongs in its own sub-subcategory
- **Quantum mechanics interpretation** (indeterminacy, wave function collapse) — belongs in Philosophy of Physics
- **The problem of evil** (theological) — belongs in Philosophy of Religion

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Adjacent Sub-subcategories

Free will and determinism is not merged with **Philosophy of Being & Reality** because, while it draws on metaphysics (causation, modality), it is a distinct problem with its own literature, thought experiments, and practical implications. The debate about whether we have free will is specific enough — and voluminous enough — to warrant its own sub-subcategory. The moral responsibility dimension, in particular, gives it a practical urgency absent from general ontology.

Free will is not merged with **Personal Identity & Philosophy of Mind** because, while they interact (the nature of agency, the relationship between mind and brain), they address distinct questions. You can have a theory of personal identity that does not settle the free will question, and vice versa. The free will debate has its own canonical arguments (the consequence argument, the mind argument, manipulation arguments) that are specific to it.

## Essential Books

1. **An Essay on Free Will** by Peter van Inwagen — The definitive defense of incompatibilism; introduces the consequence argument.
2. **Elbow Room** by Daniel Dennett — A spirited defense of compatibilism; free will as the capacity to make rational decisions in a deterministic world.
3. **Freedom and Resentment** by P.F. Strawson — A classic essay; argues that the moral emotions (reactive attitudes) are fundamental and that the metaphysical debate is secondary.
4. **Responsibility and Control** by John Martin Fischer and Mark Ravizza — Develops a sophisticated compatibilist account of moral responsibility based on reasons-responsiveness.
5. **The Significance of Free Will** by Robert Kane — The most important defense of libertarian free will in the contemporary literature.
6. **The Problem of Free Will** by David Hodgson — An accessible overview of the major positions and arguments.
7. **A Metaphysics for Freedom** by Helen Steward — Argues that agency is a fundamental feature of animal life and is incompatible with determinism.
8. **Living Without Free Will** by Derk Pereboom — Defends hard determinism; argues that we can live with the denial of free will.
9. **Free Will** by Sam Harris — A popular neuroscientific argument against free will; represents the "consciousness causes illusion" view.
10. **Free Will: The Basics** by Meghan Griffith — An accessible introduction to the main positions, arguments, and thought experiments.

- The mind argument (randomness objection)
- Ultimate responsibility (UR)

- Moral responsibility
- Agent causation
- The problem of sourcehood
- Manipulation arguments (e.g., the "four-case" argument)
- The neuroscience of volition (Libet)

## Connections to Other Subcategories

Free will connects to **Personal Identity & Philosophy of Mind** through the nature of agency and the mind-body problem. It connects to **Philosophy of Being & Reality** through causation, determinism, and modality. **Political Philosophy** engages the implications for legal responsibility and punishment. **Ethics & Moral Philosophy** depends on free will for the justification of moral praise and blame. **Philosophy of Science** connects through physics (determinism vs. indeterminism) and neuroscience. **Philosophy of Religion** engages theological determinism and predestination.
