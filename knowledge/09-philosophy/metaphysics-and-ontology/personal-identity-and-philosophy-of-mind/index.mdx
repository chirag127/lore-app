# Personal Identity and Philosophy of Mind

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Personal identity addresses one of the most intimate and enduring philosophical questions: what makes you the same person you were yesterday, and what would it take for you to cease being you? The question is not merely metaphysical but has profound implications for ethics (moral responsibility, promises, punishment), medicine (advance directives, dementia, brain transplants), and our self-understanding as persons. The classic debate is between psychological continuity theories (you are the same person if you have overlapping memories and psychological connections — Locke's view), bodily continuity theories (you are the same person if you have the same living body), and the brain-based or animalist view (you are a human animal).

Philosophy of mind, closely related, asks what the mind is and how it relates to the body. The mind-body problem — the question of how consciousness and mental states fit into a physical world — is one of the central problems of philosophy. Positions range from substance dualism (Descartes: mind and body are distinct substances), to physicalism (the mind is the brain, or wholly depends on it), to idealism (the physical world is mental), to panpsychism (consciousness is a fundamental feature of reality). These questions connect to consciousness, intentionality, mental causation, and the possibility of artificial consciousness.

## What Belongs Here

- **Personal identity over time**: The psychological continuity view (Locke, Parfit), the bodily continuity view, the brain-based view, animalism (Olson), the complex view
- **The self**: The unity of consciousness, the narrative self, the no-self view (Buddhist), the illusion of the self (Hume, Metzinger)
- **Personal identity and ethics**: The relationship between identity and moral responsibility, advance directives, the non-identity problem
- **The mind-body problem**: Dualism (Cartesian, property dualism), physicalism (type identity, token identity, functionalism), idealism, neutral monism
- **Consciousness**: The hard problem (Chalmers), qualia, the explanatory gap, the knowledge argument (Mary's room), the zombie argument
- **Mental causation**: How mental states can cause physical events if the physical world is causally closed
- **Intentionality**: The "aboutness" of mental states; Brentano's thesis, naturalizing intentionality
- **Artificial intelligence and mind**: Can machines think? The Turing test, strong AI vs. weak AI, the Chinese room argument (Searle)
- **The extended mind**: Clark and Chalmers's hypothesis that cognitive processes extend beyond the brain into the environment

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- **Empirical psychology** — belongs in Cognitive Psychology
- **Neuroscience** (brain structure, neural correlates of consciousness) — belongs in Neuroscience
- **The metaphysics of time and causation** — belongs in Philosophy of Being & Reality
- **Free will** — belongs in its own sub-subcategory
- **Social identity** (race, gender, ethnicity as social identities) — belongs in Social and Political Philosophy or Critical Theory
- **Phenomenology of selfhood** (first-person experience) — belongs in Phenomenology

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Adjacent Sub-subcategories

Personal identity and philosophy of mind are not merged with **Philosophy of Being & Reality** because they address specific, focused questions about the self and the mind that have generated their own distinct literatures. The puzzle cases of personal identity (teletransportation, brain transplants, fission and fusion) are specific to this sub-subcategory. The mind-body problem and the hard problem of consciousness are not subsumed under general ontology.

These topics are not merged with **Free Will & Determinism** because, while they interact (mind-body dualism makes room for free will, physicalism constrains it), the core questions are distinct. You can have a theory of personal identity without taking a position on free will.

## Essential Books

1. **An Essay Concerning Human Understanding, Book II, Chapter 27** by John Locke — The founding text of the personal identity debate; introduces the psychological continuity criterion.
2. **Reasons and Persons** by Derek Parfit — The most important work on personal identity since Locke; argues that identity is not what matters, survival is.
3. **Meditations on First Philosophy** by René Descartes — The founding text of the mind-body problem; argues for substance dualism.
4. **The Conscious Mind** by David Chalmers — The definitive statement of the hard problem of consciousness; defends property dualism.
5. **Minds, Brains, and Science** by John Searle — An accessible introduction to the philosophy of mind; includes the Chinese room argument.
6. **What Is It Like to Be a Bat?** by Thomas Nagel — A classic essay arguing that consciousness is irreducibly subjective and resists physicalist reduction.
7. **The Philosophy of Mind: Classical Problems/Contemporary Issues** edited by Brian Beakley and Peter Ludlow — A comprehensive anthology covering the major issues.
8. **The Case for Animalism** by Eric Olson — The definitive defense of the view that we are human animals.
9. **The Ego Tunnel** by Thomas Metzinger — A neuroscientifically-informed argument that the self is an illusion generated by the brain.
10. **Supersizing the Mind** by Andy Clark — The definitive statement of the extended mind hypothesis.

## Connections to Other Subcategories

Personal identity and philosophy of mind connect to **Free Will & Determinism** through the implications of physicalism for moral responsibility. They connect to **Philosophy of Being & Reality** through the ontology of persons and mental states. **Philosophy of Consciousness** explores the nature of phenomenal consciousness in depth. **Ethics & Moral Philosophy** engages personal identity for ethical theory. **Buddhist Philosophy** offers a rigorous no-self view and has entered dialogue with Western philosophy of mind. **Philosophy of Science** connects through the neuroscience of consciousness and AI.
