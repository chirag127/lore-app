# Philosophy of Being and Reality

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Philosophy of being and reality — historically called "first philosophy" or "ontology" — is the most fundamental branch of metaphysics. It asks what exists, what it means to exist, and into what categories existing things fall. Are there only material objects, or are there also abstract objects (numbers, properties, propositions)? What is a substance? What is the relationship between universals (properties like redness) and particulars (individual red objects)? What is the nature of time — does the past exist? the future? Is causation a real connection between events or a projection of our minds?

These questions are presupposed by every other branch of philosophy and every empirical science. Before you can ask what we know (epistemology), what we ought to do (ethics), or what the fundamental laws of physics are (physics), you need some account of what the world is like — what kinds of things it contains and how they hang together. Ontology also has practical implications: debates about the reality of race, gender, mental illness, and moral facts all turn on ontological questions about what kinds of things exist and whether they are "real" in the same sense as physical objects.

## What Belongs Here

- **Ontology**: The study of what exists; the debate between realists and nominalists about abstract objects
- **Substance**: Aristotelian substance (hypokeimenon), Cartesian substance, Spinoza's substance monism, Leibniz's monads
- **Universals and particulars**: The problem of universals, realism vs. nominalism (Plato, Aristotle, Ockham, Quine, Armstrong)
- **Particulars**: Bare particulars, substratum theory, bundle theory, trope theory
- **Properties**: Intrinsic vs. extrinsic, essential vs. accidental, categorical vs. dispositional
- **Relations**: Internal vs. external relations, the reality of relations (Bradley's regress)
- **Time and space**: A-theory vs. B-theory of time, eternalism vs. presentism, spacetime substantivalism vs. relationism
- **Causation**: Humean regularity, counterfactual theories (Lewis), causal powers, agent causation, the causal exclusion problem
- **Modality**: Possibility and necessity, possible worlds (Lewis's modal realism vs. actualism), essence and accident
- **The identity of indiscernibles**: The principle that no two distinct things can share all their properties (Leibniz)
- **Grounding and fundamentality**: The relationship between more fundamental and less fundamental reality; the debate about metaphysical foundationalism vs. infinitism

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- **Free will and determinism** — belongs in its own sub-subcategory
- **Personal identity and the self** — belongs in its own sub-subcategory
- **The philosophy of mind** (consciousness, intentionality) — belongs in Philosophy of Consciousness
- **Empirical physics** (what the universe is actually made of) — belongs in Physics
- **Philosophy of language** (meaning, reference, truth) — belongs in Language, Meaning & Truth
- **Ethics** (what we ought to do) — belongs in Ethics & Moral Philosophy

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Adjacent Sub-subcategories

Philosophy of being is not merged with **Free Will & Determinism** or **Personal Identity** because it addresses broader, more general questions about the structure of reality. The nature of causation, the reality of universals, and the structure of time are presupposed by the free will debate but are not identical to it. You can have a theory of universals or a theory of time without taking a position on free will. Merging them would lose this generality.

Philosophy of being is not merged with **Metaphysics** as a whole (which includes this topic) because the parent subcategory includes three distinct sub-subcategories — being, free will, and personal identity — each with its own literature and problems.

## Essential Books

1. **Being and Time** by Martin Heidegger (1927) — The phenomenological magnum opus that revived the question of Being through an analysis of Dasein, temporality, and authenticity; argues that Western metaphysics has forgotten the fundamental question of what it means to be.
2. **Categories** by Aristotle — The founding work of ontology; establishes the ten categories of being and the distinction between substance and accident.
2. **Metaphysics** by Aristotle — The most influential work of first philosophy; explores being qua being, substance, form and matter, potentiality and actuality.
3. **A Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge** by George Berkeley — The classic defense of idealism; esse est percipi.
4. **On the Plurality of Worlds** by David Lewis — The most systematic defense of modal realism; possible worlds, counterpart theory, and Humean supervenience.
5. **Ontological Relativity** by W.V.O. Quine — The classic essays on ontological commitment, the nature of existence, and the indeterminacy of reference.
6. **Word and Object** by W.V.O. Quine — Develops the criterion of ontological commitment ("to be is to be the value of a variable").
7. **Universals: An Opinionated Introduction** by D.M. Armstrong — A defense of realism about universals within a scientific naturalist framework.
8. **The Cement of the Universe** by J.L. Mackie — A study of causation; develops an account of causal regularity within an empiricist framework.
9. **The Possibility of Metaphysics** by E.J. Lowe — A defense of a neo-Aristotelian approach to ontology and metaphysics.
10. **Metametaphysics: New Essays on the Foundations of Ontology** edited by David Chalmers, David Manley, and Ryan Wasserman — The seminal collection on methodology in ontology; addresses the question of whether ontological disputes are substantive.

## Key Concepts

- Being qua being
- Substance (ousia)
- Universal vs. particular
- Realism vs. nominalism
- Essential vs. accidental properties
- Concrete vs. abstract objects
- Possible worlds
- A-theory vs. B-theory of time
- Eternalism vs. presentism
- Humean regularity theory of causation
- Counterfactual dependence
- Grounding and fundamentality
- Ontological commitment
- Mereology (the metaphysics of parts and wholes)

## Connections to Other Subcategories

Philosophy of being connects to **Free Will & Determinism** through the nature of causation and modality. It connects to **Personal Identity** through the nature of substances and the persistence conditions of objects. **Philosophy of Science** engages ontological commitment in scientific theories. **Philosophy of Language** connects through reference, meaning, and the relationship between language and reality. **Aesthetics** engages the ontological status of artworks. **Philosophy of Consciousness** connects through the mind-body problem and the ontology of mental states.
