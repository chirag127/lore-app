## Part I: The Critique of the Augustinian Picture

### §§1-20: The Augustinian Picture of Language

Wittgenstein opens with a quotation from Augustine's *Confessions*, which describes language learning as the process of associating words with objects. This "Augustinian picture" holds that every word names an object and that sentences are combinations of such names. Wittgenstein argues this is the root of most philosophical confusion about language.

He contrasts Augustine's picture with a simple language-game: a builder (A) calls out "slab!", "beam!" and an assistant (B) brings the corresponding building stone. The word "slab" does not name an object; it functions as an order within a specific activity. "The speaking of language is part of an activity, or of a form of life" (§23).

### §§21-38: Ostensive Definition and Its Limits

Pointing to an object does not settle the meaning of a word, because ostensive definition can be interpreted in multiple ways. Pointing to a pencil could define its shape, colour, material, or function — the gesture alone does not determine which. Understanding an ostensive definition presupposes a grasp of the role the word plays in the language.

Wittgenstein introduces the famous image: "Philosophical problems arise when language goes on holiday" (§38). When words are removed from their everyday contexts, philosophers project metaphysical theories onto them.

### §§39-64: Simples, Names, and Analysis

The *Tractatus* held that the world consists of simple objects named by logically proper names. Wittgenstein now dismantles this: what counts as "simple" depends on the context of inquiry. A chessboard is simple relative to a game of chess, but complex relative to its constituent squares. There is no absolute simplicity.

### §§65-71: Family Resemblances

The most influential methodological move of the book: the concept "game" cannot be defined by a set of necessary and sufficient conditions. Board games, card games, ball games, Olympic games — they share no single feature but a criss-crossing network of similarities: "family resemblances" (§67). The boundaries of concepts are not sharp, and demanding precise definitions where none exist is a philosophical error.

### §§72-88: The Quest for Essence

Philosophers seek the essence of language, the essence of the proposition, the essence of thought. Wittgenstein argues this quest rests on a misunderstanding. Language has no single essence; it is a motley of different functions. The proper task is not to find hidden essences but to assemble reminders of how words are actually used.

### §§89-133: The Nature of Philosophy

Wittgenstein presents his therapeutic conception of philosophy. Philosophy is not a body of doctrine but an activity: "A philosophical problem has the form: 'I don't know my way about'" (§123). The aim of philosophy is "to shew the fly the way out of the fly-bottle" (§309). Philosophers create their own puzzles by demanding explanations where only descriptions are needed. "We must do away with all *explanation*, and description alone must take its place" (§109).

### §§134-137: General Propositional Form

The *Tractatus* claimed that all propositions share a general form. Wittgenstein now rejects this: propositions are as various as the language-games they belong to. There is no single logical form underlying all meaningful discourse.

## Part II: Rules and Rule-Following

### §§138-184: Understanding and Meaning

What is it to understand a word? Wittgenstein attacks the idea that understanding is a mental process that accompanies utterance. Understanding a series like "add 2" is not a private mental act that occurs when the rule is grasped; it is shown in the ability to continue the series correctly. "Understanding is a state which is the source of the correct use" (§146), but this state is not a mental mechanism.

### §§185-201: The Rule-Following Paradox

Wittgenstein presents a student learning to extend a series (the famous "+2" example). The student writes 0, 2, 4, 6, 8... up to 1000, then continues 1004, 1008, 1012. When asked why he did not write 1000, 1002, 1004, he says he was following the rule "add 2" in the same way as before. Wittgenstein argues: "No course of action could be determined by a rule, because every course of action can be made to accord with the rule" (§201). This is the rule-following paradox.

The resolution is not a theory but a practice: following a rule is a custom, an institution, a practice. "To obey a rule, to make a report, to give an order, to play a game of chess, are *customs* (uses, institutions)" (§199). There is no fact of the matter that determines correctness independently of a community of practice.

### §§202-242: Agreement and Normativity

Agreement in judgments is not agreement in opinions but in form of life. "It is what human beings say that is true and false; and they agree in the *language* they use. That is not agreement in opinions but in form of life" (§241). Wittgenstein rejects both Platonism (rules as abstract objects) and mentalism (rules as psychological states), grounding normativity in shared practices.

## Part III: The Private Language Argument

### §§243-255: Can There Be a Private Language?

A private language would be one whose words "refer to what can only be known to the speaker; to his immediate private sensations" (§243). Wittgenstein argues such a language is impossible. The key claim: if a language is logically private — if its terms are defined solely by reference to inner experiences that no one else can access — then it lacks criteria of correctness, and without criteria, there is no distinction between using a word correctly and merely believing one is using it correctly.

### §§256-279: The Diary of Sensation S

Wittgenstein imagines someone keeping a diary of the recurrence of a sensation they label "S". Each time the sensation occurs, they make a mark. But how can they verify that the sensation is the same? They cannot appeal to an external standard; they can only consult their memory. This is like "buying several copies of the morning paper to assure yourself that what it said was true" (§265). Without public criteria, the distinction between correct and incorrect use collapses.

### §§280-307: The Beetle in the Box

The most famous thought experiment: "Suppose everyone had a box with something in it: we call it a 'beetle'. No one can look into anyone else's box, and everyone says he knows what a beetle is only by looking at his beetle." The point: if the word "beetle" functions in the language, the private object in the box "drops out of consideration as irrelevant" (§293). Our talk about sensations is not about private objects but is tied to public criteria of behaviour and circumstance.

### §§308-363: Thinking

Wittgenstein argues that thinking is not an inner process that accompanies speech. "Thinking is not an incorporeal process which lends life and sense to speaking, and which it would be possible to detach from speaking" (§339). To ask "what is thinking?" is to misunderstand the grammar of the word.

## Part IV: Mind, Self, and Sensation

### §§364-427: The Self and Consciousness

Wittgenstein attacks the Cartesian picture of the mind as an inner realm to which the subject has privileged access. The statement "I know that I am in pain" is nonsense — not because it is false, but because knowledge implies doubt, and I cannot doubt that I am in pain. "I know what I want, wish, believe, feel..." is philosophically misleading; these are not objects of knowledge but expressions of mental states.

### §§428-465: Meaning Something

What is it to mean something by a word? Wittgenstein argues against the idea that meaning is a mental act that accompanies speaking. "When I speak, there is not already a complete meaning in my mind; rather, meaning is something that unfolds in the use of language" (paraphrasing the drift of these sections). Meaning something is not an experience but a capacity exercised in use.

### §§466-570: The Will, Intention, and Action

Wittgenstein distinguishes between voluntary and involuntary action, arguing that the feeling of effort or volition is not the essence of willing. "The will is not a phenomenon, but the condition of phenomena" (paraphrase). Intention is not a mental state hidden behind the action; it is expressed in what we do and say.

### §§571-610: Sensations

Pain, tickling, and other sensations are not private objects. The language of sensation is learned through natural expressions of sensation (crying, wincing) that are then replaced by verbal reports. "A child has hurt himself and he cries; then adults talk to him and teach him exclamations and, later, sentences. They teach the child a new pain-behaviour" (§244).

### §§611-693: Mental Images and Processes

Wittgenstein examines imagination, mental images, and the experience of meaning. He argues that mental images are not pictures in the mind; imagining is not a form of seeing. The question of whether someone "really means" something cannot be settled by introspection.

## Part V: Philosophy of Psychology — A Fragment (formerly Part II)

In the fourth edition, Part II is retitled "Philosophy of Psychology — A Fragment". It consists of 14 sections exploring:

**Aspect Perception (Seeing-As):** The duck-rabbit figure shows that perception is not neutral registration. We can see the same figure as a duck or as a rabbit — a change in aspect that is neither a change in sensation nor an interpretation. This demonstrates the concept-ladenness of perception.

**Philosophy of Psychology:** Wittgenstein criticizes the idea that psychological concepts can be analysed by introspection. Concepts like "thinking", "believing", "wishing", and "intending" are used in public language-games with public criteria.

**Certainty:** He discusses the difference between knowledge and certainty, developing ideas that would lead to his final work, *On Certainty*.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment

This summary captures the major sections and arguments of the *Philosophical Investigations*, but the book's unique form — a sequence of aphoristic remarks that circle back on themselves — resists linear summary. The experience of reading Wittgenstein is essential to understanding his method. The summary accurately represents the philosophical content but cannot convey the rhetorical texture, the humour, the dialogical voice, or the sense of philosophical therapy in action.

### Recommended Reading Path

| Reader Type | Time | What to Read |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | ~20 min | This summary |
| Interested | ~3-5 hr | Summary + §§1-133, §§243-315, Part II |
| Scholar/Practitioner | ~30-50 hr | Full book + secondary literature (Hacker, McGinn, Stern) |

### Chapters to Read in Full

- **§§1-38** — The foundation of the entire work; critique of the Augustinian picture
- **§§65-71** — Family resemblances
- **§§89-133** — Wittgenstein's conception of philosophy
- **§§138-242** — Rule-following (the most discussed section in contemporary philosophy)
- **§§243-315** — Private language argument (the most famous part of the book)
- **Part II (all sections)** — Aspect perception and philosophy of psychology

### What You'll Miss by Not Reading the Full Book

The dialogical rhythm of Wittgenstein's prose — his questions to the reader, his imagined interlocutors, his sudden shifts of perspective. The experience of being led by analogy and example rather than by argument. The cumulative effect of seeing a philosophical problem dissolve rather than being solved.
