## 03 — Narration: A Conversation in Dialogue

### Musical Scoring for Audio

*Suggested voice: calm, measured, with a hint of dry Viennese irony. Pauses between remarks should feel like Wittgenstein thinking aloud.*

**Ambient:** A quiet Cambridge study, 1945. The sound of a fountain pen occasionally scratching, a coal fire shifting.

---

**[OPENING — 0:00-1:30]**

[Voice, quiet, intimate]

"This book is really only an album."

Ludwig Wittgenstein begins his second masterwork with an admission of failure. He says he tried to write a conventional book, with a continuous line of argument. He couldn't do it. The best he could manage was a sequence of sketches — philosophical landscapes seen during long journeyings.

This is the first key to reading him: he is not being modest, and he is not being evasive. He means it. The *Philosophical Investigations* is not a treatise. It is a therapy session for the intellect, and like a real therapy session, it works through indirection, through example, through showing rather than telling.

**[THE CENTRAL PICTURE — 1:30-4:00]**

[Voice, becoming more animated]

Augustine of Hippo, writing in the fourth century, described how he learned language: "I grasped that the thing was called by the sound they uttered when they meant to point it out."

This seems innocent enough. Words name objects. Sentences combine names. This has been the dominant picture of language in the Western tradition for two thousand years.

Wittgenstein says: it's all wrong.

[Pause]

Not false, exactly — pictures aren't true or false. But it holds us captive. It makes us ask: what is the meaning of a word? What is the essence of language? What is the nature of thought? And then, because the picture generates no answers, we invent metaphysical theories.

"The confusions which occupy us arise when language is like an engine idling, not when it is doing work."

[Humorous, slightly dry]

He offers an alternative picture: language as a toolbox. Words are not labels for things; they are tools with different functions. "Slab!" does not name a slab — it is an order in a building game. "Water!" is not a statement about H2O — it is a cry for help, or an exclamation of relief, or an instruction.

The meaning of a word is its use in the language.

**[THE FLY BOTTLE — 4:00-6:30]**

[Voice, shifting to an almost Socratic tone]

*Imagined interlocutor:* "But surely there must be *something* common to all games, otherwise we wouldn't call them all 'games'."

*Wittgenstein:* Look and see. Board games have winning and losing. Ball games have teams and scores. The Olympics has medals, opening ceremonies, torch relays. What does ring-around-the-rosie share with chess? What does solitaire share with football?

They share no single feature. They share a criss-crossing network of similarities — family resemblances.

[Pause, letting the idea settle]

This is not an idle observation about games. It is a method. When philosophers seek the essence of language, the essence of meaning, the essence of thought, Wittgenstein says: stop seeking essences. Look at how the word is used. Describe the language-game.

"Philosophy is a battle against the bewitchment of our intelligence by means of language."

The aim? "To shew the fly the way out of the fly-bottle."

**[THE RULE-FOLLOWING PARADOX — 6:30-9:30]**

[A slight shift — a more intense, interrogative tone]

Here is where it gets vertiginous.

A student learns to add 2. He writes:

0, 2, 4, 6, 8 ... 1000, 1004, 1008, 1012.

When asked why he continued incorrectly, he says: "But I am following the rule in the same way as before! The pattern 0, 2, 4, 6, 8... and then 1000, 1004, 1008... are both instances of adding 2, if you interpret 'add 2' in the right way."

[Pause]

"No course of action could be determined by a rule, because every course of action can be made to accord with the rule."

This is the rule-following paradox, and it has generated a flood of secondary literature second only to the private language argument. If rules cannot determine their own application, what does?

Wittgenstein's answer: following a rule is a *practice*. It is not a mental act, not a Platonic abstraction, but a custom embedded in a community. "To obey a rule, to make a report, to give an order, to play a game of chess, are *customs*."

[Slower, weightier]

This is not scepticism. It is an attempt to bring us back to earth. Meaning is not a mysterious relation between words and world. It is what we do, together, in forms of life.

**[THE PRIVATE LANGUAGE ARGUMENT — 9:30-13:00]**

[Voice becomes very deliberate]

"What would a language be whose words referred to what can only be known to the speaker — to his immediate private sensations?"

[Pause]

Imagine you have a recurring sensation — call it "S". You keep a diary, making a mark each time S occurs. The problem: how do you know that the sensation is the same each time? You can only consult your memory. But memory can be mistaken. There is no independent check.

This is like "buying several copies of the morning paper to assure yourself that what it said was true."

[The famous image, delivered slowly]

"Suppose everyone had a box with something in it: we call it a 'beetle'. No one can look into anyone else's box, and everyone says he knows what a beetle is only by looking at his beetle."

If the word "beetle" works in this language, what does it refer to? The box could be empty — the word would still function. The private object — whatever is in the box — drops out of consideration as irrelevant.

[Pause for effect]

This is the private language argument, and it annihilates the Cartesian picture of the mind. If a word that refers to a private sensation cannot function without public criteria for its correct use, then the whole idea of a private inner realm — the mind as a theatre of mental events to which only the subject has access — is an illusion.

"An 'inner process' stands in need of outward criteria."

**[THE DUCK-RABBIT — 13:00-14:30]**

[Lighter, almost playful]

You see a picture of a duck-rabbit — Jastrow's famous ambiguous figure. It is a duck. No — wait — it is a rabbit. The lines on the page have not changed. What has changed?

Not sensation — the visual input is identical. Not interpretation — you do not infer that it is a rabbit; you simply see it as a rabbit.

This is aspect perception, and Wittgenstein uses it to show that perception is concept-laden. There is no innocent eye. Seeing is not the passive reception of sense-data — it is an activity, inseparable from the concepts we bring to it.

**[CLOSING — 14:30-17:00]**

[Voice quietens, becomes reflective]

Wittgenstein died in 1951. The *Investigations* was published in 1953. He never saw it in print, never defended it, never answered his critics.

It is not an easy book. It is not meant to be. The difficulty is not obscurity but unfamiliar demand: Wittgenstein is asking us not to learn new facts but to change how we think. "The philosopher's treatment of a question is like the treatment of an illness."

[Tone: final, firm but gentle]

"Don't think, but look!"

[Beat]

That is the entire method of the book. Stop theorising. Look at how language works. Describe the language-game. That is all. That is enough.

"Philosophy simply puts everything before us, and neither explains nor deduces anything. — Since everything lies open to view, there is nothing to explain."

### Narration Notes

| Element | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Best voice | Mature, male, with a dry Viennese-accented English (reminiscent of Max von Sydow or Christoph Waltz in a contemplative register) |
| Reading speed | 130-150 words per minute — slower than the average audiobook, with deliberate pauses |
| Dialogue tone | Playful and deflationary when presenting imagined interlocutors; intense when presenting the paradoxes; gentle and wise in the therapeutic passages |
| Sound design | Minimal — the book is intimate; perhaps paper rustling, a clock ticking, coal shifting in a grate |
| Chapter breaks | Clearly indicated by silence, with a brief musical interlude (piano, single notes) before each major section |
| Effect | The reader should feel as though Wittgenstein is in the room, thinking aloud, trying to understand their confusion |

### Rhetorical Devices Used in the Text

| Device | Example |
|---|---|
| Question and answer | "But how does he know where to look? — He does not know; he just looks." |
| Imagined interlocutor | "But this is how it is — I say it is just like this!" |
| Sudden shift | From the sublime to the mundane — the jump from private language to the builder's game |
| Deflationary analogy | "Like buying several copies of the morning paper" |
| Aphorism | "The limits of my language mean the limits of my world" — repudiated, but the form survives |
| Weakening "or" | "He reads? — Or is it mechanical?" — forcing the reader to decide |
