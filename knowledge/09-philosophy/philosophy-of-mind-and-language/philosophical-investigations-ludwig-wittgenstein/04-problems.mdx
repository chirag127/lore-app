## Key Problem

The central problem of *Philosophical Investigations* is philosophical confusion itself — the tendency of philosophers to become entangled in conceptual knots by misusing language. This manifests as a series of interconnected pseudo-problems:

1. **The problem of meaning**: How do words connect to the world? The dominant tradition answers that meaning is a relation between words and objects (naming), or between words and mental representations (ideas). Wittgenstein argues both answers rest on the same mistaken picture.

2. **The problem of mental privacy**: How can we talk about inner experiences like pain, sensation, and thought? The Cartesian tradition holds that mental states are private objects accessible only to the subject. Wittgenstein argues this makes language about mental states systematically unmysterious when it should be impossible.

3. **The problem of rule-following**: What determines whether we have applied a rule correctly? Platonism says objective facts of meaning exist independently of human practices; mentalism says mental states determine correctness. Wittgenstein argues both fail to explain how norms actually function.

4. **The problem of philosophical methodology**: What is the proper method of philosophy? The tradition seeks theories — explanations of the nature of meaning, truth, knowledge, mind. Wittgenstein argues this quest is fundamentally misguided.

## Proposed Solution

Wittgenstein does not offer solutions in the traditional sense. His "solution" is therapeutic: he aims to dissolve problems rather than solve them.

**For meaning**: The Augustinian picture is replaced by the concept of language-games. Meaning is not a relation between word and object but a function within a practice. "The meaning of a word is its use in the language" (§43). This is not a theory of meaning but a reminder of what we already know.

**For mental privacy**: The private language argument shows that a language whose terms are defined solely by reference to private inner experiences is impossible. This dissolves the problem of other minds: we do not infer that others have minds from their behaviour; we see their behaviour as expressive of mental states. "An 'inner process' stands in need of outward criteria" (§580).

**For rule-following**: There is no fact of the matter that determines correct application of a rule independently of a community of practice. Rule-following is a custom, an institution. The apparent paradox dissolves when we stop looking for a metaphysical ground of normativity and recognise that "this is simply what I do" (§217) is the rock bottom of justification.

**For methodology**: Philosophy is descriptive, not explanatory. "We must do away with all *explanation*, and description alone must take its place" (§109). The aim is not to construct theories but to assemble reminders of how words are actually used.

## Evidence & Argumentation

Wittgenstein's argumentation is distinctive and controversial. He does not present deductive arguments with premises and conclusions. Instead, he:

1. **Presents a picture** that holds philosophers captive (the Augustinian picture, the picture of the mind as an inner realm).

2. **Shows the picture generating confusion** by examining how it leads to absurd or unanswerable questions.

3. **Offers an alternative description** of the relevant linguistic practice, showing that the problem dissolves when we look at how the words actually function.

4. **Uses thought experiments** (the builder's game, the +/-2 student, the beetle in the box, the diary of sensation S, the duck-rabbit) to shift perspective.

The evidence he appeals to is not empirical data but reminders of familiar linguistic practices. A standard move: "Consider how we teach a child the word 'pain'..." or "Think of how we determine whether someone has followed a rule..."

## Unresolved Questions

**1. The nature of the therapy**: If Wittgenstein's philosophy is therapeutic, what is the status of the *Investigations* itself? If it is not a theory, what is it? Critics like Russell and Gellner charged that Wittgenstein's anti-theoretical stance is self-refuting.

**2. The scope of the private language argument**: Is the argument against private language successful? Does it rule out only a logically private language (one whose terms are defined solely by inner ostension) or does it have broader implications for the possibility of subjective experience?

**3. The metaphysics of rule-following**: Kripke's interpretation raises the question of whether Wittgenstein is a meaning-sceptic. Even if Kripke is wrong, what positive account of normativity does Wittgenstein offer? Is "this is simply what I do" a satisfactory stopping point?

**4. The status of forms of life**: Are forms of life contingent features of particular cultures, or are they the universal preconditions of language? Does Wittgenstein's appeal to forms of life imply a form of cultural relativism?

**5. The relationship to the *Tractatus***: Did Wittgenstein repudiate everything he wrote in his early work, or does the *Investigations* complement the *Tractatus* by addressing a different set of problems?

## Criticisms

**Bertrand Russell** (1959): Russell dismissed the *Investigations* as vacuous. He accused Wittgenstein of abandoning the scientific approach to philosophy in favour of a linguistic quietism that could not address substantive philosophical questions about truth, reality, and knowledge.

**Ernest Gellner** (1959): Gellner's *Words and Things* argued that ordinary language philosophy — the movement inspired by Wittgenstein — was a form of linguistic conservatism that uncritically enshrined the conceptual scheme of a particular culture. He saw it as a retreat from serious philosophical engagement with science and rationality.

**Saul Kripke** (1982): Kripke's "sceptical paradox" interpretation has been enormously influential but widely rejected by scholars. Many argue Kripke attributes to Wittgenstein a radical scepticism that the *Investigations* is designed to overcome.

**Peter Hacker**: Hacker defends a "resolute" reading that rejects both Kripke's scepticism and postmodern appropriations. He argues that many contemporary interpretations over-systematize a work that deliberately resists systematisation.

**Hilary Putnam**: Putnam criticised the *Investigations* for a latent verificiationism about mental states, arguing that Wittgenstein sometimes lapses into the behaviourism he explicitly rejects.

**Noam Chomsky**: Chomsky's theory of universal grammar posits an innate language faculty that Wittgenstein's social conception of language cannot accommodate. Chomsky argues that language acquisition cannot be explained solely by exposure to public linguistic practices.

## Alternative Solutions

| Problem | Wittgenstein's Approach | Alternative Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Meaning as use — language-games | Formal semantics (Frege, Tarski, Montague): meaning as truth-conditions |
| Mind | Social/behavioural criteria — "an inner process stands in need of outward criteria" | Cartesian dualism (Descartes): mind as non-physical substance; computationalism (Fodor): mind as information processing |
| Rule-following | Practice-based — communal customs determine correctness | Platonism (Frege): rules as abstract objects; mentalism (sometimes attributed to Chomsky): rules as internalised representations |
| Methodology | Therapeutic description — "don't think, but look!" | Systematic theory-building (Aristotle, Kant, Carnap, Lewis) |
| Universality | Forms of life — shared human practices | Nativism (Chomsky, Fodor): universal grammar / language of thought |

## Practical Applications

Wittgenstein's philosophy has practical applications far beyond academic philosophy:

**Therapy and psychoanalysis**: Wittgenstein explicitly compared his method to psychoanalysis. His emphasis on public criteria for mental states has influenced therapeutic approaches that focus on language and social context rather than hidden inner dynamics.

**Education**: The idea that meaning is learned through participation in social practices rather than through definition and abstraction has influenced progressive education movements, particularly in language teaching.

**Artificial intelligence**: Wittgenstein's rule-following argument raises questions about whether machines can "mean" what they compute. John Searle's Chinese Room argument draws explicitly on Wittgensteinian themes to challenge strong AI.

**Dispute resolution**: The method of dissolving confused questions rather than answering them — showing that participants in a debate are talking past each other because they are operating with different language-games — has been applied to legal, political, and ethical disputes.

**Ethnography**: Clifford Geertz's concept of "thick description" — understanding cultural practices from the perspective of participants — is directly indebted to Wittgenstein's notion of forms of life.

## Open Questions

Can Wittgenstein's method be applied to questions he did not consider — such as the nature of consciousness, the foundations of mathematics, or the structure of moral reasoning? The *Investigations* has been invoked in all these domains, but whether its therapeutic approach is adequate to questions that require empirical investigation remains disputed.

Does Wittgenstein's later philosophy ultimately undermine itself? If philosophical problems are merely confusions to be dissolved, then the *Investigations* itself, as a philosophical work, would seem to be a candidate for dissolution. Wittgenstein was acutely aware of this — "My propositions are elucidatory in this way: he who understands me finally recognizes them as senseless" (Tractatus 6.54). Whether this is a paradox or a profound insight into the nature of philosophical reflection remains the deepest unresolved question of Wittgenstein's work.
