# Philosophy of Consciousness

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Consciousness is at once the most familiar and the most mysterious feature of our existence. Every conscious being knows what it is like to be conscious — the felt quality of experience, the "what it's like" of seeing red, tasting chocolate, or feeling pain. Yet consciousness resists explanation in physical terms. The "hard problem of consciousness" (Chalmers) is the problem of explaining why and how physical processes in the brain give rise to subjective experience at all. Unlike easy problems (explaining how the brain discriminates stimuli, integrates information, or reports mental states), the hard problem asks why any of this feels like something.

Philosophy of consciousness examines competing theories that try to solve or dissolve the hard problem. Dualism holds that consciousness is a non-physical phenomenon. Physicalism holds that consciousness is identical to or supervenes on brain states. Functionalism identifies mental states by their causal roles. Panpsychism holds that consciousness is a fundamental feature of reality. Eliminativism holds that consciousness as we conceive it does not exist. Each theory faces well-known objections, and the field remains one of the most active and contested in all of philosophy.

## What Belongs Here

- **The hard problem of consciousness** vs. the easy problems; what makes the hard problem hard
- **Qualia**: The phenomenal qualities of experience; the inverted spectrum argument, Mary's room (the knowledge argument), the explanatory gap
- **Theories of consciousness**: Dualism (Cartesian, property), physicalism (type identity, token identity, the causal theory), functionalism, representationalism, higher-order theories, global workspace theory, integrated information theory (IIT), panpsychism, idealism
- **The zombie argument**: Philosophical zombies who are physically identical but lack consciousness; the conceivability argument against physicalism
- **The knowledge argument**: Mary the color scientist knows all the physical facts about color but learns something new when she sees red
- **Intentionality**: The aboutness of mental states; the naturalization of intentionality, the tracking theory, teleosemantics
- **Self-consciousness**: The nature of self-awareness, the sense of agency, the minimal self
- **Animal and machine consciousness**: Which animals are conscious? Could AI be conscious? The ethical implications
- **Altered states of consciousness**: Dreaming, meditation, psychedelic experience; what they reveal about the nature of consciousness
- **The metaphysics of consciousness**: The place of consciousness in nature; consciousness and the physical world

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- **Neuroscientific data** (neural correlates of consciousness, brain imaging studies) — belongs in Neuroscience
- **Psychology of perception** (visual illusions, attention, working memory) — belongs in Cognitive Psychology
- **Meditation practice** (mindfulness, techniques) — belongs in Psychology or Religion
- **Personal identity** — belongs in its own sub-subcategory
- **Artificial intelligence engineering** (building AI systems) — belongs in Computer Science
- **Phenomenology as a philosophical method** — belongs in Phenomenology

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Adjacent Sub-subcategories

Philosophy of consciousness is not merged with **Language, Meaning & Truth** or **Philosophy of Perception** because it focuses on the core phenomenon of phenomenal consciousness — felt experience, qualia, the hard problem — which raises distinct questions not addressed in the philosophy of language or perception. The philosophy of language addresses meaning and reference; the philosophy of perception addresses the nature of perceptual experience. While they overlap with consciousness studies, each has its own canonical texts, debates, and problems.

Philosophy of consciousness is not merged with **Personal Identity & Philosophy of Mind** under Metaphysics because the consciousness debate has become a large, specialized field with its own theoretical options, experimental philosophy, and connections to neuroscience. The hard problem, the explanatory gap, and the zombie argument are specific to this literature.

## Essential Books

1. **The Conscious Mind** by David Chalmers — The most important book in contemporary philosophy of consciousness; introduces the hard problem and defends property dualism.
2. **What Is It Like to Be a Bat?** by Thomas Nagel — The classic essay that poses the problem of subjective experience for physicalism.
3. **Consciousness Explained** by Daniel Dennett — A polemical defense of functionalism and eliminativism about qualia; challenges the very idea of the hard problem.
4. **The Presence of Mind** by Daniel Dennett — Further develops Dennett's heterophenomenological method and the multiple drafts model of consciousness.
5. **A Materialist Theory of the Mind** by D.M. Armstrong — The classic defense of the central-state identity theory; consciousness as inner perception.
6. **The Phenomenology of Internal Time-Consciousness** by Edmund Husserl — The foundational work of phenomenological philosophy of consciousness.
7. **The Philosophy of Consciousness: The Complete Course** by John Searle — A defense of biological naturalism; consciousness as an emergent property of biological systems.
8. **Consciousness and the Brain** by Stanislas Dehaene — A scientific account of consciousness as global neuronal workspace; connects philosophy to empirical research.
9. **Panpsychism: Contemporary Perspectives** edited by Godehard Bruentrup and Ludwig Jaskolla — A comprehensive collection on panpsychism as a solution to the hard problem.
10. **The Character of Consciousness** by David Chalmers — A collection of essays developing the two-dimensional semantic framework for understanding consciousness.

## Connections to Other Subcategories

Philosophy of consciousness connects to **Philosophy of Perception** through the nature of perceptual experience. It connects to **Language, Meaning & Truth** through the relationship between thought and language and the expression of conscious states. **Personal Identity & Philosophy of Mind** explores the nature of the self and the mind-body problem. **Buddhist Philosophy** offers sophisticated accounts of consciousness without a self. **Chinese Philosophy** engages consciousness through Neo-Confucian and Chan/Zen traditions. **Philosophy of Science** connects through neuroscience and AI. **Ethics & Moral Philosophy** addresses the moral status of conscious beings.
