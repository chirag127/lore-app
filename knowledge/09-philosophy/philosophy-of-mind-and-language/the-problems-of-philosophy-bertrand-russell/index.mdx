Bertrand Russell's *The Problems of Philosophy* (1912) is the most widely read introduction to philosophy in the English language. Written as a "shilling shocker" for the Home University Library, it distills the central questions of epistemology and metaphysics into a clear, argument-driven text that has introduced generations of readers to philosophical thinking. The book opens with Russell's famous meditation on a table — a seemingly ordinary object that, under scrutiny, reveals the profound gap between appearance and reality.

<BookHeader />

Bertrand Arthur William Russell (1872–1970) was one of the most influential philosophers of the twentieth century. A co-author of *Principia Mathematica* with Alfred North Whitehead, he founded analytic philosophy alongside G. E. Moore and Gottlob Frege. He won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1950 "in recognition of his varied and significant writings in which he champions humanitarian ideals and freedom of thought." *The Problems of Philosophy*, though written for a general audience, advances original views on knowledge, truth, and the nature of reality that Russell would develop across his long career.
