*A Theory of Justice* is divided into three Parts (nine chapters total, 87 sections). The work is systematic: Part I lays out the theory, Part II applies it to institutions, and Part III connects justice with the good.

---

## Part One: Theory

### Chapter I — Justice as Fairness (sections 1-9)

Rawls begins with the bold claim that "justice is the first virtue of social institutions." In a just society, the basic structure — the way major social institutions distribute fundamental rights and duties and determine the division of advantages from social cooperation — must satisfy principles that free and rational persons would accept in an initial situation of equality.

The target is utilitarianism, then the reigning orthodoxy. Under utilitarianism, it is permissible to sacrifice the well-being of some for the greater good of others — for example, slavery could in principle be justified if it produced enough total happiness. Rawls's "justice as fairness" treats each person as inviolable: "Each person possesses an inviolability founded on justice that even the welfare of society as a whole cannot override."

Rawls introduces the **original position** as his device of representation. It is a hypothetical situation in which parties — stripped of knowledge of their race, class, gender, talents, and even their conception of the good (the **veil of ignorance**) — choose the principles of justice. Because no one knows how they will fare in the lottery of life, everyone will be cautious. Rawls argues they will apply the **maximin rule**: choose the principles that make the worst possible outcome as good as possible.

The chapter also contrasts justice as fairness with intuitionism (the view that there are multiple irreducible principles and no priority rules) and classical utilitarianism. Rawls argues that his theory solves the "priority problem" — it provides a determinate ordering of principles that intuitionism cannot.

### Chapter II — The Principles of Justice (sections 10-19)

Rawls states his famous two principles in their final formulation:

1. **Equal Liberty Principle**: Each person has an equal right to a fully adequate scheme of equal basic liberties compatible with a similar scheme for all.
2. **The Difference Principle** (with two parts):
   - (a) **Fair Equality of Opportunity**: Social and economic inequalities are to be attached to offices and positions open to all under conditions of fair equality of opportunity.
   - (b) **The Difference Principle**: Social and economic inequalities are to be to the greatest benefit of the least advantaged members of society.

The principles are **lexically ordered**: liberty cannot be traded for economic gain. Only after equal liberty is secured may considerations of fair opportunity and the difference principle apply.

Rawls discusses several interpretations of the second principle — the **system of natural liberty** (formal equality + laissez-faire), **liberal equality** (formal equality + fair opportunity), and **democratic equality** (the difference principle). He endorses democratic equality, arguing that the distribution of natural talents is a "common asset" and that the better-off may benefit from their talents only in ways that help the worse-off.

The chapter also covers the **principle of fairness** (obligations arise from accepting benefits of cooperative schemes), the **natural duties** (positive and negative duties that apply to all regardless of consent), and the argument that the two principles are more stable than utilitarianism.

### Chapter III — The Original Position (sections 20-30)

This is the heart of the argument for the two principles. The original position models the conditions under which fair agreement is possible. Rawls specifies the **circumstances of justice** — moderate scarcity, conflicting claims, and mutual disinterest — and argues that the veil of ignorance ensures impartiality.

The parties are rational (they want to maximize their share of primary goods) but not envious. They know general facts about society — economics, psychology, political sociology — but nothing about their own place in it. Rawls argues that under these conditions, the parties would reject utilitarianism (because it might demand terrible sacrifices from them) and choose the two principles instead.

The **maximin argument** is crucial: because the parties cannot calculate probabilities (they don't know the odds of being born rich or poor, talented or disabled), they will act conservatively. They will choose the scheme that makes the worst position as good as possible. The two principles, Rawls claims, would be chosen because they guarantee equal liberty and ensure that even the least advantaged share in economic growth.

## Part Two: Institutions

### Chapter IV — Equal Liberty (sections 31-40)

Rawls applies the first principle to the constitutional and political process. He introduces the **four-stage sequence**: (1) choosing principles in the original position, (2) adopting a constitution, (3) passing laws, (4) applying rules to particular cases. At each stage, the veil of ignorance is progressively lifted.

Equal liberty includes: political liberty (the right to vote and hold office), freedom of speech and assembly, liberty of conscience, freedom of thought, freedom of the person (including the right to hold personal property), and freedom from arbitrary arrest. Rawls provides a detailed defense of **equal liberty of conscience** and **toleration**, arguing that parties in the original position would not risk persecution.

The **priority of liberty** means that liberty can only be restricted for the sake of liberty itself — never for economic efficiency. A less extensive liberty must strengthen the total system of liberty; a less equal liberty must be acceptable to those with lesser liberty.

The chapter ends with the **Kantian interpretation**: justice as fairness expresses the Kantian idea that persons are autonomous — they give themselves the law rather than being subject to external contingencies. The original position makes this ideal operational.

### Chapter V — Distributive Shares (sections 41-50)

This chapter applies the second principle to economic institutions. Rawls does not endorse any particular economic system (capitalism or socialism) but argues for a **property-owning democracy** or a **liberal socialist regime** — both of which widely disperse capital and prevent concentrations of wealth that would undermine fair value of political liberty.

Key concepts:
- **Pure procedural justice**: If the background institutions are just, any resulting distribution is just (regardless of desert). Rawls rejects the idea that distributive shares should track moral desert, since natural talents are arbitrary from a moral point of view.
- **Legitimate expectations**: Entitlements arise from the rules of a just basic structure, not from pre-institutional moral desert.
- **The role of the difference principle**: It functions as a **principle of fraternity**, ensuring that the better-off do not benefit unless the worse-off benefit too.

Rawls contrasts his view with the **principle of perfection** (the idea that society should maximize the achievement of excellence) and with other mixed conceptions.

### Chapter VI — Duty and Obligation (sections 51-59)

Rawls distinguishes **natural duties** (which bind all persons regardless of consent — e.g., the duty to help others in need, to support just institutions, to not injure the innocent) from **obligations** (which arise from voluntary acts — e.g., promises, accepting benefits). The **principle of fairness** generates obligations: those who voluntarily accept the benefits of a cooperative scheme have an obligation to do their share.

The chapter also addresses **civil disobedience**. Rawls defines it as a public, nonviolent, conscientious breach of law undertaken with the aim of bringing about a change in law or policy. For a nearly just society, civil disobedience is justified when (1) it addresses a substantial and clear injustice, (2) it has exhausted normal legal remedies, and (3) it does not threaten the fabric of the constitution. These conditions locate civil disobedience as a stabilizing force in a democratic society.

## Part Three: Ends

### Chapter VII — Goodness as Rationality (sections 60-68)

Rawls develops a **thin theory of the good** — the good is the satisfaction of rational desire. This is "thin" because it avoids controversial metaphysical commitments, making it acceptable across a wide range of comprehensive doctrines.

He defines **primary goods** as things every rational person wants: rights and liberties, opportunities, income and wealth, and the social bases of self-respect. These provide a public metric for comparing citizens' shares without invoking any particular conception of the good life. The list of primary goods is determined by what persons need as free and equal citizens pursuing their own ends.

Rawls also discusses the **Aristotelian Principle**: other things equal, human beings enjoy the exercise of their realized capacities, and this enjoyment increases the more the capacity is realized or the greater its complexity. This principle explains why we value meaningful work and varied activities, though Rawls acknowledges it is an empirical generalization.

### Chapter VIII — The Sense of Justice (sections 69-77)

A just society must be **stable**: its members must develop a reliable sense of justice that leads them to comply with its principles. Rawls draws on developmental psychology (Piaget, Kohlberg) to argue that persons in a well-ordered society naturally acquire a sense of justice through three stages: the **morality of authority**, the **morality of association**, and the **morality of principles**.

The **congruence argument** claims that in a well-ordered society, a person's good (their rational plan of life) and their sense of justice will coincide. Acting justly is part of the good for members of such a society. This is Rawls's response to Glaucon's challenge in Plato's *Republic*: it is better to be a just person than an unjust one, not because of consequences, but because justice and the good harmonize.

### Chapter IX — The Good of Justice (sections 78-87)

The final chapter ties the theory together. The **idea of social union** shows that a well-ordered society is itself a form of social union — a community in which citizens share final ends and recognize their mutual good. This addresses the objection that liberalism is atomistic: Rawls argues that participation in just institutions enables forms of human flourishing impossible in isolation.

**Happiness and dominant ends**: Rawls rejects hedonism and the idea of a single dominant end. A rational plan of life balances multiple aims. The **unity of the self** is achieved not by subordinating all aims to one, but by integrating them through a rational plan informed by the principles of right.

The book concludes with a reflection on justification. Rawls denies that any philosophical theory can be proven from self-evident premises. Instead, justification is a matter of **reflective equilibrium** — the mutual adjustment of general principles and particular judgments until they cohere. The theory is justified to the extent that it fits our considered convictions about justice, once we have examined them in the light of the original position.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment

This summary captures the core architecture of the book — the original position, the two principles, the four-stage sequence, the sense of justice, and reflective equilibrium. It covers all nine chapters and major sections. What it necessarily omits: the subtlety of Rawls's arguments against utilitarianism, the formal reasoning in sections 20-30, the detailed discussion of envy, the treatment of intergenerational justice (a surprisingly brief section), and Rawls's complex engagement with Kant.

### Recommended Reading Path

| Reader Type | Time | What to Read |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | ~30 min | This summary + IEP/Stanford Encyclopedia entry |
| Interested | ~8-10 hr | Summary + Chapters I, II, III, IV, IX in full |
| Scholar/Practitioner | ~30-40 hr | Full book + *Political Liberalism* + a secondary reader |

### Chapters to Read in Full

- **Chapter I** — Sets up the entire project; essential for the motivation against utilitarianism
- **Chapter III** — The original position argument; the most philosophically rigorous section
- **Chapter IV** — The application to liberty; crucial for understanding Rawls's political commitments

### Chapters to Skim or Skip

- **Chapter VI** — Civil disobedience discussion is influential but narrower than the rest
- **Chapter VII** — Dense discussion of goodness as rationality; important but technical

### What You'll Miss by Not Reading the Full Book

The full book's power lies in its systematic interconnection. The argument for the difference principle cannot be fully grasped without the original position, and the original position's plausibility depends on the theory of the good and the sense of justice. Rawls's writing is measured, careful, and cumulative — reading selected chapters loses the architectonic quality that makes the theory compelling.
