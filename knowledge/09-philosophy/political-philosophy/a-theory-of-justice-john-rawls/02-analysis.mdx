## Book Context & Background

When *A Theory of Justice* appeared in 1971, Anglo-American political philosophy was dominated by two approaches. Utilitarianism — in the tradition of Bentham, Mill, and Sidgwick — held that the just society maximizes aggregate welfare. Intuitionism — represented by W.D. Ross and others — held that moral principles are self-evident and cannot be systematically ordered. Both approaches were increasingly felt to be inadequate. The civil rights movement, the Vietnam War, and the growing resistance to the welfare state's compromises created demand for a rigorous theory of justice that could defend liberal egalitarian principles. Rawls, who had been working on "justice as fairness" since the 1950s, provided exactly that: a systematic alternative that drew on the social contract tradition (Locke, Rousseau, Kant) but gave it a modern, analytic form.

## About the Author

John Rawls (1921-2002) was born in Baltimore, Maryland. He studied at Princeton (BA 1943, PhD 1950), served as an infantryman in the Pacific during World War II (an experience that led him to abandon the Episcopalian faith of his youth), and taught at Princeton, Cornell, and MIT before joining Harvard's philosophy department in 1962, where he remained for the rest of his career. His philosophical development was shaped by Wittgensteinian linguistic philosophy (through his mentor Norman Malcolm) and by a deep engagement with Kant. Before *A Theory of Justice*, Rawls had published a series of influential articles — "Justice as Fairness" (1958), "The Sense of Justice" (1963), and "Distributive Justice" (1967) — that sketched the theory he would elaborate in the book. After 1971, Rawls continued to refine and qualify his views, producing *Political Liberalism* (1993), *The Law of Peoples* (1999), and *Justice as Fairness: A Restatement* (2001). He received the National Humanities Medal in 1999.

## Core Thesis & Argument

The core thesis: the basic structure of a just society must satisfy principles that free and equal persons would choose behind a veil of ignorance — principles that secure equal basic liberties, fair equality of opportunity, and a distribution of economic advantages that maximally benefits the least well-off. The argument proceeds in three moves: (1) the original position models fair conditions for choosing principles; (2) the two principles are the unique solution to the choice problem under those conditions; (3) these principles generate a stable, well-ordered society because they cohere with our best considered judgments about justice.

## Thematic Analysis

**Liberty and equality**: Rawls's most striking claim is that liberty and equality are not in tension but mutually supporting. The priority of liberty rules out trading basic freedoms for economic gain; the difference principle ensures that economic inequalities serve everyone, including the worst-off.

**Fairness and the social contract**: Rawls revives the idea of a hypothetical contract but uses it not to justify a sovereign's power (as in Hobbes) but to model the conditions of impartial choice. The veil of ignorance ensures that no one can tailor principles to their own advantage, making the resulting agreement genuinely fair.

**The inviolability of the person**: Rawls's sharpest disagreement with utilitarianism is his insistence that each person possesses an inviolability that even the general welfare cannot override. The lexical priority of the first principle (liberty) over the second (distribution) is the operational expression of this commitment.

**Community and individuality**: In Part III, Rawls argues that justice as fairness can account for community through the idea of social union — citizens share a common commitment to just institutions and develop their individual talents within a cooperative framework. This is Rawls's answer to the charge that liberalism is atomistic.

## Argumentation & Evidence

Rawls uses a distinctive method: **reflective equilibrium**. He does not claim to deduce the two principles from self-evident axioms. Instead, he starts from "considered judgments" — the fixed points of our moral consciousness (e.g., that religious intolerance is unjust, that slavery is wrong) — and constructs a theoretical framework (the original position) that systematizes and explains them. The justification is circular in the best sense: the theory is convincing to the extent that, after reflection, it fits with and illuminates our moral convictions. This method is both Rawls's greatest methodological innovation and a target for criticism: it seems to rely on the particular moral intuitions of a liberal academic, and it is unclear whether the process can reach a unique equilibrium.

## Strengths

- **Systematic power**: Rawls provides a single, coherent framework for thinking about liberty, opportunity, and distribution — questions that had been treated separately or by different disciplines.
- **Revival of normative political philosophy**: Rawls showed that analytic philosophy could engage substantively with moral and political questions, ending the dominance of meta-ethics and linguistic analysis.
- **The original position**: A genuinely ingenious device that crystallizes the demand for impartiality. It is flexible enough to generate different principles under different specifications, making it a tool for ongoing debate, not just a one-time argument.
- **The difference principle**: A unique and powerful principle that gives precise content to the idea that a just society does not leave anyone behind. Its implications — that talents are a "common asset" and that the successful owe their success — have generated entire literatures.
- **Institutional focus**: Rawls insists that justice applies to the "basic structure" — the background framework of law, property, and politics — not directly to individual transactions. This allows him to avoid the naive anti-capitalism of some egalitarians while still demanding significant redistribution.

## Criticisms & Weaknesses

**Robert Nozick's libertarian critique** (in *Anarchy, State, and Utopia*, 1974): Nozick argues that the difference principle requires a "patterned" distribution that violates individuals' property rights. If people freely exchange goods, any resulting distribution — however unequal — is just, as long as it arose from legitimate initial acquisitions and voluntary transfers. For Nozick, Rawls's principles require constant state interference to maintain the pattern. Nozick also objects that the difference principle unfairly advantages the less talented against the more talented by treating natural abilities as "common assets." Rawls's response would be that natural talents are arbitrary from a moral point of view, so the distribution they produce without regulation is not automatically just.

**Michael Sandel's communitarian critique** (in *Liberalism and the Limits of Justice*, 1982): Sandel argues that Rawls's conception of the person — the "unencumbered self" who can step back from all attachments to choose principles — is metaphysically and psychologically implausible. Real persons are constituted by their communities, traditions, and relationships. The veil of ignorance strips away precisely what makes us who we are. Sandel also contends that Rawls's priority of the right over the good cannot generate a sufficiently rich sense of community. Richard Rorty praised Sandel's book as "clear and forceful," calling it one of the most important critiques of Rawls.

**Amartya Sen's capabilities critique** (in *The Idea of Justice*, 2009): Sen argues that Rawls commits to "transcendental institutionalism" — the search for perfectly just institutions — when what we need is a comparative framework for ranking actual states of affairs. Rawls's focus on primary goods as the metric of advantage is also problematic: people differ in their ability to convert resources into valuable functionings. A disabled person may need more resources to achieve the same functioning as an able-bodied person. Sen proposes the **capabilities approach** as an alternative: justice should be assessed by what people are actually able to do and be, not by what resources they possess.

**Martha Nussbaum** (in *Women and Human Development*, 2000, and *Frontiers of Justice*, 2006) extends Sen's critique, arguing that Rawls's theory cannot adequately handle justice for people with disabilities, for non-human animals, and for global justice. Nussbaum argues that the social contract model, with its assumption of rough equality and mutual advantage, excludes those who cannot participate as "fully cooperating members of society over a complete life."

**Iris Marion Young** (in *Justice and the Politics of Difference*, 1990) argues that Rawls's focus on distribution obscures other dimensions of justice, particularly domination, oppression, and decision-making power. For Young, justice is not primarily about who gets what but about the institutional conditions for self-development and self-determination. She also charges that Rawls's ideal of impartiality masks the perspectives of marginalized groups.

**G.A. Cohen** (in *Rescuing Justice and Equality*, 2008) mounts a left-wing critique: the difference principle is too permissive. Rawls allows incentives for the talented (high salaries for doctors to attract them to underserved areas) as compatible with the difference principle, but Cohen argues that if the talented were genuinely committed to justice, they would work just as hard without such incentives. The difference principle, on Cohen's reading, justifies the very inequalities it claims to regulate by building in concessions to self-interest.

## Comparative Analysis

Rawls's theory is best understood as the third great social contract theory, after Hobbes and Rousseau. Unlike Hobbes, who builds from fear and self-preservation, Rawls builds from a Kantian conception of free and equal persons. Unlike Rousseau's general will, Rawls's original position yields determinate principles lexically ordered. The most important comparative relationships are:

- **vs. Hobbes**: For Hobbes, justice is what the sovereign commands; for Rawls, justice is what free and equal persons would agree to under fair conditions.
- **vs. Kant**: Rawls's original position is, in his own words, a "procedural interpretation" of Kant's categorical imperative. But Rawls avoids Kant's metaphysical commitments (the noumenal self, the postulate of freedom).
- **vs. Utilitarianism**: This is Rawls's primary antagonist. Utilitarianism permits sacrificing individuals for the greater good; Rawls's theory forbids it.
- **vs. Nozick**: The debate between patterned (Rawls) and historical (Nozick) justice defines the central cleavage in late twentieth-century Anglo-American political philosophy.
- **vs. Walzer's *Spheres of Justice***: Walzer agrees with Rawls that justice is social, but he is a pluralist: different goods (medical care, education, political office) should be distributed according to different principles, not a single formula.

## Impact & Legacy

*A Theory of Justice* was immediately recognized as a landmark. The *New York Times Book Review* called it "an event of great importance." Within a decade, it had generated a secondary literature of hundreds of articles and books. It revived normative political philosophy as a serious academic enterprise. Its concepts — the original position, the veil of ignorance, the difference principle, reflective equilibrium, primary goods — are now standard tools in moral and political philosophy.

Rawls's later work, especially *Political Liberalism* (1993), responded to critics by reframing justice as fairness as a "political, not metaphysical" doctrine suitable for a pluralistic society. The 1999 revised edition of *A Theory of Justice* incorporated responses to some criticisms. The book remains foundational: every serious work of political philosophy written since 1971 defines itself in relation to Rawls.
