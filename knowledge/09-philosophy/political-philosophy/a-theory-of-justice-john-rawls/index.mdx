## Overview

John Rawls's *A Theory of Justice* (1971) is the single most important work of political philosophy published in the twentieth century. It single-handedly revived the social contract tradition — dormant since Kant — and displaced utilitarianism as the dominant framework for thinking about social justice. Its central idea, "justice as fairness," proposes that the principles governing the basic structure of society are those that free and equal persons would choose in an "original position" behind a "veil of ignorance," where no one knows their social status, natural talents, or conception of the good. From this device, Rawls derives two principles: equal basic liberties for all, and a requirement that social and economic inequalities both attach to offices open under fair equality of opportunity and work to the greatest benefit of the least advantaged (the difference principle). The book generated an extraordinary wave of commentary — from libertarians like Nozick, communitarians like Sandel, egalitarians like Sen, and feminists like Young — and reshaped the entire field of political philosophy.

## Why This Book Matters

Before Rawls, Anglo-American political philosophy was dominated by utilitarianism (maximize aggregate happiness) and intuitionism (a plurality of self-evident principles with no ordering). Rawls offered a systematic, constructivist alternative grounded in the Kantian idea that persons are free and equal. His theory provided a principled defense of the welfare state, of civil liberties, and of a robust public commitment to equality of opportunity — all from a single, elegant contractualist framework. The book has been translated into 27 languages, cited tens of thousands of times, and is required reading in philosophy, political science, law, and economics. Whether one accepts or rejects his conclusions, every serious contemporary theory of justice defines itself in relation to Rawls.
