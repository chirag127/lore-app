# Complete Summary of Leviathan

## Part I: Of Man (Chapters 1–16)

### Chapters 1–3: Sense, Imagination, and the Train of Thoughts

Hobbes begins with a thoroughly materialist account of human psychology. All thought originates in **sense** — the motion of external objects pressing on our organs, transmitted through the nerves to the brain and heart. Imagination is "decaying sense"; memory is a storehouse of such decaying images. The "train of thoughts" is either unguided (as in dreams) or regulated by a desire for some end. This mechanical model of mind — man as matter in motion — sets the stage for everything that follows: there is no immaterial soul, no innate ideas, no transcendent source of moral knowledge. We are sophisticated machines driven by appetite and aversion.

### Chapters 4–5: Speech, Reason, and Science

Speech is the invention that sets humans apart. Words are "marks" for recalling thoughts and "signs" for communicating them. But words are also sources of confusion when used imprecisely. Hobbes launches a blistering attack on the "insignificant speech" of the Schoolmen — terms like "incorporeal substance" are for him contradictions in terms. Reason is nothing but "reckoning" — adding and subtracting the consequences of names. Science is the knowledge of these consequences, built from definitions. True philosophy proceeds by the resolutive-compositive method: breaking phenomena into their components and then reconstructing them.

### Chapter 6: The Passions

All voluntary motion is caused by a passion — either **appetite** (motion toward something) or **aversion** (motion away). What we desire we call "good"; what we avoid we call "evil." There is no objective good, only what each person's appetite drives them toward. Hobbes provides an exhaustive taxonomy of the passions: hope, fear, courage, anger, emulation, envy, and dozens more. The key passions for his political theory are **fear of violent death** (the strongest and most reliable passion) and **desire for power** (a restless pursuit that ends only in death).

### Chapters 7–9: Ends of Discourse and Intellectual Virtues

Discourse ends either in belief (opinion) or knowledge (science). Intellectual virtues are either natural (wit, imagination) or acquired (prudence from experience, sapience from science). Hobbes distinguishes two kinds of knowledge: knowledge of fact (history, memory) and knowledge of the consequence of affirmations (philosophy, science).

### Chapter 10: Power, Worth, Dignity, Honour

Power is the means to obtain some future apparent good. Natural power derives from physical or mental gifts; instrumental power comes from wealth, reputation, friends. The value of a person — their "worth" — is simply the price others will pay for their power. Honour is the recognition of another's power. This reduction of social value to market value is one of the most striking features of Hobbes's anthropology.

### Chapter 11: Difference of Manners

"Manners" here means the qualities of human behaviour that lead to peace or war. The restless desire for power "ceaseth only in death" — not because people seek unlimited power for its own sake, but because they cannot secure their current power without acquiring more. The primary causes of quarrel are **competition** (for gain), **diffidence** (for safety), and **glory** (for reputation). These three drives make the state of nature a state of war.

### Chapter 12: Of Religion

Hobbes offers a naturalistic account of religion. Anxiety about the future, ignorance of natural causes, and the desire to know one's fate lead humans to invent invisible powers. In monotheistic religions, these fears are organised into worship of one God. But the "seed of religion" is always fear — and fear can be exploited by those who claim to know God's will.

### Chapter 13: Of the Natural Condition of Mankind

This is the most famous chapter in the book. Hobbes describes the **state of nature**: a condition of equality (roughly equal physical and mental abilities), scarcity, and uncertainty. Given the three causes of quarrel, every man is potentially every other man's enemy. Without a common power to keep them in awe, people live in a state of war — "such a war as is of every man against every man." In this condition, there is no industry, no agriculture, no navigation, no arts, no letters, no society, and "the life of man, solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short."

The "laws of nature" — rational precepts that forbid doing what destroys life — are discernible by reason. The fundamental law is **to seek peace and follow it**. The second law is to be willing to lay down one's right to all things when others are similarly willing — that is, to make a contract.

### Chapters 14–15: The Laws of Nature

Hobbes enumerates nineteen laws of nature, including: keep your covenants (the third law); show gratitude; be accommodating; forgive; avoid cruelty; recognise natural equality; agree to arbitration; and more. These are not laws in the strict sense — there is no sovereign to enforce them — but theorems of reason concerning what conduces to preservation. In the state of nature they bind only *in foro interno* (in conscience), not *in foro externo* (in action), because complying while others do not is suicidal.

The crucial point: the laws of nature are not self-enforcing. Without a common power, the rational agent will break them whenever survival seems to require it. Hence the need for the commonwealth.

### Chapter 16: Persons, Authors, and Personated Things

Hobbes introduces the concept of **personation** — the idea that one person can represent the actions of another. When we authorise someone to act in our name, we own their actions as our own. This concept is the bridge from natural law to the artificial person of the commonwealth.

## Part II: Of Commonwealth (Chapters 17–31)

### Chapter 17: Causes, Generation, and Definition of a Commonwealth

The only way to escape the state of nature is to erect a common power: a **commonwealth**. This is achieved when everyone agrees to transfer their right of self-government to a single person or assembly, on condition that all others do the same. The multitude becomes one person — the **Leviathan** — an artificial man whose body is composed of individual citizens, whose soul is the sovereign, whose laws are its will, and whose end is the peace and safety of the people.

### Chapters 18–19: Rights of Sovereigns and Types of Commonwealth

The sovereign by institution has thirteen rights: subjects cannot change the form of government; the sovereign cannot breach the covenant (because he is not party to it); the minority must acquiesce to the majority's choice; the sovereign cannot be punished by subjects; he judges what doctrines are conducive to peace; he makes civil law; he is final judge in all disputes; he makes war and peace; he appoints counsellors and magistrates; he rewards and punishes. These rights are **indivisible** and **inalienable** — to divide sovereignty is to destroy it.

There are three forms of commonwealth: **monarchy** (one rules), **aristocracy** (a part rules), and **democracy** (all rule). Tyranny and oligarchy are not distinct forms but names given to monarchy and aristocracy by those who dislike them. Monarchy is best because in it the public and private interest coincide most closely: the monarch's wealth and power depend on the strength of the commonwealth.

### Chapter 20: Paternal and Despotic Dominion

Commonwealths are either by **institution** (people voluntarily contract) or by **acquisition** (conquest). Hobbes controversially argues that a commonwealth by acquisition is essentially the same as one by institution — the subject's obligation arises from consent (fear of death), not from the justice of the conqueror's cause.

### Chapter 21: The Liberty of Subjects

Hobbes defines liberty as the absence of external impediments to motion. The liberty of subjects is not freedom from the law, but freedom to do what the law is silent upon — to buy, sell, choose one's abode, etc. The fear of punishment does not negate liberty; it merely influences the will. True liberty in the commonwealth is the silence of the law. Hobbes explicitly denies that republican "liberty" — freedom from domination — is distinct from this negative conception.

### Chapters 22–25: Systems, Public Ministers, Counsel

Hobbes discusses subordinate groups within the commonwealth: families, corporations, universities. All are subject to sovereign control. **Public ministers** are those the sovereign employs to exercise authority. **Counsel** must be distinguished from **command** — counsel aims at the benefit of the receiver, command at the benefit of the commander.

### Chapter 26: Civil Laws

Law is the command of the sovereign, addressed to those who are obliged to obey. Custom is law only because the sovereign permits it. Natural law and civil law are not distinct kinds — the sovereign incorporates natural law into civil law. The **interpretation of law** belongs to the sovereign (through his judges), not to private persons or scholarly commentators.

### Chapters 27–28: Crimes, Excuses, Punishments

A crime is a sin consisting in both a law and a violation. Ignorance of the law is no excuse (the law is publicly declared), but ignorance of fact may be. **Punishment** is an evil inflicted by public authority on a transgressor, to deter others. Hobbes rejects punishments that serve no deterrent purpose: revenge, gratuitous cruelty, punishment of the innocent.

### Chapter 29: Things That Weaken or Dissolve a Commonwealth

Internal dissolution is the greater threat. Hobbes lists the "poisons" that destroy commonwealths: the opinion that private judgment of good and evil is legitimate; that subjects may resist the sovereign; that tyrannicide is lawful; that sovereignty is divisible; that faith and civil obedience are separate. The **English Civil War** is the implicit case study throughout.

### Chapter 30: Office of the Sovereign Representative

The sovereign's duty is to procure the safety of the people — not mere survival but a "contented life." This includes: good laws (frequent but not arbitrary), equitable taxation, public instruction in the grounds of obedience (sovereigns should teach their own doctrine in universities and churches), prevention of idleness, encouragement of industry, and equal justice.

### Chapter 31: The Kingdom of God by Nature

God's natural kingdom is founded on his irresistible power, not on contract. The laws of God's natural kingdom are the same as the laws of nature. This chapter transitions to Part III by showing that natural reason alone cannot settle religious disputes — scripture must be interpreted by the sovereign.

## Part III: Of a Christian Commonwealth (Chapters 32–43)

### Chapters 32–33: Principles of Christian Politics

Hobbes argues that scripture must be interpreted by the sovereign, not by private individuals or church authorities. He distinguishes necessary articles of faith (Jesus is the Christ) from non-essential doctrines. The authority of scripture derives from the civil sovereign who authorises it, not from the church.

### Chapters 34–36: Biblical Interpretation

Hobbes provides materialist readings of key biblical concepts. The Kingdom of God is a literal, earthly kingdom, not a heavenly one. Angels are corporeal beings. The Holy Spirit is not a person but a power. Miracles have ceased. These interpretations systematically undermine the independent authority of the church.

### Chapters 37–40: The Kingdom of God and Ecclesiastical Power

The Kingdom of God was a temporal kingdom established with the Israelites at Sinai, administered by priests and later by kings. With the rejection of Christ, this kingdom was suspended until his return. In the meantime, civil sovereigns — whether Christian or not — have full authority over ecclesiastical matters within their territories.

### Chapters 41–43: Salvation, Baptism, and the Power of the Church

Salvation requires only faith that Jesus is the Christ — not adherence to particular doctrines imposed by priests. Baptism and the sacraments are public testimonies of faith, not mechanisms of grace. The pope has no authority outside his own civil domain. The church is a "company of men professing Christian faith" united under a sovereign — not a transnational corporation with legislative power.

This section is Hobbes's most direct intervention in the religious conflicts of his time. By subordinating ecclesiastical to civil authority, he aims to eliminate the jurisdictional dualism that had fuelled a century of religious war.

## Part IV: Of the Kingdom of Darkness (Chapters 44–47)

### Chapter 44: Spiritual Darkness from Misinterpretation of Scripture

The Kingdom of Darkness is not Hell but a "confederacy of deceivers" who have obscured true religion for worldly power. Hobbes identifies four causes: (1) extinguishing natural reason through false philosophy; (2) demonology — teaching that spirits are incorporeal; (3) mixing religion with vain philosophy; (4) false traditions about the afterlife.

### Chapter 45: Demonology and Other Relics of Paganism

The Catholic doctrine of incorporeal spirits is a survival of pagan demonology. Hobbes attacks the doctrine of purgatory, the invocation of saints, and the use of images in worship as remnants of Gentile superstition. The Schoolmen's philosophy of "separate essences" and "substantial forms" is empty verbalism.

### Chapter 46: Darkness from Vain Philosophy and Fabulous Traditions

Hobbes launches his most sustained critique of Aristotelian-Scholastic philosophy. The doctrines of "substantial forms," "separate essences," "intelligible species," and "free will" are not merely false — they are "insignificant speech" that has no basis in the real motions of bodies. The universities are the "kingdom of darkness" because they teach this nonsense, producing priests and lawyers who claim authority independent of the sovereign.

### Chapter 47: The Benefit of Darkness and Who Is Responsible

The Kingdom of Darkness serves the interests of those who profit from ignorance: the Roman Church, ambitious presbyters, and university men who claim authority over the sovereign. Hobbes concludes with a powerful image: the Church of Rome is the ghost of the deceased Roman Empire, sitting crowned on its grave.

### Review and Conclusion

Hobbes defends his method (resolutive-compositive), reiterates the authority of the sovereign over all matters of peace and doctrine, and argues that his theory of obligation leaves no room for rebellion. His final words: the only check on the sovereign's power is the subject's natural right of self-defence when the sovereign directly threatens his life — but even this is a narrow exception that does not justify organised resistance.

## Key Concepts in Detail

**Authorisation and Representation**: Hobbes's theory of authorisation is his most original contribution to political theory. When individuals covenant to create a commonwealth, they do not merely promise to obey; they *authorise* the sovereign to act in their name. This means every act of the sovereign is, by definition, the act of each subject. The subject cannot complain of injustice because the subject is the *author* of the sovereign's action. This solves a deep problem in earlier contract theories: if the sovereign breaches the contract, what remedy do subjects have? For Hobbes, the sovereign is not a party to the contract at all — the contract is among the subjects — so no breach is possible. The sovereign's actions are the subjects' own actions, and one cannot be unjust to oneself.

**The Laws of Nature as Theorems**: Hobbes's nineteen laws of nature are not moral laws in the traditional sense. They are not commands from God or requirements of natural teleology. They are "theorems of reason" — conclusions about what conduct conduces to self-preservation deduced from the premise that peace is preferable to war. Hobbes calls them "laws" only because scripture treats them as commands of God. This is a radically new foundation for morality: it is based on rational self-interest, not on any objective good. The laws include: seek peace; lay down the right to all things when others do likewise; keep covenants; show gratitude; accommodate others; forgive; avoid cruelty; recognise natural equality; agree to arbitration. All are derivable from the fundamental imperative of self-preservation.

**The Right of Nature and Its Transfer**: In the state of nature, every man has a *right to all things* — including another's body. This is not a moral right but a liberty: the absence of any external impediment. To escape the state of nature, individuals must lay down this right — they must "divest" themselves of the liberty of hindering others. The mechanism is *contract*: a mutual transfer of rights. But contracts in the state of nature are insecure — there is no power to enforce them. Hence the need for a commonwealth: a power that can make performance of covenants rational by threatening punishment for breach.

**Sovereignty by Institution vs. Acquisition**: Hobbes distinguishes commonwealths formed by *institution* (voluntary agreement) from those formed by *acquisition* (conquest). The difference is less significant than it appears: in both cases, the subject's obligation arises from fear — fear of others in the state of nature, or fear of the conqueror. The conqueror who spares a subject's life thereby gains the subject's covenant of obedience. This argument had radical implications: it implied that the Commonwealth government that emerged from the Civil War (during which Hobbes wrote) was as legitimate as the monarchy it replaced.

**The Problem of Succession**: Hobbes devotes careful attention to succession, especially in monarchies. Since the sovereign's power includes the right to name a successor, succession is always determined by the current sovereign's will — expressed or tacit. If no explicit declaration exists, custom governs. This avoids a destructive power vacuum while preserving the principle that no one but the sovereign determines who rules next.

**Public Instruction and the Universities**: Hobbes places surprisingly heavy emphasis on the education of subjects. The sovereign must ensure that the principles of obedience are taught in universities and churches. This is not merely authoritarian propaganda: Hobbes believed that civil war was caused by false doctrines taught in the universities (republican ideas, papal supremacy, the right of resistance). The cure, therefore, is true doctrine faithfully taught. This is why Part IV attacks the universities as the "Kingdom of Darkness" — they are the source of the seditious opinions that destroyed England.

## Reading Guide

*Leviathan* is a long and dense work. The essential core for understanding Hobbes's political theory is Part I (Chapters 13–15) and Part II (Chapters 17–21, 29–30). Part I Chapters 1–6 establish the materialist psychology; they are philosophically important but skippable for a first reading of the political argument. Part III and Part IV are crucial for understanding Hobbes's historical context — the religious politics of the Civil War — but are often omitted in courses focused on the secular social contract.

A recommended first reading path:

1. **Chapter 13**: The state of nature (the famous passage)
2. **Chapters 14–15**: The laws of nature and the need for a sovereign
3. **Chapter 17**: The generation of the commonwealth
4. **Chapter 18**: The rights of sovereigns
5. **Chapter 21**: The liberty of subjects
6. **Chapter 29**: Things that dissolve a commonwealth
7. **Chapter 30**: The duty of the sovereign

For advanced readers, Parts III–IV repay study as Hobbes's most detailed engagement with the religious dimension of politics — a dimension many modern secular readings neglect.

**Recommended editions**: The Penguin Classics edition (edited by C. B. Macpherson) and the Cambridge University Press edition (edited by Richard Tuck) are both excellent. The Yale University Press edition (edited by Ian Shapiro) includes interpretive essays by leading scholars. All three modernise spelling and punctuation while preserving the original text.
