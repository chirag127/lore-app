# Critical Analysis of Leviathan

## 1. Historical Context

*Leviathan* was written between 1649 and 1651, during the final years of the English Civil War. Charles I had been executed in 1649; the monarchy and the House of Lords had been abolished; England was a republic under Oliver Cromwell. Hobbes had fled to Paris in 1640, fearing persecution by the Long Parliament, and tutored the exiled Charles, Prince of Wales. The chaos of civil war — the collapse of central authority, the proliferation of religious sects, the fragmentation of sovereignty between king and Parliament — provided the experiential foundation for Hobbes's argument. The question *Leviathan* answers is not abstract: it is the concrete question of how to prevent England from tearing itself apart again.

## 2. Philosophical Context

Hobbes was responding to two intellectual traditions. The first was Aristotelian-Scholastic natural law theory, which held that humans are naturally social and that political authority derives from the natural order. The second was the Renaissance republican tradition, which argued that liberty consists in self-governance and that a mixed constitution best preserves freedom. Hobbes rejected both: humans are not naturally social but self-interested; liberty is not political participation but the absence of impediment. His mechanistic materialism — drawn from Galileo's physics and Harvey's physiology — replaced Aristotelian teleology with a universe of bodies in motion.

## 3. Key Arguments

**The state of nature argument**: Hobbes's most famous contribution is the claim that without a common power, human life is a war of all against all. This is a conceptual argument, not a historical claim. It shows why rational, self-interested individuals would agree to submit to an absolute sovereign.

**The social contract**: The commonwealth is created when each individual covenants with every other to transfer their right of self-government to a representative. This is not a contract between subject and sovereign, but among subjects — the sovereign is not a party to it and therefore cannot breach it.

**Sovereignty as artificial person**: Hobbes's theory of authorisation — the sovereign acts as the "person" of the multitude — solves the problem of collective action. The multitude becomes one person, the state, whose will is the sovereign's.

**Indivisibility of sovereignty**: To divide sovereignty — between king and Parliament, or between church and state — is to invite civil war. Sovereignty must be absolute, undivided, and above the law.

## 4. Chapter Structure

*Leviathan* is organised into four books of unequal length, framed by an Introduction and a Review and Conclusion. Part I (16 chapters) establishes a materialist psychology and derives the laws of nature. Part II (15 chapters) constructs the theory of sovereignty and commonwealth. Part III (12 chapters) subordinates religious authority to civil authority through scriptural interpretation. Part IV (4 chapters) attacks the "Kingdom of Darkness" — the Catholic Church and Aristotelian philosophy. The movement is systematic: from the nature of man to the nature of the commonwealth to the proper relationship between religion and politics.

## 5. Quality of Reasoning

*Leviathan* is a triumph of deductive argument. Hobbes proceeds from clear definitions (sense, passion, contract, person) through a chain of deductions to his conclusions about sovereignty and obligation. The argument's strength is its relentless logic: if you accept the premises — that humans are matter in motion, that fear of death is the strongest passion, that the state of nature is war — the conclusion that absolute sovereignty is necessary follows with near-geometric necessity.

However, the argument's weakness is also its deductive character. The premises are contestable. Are humans really as unsocial as Hobbes assumes? Is the fear of death truly the overriding passion? Would rational agents really agree to a sovereign with *all* the rights Hobbes specifies, or would they bargain for more limited government? The argument also has a circularity: Hobbes derives the laws of nature from self-interest, but then needs a sovereign to enforce them — yet the sovereign himself must be motivated by self-interest, creating a regress.

## 6. Use of Sources

Hobbes draws on a remarkable range of sources. The materialist physics comes from Galileo and Descartes; the physiology from Harvey; the legal theory from Roman law and Grotius; the scriptural interpretation from Protestant biblical criticism. He engages critically with Aristotle (whom he attacks in Part IV), with the Scholastic tradition (dismissed as "vain philosophy"), and with the republican tradition (implicitly challenged in Chapter 21). Uniquely for a work of political philosophy, *Leviathan* includes extensive biblical exegesis — nearly half the book is devoted to showing that scripture supports the absolute authority of the civil sovereign.

## 7. Critical Reception — Contemporary

*Leviathan* was greeted with outrage. It was condemned by both royalists (for its contractual basis of authority, which undermined divine right) and parliamentarians (for its absolutist conclusions). The Anglican clergy condemned it as atheistical. The book was burned at Oxford in 1683. Hobbes was barred from publishing on controversial topics and spent his final decades defending himself against charges of atheism and materialism. Despite the hostility, *Leviathan* circulated widely and was read by the leading minds of the age.

## 8. Named Critics — 20th & 21st Century

**C. B. Macpherson** (1911–1987), in *The Political Theory of Possessive Individualism* (1962), offered a Marxist reading. Hobbes, Macpherson argued, assumed not a universal human nature but the specific psychology of market society — possessive individualists who see themselves as owners of their own capacities, entering into market relations. The state of nature is not a pre-political condition but capitalist society with the political order removed. Macpherson's Hobbes is a theorist of bourgeois individualism rather than of human nature as such.

**Leo Strauss** (1899–1973), in *Natural Right and History* (1953), placed Hobbes as the founder of modern political philosophy. Strauss argued that Hobbes broke decisively with the classical and Christian tradition by replacing the *summum bonum* (greatest good) with the *summum malum* (greatest evil — violent death) as the foundation of politics. This shift, for Strauss, inaugurated the modern project of constructing morality on the basis of self-interest rather than on objective natural ends, ultimately leading to the crisis of nihilism.

**Quentin Skinner** (b. 1940), in *Reason and Rhetoric in the Philosophy of Hobbes* (1996) and *Hobbes and Republican Liberty* (2008), placed *Leviathan* in its rhetorical and political context. Skinner argued that Hobbes was responding to the classical republican tradition, which defined liberty as freedom from domination. Hobbes redefined liberty negatively — as absence of external impediment — to neutralise the republican claim that living under an absolute monarch is incompatible with freedom. Skinner also showed that *Leviathan*'s rhetorical style — a departure from Hobbes's earlier works — was itself a strategic choice, adopting the humanist rhetorical tradition Hobbes ostensibly rejected.

**David Gauthier** (1932–2023), in *The Logic of Leviathan* (1969), applied game theory and rational choice analysis to Hobbes. Gauthier argued that the state of nature is best understood as a prisoner's dilemma: each individual rationally defects (attacks or preempts), producing a collectively worse outcome. The sovereign solves the dilemma by enforcing cooperation. Gauthier's interpretation made Hobbes's argument newly relevant to contemporary political philosophy and sparked a revival of contractarian ethics.

**Jean Hampton** (1954–1996), in *Hobbes and the Social Contract Tradition* (1986), offered a feminist and analytical critique. She argued that Hobbes's social contract fails as a solution to the prisoner's dilemma because the sovereign himself is a rational agent with no incentive to govern justly. Hampton also showed that Hobbes's argument presupposes a form of "alienation contract" (subjects permanently transfer their rights) rather than the more defensible "agency contract" (subjects loan their rights). Her critique challenged both Hobbes's internal consistency and the broader contractarian project.

**A. P. Martinich** (b. 1944), in *The Two Gods of Leviathan* (1992), offered a revisionist theological reading. Against the dominant secular interpretation, Martinich argued that Hobbes was a sincere Christian whose theology was central to *Leviathan*. He placed Hobbes within the Reformed tradition, showing that Hobbes's materialism (God as a corporeal being) had precedents in Christian thought. Martinich's interpretation has been controversial but has forced scholars to take the religious dimension of *Leviathan* more seriously.

**Richard Tuck** (b. 1949), in *Natural Rights Theories* (1979) and *Hobbes: A Very Short Introduction* (1989, 2002), argued that Hobbes transformed the language of natural rights. Where earlier theorists saw rights as objective features of the natural order, Hobbes made them subjective powers or liberties of the individual. Tuck also emphasised the democratic implications of Hobbes's theory: the sovereign is authorised by the people and represents them, a framework that could be — and was — turned against absolute monarchy.

**Tom Sorell** (b. 1948), in *Hobbes* (1986), examined the relationship between Hobbes's science and his politics. Sorell argued that Hobbes's political theory is not simply applied mechanics but has its own method and principles. He showed that Hobbes's "civil philosophy" was intended as a genuine science, parallel to geometry and physics, and that its failure to achieve axiomatic certainty does not undermine its value as political theory.

## 9. Impact

*Leviathan* is the fountainhead of the social contract tradition. John Locke's *Two Treatises of Government* (1689) accepts Hobbes's contractual framework but reverses his conclusions — limited government, popular sovereignty, right of revolution. Jean-Jacques Rousseau's *The Social Contract* (1762) reworks the contract as the foundation of popular sovereignty and general will. Immanuel Kant's political philosophy (1790s) assimilates the contract as an "idea of reason" for testing the legitimacy of laws. In the twentieth century, John Rawls's *A Theory of Justice* (1971) revived the contract tradition in a Kantian form, and Robert Nozick's *Anarchy, State, and Utopia* (1974) used a Hobbesian state of nature to defend libertarianism.

Beyond the contract tradition, *Leviathan* influenced Carl Schmitt's concept of sovereignty (the sovereign is he who decides the exception), Michel Foucault's analysis of biopower (sovereignty as the power to let live and make die), and contemporary international relations theory (Hobbes as the paradigmatic "realist").

## 10. Contemporary Relevance

*Leviathan* remains relevant wherever the problem of political order arises. Debates about executive power in emergencies, the limits of free speech (Hobbes's arguments for censorship), the relationship between security and liberty, the legitimacy of international institutions (a Hobbesian state of nature between nations), and the political implications of technological surveillance (Hobbes's sovereign as the original Big Brother) all draw on Hobbesian themes. The war on terror, the COVID-19 pandemic, and the rise of authoritarian populism have all provoked "Hobbesian" analyses.

## 11. Sufficiency

*Leviathan* is sufficient as a complete statement of Hobbes's mature political philosophy. It supersedes his earlier *Elements of Law* (1640) and *De Cive* (1642), incorporating their arguments while adding the extensive theological apparatus and the theory of personation. The main limitation for the modern reader is that the argument's force depends on premises — materialist psychology, egoistic motivation, the overridingness of the fear of death — that many contemporary philosophers reject. Hobbes's deductive method also means that the argument is only as strong as its definitions: if one rejects the definition of "liberty" as absence of external impediment, the conclusions about political obligation shift dramatically.

Another limitation is the tension between Hobbes's psychological egoism and his normative conclusions. If humans are as relentlessly self-interested as Hobbes claims, why would they keep their covenants once the sovereign is established? The sovereign's coercive power provides an answer, but this creates a regress: who enforces the sovereign? Hobbes must rely on the sovereign's self-interest (the desire to remain in power) as the ultimate guarantee, which is a thinner reed than his theory of obligation seems to require. Jean Hampton's critique develops this objection most rigorously.

The theological argument of Parts III–IV has also been questioned. Even if Hobbes's reading of scripture is plausible, it is not obviously compulsory — the same texts can be read to support very different conclusions about the relationship between ecclesiastical and civil authority. Martinich's defence of Hobbes's sincerity has not settled the question of whether the theological apparatus is a genuine argument or a rhetorical shield against charges of atheism.

For a full picture, *Leviathan* should be read alongside Locke's *Second Treatise* and Rousseau's *Social Contract*, which accept the contractual framework but reach very different conclusions about the limits of sovereignty and the right of revolution. Rawls's *A Theory of Justice* can be seen as a Kantian reworking of the contract tradition that avoids Hobbes's absolutist conclusions while preserving the idea that legitimate government must be justifiable to those subject to it.
