# Narration & Style of Leviathan

## Overall Style

*Leviathan* is written in a distinctive prose that combines analytic precision with rhetorical power. Hobbes's sentences are long but tightly constructed, proceeding through chains of "for" and "therefore" that mirror the deductive structure of his argument. The vocabulary is largely plain and concrete — Hobbes avoids the Latinate abstraction of his Scholastic opponents and prefers the language of sense, motion, appetite, and fear. This is a deliberate choice: the style enacts the philosophy. Just as the argument reduces complex political phenomena to the motions of bodies, the prose reduces complex ideas to clear definitions and logical consequences.

## Rhetorical Strategy

Quentin Skinner has shown that *Leviathan* represents a shift in Hobbes's rhetorical strategy. In *The Elements of Law*, Hobbes had adopted a "scientific" style modelled on geometry; in *Leviathan*, he returns to the humanist rhetorical tradition he earlier rejected. The famous images — the state as artificial man, life as "nasty, brutish, and short," the sovereign as Leviathan — are not decorative but functional: they make abstract arguments vivid and memorable. Hobbes also uses strategic repetition, aphorism, and the occasional flash of sarcasm (the Schoolmen's "insignificant speech" is "vain philosophy"). The frontispiece, showing the sovereign composed of hundreds of tiny subjects, is the visual equivalent of the authorisation argument: the multitude becomes one person.

## Readability

*Leviathan* is difficult for the modern reader, but less difficult than its reputation suggests. The main obstacles are: (1) the 17th-century English (spelling, punctuation, word order); (2) the unfamiliar theological framework of Parts III–IV; and (3) the sheer density of the argument — every sentence does work. A modernised edition (e.g., the Yale or Cambridge editions) significantly reduces the first obstacle. The second and third require patience. The most accessible entry points are the famous Chapters 13–17, which contain the core argument in relatively compressed form.

## Narrative Voice

Hobbes writes in the first person but with the authority of a scientist reporting the results of inquiry. The tone is confident, even dogmatic: "I have shown," "it is manifest," "there can be no doubt." This authoritative voice is central to the argument's persuasive force. Hobbes is not offering opinions but demonstrating truths. Yet there is also a note of urgency and fear beneath the confidence — the memory of civil war presses on every page. The voice is that of a man who has seen the worst and wants to prevent its return.

## Structural Coherence

The four-part structure is more coherent than it sometimes appears. Part I diagnoses the disease (human passions lead to war); Part II prescribes the cure (absolute sovereignty); Part III shows that the cure is compatible with Christianity (scripture supports the civil sovereign); Part IV attacks the competitors (the church, the universities) that would undermine the cure. The argument thus moves from natural philosophy to politics to theology, each stage building on the last. The "Review and Conclusion" ties the threads together by responding to anticipated objections.

## Effectiveness for Purpose

*Leviathan* succeeds brilliantly at its primary purpose: justifying absolute sovereignty on grounds that self-interested individuals could accept. The argument's deductive structure makes it easy (too easy, critics say) to attack: deny the premises and the conclusions fall. But the rhetorical power of the book — its unforgettable images, its relentless logic, its fusion of fear with hope — has kept it alive across four centuries. No reader finishes *Leviathan* indifferent; it is a book that demands to be engaged, accepted, or refuted.
