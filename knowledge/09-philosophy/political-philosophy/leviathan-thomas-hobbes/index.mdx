Few books have shaped the modern political imagination as profoundly as Thomas Hobbes's *Leviathan*. Written during the English Civil War — a period of regicide, religious upheaval, and social collapse — it is a work of breathtaking ambition: a complete materialist philosophy of human nature, a rigorous deduction of the moral law from self-interest, a theory of absolute sovereignty, and a radical reinterpretation of Christian theology — all woven into a single argument for why we must obey.

Its central claim is unforgettable: without a common power to keep everyone in awe, life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." The solution is a social contract in which each individual authorises a sovereign to act in their name, surrendering the private judgment that makes peace impossible. The sovereign — whether a monarch or an assembly — must be absolute, undivided, and above the law, because only such a power can guarantee the security that makes civil life possible.

*Leviathan* is not merely a defence of authoritarianism. It is a searching inquiry into the foundations of political obligation, the relationship between fear and reason, and the psychological mechanisms that drive human beings into conflict — and that might, with the right institutional design, drive them toward peace. It remains the indispensable starting point for any serious engagement with social contract theory, sovereignty, and the problem of political order.

Thomas Hobbes (1588–1679) was an English philosopher, mathematician, and translator whose long life spanned the Elizabethan era, the English Civil War, the Commonwealth, and the Restoration. A tutor to the Cavendish family and later to the future Charles II, he was a central figure in the European republic of letters, corresponding with Galileo, Mersenne, and Descartes. *Leviathan* was his masterpiece, but his corpus includes *The Elements of Law* (1640), *De Cive* (1642), *De Corpore* (1655), and *Behemoth* (1681).
