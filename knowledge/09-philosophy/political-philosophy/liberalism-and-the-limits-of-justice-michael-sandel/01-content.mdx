---

*Liberalism and the Limits of Justice* is divided into an introduction and four tightly argued chapters, with the second edition (1998) adding a new introduction and a concluding response to critics. The book moves from deconstructing the metaphysical underpinnings of Rawlsian liberalism (chapters 1-2) to examining contractarian justification (chapter 3) and finally to the relationship between justice and the good (chapter 4).

---

## Introduction: Liberalism and the Primacy of Justice

Sandel frames the debate by identifying the core commitment of modern deontological liberalism: the primacy of justice. Deontology (from the Greek *deon*, duty) holds that the right is prior to the good — that principles of justice can and should be identified independently of any particular conception of the good life. This is the defining thesis of Kantian moral philosophy and, Sandel argues, of Rawls's justice as fairness.

The priority of the right over the good has two dimensions. The **moral** dimension: individual rights are trumps that cannot be sacrificed for the general welfare. The **foundational** dimension: principles of justice are derivable from a purely procedural thought-experiment (the original position) that does not presuppose any substantive conception of human flourishing. Sandel intends to show that these dimensions cannot be separated: the moral priority of justice depends on a particular metaphysics of the person.

## Chapter 1: Justice and the Moral Subject

### The Primacy of Justice and the Priority of the Self

Sandel begins by unpacking what it means to say the right is prior to the good. For Rawls, this priority is not merely political — it rests on a conception of the person as a free and independent self, capable of choosing and revising its ends at will. The self is "prior to" its ends in the sense that no end is so constitutive that the self could not step back from it and evaluate it. This is what Sandel calls the "unencumbered self."

The deep problem: if the self is prior to its ends, how can moral obligation ever be anything more than the product of choice? Sandel draws on the Hegelian critique of Kant: a purely autonomous will, detached from all substantive commitments, cannot generate obligations that bind us. It can only produce hypothetical imperatives (if you want X, do Y) or empty formalism (act according to universal law, where universalization alone yields no determinate content).

### Liberalism without Metaphysics: The Original Position

Rawls famously claimed that the original position is a "device of representation" that does not presuppose any particular metaphysical doctrine about the self. Sandel responds that this is false. The original position models a particular conception of the person — one in which the parties are stripped of all knowledge of their talents, social position, and, crucially, their conception of the good. They are pure choosing agents, individuated antecedently to any of their ends. This is precisely the "unencumbered self" Sandel challenges.

### The Circumstances of Justice

Sandel examines Rawls's adoption and modification of Hume's "circumstances of justice" — moderate scarcity, conflicting claims, and mutual disinterest. Sandel argues that Rawls's circumstances are not neutral but already presuppose a particular picture of human motivation and social life. The assumption of "mutual disinterest" — that parties take no interest in one another's interests — rules out, from the start, the possibility that justice might arise from solidarity or shared ends rather than from conflicting interests.

### In Search of the Moral Subject

This is the philosophical heart of the book. Sandel argues that for Rawls, the self must be both prior to and independent of its ends. But the notion of a self that is fully constituted prior to its ends faces a dilemma. If the self is wholly independent of its ends, it is a bare "I know not what" — a purely formal subject with no character, no depth, no identity. If the self is partly constituted by its ends, then the self cannot stand back from all of them simultaneously; some ends are too close to revise without transforming who one is.

Sandel develops this through a distinction between two types of ends: those the self *has* (possessions it can shed) and those the self *is* (constitutive commitments). A devout Catholic does not merely *have* the end of attending Mass — this commitment partly constitutes her identity. To ask her to abstract from it in choosing principles of justice is not to ensure impartiality but to ask her to become someone else.

### The Self and the Other: The Priority of Plurality

Sandel examines Rawls's claim that the "plurality of distinct persons" is a fundamental feature of the circumstances of justice. For Kant and Rawls, respect for persons requires treating each as a distinct, separate individual whose good cannot be subsumed into a collective. Sandel does not deny the importance of pluralism but argues that Rawls's interpretation of plurality — as the radical separateness of antecedently individuated subjects — rules out a richer understanding of community in which persons share ends not by coincidence but because those ends are jointly constituted.

### The Self and Its Ends: The Subject of Possession

Sandel investigates the concept of possession at work in Rawls's theory. Rawls famously treats individuals' natural talents as "common assets" — no one deserves the benefits of their natural endowments because they did nothing to earn them. But this presupposes a clear distinction between the self and its attributes. If my talents are not "mine" in a deep sense, what *is* mine? Sandel suggests that Rawls oscillates between two models: one in which the self possesses its attributes (and can thus be held responsible for them) and another in which the self is distinct from its attributes (and thus not responsible for them). Neither, Sandel argues, provides a stable foundation for distributive justice.

### Individualism and the Claims of Community

The chapter concludes with Sandel's statement of the communitarian alternative. Community is not merely an instrument for achieving individual ends (the instrumental view) nor a source of shared sentiments (the sentimental view) but *constitutive* of individual identity. To belong to a community is not simply to feel solidarity with others but to have one's identity partly defined by that belonging. This "constitutive conception of community" is Sandel's alternative to the atomistic individualism he finds in Rawls.

## Chapter 2: Possession, Desert, and Distributive Justice

This chapter moves from metaphysics to distributive justice. Sandel argues that Rawls's theory of desert is internally unstable, and that the instability traces back to the problematic conception of the self identified in Chapter 1.

### Libertarianism to Egalitarianism

Sandel traces the debate between Nozick's libertarianism and Rawls's egalitarianism. Nozick argues that individuals deserve the fruits of their labor and that redistribution violates self-ownership. Rawls responds that natural talents are undeserved, so the distribution they yield is not morally privileged. Sandel argues that both positions share a hidden premise: they treat the self as distinct from its attributes. For Nozick, the self *owns* its talents; for Rawls, the self *does not own* its talents. Neither asks whether the self *is* partly constituted by its talents and attributes — which would dissolve the entire framework of ownership and desert.

### Meritocracy versus the Difference Principle

Sandel examines Rawls's rejection of meritocracy (or "careers open to talents") in favor of the difference principle. Rawls's argument is that a meritocracy rewards natural talent and social luck, both of which are undeserved. The difference principle corrects for this by redistributing to benefit the least advantaged. Sandel objects that this argument depends on a radical distinction between the self and its talents that is hard to sustain. If my talents partly constitute who I am, it is not clear I can say they are "not mine" without denying my own identity.

### Defending Common Assets

Rawls argues that natural talents are "common assets" — the distribution of talents is a collective resource. Sandel finds this formulation striking: it treats the natural distribution of abilities as if it belonged to the community rather than to individuals. This is not an innocent metaphor but a deeply contestable claim about ownership and identity. Sandel suggests that Rawls cannot simultaneously hold that (a) talents are arbitrary from a moral point of view and (b) the self is prior to its ends. If the self is prior to its ends, then the self *is* distinct from its talents, and the "common assets" claim makes sense. But if the self is partly constituted by its talents and attributes, the "common assets" claim becomes a violation of personal identity.

### The Basis of Desert

Sandel shows that Rawls's theory has no stable account of desert. Rawls rejects "pre-institutional" desert (the idea that people deserve rewards before the rules of the game are set) but accepts "institutional" desert (people deserve what the rules say they deserve). Sandel argues this is an unstable halfway house: either desert is a moral notion with content independent of social institutions (in which case Rawls needs a theory of it), or it is purely conventional (in which case it does no justificatory work).

### Individual and Social Claims: Who Owns What?

The chapter ends with Sandel's conclusion that distributive justice cannot be settled without a thicker account of the self and its relationship to its attributes. The Rawlsian framework cannot answer who deserves what because it has not decided who the "who" is.

## Chapter 3: Contract Theory and Justification

This chapter examines the role of the social contract in Rawls's theory. Sandel argues that the hypothetical contract cannot do the justificatory work Rawls demands.

### The Morality of Contract

Actual, real-world contracts derive moral force from two ideals: autonomy (I freely bound myself) and reciprocity (I gave my word, you gave yours). Hypothetical contracts lack the first element — no one actually agreed — so they depend entirely on reciprocity. But reciprocity, Sandel suggests, cannot bear the weight Rawls places on it.

### Contracts versus Contractarian Arguments

Sandel distinguishes between genuine contracts and contractarian thought-experiments. A genuine contract involves actual consent and creates obligations. A contractarian argument uses the idea of what rational persons *would* agree to as a test of justice. The problem: why should I be bound by what I *would* have agreed to in circumstances I never occupied? If I cannot be bound by a hypothetical contract, then Rawls's entire justificatory structure rests on a metaphor.

### Liberalism and the Priority of Procedure

Sandel argues that Rawls's commitment to pure procedural justice — the idea that if the procedure is fair, the outcome is just whatever it is — reflects the deeper liberal commitment to neutrality. But if the procedure itself presupposes a particular conception of the self (the unencumbered self), then the neutrality is illusory.

### What Really Goes on behind the Veil of Ignorance

Sandel examines the reasoning of the parties in the original position. They choose the two principles, Rawls argues, using the maximin rule (make the worst outcome as good as possible). But Sandel contends that the maximin argument only works if the parties are extremely risk-averse — and nothing in the description of the original position explains why they should be. The parties are not motivated by envy or fear but by a rational desire to advance their interests. If they are rational maximizers, they would not necessarily choose the two principles over utilitarianism. Sandel concludes that the original position smuggles in moral content (risk aversion, a concern for the worst-off) that it claims to derive from purely procedural conditions.

## Chapter 4: Justice and the Good

The final chapter draws together Sandel's positive vision. Having criticized Rawls's liberalism, he sketches what a politics that takes constitutive community seriously might look like.

### The Unity of the Self

Sandel argues that the Rawlsian self, confronted with multiple ends, has no principled way to order them. If the self is prior to its ends, all ends are equally revisable and no end is more central to identity than any other. This makes moral deliberation impossible: without some ends that are constitutive of who I am, I have no standpoint from which to evaluate my other ends. The unified self requires constitutive commitments.

### The Case of Affirmative Action

Sandel uses affirmative action as a test case for his claims. Liberals typically defend affirmative action either on grounds of distributive justice (correcting for past disadvantage) or on grounds of diversity (benefiting the educational environment). Sandel suggests that neither justification captures the full moral reality of the issue. Affirmative action is not just about distributing benefits fairly — it is about acknowledging group membership as partly constitutive of identity and about the collective responsibility of communities for their histories. A purely liberal framework, with its individualist language of rights and opportunities, cannot do justice to these dimensions.

### Three Conceptions of Community

Sandel formalizes his typology of community:

1. **Instrumental**: Community is a private good — individuals cooperate only because they need each other to achieve their independently defined ends. This is the view of the libertarian tradition.

2. **Sentimental**: Community involves shared feelings and attachments — individuals have "fellow feelings" for one another. This is the view Rawls ultimately adopts in Part III of *A Theory of Justice* (the "social union" and "sense of justice" accounts).

3. **Constitutive**: Community is partly definitive of individual identity — the community's ends are not merely shared but *constitutive* of who the members are. This is Sandel's preferred view.

Each conception corresponds to a different account of the self, and only the constitutive view, Sandel argues, captures the full depth of human belonging.

### Agency and the Role of Reflection

Sandel distinguishes between two modes of moral agency. In the liberal model, reflection is about choosing among alternative ends, standing back from them and selecting the best. In Sandel's model, reflection is about self-discovery — not choosing what to value but discerning what values are already partly constitutive of one's identity. This is not passive submission to tradition but active interpretation of the commitments that define us.

### Agency and the Role of Choice

The book ends with a meditation on the interplay between choice and discovery in moral life. Sandel does not deny that we make genuine choices. He argues, however, that our choices are always situated within a horizon of meaning that we did not create. The task of moral and political philosophy is not to construct principles from a standpoint outside all traditions but to interpret and clarify the commitments that already partly define us.

## Second Edition Additions (1998)

The second edition adds a new introduction in which Sandel responds to the development of "political liberalism" — Rawls's turn, in *Political Liberalism* (1993), to a purely political conception of justice that avoids metaphysical claims. Sandel argues that this move fails: the attempt to bracket controversial moral and religious questions still presupposes a particular liberal view of the person and of the proper scope of politics. The book also includes a new concluding chapter addressing subsequent developments in the liberalism-communitarianism debate.

---

## Reading Guide

*Liberalism and the Limits of Justice* is a challenging but extraordinarily rewarding book. For those new to the debate, read Chapter 1 carefully — it contains the metaphysical core of Sandel's critique. Chapter 2 is more technical and deals with economic justice; it can be skimmed on a first reading. Chapter 3 on contract theory is essential for understanding the limits of the social contract tradition. Chapter 4 provides the constructive payoff and is the most accessible entry point.

The book assumes familiarity with Rawls's *A Theory of Justice*. Read that first, or at least read a summary of its main arguments (the original position, the two principles, the veil of ignorance). Sandel's prose is elegant and unusually clear for analytic philosophy, but the arguments are dense and repay careful study.
