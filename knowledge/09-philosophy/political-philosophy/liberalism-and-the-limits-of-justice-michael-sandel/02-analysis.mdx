## Book Context & Background

When *Liberalism and the Limits of Justice* appeared in 1982, Rawls's *A Theory of Justice* (1971) had reigned for a decade as the dominant work in Anglo-American political philosophy. Rawls had revived the social contract tradition, displaced utilitarianism, and provided a rigorous defense of liberal egalitarianism. But by the early 1980s, challenges were mounting from multiple directions. Robert Nozick's *Anarchy, State, and Utopia* (1974) had attacked Rawls from the libertarian right. Left-critics charged that the difference principle was insufficiently egalitarian. Sandel, a young Harvard professor who had studied under Charles Taylor at Oxford, launched a deeper challenge — one aimed not at Rawls's conclusions but at his fundamental conception of the person. The book appeared just as "communitarianism" was emerging as a distinct philosophical orientation, alongside the work of Alasdair MacIntyre (*After Virtue*, 1981), Charles Taylor, and Michael Walzer (*Spheres of Justice*, 1983).

## About the Author

Michael J. Sandel (b. 1953) is an American political philosopher and the Anne T. and Robert M. Bass Professor of Government at Harvard University. He earned his BA from Brandeis University (1975) and his DPhil from Balliol College, Oxford (1985) under the supervision of Charles Taylor. He joined the Harvard faculty in 1980 and has taught there since. *Liberalism and the Limits of Justice* was his first book, developed from his Oxford doctoral thesis ("Liberalism and the Problem of the Moral Subject," 1980). Sandel later became a globally recognized public intellectual through his Harvard course "Justice" — the first Harvard course made freely available online and on television, viewed by tens of millions worldwide — and through his popular books *Justice: What's the Right Thing to Do?* (2009) and *What Money Can't Buy* (2012). He was elected to the American Academy of Arts and Sciences in 2002 and was named "most influential foreign figure of the year" by *China Newsweek* in 2011. Sandel resists the "communitarian" label, though his work is universally classified as communitarian in its orientation.

## Core Thesis & Argument

Sandel's core thesis: the liberal conception of justice as prior to the good — the defining commitment of deontological liberalism from Kant through Rawls — depends on a conception of the self that is metaphysically indefensible and phenomenologically inaccurate. Liberalism posits an "unencumbered self," a subject capable of standing back from all contingent attachments, social roles, and constitutive ends to choose principles of justice from an impartial standpoint. This self, Sandel argues, does not exist and could not have moral experience. Real persons are "encumbered" — partly constituted by their communities, traditions, relationships, and ends. If the self is not prior to its ends, then justice cannot be prior to the good. The argument proceeds in four steps: (1) showing that Rawls's original position presupposes an unencumbered self; (2) demonstrating that this conception is unstable in its account of possession and desert; (3) arguing that the hypothetical contract cannot do the justificatory work Rawls demands; and (4) offering a constitutive conception of community as an alternative foundation for political philosophy.

## Thematic Analysis

**The unencumbered self**: Sandel's most influential concept. The liberal self is pictured as a free, rational agent who exists independently of any particular ends, values, or relationships. This self *chooses* its ends rather than *discovering* them as partly constitutive of identity. Sandel argues this misdescribes moral experience: we do not choose our family ties, our religious commitments, or our deep loyalties — we find ourselves already claimed by them.

**The priority of the right over the good**: Sandel attacks the claim that justice can be identified and justified independently of any particular conception of human flourishing. He argues that procedural justice always presupposes substantive judgments about the good — the very judgments it claims to bracket.

**Constitutive community**: Sandel's positive alternative. Community is not merely instrumental to individual ends nor a matter of shared sentiment. It is partly definitive of who we are as individuals. The community's good is internal to the individual's good, not merely contingently aligned with it.

**Possession and desert**: Sandel shows that Rawls's treatment of natural talents as "common assets" presupposes a problematic distinction between the self and its attributes. This distinction, Sandel argues, cannot be sustained without undermining the very idea of moral responsibility.

**Affirmative action as a test case**: Sandel uses affirmative action to show the limits of liberal neutrality. A purely individualist framework cannot capture the moral significance of group membership or historical responsibility.

## Argumentation & Evidence

Sandel's method is primarily critical and dialectical. He proceeds by close, charitable reading of Rawls — what Charles Fried in the *Harvard Law Review* called "always fair to its target." Sandel identifies internal tensions in Rawls's arguments, showing that they yield conclusions Rawls would not accept, or that they presuppose premises Rawls officially denies. The book does not present empirical evidence in the social-science sense but relies on philosophical analysis: the logical coherence of concepts, the phenomenological adequacy of descriptions of moral experience, and the consistency of theoretical commitments. Sandel draws on Kant, Hegel, Aristotle, and Wittgenstein as resources. His use of the affirmative action case study is the book's most sustained engagement with concrete political practice, though the Second Edition adds discussion of subsequent political developments.

## Strengths

- **Philosophical depth**: Sandel goes deeper than surface-level policy disagreements to challenge the metaphysical foundations of liberal theory itself.
- **Charitable reading**: The critique is conducted with extraordinary fairness to Rawls. Sandel develops Rawls's arguments in their strongest form before attacking them.
- **Conceptual innovation**: "The unencumbered self" and "constitutive community" entered the lexicon of political philosophy and remain indispensable terms of debate.
- **Phenomenological grounding**: Sandel appeals to lived moral experience in a way that makes abstract philosophy feel urgent and personal.
- **Elegant prose**: Unlike much analytic philosophy, the book is written with style, clarity, and literary grace. Ronald Beiner called it "genuinely important and philosophical, written with style and precision."
- **Galvanizing effect**: The book launched the liberalism-communitarianism debate that dominated Anglo-American political philosophy for two decades, forcing Rawls himself to respond.

## Criticisms & Weaknesses

**Will Kymlicka's liberal defense** (in *Liberalism and Communitarianism*, 1988, and *Contemporary Political Philosophy*, 1990): Kymlicka argues that Sandel mischaracterizes liberalism. Liberals do not claim the self is "unencumbered" in the strong sense Sandel attacks; they claim only that individuals must be free to question and revise their current ends. This is compatible with the self being deeply shaped by its social context — what matters is the *capacity* for revision, not the *absence* of constitutive attachments. Kymlicka writes that Sandel's book "helped start the liberalism-communitarianism debate" but contends that the debate was founded on a misinterpretation.

**Brian Barry** (review in *Ethics*, 1984): Barry, a leading political theorist, reviewed the book critically. He argued that Sandel's positive alternative — a politics oriented toward the common good — is dangerously vague and potentially authoritarian. Without clear limits on what the community can demand of individuals, Barry worried, communitarianism risks legitimizing oppression in the name of shared values.

**Richard Rorty** (in "The Priority of Democracy to Philosophy," 1990): Rorty praised Sandel's critique as "clear and forceful" and credited him with "very elegant and cogent arguments" against liberal metaphysics. But Rorty also argued that Sandel's critique is ultimately irrelevant to politics: we can be pragmatists about the self — denying that there is any deep truth about human nature to be discovered — and still defend liberal institutions on historical and practical grounds. Sandel, Rorty suggests, remains too attached to the very metaphysical project he attacks.

**Rawls's response** (in *Political Liberalism*, 1993): Rawls did not respond directly by name to Sandel in his major works, but *Political Liberalism* is widely read as a response to the communitarian critique. Rawls's key move: he abandons the claim that justice as fairness is grounded in a comprehensive Kantian conception of the person and instead presents it as a "political, not metaphysical" doctrine — freestanding, based on the public political culture of a democratic society, and not dependent on any particular metaphysics of the self. If this move succeeds, Sandel's critique is disarmed.

**Mark Sagoff** (*Yale Law Journal*, 1983): Sagoff endorsed Sandel's "criticism of contemporary utilitarian and Kantian conceptions of the good" and agreed with his critique of Rawls's "possessive individualism." But Sagoff also argued that Sandel's view did not advance sufficiently beyond the British Idealist tradition (F.H. Bradley, T.H. Green, Bernard Bosanquet) and left unresolved questions about how to determine the content of the common good.

**Sheldon Wolin** (2004): Wolin called the book "the best political critique of Rawls from a communitarian and participatory perspective," praising its emphasis on democratic engagement. But Wolin, a radical democratic theorist, worried that Sandel's communitarianism lacks a critical edge — it risks celebrating existing communities uncritically rather than subjecting them to democratic contestation.

## Comparative Analysis

Sandel's book stands alongside Nozick's *Anarchy, State, and Utopia* (1974) and MacIntyre's *After Virtue* (1981) as one of the three great critical responses to Rawls. Each attacks from a different direction:

- **vs. Nozick** (libertarian): Nozick objects to Rawls's conclusions (redistribution violates property rights). Sandel objects to Rawls's premises (the conception of the self).
- **vs. MacIntyre** (neo-Aristotelian): MacIntyre rejects the entire Enlightenment project of grounding morality in universal reason. Sandel is more moderate: he seeks to reform liberalism, not replace it entirely.
- **vs. Walzer** (pluralist): Walzer in *Spheres of Justice* (1983) argues for complex equality — different goods for different spheres. Sandel focuses more on the self than on the structure of goods. Both share a suspicion of universal principles and a commitment to contextual judgment.
- **vs. Taylor**: Charles Taylor's "Atomism" (1979) and *Sources of the Self* (1989) develop similar critiques of liberal individualism from a Hegelian-hermeneutic perspective. Sandel's contribution is to focus these critiques specifically on Rawls's original position with greater precision than Taylor.

## Impact & Legacy

*Liberalism and the Limits of Justice* has been cited over 571 times (per Crossref) and remains a standard text in political theory courses worldwide. It defined the communitarian critique of Rawls, forced liberals to articulate the metaphysical commitments (or lack thereof) in their theories, and shaped the agenda of political philosophy for a generation. The book directly influenced Rawls's turn to "political liberalism" and continues to resonate in debates about multiculturalism, identity politics, and the limits of liberal neutrality. Sandel's phrase "the unencumbered self" has become perhaps the most frequently invoked term in the critical literature on Rawls. Beyond academia, the book helped launch Sandel's career as a public intellectual whose accessible treatments of justice and ethics have reached an audience of millions.

## Sufficiency & Final Assessment

*Liberalism and the Limits of Justice* is an indispensable work of political philosophy that succeeds brilliantly in identifying the deepest tensions in Rawlsian liberalism. Its critique of the unencumbered self remains powerful even if one ultimately rejects its conclusions. The book's main limitation is that its positive vision — a politics of the common good grounded in constitutive community — remains underspecified. Sandel tells us what is wrong with liberalism more convincingly than he tells us what to put in its place. The second edition partially addresses this by engaging with later developments, but the constructive project remains incomplete. Even so, the book is rightly regarded as a classic: a critique so penetrating that it changed the theory it criticized.
