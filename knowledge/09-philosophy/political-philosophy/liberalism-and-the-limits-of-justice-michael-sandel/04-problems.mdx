## Key Problem

The central problem Sandel addresses: can liberal political philosophy — specifically Rawlsian deontological liberalism — provide a coherent justification for justice that is genuinely prior to the good? Rawls claims that principles of justice can be derived from a purely procedural thought-experiment (the original position behind the veil of ignorance) that does not presuppose any particular conception of the good life. Sandel argues that this is impossible: every procedure presupposes a substantive conception of the person, and every attempt to bracket the good only smuggles it back in disguised as neutrality. The deeper problem is that liberalism's conception of the self — as a free, rational agent antecedently individuated from its ends — is false to moral experience and cannot sustain the obligations and solidarities a just society needs.

## Proposed Solution

Sandel does not offer a full alternative theory of justice. His project is primarily critical and diagnostic. But the positive vision that emerges is a politics centered on the "constitutive conception of community." Instead of treating individuals as radically free choosers whose ends are merely possessions they can shed, Sandel proposes that we understand persons as partly constituted by their communal attachments. Political philosophy should not aim for neutrality about the good life but should engage openly with questions about the kind of society we want to be. Justice and the good are not separable: what we owe each other depends partly on who we are and what we value together. Sandel advocates for a "republican" politics of the common good in which citizens deliberate not only about rights and procedures but about shared purposes and moral commitments.

## Evidence & Argumentation

Sandel's evidence is primarily conceptual and phenomenological rather than empirical. He argues from:
- **The logic of the original position**: the veil of ignorance strips away precisely those features (constitutive attachments, social identities, moral commitments) that make principled moral deliberation possible.
- **The instability of Rawls's account of possession**: Rawls cannot consistently hold both that talents are "common assets" and that the self is prior to its ends.
- **The failure of hypothetical contract**: a merely hypothetical agreement cannot generate actual moral obligations.
- **The affirmative action case**: purely individualist liberal arguments miss the moral significance of collective identity and historical responsibility.
- **Phenomenology of moral experience**: we experience our deepest commitments not as chosen but as constitutive of who we are. The liberal picture does not match our lived moral reality.

## Unresolved Questions

- How do we determine which communities are genuinely constitutive of identity and which are oppressive? Sandel gives no clear criteria.
- If the good is prior to the right, how do we protect individuals from majoritarian tyranny? Brian Barry raised this concern sharply.
- Can a politics of the common good work in deeply pluralistic, multicultural societies where citizens disagree profoundly about the good?
- Does Sandel's view collapse into relativism — the idea that whatever a community values is, for that community, just?
- How do we handle conflicts between different constitutive communities (e.g., religious community vs. national community)?

## Criticisms

**Will Kymlicka** (1988, 1990) argued that Sandel mischaracterizes liberalism: liberal individuals can have deep, constitutive attachments and still be free to question and revise them. The capacity for revision, not the absence of attachments, is the liberal commitment. Kymlicka maintained that Sandel's critique rested on a caricature.

**Brian Barry** (*Ethics*, 1984) warned that Sandel's communitarianism lacks safeguards against oppression. If the community's good is prior to individual rights, what prevents the majority from imposing its conception of the good on minorities?

**Rawls** (through *Political Liberalism*, 1993) responded indirectly by reconceiving justice as fairness as a "political, not metaphysical" doctrine. If the original position is simply a device for modeling the public political culture of a democratic society — not a claim about the nature of the self — then Sandel's metaphysical critique may miss its target.

**Richard Rorty** (1990) granted Sandel's critique of liberal metaphysics but argued that the critique was politically irrelevant. Liberal institutions can be defended on pragmatic, historical grounds without any deep philosophical foundation.

**Mark Sagoff** (*Yale Law Journal*, 1983) argued that Sandel's position does not advance beyond the British Idealists (Bradley, Green, Bosanquet) and leaves unresolved how to determine the content of the common good.

## Alternative Solutions

- **Rawls's political liberalism**: abandon comprehensive Kantian foundations and present justice as fairness as a freestanding political conception drawing on the public culture of a democratic society.
- **Kymlicka's liberal multiculturalism**: retain liberal individualism while acknowledging that cultural membership is a "primary good" that individuals need for autonomous choice.
- **Walzer's complex equality**: replace one universal principle of justice with a pluralist account — different social goods should be distributed by different principles in different spheres.
- **Habermas's deliberative democracy**: ground legitimacy in free and open public deliberation rather than in either individual rights or communal traditions.
- **MacIntyre's virtue ethics**: reject liberalism entirely and return to an Aristotelian tradition of the virtues within particular communities.

## Practical Applications

Sandel's critique has practical implications for:
- **Affirmative action**: understanding it as a matter of collective responsibility and identity, not merely individual compensation.
- **Education**: public schools cannot be neutral about values; they must cultivate the virtues and commitments a democratic community needs.
- **Immigration and citizenship**: membership in a political community involves not just legal status but shared identity and mutual responsibility.
- **Free speech**: the liberal insistence on neutrality among conceptions of the good may fail to capture the real stakes in debates about hate speech, religious expression, and public discourse.
- **Economic policy**: markets are not neutral mechanisms; they express and shape our values. Sandel later developed this theme in *What Money Can't Buy*.
- **Civil society**: strengthening the institutions — families, churches, unions, community organizations — that mediate between the individual and the state, sustaining the constitutive communities that liberal individualism neglects.
