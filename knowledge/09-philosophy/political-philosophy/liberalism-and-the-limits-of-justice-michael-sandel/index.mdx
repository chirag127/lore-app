## Overview

Michael Sandel's *Liberalism and the Limits of Justice* (1982) is the most important single critique of John Rawls's *A Theory of Justice* and the founding document of the communitarian challenge to liberal political philosophy. Sandel argues that the liberal conception of the person — a free, rational, "unencumbered" self capable of standing back from all its attachments to choose principles of justice — is both metaphysically incoherent and phenomenologically false. Our identities are not antecedently individuated but partly constituted by the communities, traditions, and relationships we inhabit. If Sandel is right, justice cannot be "the first virtue of social institutions" in the way Rawls claimed; it must share the stage with the good, and liberal neutrality about the good life is an impossible aspiration.

## Why This Book Matters

The liberalism-communitarianism debate that Sandel's book ignited reshaped Anglo-American political philosophy for two decades. Rawls himself felt compelled to respond — his *Political Liberalism* (1993) can be read, in part, as an answer to Sandel's charge that the original position presupposes a metaphysically loaded conception of the self. Beyond political philosophy, Sandel's critique of neutrality resonates in constitutional law, democratic theory, and public reason debates. The book launched Sandel's career as a public intellectual and remains essential reading for anyone who wants to understand what is at stake — philosophically and politically — between individual rights and the claims of community.
