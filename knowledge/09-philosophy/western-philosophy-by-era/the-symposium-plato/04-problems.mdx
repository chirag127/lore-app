## 1. Key Problem

The *Symposium* addresses a single overarching problem: what is
the nature of erotic love (eros), and how should it be directed
to achieve human flourishing? This question branches into several
sub-problems: Is love a god or something else? Does it aim at
beauty, goodness, or immortality? Is the love of a particular
person compatible with the love of wisdom? And why does love hurt?

The problem is urgent because the stakes are existential. If love
is merely a physiological drive (as some Presocratic thinkers
held), then it has no special connection to the good life. If
love is a god (as the earlier speakers claim), then it is
perfect and needs no guidance. Plato needs a third option — one
that explains love's power, its incompleteness, and its capacity
for education.

---

## 2. Proposed Solution

Diotima's answer: love is not a god but a daimon — an intermediate
spirit between mortal and immortal, ignorance and wisdom. Love is
the desire for the perpetual possession of the good. Because
mortals cannot possess the good permanently (they die, things
change), they seek immortality through procreation — physical
procreation (children) or spiritual procreation (wisdom, virtue,
poetry, law). The highest expression of love is the philosophical
ascent from a single beautiful body to Beauty itself — the eternal
Form. Once the lover has seen Beauty itself, he gives birth to true
virtue and becomes, insofar as a human can, immortal.

---

## 3. Evidence and Argumentation

Diotima supports her account with:

- **The Socratic refutation of Agathon (199c-201c).** Love desires
  what it lacks. If love desires beauty, love is not beautiful. If
  love desires the good, love is not good. This is the most
  rigorous stretch of argument in the dialogue.

- **The myth of Love's birth (203b-e).** Poros (Resource) and Penia
  (Poverty) conceive Eros at Aphrodite's birthday feast. The myth
  explains the intermediate nature of love — always desiring,
  always resourceful, always in between.

- **The argument from universal desire (205a-206a).** Everyone
  desires the good. Everyone wants to be happy. Love is the name
  for the specific drive toward the good through beauty.

- **The procreation argument (206b-209e).** The desire for
  immortality explains why love seeks reproduction. Physical
  procreation perpetuates the species; spiritual procreation
  perpetuates virtue and wisdom. Both are forms of the same drive.

- **The Ladder of Love (210a-212b).** The ascent from particular
  to universal is presented as a step-by-step initiation, modeled
  on the Eleusinian Mysteries. Each stage is a necessary
  preparation for the next.

## 4. Unresolved Questions

### The Impersonality Problem

Does the lover abandon the particular individual at the final stage
of the ascent? If the Form of Beauty is the ultimate object of
love, what place remains for love of this person — with their
particular face, their flaws, their irreplaceable singularity? The
text is ambiguous: Diotima says the lover "despises" (*kataphroneo*)
the lower stages, but she also says the lover produces "beautiful
discourses" in relationship with a particular beloved. The
inclusive vs. exclusive reading debate (Price vs. Vlastos) is
unresolved.

### The Problem of Embodiment

Alcibiades' speech raises a question the dialogue does not answer:
can the philosophical lover also be a fully embodied, passionate
lover of a particular person? Socrates seems to have transcended
sexual desire, but Alcibiades — representing the ordinary human
experience of love — has not. Is Socrates's transcendence a
philosophical achievement or a psychological deficit?

### Comedy and Tragedy

Socrates's final argument — that the same poet should write both
comedy and tragedy — suggests that the *Symposium* itself should
be read as both. But what does it mean for philosophy to include
both modes? Does the blend of comedy (Aristophanes' hiccups) and
tragedy (Alcibiades' confession) undercut the seriousness of
Diotima's ascent?

## 5. Criticisms

### Vlastos (1973)

Gregory Vlastos's "The Individual as Object of Love in Plato's
Dialogues" is the most influential critique. Vlastos argues that
Platonic love is not love of persons but of their properties.
The beloved is a "vessel" for beauty and goodness, not valued for
themselves. Vlastos writes that Plato's account "cannot be
reconciled with the experience of love as we live it" — which
is irreducibly particular, attached to the individual as such.

### Nussbaum (1986)

Martha Nussbaum's *The Fragility of Goodness* offers a more
sympathetic but ultimately more damning critique. She reads the
*Symposium* as a Platonic self-indictment: by placing Alcibiades'
speech after Diotima's, Plato acknowledges that the philosophical
ascent comes at a cost — the loss of the ability to love an
individual as an individual. Nussbaum: "Socrates has made himself
into a stone" — he has become invulnerable by becoming inhuman.

### Stern-Gillet (1995)

Suzanne Stern-Gillet argues that the *Symposium* conflates two
different things: the desire to possess the good and the desire
to benefit the beloved. Diotima's account is fundamentally
self-regarding — the lover wants the good for himself. But love
of another person involves wanting what is good for *them*, not
just for oneself. The dialogue has no concept of altruistic love.

### Ferrari (1992)

G. R. F. Ferrari argues that the dramatic form of the dialogue
undermines its philosophical content. The fact that the deepest
truths are reported second- or third-hand (Socrates heard them
from Diotima, Apollodorus reports them) suggests that Plato
wants us to hold Diotima's doctrine at a distance. We are not
meant to accept it uncritically.

### Sheffield (2006)

Frisbee Sheffield offers the most comprehensive recent defense
of the *Symposium* against Vlastos. She argues that the Ladder
is not about abandoning individuals but about understanding
them better. The lover who has seen Beauty itself loves the
particular beautiful person as an *instance* of the Form — not
as a mere means but as a participant in the very nature of
beauty.

## 6. Alternative Solutions

### Aristotle on Friendship

Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* VIII-IX offers a completely
different approach. Instead of focusing on erotic desire and
metaphysical ascent, Aristotle analyzes friendship (*philia*)
as a relationship of mutual virtue, equality, and shared life.
For Aristotle, the best love is between two virtuous equals who
wish each other well for their own sake. This avoids the
impersonality problem but says nothing about erotic passion.

### Freud on Sublimation

Freud's theory of sublimation — the redirection of sexual energy
toward culturally valued ends — is arguably a naturalized version
of Diotima's ascent. But Freud insists that sublimation is always
partial and unstable: the repressed desire for a particular person
does not disappear but haunts its substitutes. For Freud, the
ascent is a neurotic compromise, not a philosophical achievement.

### Modern Theories of Love

Contemporary philosophers (Harry Frankfurt, Irving Singer, Simon
May) have tried to rehabilitate the love of individuals without
Platonic metaphysics. For Frankfurt, love is a disinterested
concern for the flourishing of the beloved. For Singer, love is
"bestowal of value" — we create value in the beloved through our
attention. These theories preserve the particularity Vlastos
misses in Plato but lose the metaphysical ambition.

## 7. Practical Applications

### For Understanding Your Own Desires

The Ladder of Love is a useful diagnostic tool. Ask yourself: what
do I actually want when I desire someone? A body? A soul? A sense
of completion? Immortality through children or creative work? The
*Symposium* pushes you to be honest about the structure of your
own eros.

### For Evaluating Relationships

Pausanias's distinction between common and heavenly love, crude as
it is, raises the right question: does this relationship make both
parties better? Is love a vehicle for mutual growth or just
mutual gratification? Diotima's account adds a further dimension:
does this relationship help you see the world more beautifully?

### For Thinking About Legacy

The procreation argument — that we love in order to achieve
immortality by leaving something behind — is a powerful framework
for thinking about ambition, creativity, and parenting. Why do we
want to have children? Why do we want to write books, build
companies, make art? The *Symposium* says: because we want to
live forever, and this is the only way mortals can do it. This
framework gives a powerful answer to the question of meaning:
we create because we love, and we love because we want what
matters to outlast us.

### For Reading Plato

The *Symposium* is the best entry point to Plato's middle-period
thought. Read it after the early dialogues (to see the shift from
aporetic to constructive) and before the *Republic* (where the
Theory of Forms receives its full treatment) and the *Phaedrus*
(which revisits love from the perspective of divine madness).
Each dialogue corrects and complements the other, and together
they form Plato's most complete statement on the nature of
erotic desire and its place in the philosophical life.

## 8. Open Questions for Further Study

1.  Is Aristophanes' myth compatible with Diotima's ascent, or
    do they offer fundamentally different accounts of love?

2.  Why does Plato frame the dialogue with such elaborate
    narrative distance? What does the frame tell us about how to
    read the central doctrine?

3.  Does the *Symposium* endorse Socrates's behavior with
    Alcibiades, or criticize it?

4.  Can the inclusive reading (Price) be reconciled with the
    language of "despising" the lower stages?

5.  How does the *Symposium* relate to the *Phaedrus*, which
    offers a more positive account of erotic madness?

6.  Is Diotima a real historical figure or entirely Plato's
    fiction, and what difference does the answer make?

7.  What would an Aristotelian or Kantian critique of Diotima's
    eudaimonism look like?
