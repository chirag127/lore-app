## Writing Style & Voice

Weinschenk writes with the clarity of an experienced educator. Each chapter follows the same pattern: a question or scenario, the research finding, and the design implication. The voice is authoritative but not academic — she translates research into plain language without dumbing it down. The writing is efficient; every sentence serves the goal of conveying useful information.

## Narrative Structure

The book is organized thematically into 10 sections (How People See, Read, Remember, Think, Decide, etc.), each containing 10 chapters. Within each section, chapters are independent and can be read in any order. The structure is reference-like but more browseable than an encyclopedia.

## Rhetorical Techniques

Weinschenk uses the "surprising research finding" as her primary rhetorical device: "Did you know that people cannot actually multitask?" Each chapter opens with an intriguing claim, presents the evidence, and then reveals the design implication. This pattern makes even familiar research feel novel.

## Readability & Accessibility

Highly readable. Each 2-3 page chapter can be read in a few minutes. Technical terms are explained. No psychology background required. Suitable for anyone who designs anything.

## Comparative Context

Weinschenk's book is the most accessible entry point for design psychology. It is less theoretically ambitious than Norman's work, less comprehensive than Lidwell's encyclopedia, and more science-based than Krug's practical guide. It occupies a unique niche: the bridge between academic psychology and everyday design practice.
