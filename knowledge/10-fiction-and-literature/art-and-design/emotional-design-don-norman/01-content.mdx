## Three Levels of Processing: Visceral, Behavioral, Reflective

Norman's central model divides human emotional response into three interconnected levels, each corresponding to a different layer of brain processing. Visceral processing is the most basic — the automatic, pre-wired reactions we have to sensory input. This is fight-or-flight, attraction-repulsion, the gut feeling that something is beautiful or ugly. Visceral design is about look, feel, sound, and smell — the immediate physical impact of an object.

Behavioral processing is about use — the pleasure and effectiveness of performing a task. This is the realm of usability, function, and performance. Behavioral design is what *The Design of Everyday Things* focused on: making things that work well, are easy to learn, and provide satisfaction in use. Norman now argues that behavioral design alone is insufficient.

Reflective processing is the highest level — it involves conscious thought, interpretation, and meaning-making. This is where culture, identity, memory, and self-image come into play. Reflective design is about the story a product tells, the status it confers, and the memories it evokes. A watch inherited from a grandparent has value far beyond its functional utility because of reflective processing.

## Why Attractive Things Work Better

Norman reviews evidence from cognitive neuroscience showing that positive affect — feeling happy, comfortable, or pleased — enhances creative problem-solving and broadens attention. This is the "broaden-and-build" theory of positive emotions developed by Barbara Fredrickson. Aesthetic pleasure puts the brain in a state more tolerant of minor difficulties, making products seem easier to use. Ugly or frustrating designs induce stress and narrow attention, making even simple tasks feel harder. This is why attractive things genuinely work better: not because aesthetics improve physical function, but because they change the user's cognitive state.

## The Three Levels of Design

Norman maps his three processing levels onto three corresponding design approaches. Visceral design focuses on appearance: shape, color, texture, weight. This is the domain of industrial designers and stylists. Behavioral design focuses on usability: function, performance, ergonomics. This is the domain of usability engineers and interaction designers. Reflective design focuses on meaning: personal significance, cultural message, self-image. This is the domain of branding, advertising, and product narrative.

Successful products integrate all three. The iPod succeeded not just because it worked well (behavioral) or looked beautiful (visceral) but because it made owners feel cool and part of a cultural movement (reflective). Norman explores how different products emphasize different levels: a well-designed teakettle might excel at visceral appeal, a medical device must prioritize behavioral performance, and a luxury watch is almost pure reflective value.

## Robots, Emotion, and Social Interaction

Norman extends his analysis to autonomous robots and intelligent systems. He argues that for robots to interact effectively with humans, they need emotional systems — not because emotions are nice, but because they are computationally necessary. Emotion guides decision-making in complex environments by providing rapid evaluation (this is good/bad, safe/dangerous). Robots without emotional systems would be paralyzed by infinite deliberation. This chapter prefigures Norman's later work on human-machine interaction in *The Design of Future Things*.

## Design for Pleasure

A significant portion of the book focuses on pleasure beyond mere usability. Norman explores seven sources of product pleasure identified by Tiger: sensory pleasure of materials and textures, emotional pleasure of attachment and memory, intellectual pleasure of mastery and understanding, and social pleasure of shared identity and status. He argues that design education overemphasizes cognitive usability and neglects the emotional and social dimensions of the user experience.
