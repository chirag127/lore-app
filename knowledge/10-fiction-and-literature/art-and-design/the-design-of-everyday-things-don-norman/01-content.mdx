## The Psychopathology of Everyday Things

Norman opens with the fundamental paradox of modern life: simple objects like doors, faucets, and light switches regularly defeat intelligent people. The culprit is not human incompetence but design that violates basic psychological principles. He introduces the concept of affordances — the perceived and actual properties of an object that determine how it could be used. A flat plate on a door affords pushing; a handle affords pulling. When design violates these perceived affordances, confusion results.

Signifiers are the perceptual signals that indicate where an action should take place. Unlike affordances, which are relationships between an object and a user, signifiers are the visible indicators — arrows, labels, icons, physical shapes — that communicate possibility. Norman argues that good design makes affordances and signifiers work together intuitively.

## The Seven Stages of Action

Norman presents his most famous conceptual model: the seven stages of action. When a person interacts with anything, they cycle through seven steps:
1. Goal (what they want to accomplish)
2. Plan (how to do it)
3. Specify (the sequence of actions)
4. Perform (execute the action)
5. Perceive (the state of the world)
6. Interpret (what the change means)
7. Compare (does it match the goal?)

This framework reveals two critical gaps in interaction. The Gulf of Execution is the difference between what the user wants to do and what the object allows them to do. The Gulf of Evaluation is the difference between what the object communicates and what the user understands. Good design minimizes both gulfs.

## Knowledge in the World vs. Knowledge in the Head

A central distinction: people rely on two types of knowledge. Knowledge in the world is information available in the environment — labels, instructions, physical constraints. Knowledge in the head is what we remember. Norman argues that good design takes advantage of both, but should not require the user to remember critical information. He introduces four types of constraints that guide behavior without requiring memory: physical (doors only open one way), semantic (the natural meaning of a situation), cultural (social conventions like red for stop), and logical (natural relationships like mapping of burners to controls).

## The Design of Everyday Things: The Case of the Door

Norman devotes attention to one of the most ubiquitous yet confusing objects: the door. A well-designed door requires no instruction — you know immediately whether to push or pull. A poorly designed door has handles that suggest pulling when the door only opens by pushing. This simple example illustrates the relationship between signifiers, affordances, and conceptual models throughout the book.

## Human Error and The Design of Failures

Norman challenges the common assumption that human error is the cause of accidents. In aviation, medicine, and industrial settings, what appears as operator error usually results from systems designed without consideration of human cognition. He distinguishes between slips (unintended actions) and mistakes (incorrect goals or plans). Root cause analysis reveals that most errors trace back to design choices that set people up to fail. Norman advocates for forcing functions — design features that prevent errors before they happen — and forgiving design that makes errors reversible.

## Design Thinking and Human-Centered Design

The final chapters outline Norman's approach to design thinking: a human-centered, iterative process grounded in observation and testing. He emphasizes that designers are not typical users and cannot rely on intuition about what people will find usable. The only reliable method is to test designs with real users, observe where they struggle, and iterate. This process — observe, ideate, prototype, test, repeat — has become the foundation of modern design thinking. Norman also discusses the tension between design for usability and design for aesthetics, noting that the best designs satisfy both.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment
This summary captures the core conceptual framework of affordances, signifiers, the seven stages of action, and the Gulf of Execution/Evaluation. It omits the book's extended case studies on industrial accidents, toilet design, and the failures of early computer interfaces.

### Recommended Reading Path
| Reader Type | Time | What to Read |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | ~15 min | This summary |
| Interested | ~2-4 hr | Summary + Chapters 1, 5, 7 |
| Designer/Practitioner | ~10-15 hr | Full book + exercises |

### Chapters to Read in Full (if not reading the whole book)
- **Chapter 1** — All key concepts (affordances, signifiers, conceptual models)
- **Chapter 5** — Human error and the design of failures
- **Chapter 7** — User-centered design process

### Chapters to Skim or Skip
- **Chapter 3** — Extended examples from early computer interfaces (dated)

### What You'll Miss by Not Reading the Full Book
- Rich case studies: Three Mile Island, the Titanic, medical device errors
- The full depth of the seven stages of action analysis
- Norman's revised thinking in the 2013 edition on affordances vs. signifiers
