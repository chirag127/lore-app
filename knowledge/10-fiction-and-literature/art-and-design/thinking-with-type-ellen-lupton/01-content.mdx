## Letter: Anatomy and Classification

Lupton begins with the building blocks: the anatomy of letterforms. She introduces the terminology — ascender, descender, x-height, cap height, baseline, serif, counter, bowl, stem, terminal — that enables precise discussion of type. Understanding this vocabulary is essential for communicating about typography with other designers and for making informed type choices.

Type classification provides a historical framework. Lupton covers the major categories: Old Style (Garamond), Transitional (Baskerville), Modern (Bodoni), and Sans Serif (Helvetica, Futura). She explains the visual characteristics of each category and their appropriate uses. Slab serifs, scripts, and display types get brief treatment. The emphasis is on understanding how typeface design reflects technological and cultural context.

## Letter: Typefaces and Fonts

Lupton clarifies the distinction between typeface (the design) and font (the file). She discusses how digital technology has transformed typography: anyone can now access thousands of typefaces, but this abundance creates new challenges of choice and quality. Licensing, webfonts, and the economics of type design are covered briefly.

## Text: Size, Line Spacing, and Measure

The "text" section addresses the practical craft of setting readable text. Lupton explains how type size (traditionally measured in points), line spacing or leading (the vertical space between lines), and measure (line length) interact to affect readability. The classic guideline: 45-75 characters per line is optimal for reading comfort. Leading should generally be 2-5 points larger than the type size. These principles apply to both print and screen.

## Text: Hierarchy and Structure

Visual hierarchy guides the reader through content. Lupton shows how size, weight, color, spacing, and position create levels of importance. A clear hierarchy helps readers find what they need and understand the structure of information. She advocates for establishing a clear typographic scale — a planned range of sizes for different levels of headings and body text — rather than choosing sizes arbitrarily.

## Text: Alignment and Formatting

Lupton covers the four alignment options: flush left (most common for extended reading), flush right (used for specific effects), centered (for headings and invitations), and justified (creates clean edges but can produce rivers of white space). She discusses hyphenation, widows, orphans, and other typographic details that distinguish professional from amateur typesetting.

## Grid: The Grid as a Framework

Grids bring order to complex layouts. Lupton traces the grid's history from modernist design (Jan Tschichold, Josef Muller-Brockmann) to contemporary web design (CSS grid, Bootstrap). A grid is a system of columns and gutters that provides structure for placing text and images. Lupton presents grid basics: column grids (newspapers), modular grids (brochures), and hierarchical grids (websites). She emphasizes that grids should serve content, not constrain it.

## Grid: Type on Screen

The final section addresses typography for digital media. Screen typography differs fundamentally from print: resolution is lower, reading distance is greater, and the user controls the viewing environment. We've come a long way since early web designers had only a handful of system fonts, but the principles of good typography remain constant: readability, hierarchy, and appropriateness.
