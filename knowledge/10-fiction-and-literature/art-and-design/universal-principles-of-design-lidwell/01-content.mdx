## Structure and Format

Each of the 125 principles follows a consistent two-page format: the left page provides the principle name, definition, and detailed explanation with research references; the right page shows visual examples and case studies. Principles are organized alphabetically rather than thematically, encouraging serendipitous discovery. Cross-references at the bottom of each entry connect related principles, creating a web of interconnected knowledge rather than a linear taxonomy.

## Design Principles: A Representative Selection

**80/20 Rule (Pareto Principle):** Roughly 80% of effects come from 20% of causes. In design, this means focusing on the 20% of features that deliver 80% of the value. Examples include software where 20% of functions account for 80% of usage.

**Accessibility:** Design for users of all abilities. Principles include providing equivalent experiences, considering situational disabilities, and designing for universal access. The book covers specific guidelines for visual, auditory, motor, and cognitive accessibility.

**Affordance:** A property in which the physical characteristics of an object influence its function. A button affords pushing; a handle affords pulling. The concept, drawn from J.J. Gibson and popularized by Don Norman, is one of the most referenced principles in design.

**Chunking:** Grouping information into meaningful units to improve processing and memory. Phone numbers are chunked (555-867-5309), not presented as a continuous string. Chunking applies to interface design, information architecture, and instructional design.

**Cognitive Dissonance:** The mental discomfort experienced when holding contradictory beliefs or when behavior conflicts with beliefs. Design can reduce dissonance by providing consistent experiences and confirming user choices. The principle explains why post-purchase rationalization affects user satisfaction.

**Constraint:** Limiting the range of possible actions to guide users toward correct behavior. Physical constraints (a key only fits one way), semantic constraints (cultural conventions), logical constraints (natural relationships), and cultural constraints (social norms) all guide behavior without requiring instructions.

**Fitts' Law:** The time to acquire a target is a function of the distance to the target and its size. Larger, closer targets are faster to acquire. This principle governs button size, menu placement, and touch target design in interfaces.

**Hierarchy of Needs:** A design must satisfy basic needs (functionality) before higher needs (reliability, usability, proficiency, creativity). Based on Maslow's hierarchy, this principle reminds designers that aesthetics cannot compensate for broken functionality.

**Mapping:** The relationship between controls and their effects. Good mapping makes relationships obvious (stove burner controls arranged to match burner layout). Poor mapping requires labels and learning.

**Recognition Over Recall:** Memory for recognizing familiar items is better than memory for recalling items from scratch. Interfaces should present options for recognition (menus, icons) rather than requiring recall (commands, codes).

**Visibility:** The more visible functions are, the more likely users will understand and use them. Critical controls and information should be prominent; less important features can be progressively disclosed.

## How to Use the Book

The authors suggest several approaches: read cover-to-cover for a broad design education, use as a reference when facing specific design problems, or browse randomly for inspiration. Cross-references at the end of each entry encourage exploration of related principles, creating a non-linear learning experience.
