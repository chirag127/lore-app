## Writing Style & Voice

The writing is deliberately neutral and encyclopedic. Each entry follows the same format — definition, explanation, examples — with minimal authorial voice. The goal is clarity and utility, not personality. This approach suits the reference format but lacks the engaging voice of Norman or Krug.

## Narrative Structure

There is no narrative structure — the book is alphabetically organized for reference use. This is a deliberate design choice that prioritizes findability over narrative flow. The cross-references at the bottom of each entry provide the only connective tissue between entries.

## Rhetorical Techniques

The book's primary rhetorical strategy is the claim to universality and evidence. By presenting principles as scientific findings rather than designer opinions, the authors establish authority. The consistent two-page format builds trust through predictability — readers know what they will get.

## Readability & Accessibility

Highly readable in small doses. Each two-page entry is self-contained. The writing is clear and jargon-free where possible. Technical terms from psychology or engineering are explained. Best used as a reference rather than read cover-to-cover.

## Comparative Context

In design literature, this is a unique work — a reference encyclopedia rather than a methodology book or manifesto. Its nearest relatives are design pattern collections like Alexander's *A Pattern Language* and Borchers' *A Pattern Approach to Interaction Design*. The book's success spawned imitators like *100 Things Every Designer Needs to Know* and *Design Thinking* references.
