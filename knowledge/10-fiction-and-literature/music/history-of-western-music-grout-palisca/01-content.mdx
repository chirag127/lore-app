## Part I: The Ancient and Medieval Worlds

### Chapter 1-2: Music in Antiquity and the Early Middle Ages

The book opens with the music of ancient Greece and Rome, including Greek theories of ethos and the mathematical basis of pitch. It then traces the development of Gregorian chant, the earliest preserved body of Western music. The liturgical context of chant, the modal system, and the evolution of music notation from neumes to staff notation are explained.

### Chapter 3-4: The Romanesque and Gothic Eras

The development of polyphony -- music in multiple simultaneous voices -- was the decisive innovation of the medieval period. The book traces the evolution from organum (parallel voices) to the Notre Dame school of Leonin and Perotin, who composed the first polyphonic music with measured rhythm.

### Chapter 5-6: The Ars Nova and the Fourteenth Century

The fourteenth century brought rhythmic complexity, secular influences, and the compositional innovations of Guillaume de Machaut. The book covers the development of isorhythm, the mass ordinary, and the flourishing of secular song.

## Part II: The Renaissance (c. 1450-1600)

### Chapter 7-9: The Renaissance

The Renaissance in music was characterized by the flowering of imitative polyphony, the rise of the secular madrigal and chanson, and the perfection of sacred forms (mass, motet). Key figures include Josquin des Prez, Palestrina, Lassus, and Byrd. The book traces the spread of music printing, the Reformation's impact on sacred music, and the development of instrumental music.

## Part III: The Baroque Era (c. 1600-1750)

### Chapter 10-13: The Baroque

The Baroque era saw the invention of opera, the development of functional tonality, the rise of the virtuoso performer, and the standardization of instrumental forms (concerto, sonata, suite, fugue). Major figures include Monteverdi, Vivaldi, Bach, and Handel. The book provides detailed analysis of Bach's fugues, Handel's oratorios, and the development of the orchestra.

## Part IV: The Classical Era (c. 1750-1815)

### Chapter 14-17: The Classical Period

The Classical era emphasized clarity, balance, and formal structure. The book covers the development of sonata form, the symphony, the string quartet, and comic opera. Key figures: Haydn, Mozart, and the young Beethoven. The transition from the galant style to high classicism is traced through Haydn's symphonies and Mozart's operas.

## Part V: The Romantic Era (c. 1815-1900)

### Chapter 18-23: The Romantic Century

Romanticism brought expanded forms, heightened emotional expression, program music, and nationalism. The book covers the Lied (Schubert), the virtuoso tradition (Liszt, Paganini), absolute music (Brahms), program music (Berlioz, Liszt), opera (Wagner, Verdi), and the rise of nationalism in Russia, Bohemia, and Scandinavia.

## Part VI: The Twentieth Century and Beyond

### Chapter 24-28: Modernism and Postmodernism

The twentieth century brought the breakup of tonality, the emergence of atonality and serialism (Schoenberg, Webern), neoclassicism (Stravinsky), American experimentalism (Ives, Cage), minimalism (Reich, Glass), and the pluralism of the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries.
