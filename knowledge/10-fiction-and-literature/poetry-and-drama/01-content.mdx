## What Is the Poetics?

The *Poetics* is a compact, sometimes fragmentary treatise that Aristotle composed for his students at the Lyceum. Its Greek title *Peri poietikês* means "On the Poetic Art." The surviving text covers epic poetry and tragedy, with a lost second book that dealt with comedy. Aristotle's method is empirical and taxonomic: he examines existing Greek poetry — chiefly Homer's epics and the tragedies of Aeschylus, Sophocles, and Euripides — and extracts general principles from them.

The treatise opens by classifying poetry by its medium (language, rhythm, melody), its objects (agents who are better, worse, or like us), and its mode (narrative or dramatic). This three-part framework establishes the analytical vocabulary for everything that follows.

## Mimesis: Aristotle's Answer to Plato

Plato, in Book X of the *Republic*, had condemned poetry as a copy of a copy — an imitation of the sensory world, which itself is merely an imitation of the Forms. For Plato, the poet is a purveyor of illusions who appeals to the lower parts of the soul and leads audiences away from truth and virtue.

Aristotle fundamentally redefines mimesis. For him, imitation is not a debased copy but a natural and universal human instinct. Humans learn through imitation, and poetry's imitations are not of mere appearances but of actions, character, and human life in its universal aspects. Where the historian records what happened (particulars), the poet represents what could happen according to probability or necessity (universals). This makes poetry "more philosophical and more serious than history."

## Definition of Tragedy

Aristotle's definition of tragedy in Chapter 6 is the most quoted passage in the *Poetics*:

> Tragedy is an imitation of an action that is serious, complete, and of a certain magnitude; in language embellished with each kind of artistic ornament, the several kinds being found in separate parts of the play; in the form of action, not of narrative; through pity and fear effecting the proper catharsis of these emotions.

Every clause is significant. The action must be serious (*spoudaios*) — dealing with matters of consequence. It must be complete and of a certain magnitude, meaning it has a beginning, middle, and end, and its scale allows the plot to unfold believably. "Embellished speech" refers to the rhythmic and musical elements. "In the form of action, not narrative" distinguishes drama from epic. And the purpose — the final cause — of tragedy is catharsis.

## The Six Elements of Tragedy

Aristotle identifies six qualitative parts of tragedy, ranked by importance:

**1. Plot (mythos).** The "soul of tragedy." Plot is the organisation of the incidents — the structural arrangement of the action. Aristotle insists that plot comes first, character second, because tragedy is an imitation of action, not of people. A tragedy can exist without distinctive characterisation but not without a coherent plot. The best plots are "complex" — they involve *peripeteia* (reversal of fortune) and *anagnorisis* (recognition), ideally occurring simultaneously, as in Sophocles' *Oedipus the King* where Oedipus's recognition of his true identity coincides with the reversal of his fortunes.

**2. Character (ethos).** Characters are the agents of the action. They must be four things: good (though not perfectly so — that would be shocking), appropriate (a woman should not be given manly courage), consistent (a character who changes should change plausibly), and consistently inconsistent if that is their nature. The ideal tragic hero is a person "not pre-eminently virtuous and just" who falls into misfortune "not through vice or depravity, but through some hamartia" — a mistake or error of judgment.

**3. Thought (dianoia).** The intellectual content — what characters say to prove or disprove a point, to express general truths, or to reveal their reasoning. Thought is the province of rhetoric within the play.

**4. Diction (lexis).** The expression of meaning through language — word choice, metaphor, and verbal art. Aristotle devotes three chapters (20–22) to linguistic analysis, discussing the different kinds of words, compound words, and the appropriate use of unfamiliar terms.

**5. Melody (melos).** The musical component, which in Greek tragedy included choral odes. The chorus should be treated as one of the actors and integrated into the whole.

**6. Spectacle (opsis).** The visual production — costumes, scenery, stage effects. Aristotle notoriously ranks spectacle as the least artistic element because it depends more on the stage machinist than on the poet. A good tragedy should produce its effect even when read, without performance.

## Catharsis

No concept in the *Poetics* has generated more debate than catharsis. The word appears only once in the treatise — in the definition of tragedy — but its interpretation affects how we understand the entire work.

There are three main readings:
- **Purgation (Jacob Bernays).** Tragedy provides a medical or emotional release, like a purge that rids the body of excess. The audience experiences pity and fear and is relieved of them.
- **Purification (less common today).** The emotions are not expelled but refined, purified of their selfish elements.
- **Clarification (Stephen Halliwell, Leon Golden, Martha Nussbaum).** Catharsis is an intellectual clarification — the audience gains understanding of the general nature of pity and fear through the specific representation on stage. This reading aligns with Aristotle's emphasis on poetry's cognitive value.

Aristotle himself gives remarkably little direct explanation, which is typical of the *Poetics*' compressed, note-like style.

## Hamartia, Peripeteia, Anagnorisis

These three interconnected concepts form the engine of complex tragedy:

**Hamartia** (often mistranslated as "tragic flaw") literally means "missing the mark" — an error of judgment. The tragic hero makes a mistake, sometimes from ignorance (Oedipus not knowing his parents' identity), sometimes from a morally understandable choice. It is not a character defect in the modern psychological sense; it is the action that sets the tragic mechanism in motion.

**Peripeteia** is the reversal of the situation from good to bad (or, in the less preferred form, from bad to good). It should arise from the internal logic of the plot, not from external intervention.

**Anagnorisis** is the change from ignorance to knowledge. The most powerful recognitions are those that coincide with the reversal, as when Oedipus learns the truth about his identity simultaneously with the destruction of his happiness.

## The Three Unities

A persistent myth about the *Poetics* is that Aristotle prescribed "three unities" of action, time, and place. What Aristotle actually says:

- **Unity of action:** explicitly required. A plot must represent a single, complete action, not a series of episodes.
- **Unity of time:** mentioned in passing — "Tragedy endeavours, as far as possible, to confine itself to a single revolution of the sun." This is a descriptive observation about Greek practice, not a prescriptive rule.
- **Unity of place: never mentioned.** The unity of place was added by Lodovico Castelvetro in his 1570 Italian commentary.

The three unities thus represent the Renaissance Neo-classical reading of Aristotle, not the *Poetics* itself.

## Comedy and Epic Poetry

The surviving *Poetics* focuses overwhelmingly on tragedy. Aristotle promises to discuss comedy later — treatment of "the ridiculous" and its emotional effect — but these sections were in the lost second book. The *Tractatus Coislinianus*, a later Byzantine summary, may preserve some of its content, but this is disputed.

On epic poetry, Aristotle compares it favourably to tragedy. Both are serious imitations of noble actions, but epic is narrative (not dramatic) and uses a single metre (dactylic hexameter). Homer is the supreme epic poet because his plots have the same structural unity as good tragedies. In Chapter 26, Aristotle argues that tragedy is superior to epic because it has more powerful emotional effects, achieves its effect in less time, and has music and spectacle in addition to language.

## Influence and Legacy

The *Poetics* was lost to the Western world during the Middle Ages and reached Europe through two channels: an Arabic translation via Averroes (12th century), which coloured interpretations with Islamic moral philosophy, and William of Moerbeke's Latin translation (1278), which was largely ignored. The treatise became influential only in the Renaissance, after Giorgio Valla's Latin translation (1498) and the publication of the Greek text by Aldus Manutius (1508).

From the sixteenth to the eighteenth centuries, the *Poetics* was treated as a rulebook for dramatic composition. Neo-classical critics — Castelvetro in Italy, Corneille and Boileau in France — derived rigid prescriptions from Aristotle's descriptive observations. In the twentieth century, many of Aristotle's concepts were recovered in more nuanced forms. The *Poetics* remains foundational for screenwriting manuals (the "Save the Cat" beat sheet is essentially an applied version of Aristotelian plot analysis), narrative theory, and genre studies.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment

This summary covers the major concepts and structure of the *Poetics* but omits several chapters of detailed linguistic analysis (chapters 20–22) and the treatment of critical objections (chapters 25). The compressed, note-like quality of the original is also impossible to convey in summary.

### Recommended Reading Path

| Reader Type | Time | What to Read |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | ~15 min | This summary |
| Interested | ~2–4 hr | This summary + the full Heath translation (Penguin) + introduction |
| Scholar | ~10–20 hr | Full text + Halliwell's commentary + Else's *The Argument* |

### Chapters to Read in Full (if not reading the whole book)

- **Chapters 1–5** — Classification of poetry and the origins of tragedy
- **Chapter 6** — The definition of tragedy and its six elements
- **Chapters 7–11** — Plot construction, peripeteia, anagnorisis
- **Chapters 13–14** — The best kind of tragic plot and the hamartia debate
- **Chapter 26** — Comparison of tragedy and epic

### Chapters to Skim or Skip

- **Chapters 20–22** — Detailed linguistic analysis of Greek parts of speech and metaphor; highly language-specific
- **Chapters 23–24** — Epic poetry analysis; useful but repetitive

### What You'll Miss by Not Reading the Full Book

- The compressed, dialectical texture of Aristotle's lecture notes — the feeling of watching a great mind think through a problem in real time
- The detailed examples from specific Greek plays that Aristotle assumes his audience knows
- The linguistic analysis, which illuminates how seriously Aristotle took the craft of poetic language
- Aristotle's answers to actual contemporary criticisms of poetry (Chapter 25), which reveal the debates of his time
