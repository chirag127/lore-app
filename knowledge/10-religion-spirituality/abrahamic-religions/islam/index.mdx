# Islam

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Islam originated in 7th-century Arabia with the prophethood of Muhammad ibn Abdullah, to whom the Quran — Islam's sacred scripture — was revealed over approximately 23 years. Muslims regard the Quran as the literal word of God (Allah) and Muhammad as the seal of the prophets in a line stretching from Adam through Abraham, Moses, and Jesus. Islam means "submission" to the divine will, and a Muslim is one who submits.

From its Arabian origins, Islam spread across the Middle East, North Africa, Persia, South Asia, and Southeast Asia, developing rich theological (kalam), legal (fiqh), philosophical, and mystical (Sufi) traditions. The Sunni-Shia split, originating in the question of succession after Muhammad's death, remains the most significant internal division. Today Islam has approximately 1.9 billion adherents worldwide.

## What Belongs Here

Quranic studies (tafsir, asbab al-nuzul), hadith criticism, Islamic law (Sharia, fiqh, the five legal schools), Islamic theology (kalam — Ash'ari, Maturidi, Mu'tazili), Shi'a Islam (Twelver, Ismaili, Zaydi), Islamic philosophy (Avicenna, Averroes, Al-Ghazali), Islamic ethics, Islamic art and architecture in religious context, modern Islam (Islamism, reform movements, Islamic feminism).

## What Does NOT Belong Here

Sufism as a distinct mystical tradition (belongs in Sufi Mysticism), Islamic history as secular historiography (belongs in History of the Islamic World), Islamic art separated from religious context (belongs in Art History), the historical study of the Quran's textual transmission from a secular-critical perspective (belongs in Historical Criticism).

## Key Scriptures/Texts

- **Quran** — 114 surahs revealed to Muhammad
- **Hadith** — Collections of prophetic sayings (Bukhari, Muslim, Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi, Ibn Majah, Nasa'i)
- **Sira** — Biographies of the Prophet (Ibn Ishaq, Ibn Hisham, al-Tabari)
- **Muwatta of Malik** — The earliest surviving legal work
- **Al-Ghazali**: The Revival of the Religious Sciences (Ihya Ulum al-Din)
- **Ibn Rushd (Averroes)**: The Incoherence of the Incoherence

## Key Concepts

Tawhid (oneness of God), Risala (prophethood), Akhira (afterlife), Five Pillars (Shahada, Salat, Zakat, Sawm, Hajj), Jihad (struggle — greater and lesser), Sharia (divine law), Ijma (consensus), Qiyas (analogical reasoning), Ijtihad (independent legal reasoning), Sunna (prophetic custom), Ummah (global community of believers).

## Connections

- **Judaism** and **Christianity** share prophetic figures, moral teachings, and the monotheistic framework.
- **Sufi Mysticism** represents Islam's esoteric and contemplative dimension.
- **Comparative Religion** situates Islam among world religious traditions.
- **Religion & Society** addresses Islam's role in modern politics and secular states.
