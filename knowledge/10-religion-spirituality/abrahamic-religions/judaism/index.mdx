# Judaism

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Judaism is the oldest of the Abrahamic religions, tracing its origins to the covenant between God and Abraham approximately 4,000 years ago. Its foundational document is the Torah (the Five Books of Moses), which together with the Talmud — the vast corpus of rabbinic legal and interpretive literature — forms the basis of Jewish law (halakha) and practice. Judaism's development spans biblical Israelite religion, Second Temple Judaism, rabbinic Judaism, medieval Jewish philosophy (Maimonides, Judah Halevi), and the modern movements of Orthodox, Conservative, Reform, and Reconstructionist Judaism.

As the root tradition from which both Christianity and Islam emerged, Judaism merits a distinct section to properly treat its unique covenantal theology, legal tradition, liturgical life, and historical experience — including the exile, diaspora, persecution, the Holocaust, and the establishment of the State of Israel.

## What Belongs Here

The Hebrew Bible (Tanakh), Talmudic literature, Jewish philosophy and theology (Maimonides' Guide for the Perplexed, Buber, Rosenzweig, Heschel), Jewish mysticism (Kabbalah, Hasidism — though see Jewish Mysticism for detailed treatment), Jewish ethics, modern Jewish movements, Zionism, antisemitism and Holocaust studies, Jewish liturgy and lifecycle events, Hebrew language studies insofar as they relate to religious texts.

## What Does NOT Belong Here

Biblical archaeology (belongs in Archaeology or Ancient History), the history of ancient Israel as secular historiography (belongs in History of the Ancient Near East), Christian or Islamic theology that uses Jewish sources as proof-texts, standalone treatments of Kabbalah that belong in Jewish Mysticism.

## Key Scriptures/Texts

- **Tanakh**: Torah (Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, Deuteronomy), Nevi'im (Prophets), Ketuvim (Writings)
- **Talmud**: Mishnah (c. 200 CE) and Gemara (c. 500 CE) — Babylonian and Jerusalem Talmuds
- **Midrash**: Exegetical and homiletical interpretations (Midrash Rabbah, Midrash Tanhuma)
- **Mishneh Torah** (Maimonides) — Codification of Jewish law
- **Shulchan Aruch** (Joseph Karo) — The most widely accepted code of Jewish law
- **Siddur** — The Jewish prayer book

## Key Concepts

Covenant (brit), Torah (teaching/law), Mitzvah (commandment), Halakha (Jewish law), Tikkun Olam (repairing the world), Tzedakah (righteousness/charity), Teshuvah (repentance), Shema (central prayer affirming God's unity), Kashrut (dietary laws), Shabbat (Sabbath), Brit Milah (circumcision), Bar/Bat Mitzvah (coming of age).

## Connections

- **Christianity** and **Islam** share Judaism's monotheistic foundation and many prophetic figures.
- **Jewish Mysticism** treats the esoteric dimensions (Kabbalah, Hasidism) in greater depth.
- **Comparative Religion** contextualizes Judaism among world religions.
- **Religion & Society** examines Judaism's role in modern politics, Zionism, and secularization.
