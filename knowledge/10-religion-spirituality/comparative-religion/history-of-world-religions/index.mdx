# History of World Religions

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

The history of world religions traces the emergence and evolution of religious traditions from prehistory to the present day. It covers the earliest forms of religious expression (Paleolithic burial practices, Neolithic mother goddess figurines, shamanism), the rise of ancient polytheistic systems (Mesopotamian, Egyptian, Greek, Roman, Norse), the Axial Age (c. 800–200 BCE) that produced Confucianism, Daoism, Buddhism, Hinduism's philosophical schools, Zoroastrianism, and Hebrew prophecy, the spread of Christianity and Islam, and the modern encounter between religions and secularization.

This historical perspective is indispensable for comparative study: it reveals how religions borrow from, react against, and develop alongside one another. Understanding the historical context of a tradition's emergence is essential for interpreting its doctrines and practices.

## What Belongs Here

Prehistoric religion, ancient Near Eastern religions, classical Greco-Roman religion, the Axial Age thesis (Karl Jaspers), the spread and institutionalization of world religions, missionary movements, religious syncretism (Hellenistic mystery cults, Afro-Caribbean traditions), secularization theory from a historical perspective, new religious movements in historical context.

## What Does NOT Belong Here

The internal theological or doctrinal development of a particular tradition (belongs in that tradition's subcategory), the sociology or anthropology of religion as systematic disciplines (belongs in sociology or anthropology), philosophical arguments for or against religious claims (belongs in Philosophy of Religion).

## Key Scriptures/Texts

- Mircea Eliade, A History of Religious Ideas (3 vols.)
- Karen Armstrong, A History of God
- Huston Smith, The World's Religions
- Karl Jaspers, The Origin and Goal of History (Axial Age thesis)
- Ninian Smart, The World's Religious Traditions
- Jonathan Z. Smith, Relating Religion

## Key Concepts

Axial Age, syncretism, polytheism, monotheism, henotheism, animism, shamanism, myth and ritual, sacred vs. profane (Eliade), hierophany, the Great Transformation (Karen Armstrong), religious diffusion, reform movements, canon formation.

## Connections

- Individual traditions (**Judaism**, **Christianity**, **Islam**, **Hinduism**, **Buddhism**, etc.) provide the detailed doctrinal and practical content.
- **Interfaith Dialogue** builds on historical understanding to foster present-day engagement.
- **Religion & Society** examines the contemporary outcomes of the historical processes documented here.
- **Comparative Religion** as a whole provides the methodological framework for historical comparison.
