# Interfaith Dialogue

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Interfaith dialogue emerged as a formal discipline in the 20th century, driven by the recognition that religious pluralism is an enduring feature of modern societies and that religious misunderstanding has been a source of conflict. The 1893 World's Parliament of Religions in Chicago is often cited as the birth of the modern interfaith movement, but sustained institutional dialogue accelerated after Vatican II (1962–1965), whose declaration Nostra Aetate opened the Catholic Church to positive relations with other religions.

Today interfaith work encompasses theological dialogue (comparative theology, scriptural reasoning), practical cooperation (interfaith service projects, peacebuilding), and spiritual dialogue (shared meditation, interfaith prayer). It raises deep questions about truth, salvation, and religious identity: can contradictory truth claims both be valid? Is dialogue a path to truth or a pragmatic accommodation? Should participants bracket their confessional commitments?

## What Belongs Here

Comparative theology (Francis Clooney, Keith Ward), scriptural reasoning, interfaith peacebuilding, interfaith organizations (World Council of Churches, Parliament of the World's Religions, URI), interfaith education, pluralism theory (John Hick, Diana Eck), religious inclusivism and exclusivism, interfaith worship and shared spiritual practice, interfaith dialogue in divided societies.

## What Does NOT Belong Here

The independent historical study of individual religions (belongs in History of World Religions or the specific tradition), sociological surveys of religious diversity (belongs in Religion & Society or sociology), apologetics aimed at conversion, comparative religion as an academic methodology (belongs in the parent Comparative Religion category).

## Key Scriptures/Texts

- Nostra Aetate (Second Vatican Council, 1965)
- John Hick, God and the Universe of Faiths (pluralist hypothesis)
- Diana Eck, Encountering God: A Spiritual Journey from Bozeman to Banaras
- Raimon Panikkar, The Intrareligious Dialogue
- Catherine Cornille, The Im-possibility of Interreligious Dialogue
- Francis Clooney, Comparative Theology: Deep Learning Across Religious Borders

## Key Concepts

Exclusivism, inclusivism, pluralism (the threefold typology), comparative theology, scriptural reasoning, double belonging, religious hybridity, confessional vs. secular dialogue, transformative dialogue, witness vs. proselytism, receptive ecumenism, interfaith literacy.

## Connections

- **History of World Religions** provides the historical context for understanding religious diversity.
- **Religion & Society** examines the social and political conditions that enable or obstruct dialogue.
- All specific religious traditions (**Judaism**, **Christianity**, **Islam**, **Hinduism**, **Buddhism**, etc.) are conversation partners in interfaith encounter.
- **Comparative Religion** is the methodological parent discipline.
