# Jainism

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Jainism traces its origins to a line of 24 Tirthankaras ("ford-makers" who have crossed the river of samsara), the most recent of whom was Mahavira (c. 599–527 BCE), a contemporary of the Buddha. Jain teaching holds that the universe is eternal and uncreated, governed by a cosmic law of karma understood as a subtle material substance that binds the soul (jiva). Liberation (kevala, kevala-jnana) is achieved through the complete removal of karmic matter by rigorous ascetic practice and ethical purity.

Jainism's most distinctive contribution is its radical commitment to ahimsa (non-violence), which extends to all living beings and governs every aspect of Jain life — diet, occupation, even thought. This commitment has given Jainism an outsized ethical influence on Indian culture far beyond its numbers. Jains comprise about 0.4% of India's population today but have profoundly shaped Indian vegetarianism, environmental ethics, and non-violent political philosophy (including Gandhi's satyagraha).

## What Belongs Here

The life and teachings of Mahavira and the other Tirthankaras, Jain cosmology (the three worlds, time cycles), Jain epistemology (anekantavada — many-sidedness, syadvada — conditional predication), Jain ethics (the five great vows), Jain scriptures (Agamas), Digambara and Shvetambara sectarian differences, Jain monasticism and lay practice, Jain art and temple architecture, Jain contributions to Indian philosophy and logic, Jain diaspora communities.

## What Does NOT Belong Here

Buddhist or Hindu philosophy that parallels Jain ideas (belongs in their respective subcategories), the secular history of Jain communities (belongs in Indian History), Indian vegetarianism as a secular practice (belongs in Food Studies or Indian Culture), Gandhi's political philosophy except as directly influenced by Jain thought (Gandhi belongs in Indian History/Mohandas Gandhi).

## Key Scriptures/Texts

- **Purvas** (lost ancient texts, known only through later summaries)
- **Agamas/ Siddhanta**: The canon of Jain scriptures (45 texts for Shvetambaras)
- **Tattvartha Sutra** (Umasvati/Umaswami) — The most authoritative Jain philosophical text
- **Kalpa Sutra** (Bhadrabahu) — Biographies of the Tirthankaras
- **Yoga Shastra** (Hemachandra) — Jain ethics and practice
- **Samayasara** (Kundakunda) — Spiritual exposition in the Digambara tradition

## Key Concepts

Ahimsa (non-violence), Anekantavada (many-sidedness of reality), Syadvada (conditional predication), Karma (subtle matter binding the soul), Jiva (soul), Ajiva (non-soul), Kevala-Jnana (omniscience), Samsara (cycle of rebirth), Moksha (liberation), Five Great Vows (Ahimsa, Satya — truth, Asteya — non-stealing, Brahmacharya — chastity, Aparigraha — non-attachment), Digambara (sky-clad), Shvetambara (white-clad).

## Connections

- **Hinduism** and **Buddhism** share the Indian framework of karma, rebirth, and liberation.
