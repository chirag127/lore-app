# Sikhism

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Sikhism was founded in the 15th–16th century in the Punjab region of South Asia by Guru Nanak (1469–1539), who taught the oneness of God (Ik Onkar), the equality of all humans (rejecting caste and gender hierarchy), and the importance of truthful living over empty ritual. Nine successive Gurus developed and institutionalized the tradition, culminating in Guru Gobind Singh (1666–1708), who established the Khalsa (the community of initiated Sikhs) and declared the Guru Granth Sahib — the Sikh scripture — as the eternal living Guru.

Sikhism synthesizes elements of Bhakti devotionalism (drawn from Hinduism) and Islamic monotheism while maintaining a distinct identity. It is the world's fifth-largest organized religion, with approximately 25–30 million adherents, concentrated in Punjab but with significant diaspora communities worldwide.

## What Belongs Here

The lives and teachings of the ten Gurus (Guru Nanak, Guru Angad, Guru Amar Das, Guru Ram Das, Guru Arjan, Guru Tegh Bahadur, Guru Gobind Singh, and others), the Guru Granth Sahib (structure, theology, language), Sikh history (Mughal persecution, the Singh Sabha reform movement, Khalistan movement), Sikh theology (Ik Onkar, the nature of God, grace and karma), Sikh ethics and the Rahit Maryada (code of conduct), the Khalsa and the Five Ks, Sikh institutions (Gurdwara, Langar, Pangat), Sikh music and scripture study (Kirtan), Sikh diaspora communities.

## What Does NOT Belong Here

The secular history of the Punjab region (belongs in History of South Asia), Hindu Bhakti or Islamic Sufism as independent traditions (belongs in Hinduism or Sufi Mysticism), the political Khalistan movement as a contemporary political issue rather than a religious phenomenon (belongs in Political Science), comparative analysis with other monotheistic traditions (belongs in Comparative Religion).

## Key Scriptures/Texts

- **Guru Granth Sahib** (Adi Granth) — 1,430 pages of hymns and poetry composed by the Gurus, Hindu and Muslim saints including Kabir, Namdev, and Baba Farid
- **Dasam Granth** — Traditionally attributed to Guru Gobind Singh (authorship debated), includes the Jap Sahib and Chandi Di Var
- **Varan Bhai Gurdas** — Commentary and explication of Sikh doctrine
- **Rahit Maryada** — The Sikh Code of Conduct (20th century, published by the Shiromani Gurdwara Parbandhak Committee)

## Key Concepts

Ik Onkar (One God), Waheguru (Wonderful Teacher/God), Naam Japo (meditation on God's name), Kirat Karo (honest labor), Vand Chakko (sharing with others), Seva (selfless service), Simran (remembrance of God), Haumai (ego — the root of separation from God), Hukam (divine order), Chardi Kala (optimistic resilience), the Five Ks: Kesh (uncut hair), Kangha (comb), Kachera (shorts), Kirpan (sword/dagger), Kara (steel bracelet).

## Connections

- **Hinduism** contributed the Bhakti devotional framework and concepts of karma and rebirth.
- **Islam**, particularly Sufi mysticism, influenced Sikh monotheism and the concept of divine unity.
- **Comparative Religion** studies Sikhism's unique synthesis of traditions.
- **Religion & Society** examines Sikh identity in multicultural societies, turban controversies, and Khalistani separatism.
