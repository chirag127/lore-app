# Confucianism

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Confucianism is a philosophical, ethical, and political tradition originating with Kong Qiu (Confucius, 551–479 BCE) in the Spring and Autumn period of Chinese history. The Analects — sayings of Confucius compiled by his disciples — form the core text. Later developments include Mencius (Mencius, c. 372–289 BCE), who argued for the innate goodness of human nature, and Xunzi (c. 310–235 BCE), who took the opposite view. Confucianism became the official state ideology of imperial China from the Han dynasty (206 BCE–220 CE) until the fall of the Qing dynasty (1912), dominating civil service examinations, education, and family life.

Whether Confucianism is a religion or a philosophical system remains debated. It involves no worship of a personal creator god, yet it includes reverence for Heaven (Tian), ancestor veneration, and ritual (li) practices that function as religious activities. Neo-Confucianism (Zhu Xi, Wang Yangming, 11th–17th centuries) synthesized Confucian ethics with Buddhist and Daoist metaphysics.

## What Belongs Here

The Analects and their commentaries, Mencius and Xunzi, the Five Classics (Classic of Poetry, Documents, Changes/Rites, Spring and Autumn Annals) and Four Books (Great Learning, Doctrine of the Mean, Analects, Mencius), Neo-Confucianism (Zhu Xi, Lu Xiangshan, Wang Yangming), Confucian ethics (ren, li, yi, xiao), Confucian political philosophy (the Mandate of Heaven, rule by virtue), Confucian education and the examination system, Confucianism in East Asian modernity (Japan, Korea, Singapore), Confucian filial piety and family structure.

## What Does NOT Belong Here

Daoism as a distinct tradition (belongs in Daoism or Taoism), Chinese Buddhism (belongs in Buddhism under its East Asian development), Chinese folk religion (belongs in a separate category), the secular history of imperial China (belongs in Chinese History), Confucianism as a purely philosophical system separated from its ritual and religious dimensions (this is the traditional blurry boundary — both belong here).

## Key Scriptures/Texts

- **Five Classics**: I Ching (Book of Changes), Book of Poetry, Book of Documents, Book of Rites, Spring and Autumn Annals
- **Four Books**: Great Learning, Doctrine of the Mean, Analects, Mencius
- **Xunzi** — The complete works
- **Zhu Xi**: Reflections on Things at Hand
- **Wang Yangming**: Instructions for Practical Living

## Key Concepts

Ren (benevolence/humaneness), Li (ritual propriety), Yi (righteousness), Zhi (wisdom), Xin (trustworthiness), Xiao (filial piety), Tian (Heaven), Tianming (Mandate of Heaven), Junzi (exemplary person), Zhengming (rectification of names), Wuwei (effortless action — also Daoist), Zhongyong (Doctrine of the Mean), Datong (Great Harmony).

## Connections

- **Daoism** and **Taoism** developed alongside Confucianism as alternative Chinese worldviews.
- **Shinto** absorbed Confucian ethics through Sino-Japanese cultural exchange.
- **Zen-Zen** and **Buddhism** influenced Neo-Confucianism through Buddhist metaphysics.
- **Comparative Religion** examines Confucianism among world traditions.
- **Eastern Philosophy (14)** covers Confucian ethics and metaphysics.
