# Daoism (Taoism)

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Daoism (also spelled Taoism) is a Chinese philosophical and religious tradition focused on living in harmony with the Dao (Tao) — the ultimate, ineffable principle underlying all reality. Its foundational texts are the Dao De Jing (Tao Te Ching, attributed to Laozi, c. 6th–4th century BCE) and the Zhuangzi (attributed to Zhuang Zhou, c. 4th–3rd century BCE). The Dao De Jing, a brief but profoundly influential text of 81 chapters, counsels simplicity, spontaneity, and non-action (wuwei). The Zhuangzi, longer and more playful, uses parables and paradoxes to deconstruct fixed categories.

In addition to its philosophical dimension, Daoism developed as an organized religious tradition (Daojiao) with pantheons of deities, ritual, monasticism, alchemy (both external — waidan — and internal — neidan), and a vast scriptural canon (the Daozang). Daoism deeply influenced Chinese medicine, martial arts, feng shui, poetry, and painting. It interacted constantly with Confucianism and Buddhism.

## What Belongs Here

Pre-Daoist indigenous Chinese religion, philosophical Daoism (Laozi, Zhuangzi, Liezi), religious Daoism (Way of the Celestial Masters, Way of Mao Shan, Way of Complete Perfection), Daoist alchemy (neidan — internal alchemy), Daoist meditation (zuowang, "sitting in forgetting"), the Daoist pantheon and ritual, Daoist ethics and naturalness (ziran), the Daozang (Daoist canon), Daoist influence on Chinese art, poetry, medicine, and martial arts, modern Daoism.

## What Does NOT Belong Here

Confucianism (belongs in Confucianism — while complementary, the two are distinct traditions), Chinese Buddhism (belongs in Buddhism under East Asian Buddhism), Chinese folk religion except as it overlaps with Daoist practice, feng shui as a secular design trend (belongs in Chinese Culture), tai chi as a purely physical practice (belongs in Martial Arts or Physical Education).

## Key Scriptures/Texts

- **Dao De Jing** (Laozi) — The foundational text in 81 chapters
- **Zhuangzi** (Zhuang Zhou) — Philosophical parables and teachings
- **Liezi** — Another early Daoist text
- **Daozang** — The Daoist Canon (over 1,400 texts)
- **Baopuzi** (Ge Hong) — Alchemical and practical Daoism
- **The Secret of the Golden Flower** — Internal alchemy classic

## Key Concepts

Dao (the Way — ultimate reality), De (virtue/power), Wuwei (non-action/effortless action), Ziran (naturalness/spontaneity), Pu (the uncarved block — simplicity), Qi (vital energy), Yin and Yang (complementary opposites), the Three Treasures (compassion, frugality, humility), P'u (simplicity), Wu (non-being), You (being), T'ai Chi (supreme ultimate), Neidan (internal alchemy), Xian (immortal/transcendent being).

## Connections

- **Confucianism** is Daoism's complementary counterpart (yang vs. yin, social engagement vs. natural spontaneity).
- **Taoism** is an alternate romanization of the same Chinese characters — content may overlap.
- **Zen-Zen** (Chan/Zen Buddhism) was profoundly influenced by Daoist naturalism and paradox.
- **Shinto** shares animistic sensibilities and nature reverence.
- **Chinese Philosophy (14)** addresses Daoist metaphysics.
