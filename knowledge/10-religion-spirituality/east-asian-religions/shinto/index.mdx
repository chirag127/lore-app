# Shinto

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Shinto ("the Way of the Kami") is the indigenous religious tradition of Japan, rooted in prehistoric animistic practices and developing in complex interaction with Buddhism and Confucianism. At its core is the veneration of kami — spirits or deities that inhabit natural phenomena (mountains, rivers, trees, storms), ancestors, and deified human figures. Unlike scriptural religions, Shinto has no single founder and no canonical text; its mythology is recorded in the Kojiki (712 CE) and Nihon Shoki (720 CE), which recount the creation of Japan by the kami Izanagi and Izanami and the divine descent of the imperial line.

Shinto coexists with Buddhism in Japanese religious life: life-affirming events (births, weddings, New Year) are often Shinto, while funerals are Buddhist. State Shinto (Kokka Shinto) was created as a nationalistic ideology during the Meiji period and disestablished after World War II. Today Shinto takes the form of shrine Shinto (Jinja Shinto), folk practices, and sectarian movements.

## What Belongs Here

## Key Scriptures/Texts

- **Kojiki** (Records of Ancient Matters, 712 CE) — The oldest surviving Japanese text
- **Nihon Shoki** (Chronicles of Japan, 720 CE) — Official history with mythological portions
- **Engishiki** (927 CE) — Administrative codes detailing Shinto ritual procedures
- **Fudoki** (8th century) — Provincial gazetteers recording local kami and traditions
- **Norito** — Ritual prayers preserved in the Engishiki

## Key Concepts

Kami (spirit/deity), Koshin (ancestral kami), Matsuri (festival/ritual celebration), Harae (purification), Misogi (water purification), Tsumi (impurity/pollution), Kegare (defilement), O-mairi (shrine visit), Jinja (shrine), Torii (shrine gate), Shimenawa (sacred rope), Ofuda (amulet), Oharai (great purification), Makoto (sincerity), Shinbutsu-shugo (Shinto-Buddhist syncretism).

## Connections

- **Buddhism** in Japan has syncretized with Shinto for over a millennium.
- **Daoism** and **Shinto** share animistic sensibilities and nature-based spirituality.
- **Comparative Religion** analyzes Shinto among indigenous and world traditions.
- **Religion & Society** examines the legacy of State Shinto and shrine politics in modern Japan.
- **Zen-Zen** and Japanese Buddhism were shaped by the Shinto cultural substrate.
