# Taoism

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Taoism is the Wade-Giles romanization of the same Chinese term written "Daoism" in the Pinyin system. Both refer to the indigenous Chinese philosophical and religious tradition centered on the Tao (the Way), the ultimate principle underlying all reality. The Wade-Giles form appeared in most Western scholarship and translations before the late 20th century and persists in many classic texts, English-language titles, and popular culture references where it retains a distinct visual and phonetic recognition.

The content, scriptures, concepts, and traditions are identical. Both directories exist to accommodate users who search using either romanization. This directory covers the same material as Daoism, ensuring all users find the information they need regardless of which spelling convention they follow.

## What Belongs Here

The same scope as Daoism: philosophical Taoism (Lao Tzu, Chuang Tzu, Lieh Tzu), religious Taoism (the Celestial Masters, the Complete Perfection tradition), Taoist alchemy (both external and internal), Taoist meditation (Tso-wang), the Taoist pantheon, Taoist ethics, the Tao Tsang (canon), Taoist influence on Chinese culture, and the commentaries and interpretive traditions that have developed around the Tao Te Ching and Chuang Tzu.

## What Does NOT Belong Here

See Daoism for the full list. This directory is a romanization variant; the same boundaries apply.

## Key Scriptures/Texts

- **Tao Te Ching** (Lao Tzu) — The foundational text
- **Chuang Tzu** (Chuang Chou) — Philosophical parables
- **Lieh Tzu** — Early Taoist text
- **Tao Tsang** — The Taoist Canon
- **The Secret of the Golden Flower** — Internal alchemy

## Key Concepts

Tao (the Way), Te (virtue/power), Wu-wei (non-action), Tzu-jan (naturalness/spontaneity), P'u (the uncarved block), Ch'i (vital energy), Yin and Yang, the Three Treasures (compassion, frugality, humility), Hsien (immortal), Nei-tan (internal alchemy).

## Connections

- **Daoism** — Same tradition; this is an alternate romanization.
- **Confucianism** — Taoism's complementary counterpart.
- **Zen-Zen** — Influenced by Taoist naturalism.
- **Shinto** — Shares animistic sensibilities.
- **Chinese Philosophy (14)** — Taoist metaphysics and ethics.
