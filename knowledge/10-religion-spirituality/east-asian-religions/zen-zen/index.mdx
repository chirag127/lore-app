# Zen (Zen Buddhism)

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Zen (Chinese: Chan; Korean: Seon; Vietnamese: Thien) is a school of Mahayana Buddhism that emphasizes direct, experiential insight into one's own Buddha-nature over doctrinal study. It emerged in 6th–7th century China as Chan Buddhism, blending Indian Mahayana (particularly the Lankavatara Sutra and the Diamond Sutra) with Daoist naturalism and pragmatism. The legendary founder, Bodhidharma, is said to have brought the teaching from India to China. Zen flourished during the Tang dynasty, producing the classical masters of the golden age — Huineng, Mazu, Linji, Dongshan — whose recorded sayings and koans became central to the tradition.

Zen spread to Japan from the 12th century onward through figures like Eisai (who brought Rinzai Zen) and Dogen (who founded Soto Zen). In the West, Zen has exerted a powerful influence on philosophy (the Kyoto School), psychology (Zen and psychoanalysis), the arts (sumi-e, haiku, the tea ceremony, architecture), and contemporary mindfulness practice.

## What Belongs Here

The history of Chan/Zen in China (from Bodhidharma through the Five Houses), the transmission to Japan (Rinzai, Soto, Obaku), Zen doctrine (Buddha-nature, emptiness, no-mind), Zen practice (zazen — seated meditation, shikantaza — just sitting, koan introspection), Zen literature (koan collections, recorded sayings, Dogen's Shobogenzo), Zen and the arts (tea ceremony, ink painting, calligraphy, gardening, archery), the Kyoto School philosophy (Nishida, Nishitani), modern Zen in the West (D.T. Suzuki, Shunryu Suzuki, Robert Aitken, Philip Kapleau).

## What Does NOT Belong Here

Buddhism as a whole (belongs in Buddhism), Theravada and Vajrayana practice except as comparative background, Pure Land Buddhism (belongs in Mahayana Buddhism), secular mindfulness divorced from Buddhist context (belongs in Mindfulness & Meditation), Japanese aesthetics except as grounded in Zen practice (belongs in Japanese Art/Aesthetics).

## Key Scriptures/Texts

- **Platform Sutra of the Sixth Patriarch** (Huineng)
- **Transmission of the Lamp** (compilations of Zen lineage and sayings)
- **Mumonkan** (Gateless Barrier) — 48 classical koans with commentary
- **Hekiganroku** (Blue Cliff Record) — 100 koans
- **Shobogenzo** (Dogen) — Treasury of the True Dharma Eye
- **Zen Mind, Beginner's Mind** (Shunryu Suzuki)
- **Living by Zen** (D.T. Suzuki)

## Key Concepts

Zazen (seated meditation), Satori/Kensho (awakening/seeing one's nature), Koan (public case — paradoxical riddle), Shikantaza (just sitting — Soto Zen), Kinhin (walking meditation), Dokusan (private interview with teacher), Sesshin (intensive meditation retreat), Mu (emptiness/non-duality), Beginner's Mind (Shoshin), No-Mind (Mushin), Sudden Enlightenment vs. Gradual Cultivation, Zen lineage/dharma transmission, Buddha-nature (Tathagatagarbha).

## Connections

- **Buddhism** provides the Mahayana framework that Zen presupposes.
- **Daoism** and **Taoism** influenced Zen's naturalism and iconoclasm.
- **Mindfulness & Meditation** draws on Zen practice (especially zazen and shikantaza).
- **Japanese Aesthetics (Art)** — Zen has profoundly shaped Japanese arts.
- **Comparative Religion** places Zen in the context of global contemplative traditions.
