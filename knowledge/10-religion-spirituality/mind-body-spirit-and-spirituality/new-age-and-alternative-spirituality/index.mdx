# New Age & Alternative Spirituality

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

The New Age movement is a loose network of spiritual beliefs and practices that emerged in the 1960s and 1970s, drawing on a syncretic blend of Eastern religions (Hinduism, Buddhism), Western esotericism (Theosophy, Hermeticism, Kabbalah), paganism, shamanism, channeling, quantum mysticism, and humanistic psychology. The term "New Age" itself derives from the astrological concept of the Age of Aquarius. Key influences include Helena Blavatsky's Theosophical Society, Carl Jung's analytical psychology with its archetypes and collective unconscious, the Human Potential Movement (Esalen Institute), and the popularization of Eastern spiritual teachers in the West.

Alternative spirituality also encompasses neo-paganism (Wicca, Druidry, heathenry), the Goddess movement, contemporary shamanism, energy healing (Reiki, pranic healing), astrology, tarot, crystals, and a wide array of holistic health practices. The scholarly study of this terrain often uses the term "lived religion" or "vernacular religion" to describe the individualized, syncretic, and often anti-institutional character of contemporary spiritual seeking.

## What Belongs Here

New Age history and sociology (Wouter Hanegraaff, Paul Heelas), channeling and mediumship (Jane Roberts' Seth Material, Edgar Cayce, Ramtha), Astrology (as a spiritual practice), Tarot and divination practices, crystals and gemstone healing, Reiki and energy healing, neo-paganism and Wicca, Goddess spirituality and feminist spirituality, the Human Potential Movement and Esalen, shamanic journeying, near-death experience studies, the Law of Attraction, eco-spirituality and Gaia spirituality, perennialism and the "perennial philosophy," critiques of the New Age (commodification, cultural appropriation).

## What Does NOT Belong Here

Mindfulness and meditation as secular, evidence-based practices (belongs in Mindfulness & Meditation), traditional pagan or indigenous religions practiced by their originating communities (belongs in History of World Religions or their specific tradition), the academic study of astrology or divination as historical phenomena (belongs in History of Science or Intellectual History), esoteric traditions with established textual lineages like Kabbalah (belongs in Jewish Mysticism) or alchemy (belongs in History of Science).

## Key Scriptures/Texts

- Helena Blavatsky, The Secret Doctrine (Theosophical foundation)
- Jane Roberts, Seth Speaks (channeled material)
- Carlos Castaneda, The Teachings of Don Juan (shamanism)
- James Redfield, The Celestine Prophecy
- Rhonda Byrne, The Secret (Law of Attraction)
- Starhawk, The Spiral Dance (neo-paganism)
- Wouter Hanegraaff, New Age Religion and Western Culture (academic)
- David Lynch, Catching the Big Fish (transcendental meditation creative practice)

## Key Concepts

Channeling, Age of Aquarius, Indigo children, Vibrational frequency, Energy healing, Chakra balancing, Aura cleansing, The Law of Attraction, Ashtar Command, Starseed, Crystal healing, Synchronicity (from Jung), Holotropic breathwork, Past life regression, Akashic records, Lightworker, Twin flames, Wicca (the Wheel of the Year), Gaia hypothesis (spiritualized), The Secret.

## Connections

- **Mindfulness & Meditation** is adjacent but more evidence-based and institutionalized.
- **Hindu Mysticism & Tantra** and **Buddhism** are source traditions for many appropriated practices.
- **Mysticism & Esoteric Traditions** examines the older, text-based esoteric traditions that influenced the New Age.
- **Psychology (16)** — Jungian concepts deeply inform New Age thought.
- **Religion & Society** analyzes the New Age as a sociological phenomenon.
- **Spirituality & Life Meaning (20)** overlaps in the personal development sphere.
