# Christian Mysticism

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Christian mysticism concerns the direct experience and knowledge of God through contemplative practice, ascetic purification, and divine grace. Its roots lie in the New Testament (Paul's "I live, yet not I, but Christ lives in me" — Galatians 2:20) and the Alexandrian tradition of allegorical scripture interpretation (Clement, Origen). The Desert Fathers and Mothers (4th century onward) pioneered the monastic contemplative life in the Egyptian desert, their sayings collected in the Apophthegmata Patrum.

The tradition flowered in medieval Europe through figures like Bernard of Clairvaux (bridal mysticism), the Victorines (Hugh and Richard), Francis of Assisi (creation mysticism), Meister Eckhart and the Rhenish mystics (apophatic theology), Julian of Norwich (showings), and the 16th-century Spanish Carmelite reformers Teresa of Avila and John of the Cross, who mapped the soul's journey through the "dark night" to union with God. Catholic, Orthodox (hesychasm, the Jesus Prayer), and Protestant (the Quaker inner light, Pietist heart-religion) streams exist.

## What Belongs Here

The Desert Fathers and Mothers (Anthony, Pachomius, Syncletica), Patristic mysticism (Gregory of Nyssa, Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite, Maximus the Confessor), Benedictine and Cistercian mysticism, the Victorines, Franciscan mysticism (Bonaventure), Dominican/Rhenish mysticism (Eckhart, Tauler, Suso), Bridal mysticism (Bernard of Clairvaux, Hadewijch, Mechthild of Magdeburg), Spanish mysticism (Teresa of Avila, John of the Cross, Ignatius of Loyola's Spiritual Exercises), Anglican mystics (the Cloud of Unknowing, Julian of Norwich, Walter Hilton), Orthodox hesychasm (Gregory Palamas, the Jesus Prayer, Mount Athos), Quaker inner light, modern Christian contemplation (Thomas Merton, Thomas Keating, centering prayer).

## What Does NOT Belong Here

Catholic or Protestant theology as a systematic discipline (belongs in Christianity), church history independent of its mystical dimension (belongs in Christianity), secular contemplative practice (belongs in Mindfulness & Meditation), the modern study of religious experience in psychology (belongs in Psychology of Religion), Christian liturgical theology or sacramentology (belongs in Christianity).

## Key Scriptures/Texts

- Pseudo-Dionysius, Mystical Theology and The Divine Names
- The Cloud of Unknowing (anonymous, 14th century)
- Meister Eckhart, German Sermons and Latin Works
- Julian of Norwich, Showings (Revelations of Divine Love)
- Teresa of Avila, The Interior Castle and The Way of Perfection
- John of the Cross, The Dark Night of the Soul and Ascent of Mount Carmel
- Thomas à Kempis, The Imitation of Christ
- Thomas Merton, New Seeds of Contemplation
- The Philokalia (Orthodox hesychast texts)

## Key Concepts

Apophatic theology (via negativa — knowing God by negation), Kataphatic theology (via affirmativa — knowing God through affirmation), Contemplation (theoria), Union with God (theosis — deification in Orthodox tradition), Dark night of the soul (John of the Cross), Spiritual marriage (Teresa of Avila), Recollection, Detachment (Gelassenheit), Prayer of quiet, Prayer of simple regard, Lectio divina (sacred reading), The Jesus Prayer, Centering prayer, Purgation/Illumination/Union (the threefold mystical path), Ecstasy, Rapture, Stigmata.

## Connections

- **Christianity** provides the theological and sacramental framework.
- **Jewish Mysticism** shares the apophatic tradition and text-based meditative practice.
- **Sufi Mysticism** shares the path structure of purification and divine union.
- **Mindfulness & Meditation** offers secular counterparts to contemplative prayer.
- **Comparative Religion** studies mysticism as a cross-cultural phenomenon.
