# Hindu Mysticism & Tantra

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Hindu mysticism encompasses the multiple paths to direct experience of the divine within the Hindu tradition. These include the yogic (meditative) mysticism of the Upanishads and Yoga Sutras, the devotional (bhakti) mysticism of the Alvars and Nayanars and the medieval Bhakti movement, and the Tantric traditions that emerged from approximately the 5th century CE onward.

Tantra is a particularly distinctive and often misunderstood tradition. It is a body of esoteric practice and theory found in both Hinduism and Buddhism (Vajrayana) that uses ritual, mantra, visualization, and sometimes transgressive elements (the five "M"s — madya, mamsa, matsya, mudra, maithuna) to achieve liberation. Tantra is not, as commonly misrepresented in the West, primarily a sexual practice; rather it is a comprehensive spiritual path that works with the energies of the body and cosmos. Key Tantric traditions include Kashmir Shaivism (Abhinavagupta), the Shakta traditions of Devi worship (Sri Vidya, the Lalita Sahasranama), and the Nath Siddha tradition.

## What Belongs Here

Upanishadic mysticism (the inner meaning of Brahman-Atman identity), Patanjali's Yoga Sutras and the eight-limbed path (samadhi as mystical state), Bhakti mysticism (the passionate devotion of Mirabai, Chaitanya, the Alvars and Nayanars), the Nath Siddhas (Gorakhnath, Matsyendranath), Kashmir Shaivism (Abhinavagupta, Utpaladeva, the Pratyabhijna system), the Shakta Tantras and Sri Vidya (Lalita Sahasranama, Soundarya Lahari), the Tantric use of yantra, mantra, mandala and subtle body (chakras, nadis, kundalini), Hindu tantric ritual and initiatory transmission (diksha).

## What Does NOT Belong Here

Mainstream Hindu worship, ritual, and temple practice (belongs in Hinduism), the six orthodox philosophical schools (belongs in Indian Philosophy), Buddhist Tantra / Vajrayana (belongs in Buddhism), Tantra as a New Age practice divorced from its traditional context (belongs in New Age & Alternative Spirituality), Indian medicine or alchemy (belongs in History of Science), the modern Western commercialization of "tantric sex" (does not belong in this category).

## Key Scriptures/Texts

- **Yoga Sutras** (Patanjali) — Classical yoga and samadhi
- **Bhagavata Purana** — The primary text of Vaishnava Bhakti mysticism
- **Abhinavagupta**: Tantraloka, Paratrisika-Vivarana
- **Kularnava Tantra** — A foundational Hindu Tantric text
- **Lalita Sahasranama** — The Thousand Names of the Goddess
- **Kashmir Shaivism**: Shiva Sutras (Vasugupta), Spanda Karika
- **Soundarya Lahari** (attributed to Shankara) — Hymn to the Goddess
- **Ashtavakra Gita** — Advaitic (non-dual) mystical text

## Key Concepts

Kundalini (the coiled spiritual energy at the base of the spine), Chakras (energy centers — Muladhara, Svadhisthana, Manipura, Anahata, Vishuddha, Ajna, Sahasrara), Nadis (subtle energy channels — Ida, Pingala, Sushumna), Samadhi (absorption/enstasy), Bhakti (ecstatic devotion), Diksha (initiation), Mantra (sacred sound), Yantra (geometric diagram), Mandala (cosmic diagram), Puja (ritual worship), Pancha-makara (the five M's), Sapta-bhumika (seven levels of yogic realization), Pratyabhijna (recognition of one's identity with Shiva), Spanda (divine vibration), Vama (left-hand) vs. Dakshina (right-hand) Tantra.

## Connections

- **Hinduism** provides the broader religious framework; Hindu mysticism is inseparable from it.
- **Buddhism** — Buddhist Tantra (Vajrayana) developed in parallel and mutual influence.
- **Sufi Mysticism** interacted with Hindu mysticism in medieval India through the Bhakti-Sufi synthesis.
- **Christian Mysticism** and **Jewish Mysticism** offer comparative mystical frameworks.
- **Mindfulness & Meditation** draws on yogic meditative techniques.
- **New Age & Alternative Spirituality** has heavily appropriated (and often distorted) Tantric concepts.
