# Jewish Mysticism

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Jewish mysticism encompasses the esoteric dimensions of Judaism, from the earliest Merkavah (divine chariot) mysticism of the Talmudic period to medieval Kabbalah, Lurianic Kabbalah, and Hasidism. The Merkavah tradition, based on Ezekiel's vision of the divine throne-chariot, involved visionary ascents through the heavenly palaces (hekhalot) to behold the divine glory. Classical Kabbalah emerged in 12th–13th century Provence and Spain, notably through the Sefer ha-Bahir, the Sefer Yetzirah (Book of Creation), and the Zohar (Book of Splendor), the central text of Kabbalah attributed to Moses de Leon.

After the expulsion of Jews from Spain (1492), Isaac Luria (the Ari) of Safed developed a new kabbalistic system centered on the cosmic catastrophe of the "shattering of the vessels" (shevirat ha-kelim) and the human task of tikkun (repair). In the 18th century, Hasidism — founded by the Baal Shem Tov — democratized Jewish mysticism by making ecstatic prayer and joyful devotion accessible to ordinary Jews.

## What Belongs Here

Merkavah and Hekhalot mysticism, Sefer Yetzirah and early Jewish cosmological speculation, the Zohar and its composition, the Ten Sefirot (divine emanations), the Tree of Life, Lurianic Kabbalah (shevirat ha-kelim, tikkun, the partzufim), Abulafia's prophetic Kabbalah, the Kabbalah of the Ari (Isaac Luria) and his Safed circle, the Besht and early Hasidism, Habad (Chabad) intellectualist Hasidism, the Musar movement, modern academic study of Kabbalah (Gershom Scholem, Moshe Idel, Yehuda Liebes).

## What Does NOT Belong Here

Judaism as a whole (belongs in Judaism — Kabbalah is one dimension), Jewish law (halakha) and Talmud study, Christian Kabbalah (a Renaissance Christian appropriation — belongs in Western Esotericism), Hermetic Qabalah (belongs in Western Esoteric / New Age), Jewish history except as it relates to mystical movements, modern popular New Age appropriations of Kabbalah (Madonna, Red String — belongs in New Age & Alternative Spirituality).

## Key Scriptures/Texts

- **Sefer Yetzirah** (Book of Creation) — The earliest extant Jewish mystical text
- **Zohar** (Book of Splendor) — The central text of Kabbalah (13th century, attributed to Moses de Leon)
- **Sefer ha-Bahir** (Book of Brightness) — Early kabbalistic text
- **Pardes Rimonim** (Moses Cordovero) — Systematic kabbalistic synthesis
- **Etz Chaim** (Hayyim Vital) — Lurianic Kabbalah
- **Tanya** (Shneur Zalman of Liadi) — The foundational text of Chabad Hasidism
- **Gershom Scholem**, Major Trends in Jewish Mysticism (modern scholarship)

## Key Concepts

Sefirot (divine emanations — Keter, Chokhmah, Binah, Chesed, Gevurah, Tiferet, Netzach, Hod, Yesod, Malkhut), Ein Sof (the Infinite — the unknowable God beyond emanation), Shekhinah (divine presence — the feminine aspect of God), Partzufim (divine configurations), Shevirat ha-Kelim (shattering of the vessels), Tikkun (cosmic repair), Gilgul (transmigration of souls), Devekut (cleaving to God), Kavvanah (intentionality in prayer), Hitbodedut (personal meditation — especially in Hasidism), Tzimtzum (divine contraction), Hekhalot (heavenly palaces).

## Connections

- **Judaism** is the religious matrix; Kabbalah presupposes knowledge of Torah and halakha.
- **Christian Mysticism** and **Sufi Mysticism** share the path of divine union and the problem of speaking about the ineffable.
- **Sufi Mysticism** influenced the development of Kabbalah in medieval Spain.
- **Comparative Religion** studies mysticism and esotericism across traditions.
- **New Age & Alternative Spirituality** frequently appropriates kabbalistic terminology in non-Jewish contexts.
