# Sufi Mysticism

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Sufism (Arabic: tasawwuf) is the mystical tradition within Islam, focused on the direct, experiential knowledge of God through love, devotion, and spiritual discipline. It emerged in the late 7th and early 8th centuries partly as a reaction against the worldliness of the expanding Umayyad Caliphate. Early figures like Hasan al-Basri and Rabia al-Adawiyya (famous for her teaching of disinterested love of God) established the paradigm. Classical Sufism was systematized by al-Junayd of Baghdad and al-Hallaj (who was executed for exclaiming "Ana al-Haqq" — I am the Truth), and reached its philosophical zenith with Ibn Arabi (d. 1240), whose doctrine of wahdat al-wujud (unity of being) remains influential and controversial.

Sufi practice centers on dhikr (remembrance of God), often through rhythmic chanting or breathing exercises, and a path (tariqa) under a spiritual master (shaykh or pir) leading through stations (maqamat) to the ultimate goal of fana (annihilation of the ego in God) and baqa (subsistence in God). Sufi poetry — particularly Rumi, Hafiz, and Attar — represents some of the most beloved spiritual literature in world history.

## What Belongs Here

Early ascetics and mystics (Hasan al-Basri, Rabia, al-Muhasibi), the Sufi path (maqamat and ahwal), Sufi metaphysics (Ibn Arabi's wahdat al-wujud, al-Ghazali's reconciliation of Sufism with orthodoxy), the great poet-mystics (Rumi, Hafiz, Attar, Saadi, Yunus Emre), Sufi orders (turuq: Qadiriyya, Naqshbandiyya, Chishtiyya, Suhrawardiyya, Mevlevi, Bektashi), dhikr and sama (spiritual audition — whirling dervishes), Sufi cosmology and the concept of the Perfect Man (al-insan al-kamil), Sufi psychology (the nafs or ego-soul), Sufi literature in Arabic, Persian, Turkish, Urdu, and vernacular languages.

## What Does NOT Belong Here

Islam as a whole (belongs in Islam — Sufism is one dimension, not the whole), Islamic law (fiqh) and theology (kalam) except as they intersect with Sufism, Iranian/Persian history, secular literary analysis of Sufi poetry, heterodox groups that break with Islamic identity (belongs in New Age or Religious Movements), the political history of the Ottoman Empire or Mughal India.

## Key Scriptures/Texts

- Rumi, Masnavi-ye Ma'navi (Spiritual Couplets)
- Rumi, Divan-e Shams-e Tabrizi
- Ibn Arabi, Fusus al-Hikam (Bezels of Wisdom) and Futuhat al-Makkiyya
- Attar, Conference of the Birds (Mantiq al-Tayr)
- Al-Ghazali, Ihya Ulum al-Din (Revival of the Religious Sciences)
- Hafiz, Divan
- Ibn al-Farid, The Poem of the Way (al-Taiyya al-Kubra)
- Suhrawardi, The Philosophy of Illumination (Ishraq)

## Key Concepts

Tasawwuf (Sufism), Tariqa (path/order), Maqam (station), Hal (spiritual state), Dhikr (remembrance), Sama (audition/listening), Fana (annihilation of ego), Baqa (subsistence in God), Nafs (ego-soul), Qalb (heart), Ruh (spirit), Sirr (innermost secret), Al-Insan al-Kamil (the Perfect Man), Wahdat al-Wujud (unity of being), Ishq (divine love), Faqr (spiritual poverty), Tawakkul (trust in God), Murshid/Murid (master/disciple).

## Connections

- **Islam** is the religious matrix in which Sufism arose; the Quran and hadith are its foundations.
- **Christian Mysticism** and **Jewish Mysticism** share the structure of a path to divine union within a monotheistic framework.
- **Hindu Mysticism & Tantra** offers interesting parallels in concepts of union and non-duality.
- **Comparative Religion** studies mystical experience across traditions.
- **Religion & Society** addresses tensions between Sufi orders and reformist/Islamist movements.
