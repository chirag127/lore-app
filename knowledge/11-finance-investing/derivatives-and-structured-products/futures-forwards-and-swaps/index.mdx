# Futures, Forwards, and Swaps

## Why This Exists

Not all financial risk is best managed through spot markets or options. Futures, forwards, and swaps are contractual agreements to exchange cash flows or assets at future dates, enabling counterparties to lock in prices, hedge exposures, or speculate on the direction of interest rates, currencies, commodities, and credit. These instruments form the backbone of the global risk management industry, with notional amounts outstanding in the hundreds of trillions of dollars.

Futures are standardized, exchange-traded contracts with centralized clearing and daily mark-to-market settlement, making them ideal for hedging commodity price risk, equity index exposure, and interest rate risk. Forwards are customized OTC contracts between two counterparties, useful for currency hedging where exact dates and amounts are required. Swaps — particularly interest rate swaps, currency swaps, and credit default swaps — allow parties to exchange one stream of cash flows for another. The swap market transformed interest rate risk management and is central to corporate finance, asset-liability management, and speculation. Each instrument class has distinct pricing models: futures rely on the cost-of-carry model, swaps on the relationship between fixed and floating rates implied by the yield curve.

## Essential Books

- John C. Hull, *Options, Futures, and Other Derivatives* — The standard text covering all futures, swaps, and forwards
- Robert W. Kolb, *Futures, Options, and Swaps* — A thorough treatment of the institutional and pricing aspects
- David Dubofsky & Thomas W. Miller, Jr., *Derivatives: Valuation and Risk Management* — Practical coverage of futures and swaps
- Robert L. McDonald, *Derivatives Markets* — A rigorous yet accessible treatment of all derivative types
- Darrell Duffie, *Futures Markets* — The economics of futures markets and hedging
- Allan M. Malz, *Financial Risk Management: Models, History, and Institutions* — Swaps and credit derivatives in risk management
- Jon Gregory, *Central Counterparties: Mandatory Central Clearing and Bilateral Margin Requirements* — The post-crisis regulatory framework
- John Hull & Alan White, *LIBOR vs. OIS: The Derivatives Valuation Implications* — The transition away from LIBOR
