# Options Theory and Pricing Models

## Why This Exists

Options are among the most intellectually sophisticated instruments in finance. An option is a contract that gives the buyer the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset at a predetermined price within a specified period. Pricing these contracts requires an understanding of probability, stochastic processes, and no-arbitrage principles. The development of the Black-Scholes model by Fischer Black, Myron Scholes, and Robert Merton in 1973 was a watershed moment in financial economics, earning a Nobel Prize and enabling the explosive growth of options markets.

Options theory encompasses both the mathematics of pricing and the practical application of options for hedging, speculation, and income generation. The Black-Scholes model provides a closed-form solution for European option prices under idealized assumptions. The binomial options pricing model (Cox, Ross, Rubinstein) provides a discrete-time, intuitive framework. The Greeks — delta, gamma, theta, vega, rho — measure the sensitivity of option prices to changes in underlying price, time decay, volatility, and interest rates. The Black-Scholes model's assumption of constant volatility is violated in practice; implied volatility varies across strike prices (the volatility smile and skew) and over time. Advanced models incorporate stochastic volatility (Heston), jumps, and local volatility.

## What Belongs Here

Option pricing theory and model development, the Greeks and their applications, volatility surface dynamics, exotic options (barriers, Asians, lookbacks, digitals), the relationship between option prices and market expectations (implied distributions), empirical studies of option markets, and practical hedging and trading strategies using options.

## Essential Books

- John C. Hull, *Options, Futures, and Other Derivatives* — The standard MBA textbook covering the full options theory canon
- Emanuel Derman, *My Life as a Quant* — An insider's account of options model building at Goldman Sachs
- Sheldon Natenberg, *Option Volatility and Pricing* — The definitive practitioner guide to options trading and the Greeks
- Fischer Black & Myron Scholes, *The Pricing of Options and Corporate Liabilities* — The original 1973 paper
- Robert C. Merton, *Theory of Rational Option Pricing* — Merton's extension of the Black-Scholes framework
- Espen Gaardner Haug, *The Complete Guide to Option Pricing Formulas* — Compendium of exotic option pricing formulas
- Alan L. Lewis, *Option Valuation Under Stochastic Volatility* — Heston and beyond
- Mark S. Joshi, *The Concepts and Practice of Mathematical Finance* — A rigorous but practical introduction
