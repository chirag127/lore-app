# Corporate Bonds and Credit Analysis

## Why This Exists

Corporate bonds represent the single largest source of external financing for non-financial corporations worldwide. Unlike equity investors, bondholders are primarily concerned with downside risk — the probability of default and the recovery rate in the event of default. Credit analysis is the systematic evaluation of a company's ability to meet its debt obligations, drawing on financial statement analysis, industry and competitive analysis, management assessment, and an understanding of the legal covenants that protect bondholders.

The corporate bond market is vast and heterogeneous, spanning investment-grade bonds (rated BBB- or higher) to high-yield or "junk" bonds (rated BB+ or lower), private placements, secured and unsecured debt, senior and subordinated issues, convertible bonds, and floating-rate notes. Credit ratings from Moody's, S&P, and Fitch provide a shorthand for credit quality but are imperfect and sometimes conflict with market-implied ratings derived from credit default swap (CDS) spreads. Structural models (Merton model) and reduced-form models provide quantitative frameworks for estimating default probabilities. Covenant analysis examines the legal protections available to bondholders, including limitations on additional debt, asset sales, and dividend payments.

## What Belongs Here

Fundamental credit analysis of corporate issuers, the structure and documentation of corporate bond issues, credit rating methodologies and their limitations, the pricing and trading of corporate bonds, high-yield and distressed debt analysis, credit spread drivers and modeling, covenant analysis, bond covenants and their enforcement, and the relationship between credit and equity markets.

## What Does NOT Belong Here

Government bond analysis (belongs in a separate sub-subcategory), general financial statement analysis not focused on creditworthiness, equity valuation, and the institutional details of bankruptcy law outside the context of corporate bond recovery.

## Essential Books

- Martin Fridson & Fernando Alvarez, *Financial Statement Analysis: A Practitioner's Guide* — The classic text on credit analysis through financial statements
- Stephen J. Antczak, Douglas J. Lucas, & Frank J. Fabozzi, *Leveraged Finance* — Comprehensive coverage of high-yield bonds and leveraged loans
- Frank J. Fabozzi, *Bond Markets, Analysis, and Strategies* — The standard reference on bond market mechanics and analysis
- Robert C. Merton, *On the Pricing of Corporate Debt* — The structural model of default risk (original paper)
- Darrell Duffie & Kenneth J. Singleton, *Credit Risk: Pricing, Measurement, and Management* — Reduced-form credit risk models
- Ashwath Damodaran, *Corporate Finance: Theory and Practice* — Valuation and credit analysis from a corporate finance perspective
- Edward I. Altman, *Corporate Financial Distress and Bankruptcy* — The Altman Z-score and empirical analysis of default
- Moody's Investors Service, *Rating Symbols and Definitions* — Official definitions of credit rating categories

## Key Concepts

Investment grade, high yield, credit spread, option-adjusted spread (OAS), probability of default (PD), loss given default (LGD), expected loss, credit rating, Merton model, recovery rate, seniority, subordination, covenant, indenture, negative pledge, cross-default, credit default swap (CDS), Z-score.
