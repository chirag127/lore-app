## Strengths

- **Simple and actionable.** Seven clear filters. Anyone with basic
  financial literacy can apply them. No complex models or advanced
  math required.
- **India-specific.** Most investing books are written for US markets.
  Mukherjea adapts the quality investing framework to India's unique
  characteristics — promoter holding, governance challenges, economic
  growth context.
- **Well-researched.** The filters are backed by analysis of Indian
  market data over decades. The back-tested results are compelling.
- **Discipline-focused.** The emphasis on behavior — ignoring noise,
  avoiding STIRs, holding for decades — is the hardest part of
  investing, and the book provides the right framework.
- **Case studies.** Detailed analysis of actual Indian companies (Asian
  Paints, HDFC Bank, Maruti) shows exactly how the framework applies.

---

## Weaknesses

- **Back-tested results may not repeat.** The impressive CAGR shown in
  the book is historical. Future results depend on whether the same
  companies maintain their advantages.
- **Seven filters are too restrictive.** Eliminating 99% of stocks
  means very few investment opportunities. Building a 15-20 stock
  portfolio becomes difficult when the universe is tiny.
- **Past quality does not guarantee future quality.** High ROCE over 10
  years suggests a moat, but moats can disappear quickly (Kodak, Nokia,
  DHFL).
- **India-specific only.** The book's framework is tailored to Indian
  markets. US or EU investors cannot apply it directly without
  adaptation.

---

## Criticism

### Survivorship Bias in Case Studies

The case studies focus on companies that succeeded. The book does not
analyze high-ROCE companies that subsequently failed. Understanding
why quality companies decline would strengthen the framework.

### Promoter Holding as a Double-Edged Sword

High promoter holding aligns incentives, but it can also lead to poor
governance — promoters who treat the company as their personal fiefdom.
The book acknowledges this but does not give enough weight to the risk.

### Market Cap Bias

The filters tend to select large-cap companies. Small and mid-cap
stocks often lack 10 years of consistent data, causing the screen to
miss potential future quality companies.

| "Large-cap bias" | Starting with large caps is safer for retail investors |

## Historical Context

Published in 2018, *Coffee Can Investing* arrived during an Indian bull
market fueled by domestic flows and economic optimism. The book
benefited from this tailwind — investors were looking for reason to
stay invested, and the coffee can philosophy provided it.

The framework was developed during Mukherjea's tenure as CEO of Ambit
Capital, where his research team analyzed decades of Indian market
data to build the quality screens.
