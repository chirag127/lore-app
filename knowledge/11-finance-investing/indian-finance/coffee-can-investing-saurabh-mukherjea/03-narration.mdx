## Introduction

Welcome to BookAtlas. Today: *Coffee Can Investing* by Saurabh
Mukherjea, Rakshit Ranjan, and Pranab Uniyal. Published 2018, Penguin
Random House India. 352 pages.

The name comes from a 1980s story: a man bought stocks, put them in a
coffee can, and forgot about them. Decades later, he was a
multi-millionaire. The lesson: buy quality, hold forever, and do
nothing.

Today: a long-term investor who has built their entire portfolio around
coffee can principles, and a value investor who thinks the quality
screen misses the best opportunities.

---

## The Seven-Filter Screen

**Investor:** The seven-filter screen is the heart of the book, and it
is brilliant. ROCE above 15% for 10 years, strong cash flows, low
debt, high promoter holding. It eliminates 99% of stocks. That's a
feature, not a bug. The remaining 1% are the only companies worth
owning.

**Value Investor:** My problem with the screen is that it selects
companies everyone already knows. Asian Paints. HDFC Bank. Nestle.
These are wonderful companies, but they trade at premium valuations.
The screen tells you what to buy, but not when. A 40x P/E on a quality
stock is still a risky investment.

## Price vs. Quality

**Investor:** Mukherjea argues that price is secondary for quality
companies. If you buy Asian Paints at 40x P/E and hold for 20 years,
the entry valuation barely matters — earnings growth will make you
rich anyway. The evidence from the case studies supports this.

**Value Investor:** It's survivorship bias. The companies that
compounded at 20% for 20 years made any entry price look good. But we
don't know which of today's quality companies will grow at 20% for 20
years. Paying too much for a company that stumbles is disastrous. Price
always matters.

---

## Promoter Holding

**Investor:** The promoter holding filter is uniquely valuable for
India. If the founding family owns 60% of the company, their interests
are aligned with mine. They will not dilute, they will not take
excessive risks, and they will think long-term.

**Value Investor:** High promoter holding can also mean poor corporate
governance. Promoters who control 60% can appoint themselves as
directors, pay themselves high salaries, and enter into related-party
deals that benefit them at the expense of minority shareholders. High
holding is a positive signal, but it is not sufficient.

---

## The Behavior Problem

**Investor:** The most important part of the book is the behavior
framework. STIRs — Short-Term Inexplicable Reasons to sell — are the
biggest destroyer of wealth. The stock goes up 30% and you sell to
"book profits." It goes down 10% and you sell to "cut losses." Both are
wrong. The only correct action is to do nothing.

**Value Investor:** The behavioral advice is excellent. But it assumes
you have correctly identified a quality company that will continue to
perform. If you bought a company that looked good on the screen but
turns out to have a deteriorating business, holding is not patience —
it's stubbornness. The book's "never sell" advice needs more nuance.

---

## The Verdict: For Whom?

**Investor:** This book is perfect for the Indian retail investor who
is tired of tips, newsletters, and trading. It gives you a system. You
do not need to be smart. You just need to be disciplined. Apply the
seven filters, buy the stocks, and do nothing for 20 years.

**Value Investor:** It's a good book for its audience — Indian retail
investors who want a simple system. But it overpromises. "Low-risk
route to super-high returns" is marketing, not reality. Coffee can
investing worked in the past. It might work in the future. But there
are no guarantees.

**Investor:** There are no guarantees in any approach. At least this
one is based on logic and evidence, not on hope and speculation.

---

## Final Thoughts

*Coffee Can Investing* is the best practical guide to long-term equity
investing for the Indian market. The seven-filter screen is simple,
systematic, and research-backed. The behavioral framework — ignore
noise, avoid STIRs, let winners run — addresses the real challenge of
investing.

But it requires three things the book cannot provide: the discipline
to apply the screen, the patience to hold for decades, and the
judgment to know when a "quality" company has permanently changed.
Without these, the coffee can is just a container.
