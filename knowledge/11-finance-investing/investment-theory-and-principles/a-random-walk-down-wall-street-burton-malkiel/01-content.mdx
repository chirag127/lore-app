## Two Theories of Value

Malkiel opens with the fundamental tension in all of investing: how do
you know what a stock is worth? He presents two competing schools:

### Firm-Foundation Theory

Stocks have an **intrinsic value** that can be calculated by
discounting future dividend and earnings streams to the present. The
rational investor buys when price is below intrinsic value and sells
(or avoids) when it is above.

This theory traces to John Burr Williams's *The Theory of Investment
Value* (1938) and was championed by Benjamin Graham. It is the
intellectual basis for value investing and, in its modern form,
supports Warren Buffett's approach.

**The problem:** intrinsic value is impossible to calculate with
precision. Small changes in assumptions about growth rates or discount
rates produce enormous valuation swings. Analysts' earnings forecasts
beyond one year have average errors of 20-30%, making the entire
calculation speculative.

### Castle-in-the-Air Theory

Stocks are worth whatever the crowd is willing to pay. The smart
investor does not calculate intrinsic value — she predicts what other
investors will think the stock is worth next week, next month, or next
year. This is John Maynard Keynes's "beauty contest" analogy: you do
not pick the face you find prettiest; you pick the face the judges
will find prettiest.

**The problem:** crowd psychology is inherently unstable. The same
dynamics that drive prices up in a bubble drive them down in a crash.
Winning requires getting out before the crowd turns — and no one rings
a bell at the top.

Malkiel's position: both theories are useful lenses but neither works
consistently in practice.

## The Random Walk Hypothesis

The book's title concept. A **random walk** means that future steps
cannot be predicted from past steps. For stock prices, it means that
knowing yesterday's price change tells you nothing about today's.

This is not a claim that markets are chaotic or irrational. It is a
claim that markets are **efficient** — competition among millions of
informed buyers and sellers drives prices to fair value so quickly
that no exploitable pattern remains.

### The Three Forms of the Efficient Market Hypothesis

1. **Weak form** — Current prices reflect all historical price data.
   Technical analysis (chart patterns, trend following) cannot produce
   excess returns.

2. **Semi-strong form** — Prices instantly incorporate all publicly
   available information (earnings reports, news, economic data).
   Fundamental analysis cannot consistently beat the market after
   trading costs.

3. **Strong form** — Prices reflect all information including insider
   knowledge. Even corporate insiders cannot systematically
   outperform. Malkiel concedes this is an ideal rather than a
   description of reality — insiders do sometimes have an edge, but
   it is illegal to exploit it.

Malkiel's view: markets are not perfectly efficient, but they are
efficient **enough** — efficient enough that the average investor
cannot reliably beat them after accounting for fees, taxes, and
trading costs.

## Technical Analysis: The Canard That Won't Die

Malkiel devotes an entire chapter to dismantling technical analysis —
the practice of predicting stock prices from chart patterns, moving
averages, and trading volume.

### The Coin-Flip Experiment

Malkiel generated random stock charts using a series of coin flips.
When shown to technical analysts, they confidently identified "head
and shoulders" patterns, "support levels," and "breakouts" — all in
purely random data.

The conclusion: chart patterns are a form of **apophenia** — the
human tendency to see patterns in noise. They exist in the eye of the
beholder, not in the market.

### What the Data Shows

- Multiple studies testing hundreds of technical trading rules across
  decades of data have found no consistent excess returns after
  transaction costs
- Signals that appear significant in backtesting reliably fail in
  out-of-sample testing
- Professional technical analysts do not outperform the market on a
  risk-adjusted basis

### A Qualified Exception

Malkiel acknowledges that some momentum-based strategies have shown
limited predictive power in academic studies. But he argues that
transaction costs, taxes, and the difficulty of identifying momentum
in real time make these strategies impractical for individual
investors.

## Fundamental Analysis: Respectable but Flawed

Malkiel treats fundamental analysis with more respect than technical
analysis — it is at least grounded in real economic data — but
concludes it also cannot consistently beat the market.

### Why Fundamental Analysis Fails

1. **Earnings are unpredictable.** Analyst earnings forecasts have
   systematic errors:

   | Forecast Horizon | Average Error |
   |---|---|
   | 1 month | ~5% |
   | 3 months | ~10% |
   | 1 year | ~20-30% |
   | 5 years | >50% |

2. **Good companies are not always good stocks.** A wonderful business
   at a high price reliably underperforms an average business at a low
   price. The market already knows the company is good — it is priced
   in.

3. **Analysts are biased.** Sell-side analysts work for investment
   banks that need deal flow. "Buy" recommendations outnumber "sell"
   by roughly 10:1. Independent analysis is rare.

4. **Competition eliminates the edge.** Even if an analyst discovers a
   mispriced stock, the market corrects quickly. By the time a
   Morningstar report or Wall Street Journal article recommends a
   stock, the opportunity is gone.

### The Exception That Proves the Rule

Malkiel does not claim no one can beat the market. Investors like
Warren Buffett, Peter Lynch, and John Templeton have demonstrated
genuine skill. But the statistical probability that any individual
investor will select the next Buffett is vanishingly small. For every
Buffett, there are thousands of professional money managers who
underperform.

## Modern Portfolio Theory: The Science of Diversification

The book introduces the academic framework that supports the
passive-investing conclusion.

### The Key Insight

Combining assets that do not move perfectly together reduces portfolio
volatility **without proportionally reducing returns**. This is the
only free lunch in finance.

| Portfolio | Annual Return | Annual Volatility |
|-----------|--------------|------------------|
| Single stock | 10% | 40% |
| 20 random stocks | 10% | 20% |
| Total market index | 10% | 15% |

Adding stocks quickly eliminates company-specific risk. After 30-50
stocks across different industries, most diversifiable risk is gone.

### Beta and the Capital Asset Pricing Model

Beta measures how much a stock moves relative to the market:

- Beta = 0.5: half the market's movement
- Beta = 1.0: moves with the market
- Beta = 2.0: double the market's movement

Malkiel explains beta practically — it is useful for understanding
risk but backward-looking and unstable over time. It should inform,
not dictate, investment decisions.

## Behavioral Finance: The Investor's Worst Enemy

Malkiel was an early popularizer of behavioral finance, explaining
how psychological biases systematically harm investment returns:

### Overconfidence

Investors consistently overestimate their ability to pick winning
stocks. In one study, 74% of individual investors rated their
investment knowledge as "above average."

### Herd Behavior

The tendency to follow the crowd into popular investments — and out
of unpopular ones — is the primary engine of bubbles and crashes. The
herd buys at the top and sells at the bottom.

### Loss Aversion

The pain of a loss is roughly twice as powerful as the pleasure of an
equivalent gain. This leads investors to sell winners too early (to
lock in gains) and hold losers too long (to avoid realizing the loss).

### Anchoring

Relying too heavily on a reference price — the price you paid, or a
recent high — when making decisions. If a stock falls from $100 to
$60, investors view $60 as "cheap" even if the business has
deteriorated.

### Confirmation Bias

Seeking information that confirms existing beliefs while ignoring
contradictory evidence. Once an investor buys a stock, they find
reasons to believe in it.

## Malkiel's Life-Cycle Guide to Investing

The book's practical payoff: concrete asset-allocation models for
each life stage:

| Life Stage | Suggested Stock Allocation | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| 20s | 80-90% | Long time horizon; human capital is large |
| 30s | 75-85% | Still long horizon; some stability needed |
| 40s | 65-75% | Peaking earning years; growth still priority |
| 50s | 55-65% | Sequence-of-returns risk begins to matter |
| 60s+ | 45-55% | Capital preservation becomes primary |
| Retirement | 40-50% | Growth still needed to fund 20-30 year horizon |

## Key Lessons

- **You cannot predict the stock market.** Accepting this is the first
  step to investing successfully.
- **Index funds are the solution, not a compromise.** They are not the
  "average" choice — they are the optimal choice for nearly everyone.
- **Costs are the only thing you can control.** Minimize expense
  ratios, turnover, and taxes.
- **Diversify globally.** Owning the world's markets is safer and more
  profitable than owning just your home country.
- **Rebalance periodically.** Selling winners and buying losers keeps
  your risk profile on target.
- **Ignore financial media.** Noise sells; insight is rare. The best
  thing you can do is stop watching.
- **Stay the course.** The biggest gains come in unpredictable bursts.
  Missing the 10 best days in a 20-year period can halve your returns.

## Practical Action Plan

1. **Open a tax-advantaged account** (401k, IRA, or equivalent). Max
   out employer matching first.

2. **Choose a total stock market index fund** — VTI, VOO, or
   equivalent with an expense ratio below 0.10%.

3. **Select a bond allocation** based on your age using Malkiel's
   life-cycle guide above.

4. **Set up automatic monthly investments** — dollar-cost averaging
   into the market eliminates the timing problem.

5. **Rebalance annually** — return your portfolio to the target
   allocation. This forces you to sell high and buy low.

6. **Ignore the news.** Do not check your portfolio daily. Review
   quarterly at most.

7. **Never try to time the market.** The evidence is clear: it does
   not work.
