The Little Book of Common Sense Investing by John C. Bogle was first published
in 2007 and is widely regarded as the definitive case for index fund investing.
Bogle, who lived from 1929 to 2019, was the founder of the Vanguard Group and
the creator of the world's first index mutual fund. His Princeton University
senior thesis from 1951 — "The Economic Role of the Investment Company" —
predicted that the mutual fund industry would grow to dominate American finance
and that its most successful firms would be those that served investors with
the lowest possible costs. Fifty years later, he wrote this book to prove that
his thesis had been right.

The book's central argument is a mathematical identity that cannot be escaped:
the stock market delivers a certain gross return to all investors combined, and
the costs of investing — management fees, trading commissions, spreads, loads,
and taxes — reduce that gross return to a lower net return. Since active
managers charge far more than passive index funds, the average active investor
must earn less than the average passive investor. This is not a prediction. It
is arithmetic. As Bogle puts it, "The more the managers and brokers take, the
less investors make."

Bogle developed this insight over a career that began at the Wellington Fund in
1951 and culminated in the founding of Vanguard in 1974. He was fired from
Wellington in 1974 for approving what he called "an extremely unwise merger,"
and the non-compete terms of his departure forced him to create an entirely new
kind of fund company — one owned by its fund shareholders rather than by a
parent corporation. This mutual ownership structure allowed Vanguard to operate
at cost, passing all savings to investors. It was the structural innovation
that made low-cost index funds possible.

The first index fund, launched in 1976 as the First Index Investment Trust, was
a spectacular failure at birth. Of the $150 million Bogle hoped to raise in the
initial public offering, only $11.3 million materialized. The financial press
called it "Bogle's Folly." Competitors mocked it as un-American. One prominent
fund executive said, "If we all indexed, the capitalist system would break."
Bogle pressed on. He argued that a fund owning the entire S&P 500 — not trying
to beat it — was the most honest, transparent, and effective investment product
ever created. He was right. Today, Vanguard's 500 Index Fund is the world's
largest mutual fund, with over $800 billion in assets.

The book is organized around the Cost Matters Hypothesis, or CMH, which Bogle
proposes as a more useful alternative to the Efficient Market Hypothesis. EMH
says you cannot beat the market because prices already reflect all available
information. Bogle does not need EMH to be true. His argument does not depend
on market efficiency at all. Even if some managers can identify mispriced
stocks, the fees they charge consume the profits from their superior insight.
CMH simply observes that lower-cost funds deliver higher net returns to
investors — and this is true whether markets are efficient or not.

Bogle supports CMH with decades of data. From 1990 to 2016, the S&P 500
delivered an average annual return of approximately 9.0%. The average
large-cap actively managed mutual fund delivered about 6.8% after fees. The
gap is 2.2 percentage points per year — roughly the average expense ratio plus
the hidden costs of higher turnover. Over 30 years, a $10,000 investment
earning 9% grows to $132,700. The same investment earning 6.8% grows to
$74,600. The difference of $58,100 is not a small detail. It is 44% of the
ending value. Wall Street took almost half.

One of the book's most powerful sections explains the three sources of stock
market returns. The first is dividend yield — the cash that companies pay out to
shareholders. The second is earnings growth — the increase in corporate profits
over time. Together, these form what Bogle calls "fundamental return," the
business reality of investing. The third source is the change in market
valuation — the price-to-earnings ratio expanding or contracting. This is
"speculative return," driven by investor sentiment, not business fundamentals.
Over long periods, speculative return cancels out. When P/E ratios rise, future
returns fall, and vice versa. The intelligent investor focuses on the
fundamental return and ignores the speculative noise.

Bogle illustrates this with a compelling historical analysis. From 1900 to
2016, the US stock market returned about 9.4% annually. Of that, 4.3% came from
dividend yield and 4.6% from earnings growth — together explaining 95% of the
long-term return. The speculative component — P/E expansion — contributed just
0.5% annually. But look at shorter periods and the speculative component
dominates. From 1980 to 1999, P/E ratios expanded dramatically, adding 3-4%
per year to returns and creating the illusion that stocks always go up. From
2000 to 2016, P/E ratios contracted, subtracting 1-2% per year and creating the
illusion that stocks are a bad long-term investment. The fundamental return
chugged along at its usual 5-7% through both periods. The lesson: ignore the
headlines, focus on the fundamentals.

The book also offers a devastating critique of the financial services industry.
Bogle calls active fund management "a giant con game" not because fund managers
are dishonest — most are sincere and hardworking — but because the structure of
the industry guarantees that most will fail to serve their clients. Fund
companies compete for assets, not returns. They launch hundreds of funds so
that they can advertise the lucky winners and bury the losers. They charge
fees that compound into enormous sums despite having no evidence that the fees
are justified. They lobby against regulations that would require them to put
investors' interests first.

Bogle's critique extends to hedge funds, private equity, and exchange-traded
funds. Hedge funds charge "2 and 20" — 2% of assets plus 20% of profits. Over
30 years, a hedge fund that grosses 10% annually delivers just 5-6% to its
investors after fees. Private equity funds lock up capital for a decade and
charge similar fees while taking enormous leverage risk. ETFs, which Bogle
created the template for, have been hijacked by speculators who trade them
daily, and by issuers who create leveraged, inverse, and thematic ETFs that are
nothing more than gambling instruments.

The book's practical advice is startlingly simple. Bogle recommends that every
investor own two funds: a total US stock market index fund and a total US bond
market index fund. The allocation between them should roughly equal the
investor's age. A thirty-year-old would be 70% stocks, 30% bonds. A
sixty-five-year-old would be 35% stocks, 65% bonds. Rebalance once a year. Use
low-cost providers like Vanguard, Fidelity, or Schwab. Never pay more than
0.10% in expenses. Never trade. Never try to time the market. Never listen to
CNBC. Never chase performance.

Critics of the book make several fair points. Bogle's recommendations have a
strong US bias — he dismisses international stocks as unnecessary, arguing that
US companies already have global exposure. This advice would have been
disastrous for a Japanese investor following it in 1989. His opposition to ETFs
seems rooted in personal preference rather than evidence; low-cost index ETFs
like VTI and BND are functionally identical to their mutual fund counterparts
at lower costs. His fixation on costs sometimes blinds him to other important
factors like asset allocation, tax location, and withdrawal sequencing.

The book also assumes a level of discipline that most investors do not possess.
Bogle's advice to "stay the course" through a 50% market crash is mathematically
optimal but psychologically nearly impossible. The majority of individual
investors sold stocks during the 2008 crisis, locking in their losses. Bogle
offers no mechanism — no automatic rebalancing, no pre-committed investment
plan — to help investors overcome their own worst instincts. The Bogleheads
community has filled this gap with behavioral tools, but the book itself is
thin on implementation.

Warren Buffett, the most famous endorser of the book, has called Bogle "a hero
to American investors." In his 2016 letter to Berkshire Hathaway shareholders,
Buffett wrote: "If a statue is ever erected to honor the person who has done
the most for American investors, the hands-down choice should be Jack Bogle."
Buffett went on to say that Bogle helped "millions of investors realize far
better returns on their savings than they otherwise would have earned."
Charlie Munger, Buffett's partner, called the book "a useful contribution to
his fellow citizens" and praised Bogle's relentless "animosity toward folly."

The impact of The Little Book of Common Sense Investing extends far beyond its
sales figures. The book gave rise to the Bogleheads movement — an online
community of hundreds of thousands of investors who follow Bogle's principles.
The John C. Bogle Center for Financial Literacy, founded in 2012, carries his
message to underserved communities. The "Bogleheads' Guide to Investing" and
"Bogleheads' Guide to Retirement Planning" are among the most popular
investment books of the last decade, written by community members who first
learned about indexing from this book.

The 10th Anniversary Edition, published in 2017, added two new chapters that
addressed the growing complexity of the investment landscape. The chapter on
asset allocation formalized Bogle's age-based rule and addressed the new
reality of near-zero interest rates, which made bonds less attractive than they
had been historically. The chapter on retirement investing addressed the rise
of target-date funds, which Bogle endorsed for their simplicity and automatic
rebalancing.

Bogle died on January 16, 2019, at age 89, after a long battle with heart
disease. His legacy is immeasurable. The index fund he created has saved
investors trillions of dollars in fees. The Vanguard Group he founded manages
over $8 trillion in assets. And his philosophy — passive investing, low costs,
own the market, stay the course — has become the dominant investment paradigm
of the 21st century.

In summary, The Little Book of Common Sense Investing teaches that successful
investing requires three things: accepting that you cannot beat the market,
reducing your costs to the absolute minimum, and having the discipline to do
nothing for decades. It is not glamorous. It is not exciting. It does not
require intelligence, education, or inside information. It requires only common
sense and the patience to let compound interest work. As Bogle wrote, "The
winning formula for success in investing is owning the entire stock market, and
doing so at the lowest possible cost."
