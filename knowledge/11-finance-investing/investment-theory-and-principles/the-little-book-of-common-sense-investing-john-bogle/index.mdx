## Overview

*The Little Book of Common Sense Investing* (2007, 10th Anniversary Edition 2017) by
John C. Bogle is the definitive case for index fund investing. Bogle, founder of
Vanguard and creator of the world's first index mutual fund, argues with
missionary fervor that the only way to guarantee your fair share of stock market
returns is to buy and hold the entire market at the lowest possible cost.

The book's core thesis is devastatingly simple: stock market returns are a
zero-sum game before costs and a loser's game after costs. Since actively
managed funds charge high fees, trade frequently, and incur taxable events,
they systematically underperform their benchmark indices. A low-cost total
market index fund eliminates all three drags and guarantees the investor the
market's return — minus a few basis points.

Warren Buffett has called Bogle "a hero to American investors." Charlie Munger
praised this book for destroying "the nonsense" that pervades Wall Street.

---
{}
---|---|
| Gross market return | What the stock market delivers (dividends + earnings growth) |
| **minus** Investment costs | Fees, loads, operating expenses, trading costs |
| **minus** Taxes | Capital gains, dividend taxes, turnover-driven tax drag |
| **equals** Net return to investor | What you actually keep |

The gap between gross and net return is the entire problem. Active managers
capture ~100% of the market's gross return minus ~2-3% in costs, leaving
investors with less than the market. Index funds capture ~100% of the market's
gross return minus ~0.05% in costs, leaving investors with essentially the
full market return. Over 30 years, a 2% annual cost difference compounds into
a 40%+ difference in terminal wealth.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Cost is the only reliable predictor of fund performance.** The lower the
   expense ratio, the higher the probability of outperformance. This is the
   Cost Matters Hypothesis (CMH) — the opposite of the Efficient Market
   Hypothesis, which Bogle considers academic overreach.

2. **Stock returns come from three sources:** dividend yield, earnings growth,
   and change in market valuation (P/E expansion or contraction). The first
   two are fundamental business reality; the third is speculative. Over long
   periods, the first two dominate.

3. **Business reality beats market expectations.** Over 10+ years, stock
   returns converge to dividend yield plus earnings growth. Speculative
   valuation changes cancel out. This is the "relentless rules of humble
   arithmetic."

4. **Index your entire portfolio.** Not just your US stocks — your bonds,
   international stocks, and real estate too. A globally diversified,
   low-cost index portfolio beats most active strategies.

5. **Tax efficiency is a second source of alpha.** Index funds have extremely
   low turnover (2-3% vs 50-100% for active funds), minimizing capital gains
   distributions. Over a lifetime, this tax deferral compounds significantly.

6. **The magic of compounding returns is overwhelmed by the tyranny of
   compounding costs.** A 2% annual fee on a $10,000 investment earning 7%
   over 50 years consumes ~60% of the final value.

7. **"Clash of the Cultures": Speculation vs Investment.** The financial
   industry has shifted from investment (owning businesses for long-term
   returns) to speculation (betting on short-term price movements). Index
   investing is a return to genuine investment.

8. **Most active managers underperform.** Over 15-year periods, ~85-90% of
   actively managed funds fail to beat their benchmark index. Past
   outperformance does not persist.

9. **Time is your friend; impulse is your enemy.** Stay the course through
   market crashes, ignore the noise, and never try to time the market.

10. **Don't look for the needle in the haystack. Just buy the haystack.**

## Who Should Read

- Anyone paying high fees for actively managed mutual funds
- Investors who want evidence-based, academically validated strategy
- Beginners seeking a single, simple, effective investment approach
- Experienced investors who suspect their active management isn't adding value
- People approaching retirement who need to minimize cost drag on returns
- Followers of the Bogleheads investment philosophy

---

## Who Should Skip

- Day traders and short-term speculators — the book argues what you do is
  mathematically futile
- Investors convinced they can consistently pick winning fund managers
- People who believe "this time is different" and that active management will
  work for them
- Anyone seeking complex quantitative strategies or alternative investments

---

## Difficulty

Easy. Bogle writes in plain, conversational English. The math never goes beyond
basic arithmetic. The book is designed to be accessible to any reader, and it
succeeds. The concepts are simple; the discipline to follow them is hard.

---

## Reading Time

~4 hours cover to cover. The 10th Anniversary Edition is 304 pages with large
type and generous margins. Each chapter is short enough to read in one sitting.

---

## Historical Context

Bogle wrote the first edition in 2007 — the peak of the pre-crisis bubble.
Critics at the time argued that indexing worked in bull markets but would fail
in a downturn. The 2008 financial crisis proved the opposite: index investors
who stayed the course recovered fully within 4-5 years, while many active funds
never matched the market's recovery.

The 10th Anniversary Edition (2017) was published at the tail end of one of the
longest bull markets in history, with the S&P 500 at all-time highs. Bogle
added chapters on asset allocation and retirement investing, responding to the
rise of target-date funds and the explosion of ETF trading.

The book's intellectual roots trace to:
- **Paul Samuelson (1965):** Proved stock prices follow a random walk and
  called for a low-cost index fund
- **Burton Malkiel (1973):** *A Random Walk Down Wall Street* popularized the
  academic case for indexing
- **Benjamin Graham (1949):** Advocated that most investors should be
  "defensive" and avoid stock-picking
- **William F. Sharpe (1991):** Proved that before costs, active and passive
  must equal the market; after costs, passive wins

## Final Verdict

*The Little Book of Common Sense Investing* is the single best book ever
written about index investing. It is shorter, clearer, and more passionate than
Malkiel's *Random Walk*. It is more accessible than Bogle's own *Common Sense
on Mutual Funds*. And it is backed by the most successful investing innovation
of the last 50 years — the index fund — that Bogle himself created.

The book has two limitations. First, Bogle's tone can be repetitive; he makes
the same point about costs twenty different ways across twenty chapters.
Second, he is dismissive of international diversification (recommending 0-20%
foreign stocks) and of bond index funds beyond total bond market — positions
most modern advisors disagree with.

But these are minor quibbles. The core message — costs matter, buy the whole
market, and stay the course — is the closest thing to a free lunch in
investing. Anyone who follows it will outperform most professionals over their
lifetime.

**Rating: 9/10** — The most important investing book for the 21st century
individual investor. Read it before you buy another mutual fund.
