## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

**Chapter 1: Introduction.** Chan sets the stage for algorithmic
trading, explaining why systematic approaches outperform human
discretion. He covers the basic infrastructure: data sources,
broker APIs, and the technology stack needed for automated trading.

**Chapter 2: Mean Reversion Strategies.** The core of a mean
reversion strategy: assets that have moved away from their historical
average will tend to return to it. Chan covers Bollinger Bands,
Bollinger Bandit, and pairs trading. He provides MATLAB code for
each strategy, including entry and exit rules.

**Chapter 3: Momentum Strategies.** The opposite of mean reversion:
trends tend to persist. Chan covers time-series momentum (buying
recent winners) and cross-sectional momentum (buying assets that
outperformed peers). He addresses the key challenge — when trends
reverse — and provides stop-loss rules.

**Chapter 4: High-Frequency Strategies.** A brief overview of
strategies that operate at sub-second time scales. Chan notes that
HFT requires infrastructure most retail traders cannot access. He
focuses on lower-frequency strategies that retail traders can
implement.

**Chapter 5: Strategy Testing.** The critical chapter on backtesting.
Chan covers look-ahead bias, survivorship bias, and data snooping.
He emphasizes that backtesting must account for transaction costs
and market impact. The chapter includes a checklist for evaluating
backtest results.

**Chapter 6: Risk Management.** Chan argues that risk management
is more important than strategy selection. He covers position sizing
(Kelly criterion, fixed fractional), stop-loss placement, and
strategy correlation. The chapter includes a portfolio construction
framework for combining multiple strategies.

**Chapter 7: Execution.** Chan covers the mechanics of order
execution: limit orders vs market orders, VWAP, TWAP, and liquidity
detection algorithms. He explains how execution quality affects
strategy profitability.

**Chapter 8: Strategy Portfolio.** The final chapter synthesizes
everything into a practical framework. Chan shows how to combine
uncorrelated strategies, allocate capital dynamically, and monitor
performance. He provides a complete MATLAB framework for managing
a portfolio of strategies.

## Reading Guide

This is a technical book that assumes familiarity with MATLAB,
statistics, and basic finance. Readers should work through the code
examples rather than reading passively. Chan provides downloadable
code on his website. The mean reversion and momentum chapters are
the most immediately practical. The execution chapter (Chapter 7)
is underappreciated — it contains insights that can make or break
a strategy's profitability.

For a gentler introduction, read Chan's earlier book *Quantitative
Trading* first. For a more technical treatment of market
microstructure, read Larry Harris's *Trading and Exchanges*.
