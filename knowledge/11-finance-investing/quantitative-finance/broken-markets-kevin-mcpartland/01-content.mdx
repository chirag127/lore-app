## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

**Chapter 1: The Evolution of Market Structure.** McPartland traces
the history of U.S. equity markets from the NYSE floor through
NASDAQ to today's fragmented electronic landscape. He explains how
Regulation NMS (2005) unintentionally created fragmentation by
requiring trades to be routed to the venue with the best displayed
price.

**Chapter 2: Fragmentation.** The costs and benefits of market
fragmentation. McPartland argues that while competition among
exchanges has lowered fees, it has fragmented liquidity and created
opportunities for latency arbitrage. The chapter includes data on
order routing practices and market share across venues.

**Chapter 3: Dark Pools.** McPartland examines the rise of dark
pools — private trading venues that do not display orders. He
argues that dark pools promised lower market impact for large
traders but have instead created opacity and conflicts of interest.
He profiles the major dark pool operators and their business models.

**Chapter 4: Maker-Taker Pricing.** The controversial practice where
exchanges pay rebates to liquidity providers (makers) and charge
fees to liquidity takers. McPartland explains how this pricing
model creates conflicts — brokers route orders to maximize rebates
rather than improve execution quality.

**Chapter 5: High-Frequency Trading.** A balanced analysis of HFT.
McPartland acknowledges that HFT has reduced spreads and improved
liquidity but argues that these benefits are captured primarily by
HFT firms and sophisticated institutions, not retail investors.

**Chapter 6: Predatory Practices.** Specific HFT strategies that
McPartland considers predatory: latency arbitrage, quote stuffing,
electronic front running, and pinging. He provides evidence for
each practice and explains why existing regulations fail to
address them.

**Chapter 7: The Regulatory Gap.** McPartland argues that the SEC
lacks the technical expertise and resources to police modern
markets. He reviews post-Flash Crash regulatory initiatives and
finds them insufficient.

**Chapter 8: Reforming Market Structure.** McPartland proposes
specific reforms: a consolidated tape, limits on maker-taker
pricing, dark pool transparency requirements, and a minimum
resting time for orders (a speed bump).

**Chapter 9: Restoring Confidence.** The final chapter argues that
market structure reform is necessary not just for efficiency but
for legitimacy. Markets depend on trust, and trust has been eroded.

## Reading Guide

*Broken Markets* is the best single-volume overview of the market
structure debate. It is more analytical than *Flash Boys* and more
practical than *Market Microstructure Theory*. Read chapters 4-6
(maker-taker, HFT, predatory practices) for the core arguments.
The reform proposals in chapter 8 are the most original contribution.
