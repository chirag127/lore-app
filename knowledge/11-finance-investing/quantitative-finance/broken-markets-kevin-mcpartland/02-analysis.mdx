## Strengths

**Balanced perspective.** McPartland avoids the polemics of the HFT
debate. He acknowledges the benefits of electronic trading and HFT
while arguing for reform. The book is more nuanced than *Flash Boys*
and more accessible than academic texts.

**Policy focus.** The book is organized around solutions, not just
problems. McPartland's reform proposals are specific and
actionable. He engages with the regulatory trade-offs rather than
demanding simple fixes.

**Industry insider knowledge.** McPartland's experience at BlackRock
and Coalition Greenwich gives him credibility. He understands how
institutional investors actually experience market structure
problems.

## Weaknesses

**Limited scope.** The book focuses on U.S. equity markets. Fixed
income, derivatives, and foreign exchange are mentioned but not
analyzed. The market structure problems in these asset classes are
arguably worse.

**Dated data.** Published in 2014, the market share data and
regulatory examples are from the early 2010s. The SEC has since
proposed additional reforms that are not covered.

**Prescriptive tilt.** While McPartland presents both sides of the
debate, his preferred solutions favor intervention. Critics would
argue that his reforms would reduce liquidity and increase costs.

## Criticism

**Academic perspective.** Researchers note that McPartland's
evidence for "predatory" practices is largely anecdotal. Academic
studies find little evidence that HFT systematically harms retail
investors.

**Industry pushback.** HFT firms and exchanges argue that
McPartland overstates the problems. They point to narrower spreads
and lower costs as evidence that market structure works well.

## Impact

The book contributed to the policy debate that led to the SEC's
market structure reform proposals in 2022. McPartland's analysis
of maker-taker pricing influenced the SEC's consideration of
tick size reforms and access fee caps.
