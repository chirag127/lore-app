Kevin McPartland writes as a policy analyst rather than a
journalist. The prose is clear and professional but lacks the
narrative flair of Michael Lewis. McPartland's goal is to inform
and persuade, not to entertain.

The book is structured as a policy brief. Each chapter diagnoses a
specific market structure problem, presents evidence, and proposes
solutions. This makes the book useful as a reference but less
engaging as a continuous read.

McPartland's voice is measured and reasonable. He presents opposing
views fairly before explaining why he disagrees. This even-handed
tone gives the book authority but can make it feel bloodless.

Data is used effectively. McPartland supports his arguments with
market share statistics, cost estimates, and regulatory data.
Tables and charts are integrated throughout.

The writing is accessible to non-specialists. McPartland explains
technical concepts clearly and avoids jargon. The book is easier
reading than academic microstructure texts but requires more
patience than narrative journalism.

Overall, the book succeeds as a policy analysis. It will not be
as widely read as Flash Boys, but it provides a more rigorous
examination of the same issues.
