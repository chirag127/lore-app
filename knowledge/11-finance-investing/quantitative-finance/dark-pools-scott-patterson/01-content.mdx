## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

### Part I: The Revolution

**Chapter 1-3: The Dawn.** Patterson introduces Josh Levine, a
computer programmer who believed the NYSE was a corrupt monopoly.
Levine started building an electronic exchange called Island in
his apartment. The early chapters describe the primitive state of
electronic trading in the 1990s and the regulatory battles to break
the NYSE's grip.

**Chapter 4-5: The Island Rises.** Island launched in 1997 and
grew explosively. Levine designed it to be fair: first come, first
served. No special access for insiders. Island's success proved
that a pure electronic market could work. By 2000, Island handled
10% of NASDAQ volume.

**Chapter 6-7: The HFT Invasion.** The early 2000s saw the rise of
the first HFT firms. Patterson profiles firms like Getco, Tradebot,
and Citadel. These firms co-located their servers at exchanges,
built faster networks, and began dominating trading volume.

### Part II: The Arms Race

**Chapter 8-10: Speed.** The race for speed. Patterson describes
how HFT firms bought faster computers, laid fiber-optic cables,
and fought for physical proximity to exchange data centers. The
chapter on microwave transmission towers between Chicago and New
York echoes Lewis's Spread Networks story.

**Chapter 11-12: Dark Pools.** The rise of dark pools. Patterson
explains how large brokers and exchanges created private trading
venues where orders could be executed without public display. Dark
pools were marketed as safe havens from HFT but became sources of
new conflicts.

**Chapter 13-14: The Flash Crash.** May 6, 2010. Patterson provides
a minute-by-minute account of the Flash Crash, when the Dow dropped
nearly 1,000 points in minutes. He attributes the crash to HFT
algorithms that withdrew from the market simultaneously, creating
a liquidity vacuum.

### Part III: The Future

**Chapter 15-16: The Aftermath.** Regulators investigated the Flash
Crash but struggled to understand what happened. The SEC proposed
circuit breakers and other reforms. Patterson argues that these
fixes are insufficient — the fundamental structure of the market
remains vulnerable.

**Chapter 17: The Robots.** Patterson profiles the latest generation
of AI-driven trading algorithms. These systems learn from market
data and make decisions humans cannot predict. He argues that the
next crisis will be caused by algorithms that no one understands.

## Reading Guide

*Dark Pools* is best read as a companion to *Flash Boys*. Lewis
tells the story of one man (Katsuyama) discovering the problem;
Patterson tells the 20-year history that created the problem. The
Josh Levine narrative is the most compelling part — a tragic story
of idealism corrupted. The Flash Crash chapters are essential
reading for understanding systemic risk in modern markets.
