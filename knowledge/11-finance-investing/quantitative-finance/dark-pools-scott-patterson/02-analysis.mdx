## Strengths

**Historical depth.** Patterson tells the full history of electronic
trading — not just the HFT era but the origins. The Josh Levine
story is essential context for understanding how idealistic
intentions led to an unfair market.

**Technical accuracy.** Patterson explains complex technical
concepts — ECN architecture, order matching, co-location — with
accuracy. His reporting is carefully sourced and fact-checked.

**Character-driven narrative.** Like Lewis, Patterson understands
that finance stories work best through characters. Josh Levine is
a memorable protagonist: brilliant, principled, and ultimately
tragic.

## Weaknesses

**Overlong.** At 354 pages, the book is longer than *Flash Boys*
but covers similar ground. Some chapters could have been cut.

**Missing the benefits.** Patterson presents electronic trading as
a story of corruption. He underplays the benefits: lower costs,
faster execution, and broader access for retail investors.

## Criticism

**Michael Lewis comparison.** Reviewers inevitably compare *Dark
Pools* to *Flash Boys*. Some prefer Lewis's tighter narrative.
Others prefer Patterson's broader historical scope.

**Financial Times review** praised the book's "deeply researched"
history but noted that Patterson "struggles to maintain narrative
momentum" across the entire period.

**Industry reaction.** HFT firms criticized the book for
sensationalism. The Managed Funds Association argued that Patterson
overstated the dangers of machine trading.

## Impact

*Dark Pools* is the standard history of electronic trading. It is
cited in academic papers, regulatory reports, and journalistic
accounts. The Josh Levine story has become part of Wall Street
folklore. The book influenced the post-Flash Boys policy debate
by providing historical context for the market structure
transformation.
