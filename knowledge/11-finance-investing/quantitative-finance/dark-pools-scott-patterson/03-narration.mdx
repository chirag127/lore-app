Scott Patterson writes in the tradition of investigative financial
journalism. His style is more restrained than Michael Lewis — less
ironic, more reportorial. The prose is clear and workmanlike.

The book is structured as a chronological history. Patterson moves
from the 1990s through the 2010 Flash Crash to the present. This
linear structure works well for the historical material but can
feel plodding in the middle chapters.

Patterson's strength is character development. Josh Levine is drawn
with depth and nuance: his idealism, his obsession with fairness,
his ultimate disillusionment. The HFT pioneers — Dan Tierney at
Tradebot, Stephen Schuler at Getco — are portrayed as both
innovators and opportunists.

The technical explanations are excellent. Patterson can explain
how an ECN works in a paragraph. The Flash Crash narrative is
masterfully paced — suspenseful even though readers know the
outcome.

The book's biggest weakness is its density. Patterson includes
more detail than the story requires. Some readers may find the
middle chapters slow. The final chapters on AI trading feel rushed,
as if the author ran out of space.

Overall, *Dark Pools* is essential reading for anyone who wants the
full history behind the HFT debate. It lacks the narrative
efficiency of *Flash Boys* but compensates with historical breadth.
