## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

### Part I: The Problem

**Chapter 1: The Rigged Game.** Lewis opens with the story of a
Canadian trader, Brad Katsuyama, who noticed something strange at Royal
Bank of Canada's New York trading desk. When he tried to buy or sell
large blocks of stock, the price moved against him before his order
could be filled. This was not supposed to happen on modern electronic
exchanges. Katsuyama spent years trying to understand why.

**Chapter 2: The Origin of the Problem.** The story traces back to the
1970s and the rise of electronic trading. Congress passed Regulation
NMS (National Market System) in 2005, which fragmented the market
across dozens of exchanges. This fragmentation created opportunities
for high-frequency traders to arbitrage price differences between
exchanges. Lewis explains how speed — measured in microseconds —
became the new insider trading.

**Chapter 3: The Players.** Lewis introduces John Schwall, a former
Goldman Sachs technology executive who helped Katsuyama understand
the HFT infrastructure. Schwall had worked on Goldman's electronic
trading systems and knew exactly how the speed advantage worked. The
chapter also profiles Ronan Ryan, an Irish telecommunications expert
who understood fiber-optic networks and co-location.

### Part II: The Discovery

**Chapter 4: The Investigation.** Katsuyama assembled a team at RBC
to document the market's unfairness. They discovered that HFT firms
were using a technique called "electronic front-running" — detecting
incoming orders and trading ahead of them. The team built software to
prove it was happening systematically across all major exchanges.

**Chapter 5: The IEX Idea.** Katsuyama conceived a radical solution:
build a new stock exchange that would be deliberately slow. By adding
a 38-mile coil of fiber-optic cable to the exchange's data feed, IEX
would eliminate the speed advantage of HFT firms. Every participant
would see market data at exactly the same time.

**Chapter 6: Building IEX.** The founding of the Investors Exchange
is a startup story inside a banking story. Katsuyama raised capital
from his RBC bosses and recruited a team of engineers. They built
the exchange in Jersey City, deliberately locating it far from the
data centers in Mahwah, New Jersey, where HFT firms had co-located
their servers.

### Part III: The Whistleblowers

**Chapter 7: The Spy.** The story of Sergey Aleynikov, a Russian-born
programmer who worked at Goldman Sachs and was arrested for stealing
the bank's trading code. Aleynikov's case became a cause célèbre.
The code he allegedly stole was open-source, and the FBI could not
explain why it was secret. Lewis uses this story to illustrate how
Wall Street uses secrecy to protect its advantages.

**Chapter 8: The Spread Networks.** A mind-bending story about a
company that built a $300 million fiber-optic cable in a straight
line between Chicago and New York. Spread Networks drilled through
mountains and bought up land rights to shave three milliseconds off
the round-trip transmission time. HFT firms paid millions for access
to this cable. The chapter makes concrete what "speed" means in
modern markets — it is a physical asset, not an abstract concept.

### Part IV: The Revelation

**Chapter 9: The Ramp-Up.** IEX launched in 2013 and faced hostility
from every corner of Wall Street. The established exchanges (NYSE,
NASDAQ) fought its SEC application. HFT firms lobbied against it.
Lewis describes the political battle to get IEX approved as an
exchange.

**Chapter 10: The SEC.** The SEC approved IEX as a national securities
exchange in 2016, two years after the book was published. Lewis
presents the SEC as a well-intentioned but overwhelmed regulator that
lacked the technical expertise to police modern markets.

**Chapter 11: The Conclusion.** Lewis argues that the stock market
is not what most people think it is. It is not a fair, transparent
mechanism for raising capital. It is a system optimized for the
benefit of insiders. The only solution is radical transparency and
regulatory reform.

## Reading Guide

*Flash Boys* is a fast read — most readers finish it in 2-3 sittings.
Lewis writes in a breezy, novelistic style. Pay special attention to
the Spread Networks chapter (Chapter 8), which explains the physical
dimension of HFT better than any academic paper. The Aleynikov chapter
(Chapter 7) raises the most complex legal and ethical questions.

For a deeper follow-up, read Scott Patterson's *Dark Pools* for the
longer history of electronic trading, or Kevin McPartland's *Broken
Markets* for the regulatory perspective.
