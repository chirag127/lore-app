Michael Lewis writes in a distinctive narrative style that blends
investigative journalism with novelistic storytelling. *Flash Boys*
is structured as a thriller, with a clear hero (Brad Katsuyama), a
clear villain (the HFT industry), and a dramatic climax (the founding
of IEX). Lewis uses short chapters, cliffhanger endings, and vivid
character sketches to maintain momentum.

The prose is colloquial and accessible. Lewis avoids jargon without
dumbing down the material. When he must explain technical concepts —
co-location, latency, dark pools — he does so through metaphor and
anecdote rather than definition. The Spread Networks chapter is a
masterpiece of explanatory journalism.

The book's rhetorical strategy is to establish moral outrage early
and sustain it. The opening line — "The U.S. stock market is rigged"
— is a provocation that Lewis spends the rest of the book
justifying. Critics argue that this framing is manipulative; admirers
say it is effective.

The tone is conversational and opinionated. Lewis inserts himself
into the narrative occasionally but mostly lets his characters tell
the story. His authorial voice — ironic, slightly cynical, morally
engaged — is the same voice that made *Liar's Poker* a classic.

Overall, *Flash Boys* is highly readable and can be finished in a
weekend. Its accessibility is both its greatest strength and the
source of its most valid criticism: the narrative demands moral
certainty of a subject that academic research shows is morally
ambiguous.
