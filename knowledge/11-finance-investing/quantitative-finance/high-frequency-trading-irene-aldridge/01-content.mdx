## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

**Chapter 1: Introduction.** Aldridge defines HFT and distinguishes
it from algorithmic trading. HFT is characterized by high turnover,
low latency, and short holding periods. She outlines the HFT
ecosystem and the evolution of electronic markets.

**Chapter 2: Market Microstructure and HFT.** Aldridge reviews the
market microstructure concepts most relevant for HFT: limit order
books, price formation, liquidity, and volatility at short time
scales. She shows how the Kyle and Glosten-Milgrom models apply
at sub-second horizons.

**Chapter 3: HFT Strategies: Market Making.** The core HFT strategy.
Aldridge explains how automated market makers provide liquidity,
earn the bid-ask spread, and manage inventory risk. She covers
order placement strategies, adverse selection avoidance, and
timing tactics.

**Chapter 4: HFT Strategies: Arbitrage.** Statistical arbitrage
across related instruments: index arbitrage, ETF arbitrage, and
cross-exchange arbitrage. Aldridge explains how HFT firms identify
and exploit price discrepancies before they converge.

**Chapter 5: HFT Strategies: Event Trading.** Trading around news
events, earnings announcements, and economic data releases.
Aldridge covers natural language processing, sentiment analysis,
and automated news reading.

**Chapter 6: Technology Infrastructure.** The technology stack:
hardware (servers, FPGAs, GPUs), networking (low-latency switches,
fiber optics), and software (C++, Java, FIX protocol). Aldridge
explains how technology choices affect strategy performance.

**Chapter 7: Co-location and Proximity.** The importance of physical
proximity to exchange data centers. Aldridge explains how
co-location reduces latency, the costs involved, and how firms
choose between exchange data centers.

**Chapter 8: Order Types and Smart Order Routing.** Advanced order
types (iceberg, pegged, discretionary) and how HFT firms use them.
Aldridge covers smart order routers that scan multiple venues for
best execution.

**Chapter 9: Backtesting HFT Strategies.** The special challenges
of backtesting at high frequencies: tick-level data requirements,
simulation accuracy, and the risk of overfitting.

**Chapter 10: Risk Management for HFT.** Unique risks of HFT:
technology failures (sending erroneous orders), market risks
(gapping through stops), and regulatory risks. Aldridge covers
kill switches, position limits, and real-time risk monitoring.

**Chapter 11: Regulation.** Aldridge reviews the regulatory landscape
for HFT: Reg NMS, Market Access Rule, and post-Flash Boys proposals.
She discusses the debate over HFT fairness.

## Reading Guide

This is a technical book best read by chapters of interest rather
than cover to cover. Chapter 6 (Infrastructure) and Chapter 9
(Backtesting) are the most useful for practitioners. The strategy
chapters (3-5) provide useful frameworks but the specific strategies
may have degraded since publication.
