## Strengths

**Broad coverage.** The book covers both the technology and the
strategies of HFT. Most books focus on one or the other. Aldridge
provides a comprehensive view.

**Practical infrastructure detail.** The chapters on co-location,
hardware, and network optimization are valuable. Aldridge explains
real decisions HFT firms face — which data center, what hardware,
how to optimize latency.

**Strategy taxonomy.** Aldridge provides a useful classification of
HFT strategies. Readers understand the full landscape before
deciding which approach to pursue.

## Weaknesses

**Dated technology.** Published in 2009, the technology chapters
describe hardware and networking that are now obsolete. FPGA
programming, low-latency networking, and exchange technology have
evolved significantly.

**Surface-level depth.** The book covers many topics but none in
great depth. Practitioners will need additional sources for
implementation details. The strategy chapters provide frameworks
but not executable code.

**No code.** Unlike Chan or Conlan, Aldridge provides no
programming examples. This limits the book's value as a practical
guide.

## Criticism

**Academic reviews** praise the book for cataloging HFT strategies
and technology but note that it lacks the analytical rigor of
academic microstructure texts.

**Practitioner feedback** is mixed. Some appreciate the breadth of
coverage. Others find the book too shallow for practical
implementation. The second edition (2013) partially addresses these
concerns.

## Impact

The book was one of the first comprehensive HFT guides and remains
a standard reference. It is widely cited in the HFT literature and
used in university courses. Aldridge's strategy taxonomy has been
adopted by many subsequent authors.
