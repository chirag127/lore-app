## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

### Part I: Getting In

**Chapter 1: The Liar's Poker Game.** The famous opening scene:
John Gutfreund, the King of Wall Street, challenges John Meriwether,
the head of bond trading, to a single hand of Liar's Poker for one
million dollars. Meriwether blinked. The anecdote establishes the
culture — testosterone-fueled, risk-obsessed, and strangely trivial.

**Chapter 2: The Making of a Bond Salesman.** Lewis describes his
Princeton education and his journey to Salomon Brothers. He was
hired by a managing director who had known his father. The chapter
is a satire of corporate recruitment and the absurdity of Wall Street
hiring.

**Chapter 3: The Training Program.** Salomon's training program
was famously brutal. Trainees were hazed, humiliated, and tested.
Lewis describes the experience with wit and self-deprecation.

### Part II: The Business

**Chapter 4: The Bond Market.** Lewis explains the bond market for
lay readers. He describes how Salomon Brothers dominated the
mortgage-backed securities market created by Lewis Ranieri. This
was the birth of securitization — the same innovation that would
implode in 2008.

**Chapter 5: The Salesman.** Lewis moves to London and learns the
art of bond sales. The chapter profiles his colleagues: the sleazy
salesman, the eccentric trader, the alcoholic manager.

**Chapter 6: The Customers.** Lewis describes the institutional
investors who bought bonds — pension funds, insurance companies,
savings and loans. He portrays them as both victims and
accomplices.

### Part III: The Fall

**Chapter 7: The Crash of 1987.** Black Monday, October 19, 1987.
Lewis describes how Salomon Brothers survived the crash while many
firms failed. The chapter captures the terror and exhilaration of
market collapse.

**Chapter 8: The Aftermath.** The post-crash years. Salomon
Brothers' dominance began to erode. The firm was caught in the
Treasury auction scandal of 1991, which ultimately destroyed it.

**Chapter 9: The Conclusion.** Lewis leaves Salomon Brothers and
reflects on what he learned. Wall Street, he concludes, is not about
allocating capital or building wealth. It is about taking money from
people who do not understand the game.

## Reading Guide

*Liar's Poker* is best read as a companion to *The Big Short*. The
first book describes the creation of the mortgage-backed securities
market; the second book describes its destruction. Pay special
attention to the Lewis Ranieri chapters — Ranieri is the unsung hero
(later villain) of the securitization story.

The book is not about trading techniques. It is about culture.
Readers looking for technical knowledge should look elsewhere.
