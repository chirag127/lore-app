## Strengths

**The definitive insider account.** No book before *Liar's Poker* had
captured Wall Street culture from the inside. Lewis wrote as a
participant, not an observer, giving the book authenticity.

**Cultural documentation.** The book preserved a time and place —
Salomon Brothers in the 1980s — that has since vanished. It is an
invaluable historical document of finance before electronic trading
and regulation.

**Explanatory power.** Lewis explains complex financial instruments
(mortgage-backed securities, swaps, arbitrage) clearly enough for a
general reader. The mortgage bond chapter is a prequel to the 2008
crisis.

## Weaknesses

**Dated relevance.** The Wall Street of 1989 is gone. Electronic
trading, regulation, and changing culture have made the book more a
period piece than a current guide.

**Self-serving narrative.** Lewis portrays himself as a naive outsider
while omitting his own ambition and privilege. Some critics note that
he was on the fast track at Salomon and left only after the firm
collapsed.

## Criticism

**John Rothchild (The New York Times)** called it "the funniest book
about Wall Street ever written" while noting that Lewis's
self-deprecation sometimes felt like false modesty.

**Journalist Tom Wolfe** praised the book as "a modern classic" of
Wall Street literature, comparing Lewis's eye for detail to that of
a novelist.

**Academics** criticize the book for glorifying the culture it
claims to condemn. Lewis's ironic tone makes the excesses seem
entertaining rather than dangerous.

## Impact

*Liar's Poker* launched a generation of Wall Street memoirists and
became required reading in business schools. It made Lewis a
bestselling author. The book's description of mortgage-backed
securities proved prescient — the instruments he described would
cause the 2008 financial crisis.
