Michael Lewis's debut established his signature style: first-person
narrative, ironic observation, and vivid character sketches. The
book is structured as a memoir but reads like a novel — each chapter
has a narrative arc, complete with setting, conflict, and resolution.

The voice is what makes the book. Lewis writes as a young man
observing a strange new world, simultaneously appalled and amused.
He never preaches. He lets the absurdity speak for itself.

The prose is tight and efficient. Lewis can describe a person in a
single sentence. He is especially good at physical detail — the way
someone dresses, the decor of a trading floor, the posture of a
manager under pressure.

Dialogue is minimal but effective. Lewis remembers (or reconstructs)
key conversations that reveal character. The famous Liar's Poker
challenge between Gutfreund and Meriwether is only a few paragraphs,
but it defines the entire culture.

The book's biggest weakness as narrative is its loose structure.
After the training program chapters, the London chapters lack the
same narrative momentum. The book meanders in places, as real life
does. But this is a minor complaint for a book that defined a genre.
