## Overview

*Liar's Poker* launched Michael Lewis's career and defined the genre
of Wall Street memoir. The book follows Lewis from his unlikely
hiring at Salomon Brothers through his training and two years as a
bond salesman. It is a coming-of-age story set against the backdrop
of the 1980s bond market boom, when Salomon Brothers dominated the
newly created mortgage-backed securities market.

The title refers to a game played by Salomon traders — a version of
the bluffing game commonly played with dollar bill serial numbers.
For Lewis, the game was a metaphor for Wall Street itself: a place
where confidence, deception, and reading people mattered more than
technical knowledge.

## Executive Summary

Lewis arrived at Salomon Brothers in 1985 with no financial
background. He was hired through the personal connection of a Salomon
executive who had known his father. The book follows Salomon's rise
and fall, from its dominance of the mortgage bond market through the
1987 crash to the 1991 Treasury auction scandal that destroyed its
reputation. Along the way, Lewis profiles the firm's alpha males —
John Gutfreund, Lewis Ranieri, and John Meriwether — and documents
the culture of excess that defined the era.
