## Part-by-Part Summary

### Part I: Inventory Models

**Chapter 1: Introduction.** O'Hara defines market microstructure
and its relationship to traditional finance theory. She argues that
the Walrasian auctioneer assumption of classical economics obscures
the real mechanics of price formation.

**Chapter 2: The Inventory Paradigm.** The earliest microstructure
models focused on how market makers set prices to manage inventory.
Garman (1976) modeled the market maker as a monopolist facing
stochastic order flow. The bid-ask spread compensates for inventory
risk.

**Chapter 3: Inventory Models with Optimization.** Stoll (1978) and
Ho and Stoll (1981) extended the inventory approach to model
utility-maximizing dealers. The spread depends on dealer risk
aversion, transaction size, and the stochastic properties of order
flow.

### Part II: Information Models

**Chapter 4: The Information Paradigm.** Bagehot (1971) first
observed that market makers face adverse selection — they trade
against informed counterparties and lose money on those trades.
This insight launched the information approach.

**Chapter 5: The Glosten-Milgrom Model.** The key model of
informed trading. A competitive market maker sets a bid-ask spread
that reflects the probability of trading against an informed
trader. The model predicts that spreads widen when information
asymmetry is high and that prices converge to full information
over time.

**Chapter 6: The Sequential Trade Model.** O'Hara extends the
Glosten-Milgrom framework to multiple trading periods. The model
shows how order flow reveals private information and how the
market maker learns from each trade.

### Part III: Strategic Trader Models

**Chapter 7: The Kyle Model.** The most influential microstructure
model. An informed trader strategically splits his order across
multiple periods to maximize profits while minimizing price impact.
The market maker observes total order flow and sets prices
accordingly. The model shows how private information is gradually
incorporated into prices and why insider trading is profitable.

**Chapter 8: Extensions of the Kyle Model.** O'Hara covers
extensions with multiple informed traders, multiple dealers, and
different information structures. The models show how competition
among informed traders affects price discovery.

### Part IV: Empirical Implications

**Chapter 9: Empirical Testing.** O'Hara surveys empirical tests
of microstructure models. She covers the determinants of bid-ask
spreads, the information content of trades, and the behavior of
intraday prices.

**Chapter 10: Market Design.** The practical implications of
microstructure theory for exchange design. O'Hara discusses call
auctions vs continuous trading, specialist vs dealer markets, and
the trade-off between transparency and liquidity.

## Reading Guide

This is a graduate-level text requiring familiarity with
microeconomics, game theory, and statistics. Readers should have
completed a PhD-level course in financial economics before
attempting this book. The Kyle model (Chapter 7) is the most
important chapter and the most cited. The inventory chapters
(2-3) are less relevant for modern electronic markets.
