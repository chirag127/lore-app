## Strengths

**Foundational synthesis.** O'Hara organized a scattered literature
into a coherent framework. Before this book, microstructure research
was a collection of disconnected papers. O'Hara showed how they fit
together.

**Theoretical rigor.** The models are presented with their full
analytical structure. O'Hara does not skip steps. Readers who work
through the mathematics will have a thorough understanding of the
theory.

**Bridge to empirical work.** The final chapters connect theory to
evidence. O'Hara shows how theoretical models generate testable
predictions and surveys the empirical results.

## Weaknesses

**Dated.** Published in 1995, the book predates the electronic
trading revolution. There is no discussion of limit order books,
dark pools, or high-frequency trading. The models assume human
market makers who behave strategically, not algorithms executing
in microseconds.

**No computational approach.** The book is purely analytical.
There are no numerical examples, simulations, or code. Modern
readers expect computational models alongside the mathematics.

**Limited practical relevance.** The models are designed for
academic understanding, not practical application. Practitioners
will find Harris's *Trading and Exchanges* more useful.

## Criticism

**Academic reception.** The book is widely praised and cited. A
review in The Review of Financial Studies (1995) called it an
"invaluable guide" to the microstructure literature. It remains
the standard text for PhD courses.

**Practitioner feedback.** Traders generally find the book too
theoretical. The models provide intuition about market mechanisms
but cannot be directly applied to trading decisions.

## Impact

*Market Microstructure Theory* defined the field of microstructure
as an academic discipline. It is the most cited work on
microstructure theory and has influenced hundreds of subsequent
papers. O'Hara's synthesis established the research agenda that
microstructure scholars have followed for two decades. The Kyle
(1985) model, as explained by O'Hara, remains the central
theoretical framework for understanding how information affects
prices.
