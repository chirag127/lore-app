## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

### Part I: The Discovery

**Chapter 1: A Secret.** Michael Burry, a San Jose doctor turned
hedge fund manager, discovers that the subprime mortgage market is
built on fraud. He reads through thousands of mortgage prospectuses
and finds that loans are being made to people who cannot repay them.
He decides to bet against housing by buying credit default swaps.

**Chapter 2: The Machine.** Lewis explains the mortgage machine.
Brokers originated loans, banks packaged them into bonds, and rating
agencies stamped them AAA. Everyone was paid to keep the machine
running. The chapter profiles the major players: subprime lenders like
New Century and Ameriquest, and investment banks like Goldman Sachs
and Merrill Lynch.

**Chapter 3: The Bond Market.** Greg Lippmann, a Deutsche Bank
trader, also sees the bubble. He starts recommending that clients
short the mortgage market. His bosses initially resist but let him
proceed because the trades generate fees.

### Part II: The Characters

**Chapter 4: The Outsiders.** Jamie Mai and Charlie Ledley, two
friends who started Cornell Capital in a backyard shed, discover
the same trade. They are the books most unlikely heroes — young,
inexperienced, and unconnected. Their willingness to think
independently leads them to the same conclusion as Burry.

**Chapter 5: The Cynic.** Steve Eisman, a hedge fund manager at
FrontPoint Partners, is the book's most colorful character. A former
lawyer turned investor, Eisman had long believed the financial
industry was corrupt. The subprime crisis confirmed everything he
believed. Lewis follows Eisman as he investigates mortgage lenders
and discovers the fraud.

### Part III: The Collapse

**Chapter 6: The Doomsday Machine.** The creation of the synthetic
CDO — instruments that allowed Wall Street to create unlimited
bets on mortgage bonds. These instruments amplified the housing
bets exponentially. Lewis explains how Goldman Sachs's Abacus deal
exemplified the conflict: Goldman was betting against the same bonds
it was selling to clients.

**Chapter 7: The Wait.** The short sellers had to wait through
2006 and 2007 while housing prices continued rising. The chapter
describes the psychological torture of being right but early. Burry's
investors tried to withdraw their money. Eisman faced margin calls.
Only their conviction kept them in the trade.

**Chapter 8: The Crash.** The collapse of Bear Stearns, Lehman
Brothers, and AIG. Lewis describes how the financial system
unraveled. The short sellers finally made their fortunes. But the
victory felt hollow — the system they had bet against had destroyed
trillions in wealth.

### Part IV: The Aftermath

**Chapter 9: The Winners.** What happened to the investors who bet
against housing. Burry's fund returned 489%. Eisman's fund returned
hundreds of millions. The book ends with a sober reflection: no one
went to jail, and the system that caused the crisis was never fixed.

## Reading Guide

*The Big Short* is best read as the middle volume of Lewis's Wall
Street trilogy, between *Liar's Poker* (the creation of the mortgage
machine) and *Flash Boys* (the rise of electronic trading). The 2015
film adaptation is excellent but compresses the story — the book has
far more detail about the mechanics of synthetic CDOs and the
psychology of the short sellers.

For a deeper understanding of the crisis, pair with *All the Devils
Are Here* by Bethany McLean and Joe Nocera, or *Too Big to Fail* by
Andrew Ross Sorkin.
