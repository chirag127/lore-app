*The Big Short* marks the peak of Michael Lewis's narrative powers.
The book is structured as a thriller with multiple storylines that
converge in the 2008 crash. Lewis intercuts between his four
protagonist groups, building suspense even though the outcome is
known.

The prose is more confident than *Liar's Poker* and more controlled
than *Flash Boys*. Lewis has mastered the art of making technical
finance vivid. He uses metaphor generously: a synthetic CDO is "a
machine that turns lead into gold — then turns the gold into lead."

Characterization is the book's strongest suit. Each protagonist has
a distinct voice and psychology. Michael Burry is presented with
unusual empathy — Lewis spent more time with him than any other
character. Steve Eisman provides comic relief with his profane
rants.

The book's narrative weakness is its ending. After the crash, the
story deflates. Lewis rushes through the aftermath, and the final
chapters feel anticlimactic. This is perhaps unavoidable — reality
does not provide neat endings.

The tone is morally engaged but not preachy. Lewis lets the facts
speak for themselves but arranges them to deliver a clear verdict:
the financial industry committed fraud, and no one was punished.
