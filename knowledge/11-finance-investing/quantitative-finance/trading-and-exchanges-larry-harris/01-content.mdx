## Part-by-Part Summary

### Part I: The Trading Process

**Chapter 1-3: Introduction.** Harris defines trading and explains
why people trade: to transfer risk, to raise capital, to profit from
information, or to satisfy liquidity needs. He introduces the key
concepts of liquidity, volatility, and transaction costs.

**Chapter 4-6: Orders and Order Placement.** A taxonomy of order
types: market orders, limit orders, stop orders, and their
variations. Harris explains how order choice reveals trader
intentions and affects execution quality.

**Chapter 7-9: Market Structures.** The different ways markets can
be organized: quote-driven markets (dealers), order-driven markets
(auctions), and hybrid systems. Harris compares the NYSE, NASDAQ,
and electronic exchanges.

### Part II: Market Participants

**Chapter 10-13: Traders.** Harris classifies traders by their
motives: liquidity traders (need to trade), informed traders (have
information), and value traders (do research). He explains that
uninformed traders systematically lose to informed traders.

**Chapter 14-16: Brokers and Dealers.** The roles of intermediaries.
Brokers execute orders on behalf of clients; dealers provide
liquidity by maintaining inventories. Harris explains how dealers
manage risk and set bid-ask spreads.

**Chapter 17-19: Exchanges.** How exchanges create value by
aggregating orders, setting rules, and providing price
transparency. Harris discusses competition between exchanges and
the economics of exchange ownership.

### Part III: Transaction Costs

**Chapter 20-22: Measuring Costs.** Explicit costs (commissions,
fees) and implicit costs (spread, market impact, delay). Harris
provides frameworks for measuring and managing transaction costs.

**Chapter 23-25: Market Impact.** The most important cost for
large traders. Harris explains how large orders move prices and
how traders can minimize impact.

### Part IV: Regulation and Ethics

**Chapter 26-28: Market Regulation.** Why markets are regulated:
investor protection, market integrity, and systemic risk. Harris
explains the SEC's role and key regulations.

**Chapter 29-31: Ethics and Manipulation.** The line between
legitimate trading and illegal manipulation. Harris covers front
running, insider trading, and market manipulation schemes.

## Reading Guide

This is not a book to read cover to cover — it is a reference to
consult. Most readers start with the transaction costs chapters
(20-25), which directly affect trading performance. The market
structure chapters (7-9) are essential for understanding how
modern markets work. Skip the historical chapters on first reading.
