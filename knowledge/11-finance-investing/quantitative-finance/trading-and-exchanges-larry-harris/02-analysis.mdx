## Strengths

**Comprehensive.** The book covers every aspect of market
microstructure. Nothing is omitted: order types, market structures,
participants, costs, regulation, and ethics. It is the single most
complete reference on the subject.

**Authoritative.** Harris served as SEC Chief Economist. He
understands both the theory and the practice. The book reflects his
unique combination of academic rigor and regulatory experience.

**Practical framework.** Unlike many microstructure texts, this one
is written for practitioners. Harris provides frameworks for
analyzing any trading situation. The transaction cost model alone
is worth the price.

## Weaknesses

**Dated examples.** Published in 2003, the book predates the rise
of high-frequency trading, dark pools, and modern electronic
markets. The regulatory chapters reference pre-Reg NMS rules.
Readers need to supplement with current material.

**Length.** At 643 pages, the book is daunting. Many readers will
not finish it. The depth is a strength but also a barrier.

**No code.** Unlike Chan or Conlan, Harris provides no programming
examples. The book is conceptual rather than computational.

## Criticism

**Academic praise.** The book is widely cited in academic
microstructure research. It is used as a textbook in graduate
finance programs worldwide.

**Practitioner feedback.** Professional traders and institutional
investors consider it essential reading. However, some note that
the book's pre-electronic focus limits its relevance for modern
HFT-focused markets.

## Impact

*Trading and Exchanges* is the most cited book in market
microstructure. It has educated a generation of traders, regulators,
and academics. Harris's framework for understanding transaction
costs has become the industry standard. The book is often called
"the bible of market microstructure."
