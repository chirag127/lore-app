Larry Harris writes in the voice of a patient professor explaining
complex ideas to engaged students. The prose is formal but clear.
Harris defines every term precisely, builds arguments methodically,
and summarizes key points at the end of each chapter.

The book's structure is modular. Chapters are designed to be read
independently, making the book suitable as a reference. Harris uses
examples from real markets throughout, grounding abstract concepts
in concrete situations.

The tone is neutral and analytical. Harris avoids the polemics that
characterize Michael Lewis. He presents multiple perspectives on
contentious issues — market fragmentation, payment for order flow,
maker-taker pricing — and lets readers draw their own conclusions.

The book's biggest challenge is its length and density. Readers
unfamiliar with market microstructure will find the first 100 pages
overwhelming. Harris provides minimal guidance on reading order.
The book rewards patient study but frustrates casual browsing.

Overall, the writing achieves its goal: it is clear, precise, and
comprehensive. It will never be a page-turner, but it will be a
lifelong reference for serious market participants.
