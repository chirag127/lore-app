_Fooled by Randomness_ is the opening salvo of Nassim Nicholas Taleb's Incerto series — a multi-volume philosophical essay on uncertainty and probability that would eventually include _The Black Swan_, _Antifragile_, and _Skin in the Game_. Published in 2001, it arrived before the dot-com crash had fully registered and before the 2008 financial crisis provided the ultimate real-world demonstration of its thesis. The book's central argument is deceptively simple: humans are pattern-seeking animals who systematically underestimate the role of randomness in outcomes, and this cognitive flaw leads us to mistake luck for skill in everything from stock trading to career success to historical explanation.

<BookHeader />

The book is organized in three parts. Part I establishes the intellectual framework — the distinction between skill and luck, the survivorship bias that hides failed traders, and the mathematical concept of alternative histories. Part II catalogs the specific cognitive biases that fool us, from confirmation bias to the narrative fallacy. Part III offers practical and philosophical remedies, including stoicism and what Taleb calls "wax in your ears" — the deliberate filtering of noise. At its core, the book is not an attack on success but on the post-hoc rationalization of success that ignores the role of chance. Taleb writes as a practitioner — a former options trader who navigated the probabilistic world of financial derivatives — and brings the urgency of someone who has seen smart people destroyed by randomness they refused to acknowledge.
