_Nudge_ is the book that introduced the world to behavioral public policy. Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein argued that small, low-cost changes in how choices are presented can dramatically improve outcomes in areas ranging from retirement savings to organ donation to health care. The central concept — the "nudge" — has become one of the most influential ideas in modern policy design, spawning behavioral insights teams in governments around the world.

<BookHeader />

The book is grounded in the behavioral economics research that Thaler and colleagues had been developing for decades, combined with Sunstein's expertise in law and regulatory policy. Their core insight is deceptively simple: every choice is presented in some context, that context inevitably influences what people choose, and therefore there is no such thing as a neutral choice environment. The question is not whether to engage in choice architecture but whether to do it well or poorly.
