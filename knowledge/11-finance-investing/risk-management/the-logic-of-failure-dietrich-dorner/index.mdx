_The Logic of Failure_ is Dietrich Dörner's classic study of how intelligent, well-meaning people make disastrous decisions when faced with complex, dynamic systems. Drawing on decades of computer-simulated experiments, Dörner identifies recurring patterns of thought that lead to failure: linear thinking where nonlinear logic is required, focus on symptoms rather than causes, and overconfidence in the face of uncertainty. First published in German in 1989 and translated into English in 1996, the book has influenced fields from cognitive psychology to risk management to artificial intelligence.

<BookHeader />

Dörner's central insight is that failure is not random or the result of stupidity — it follows its own distinct logic. When confronted with complex problems, people tend to act in ways that are locally rational but globally disastrous. They focus on immediate problems while ignoring side effects, apply single solutions to multiple problems, and become increasingly entrenched in failing strategies. The book uses vivid case studies — from the Chernobyl disaster to the collapse of the East German economy — to illustrate the patterns it identifies, and offers practical guidance for developing the cognitive skills needed to navigate complexity without catastrophe.
