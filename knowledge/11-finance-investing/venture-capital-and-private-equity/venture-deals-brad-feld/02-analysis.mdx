## Context & Origins

*Venture Deals* was born from Brad Feld's blog, Feld Thoughts, which began in
2004. Between 2005 and 2007, Feld and Mendelson published a blog series on AskTheVC.com
- "Ask the VC" columns that answered founder questions about term sheets in plain
English. Readers kept asking where they could buy a book with all the answers in
one place. The first edition (2011, published independently via CreateSpace,
distributed by Wiley) compiled and restructured that blog content into a
systematic 288-page guide.

The timing was significant. In 2011, the startup funding landscape was
fundamentally different from what exists today. SAFEs didn't exist (Y Combinator
would release the SAFE in late 2013). Convertible notes were the primary
alternative to priced equity for seed-stage companies. Friend-and-family checks,
SAFE rounds, and micro-VC seed funds were not yet normalized. This context matters
because the book's structure is built around a single scenario: a founder raising
their first priced equity round (Series A) from a traditional venture capital
firm. Every chapter is organized around the clauses of that investment document.

The first edition's scope is deliberately narrower than what followed. It has
9 chapters, organized into 4 parts: The Players and Preparation, The Term Sheet
(Economics and Control), Alternative Financing, and How VC Funds Work. This was
a focused, opinionated guide — not the encyclopedic reference it became in later
editions.

## Originality & Intellectual Contribution

The book's primary contribution is not a new theory of venture capital — it is
*democratization of legal knowledge*. Before *Venture Deals*, term sheet literacy
was transmitted vertically: from VC partner to associate, from lawyer to client,
from experienced founder to first-time founder. The information asymmetry was
enormous. VCs negotiated hundreds of term sheets; founders negotiated one (if
they were lucky). Founders who approached their first term sheet without reading
this book were effectively signing a contract written in a language they had never
studied, enforced by people who had been writing it for decades.

Feld and Mendelson's insight was that the barrier to entry was not intelligence
or resources — it was access to information. The economics/control framework,
which sorts every term into one of two categories, is a genuinely useful
intellectual contribution. No one in the prior literature had produced this
reduction.

The chapter on VC fund economics (Chapter 8) also represents original contribution.
No VC firm had ever publicly explained how management fees, carry, fund life, and
the power law shape investor behavior in terms accessible to non-investors. By
making the economics of being a VC legible to founders, Feld and Mendelson created
a new frame for understanding why investors ask for the terms they do.

## Key Arguments & Evidence

**1. Term sheet is a blueprint, not a letter of intent.**
The authors argue vigorously that term sheets are legally binding in their most
important provisions (no-shop, exclusivity) and effectively binding in their
others. The "we'll sort out the details later" approach is how founders sign
agreements that differ materially from what they intended. Evidence: multiple
real term sheets reviewed in the book's workshops showing gaps between negotiated
terms and final legal documents.

**2. Only economics and control matter.**
The framework is defended through worked examples. Each term is shown in a payout
scenario (variously: a $10M exit, a $50M exit, a $100M exit) to demonstrate that
terms outside economics and control rarely change the founder's financial outcome.
Evidence: the $50M exit comparison table in Chapter 7 showing that information
rights and ROFR have zero payout impact while liquidation preference determines
everything.

**3. The option pool is a price adjustment, not a benefit.**
Andreessen Horowitz popularized this framing later, but Feld and Mendelson invented
the argument in book form. The proof is arithmetic: a $10M pre-money with a 20%
option pool means the effective economic pre-money to founders is $8M. A $8M pre-
money with a 10% pool means effective pre-money is $7.2M — far closer to the
stated number than founders assume. Evidence: multiple worked scenarios with
actual share counts and dilution percentages.

**4. Liquidation preference can wipe founders out.**
The book pays extraordinary attention to this term, providing worked examples at
1x non-participating, 1x participating, 2x participating, and capped participation.
The 1x participating example with a $50M exit on a $20M raise (25% VC ownership)
shows VCs receiving $22.5M and common holders receiving $27.5M before considering
additional series. Evidence: table-based math exercises in Chapter 4.

**5. VC fund structure determines behavior.**
The power law argument: in any given fund, 2-3 investments generate all returns.
This explains (and predicts) VC pressure for aggressive growth, impatience for
exits, and willingness to accept outlier risk at the company level. A "modest
success" (2-3x return) is a failure from the fund's perspective because it does
not return the fund to LPs. Evidence: LP commitment structures, public statements
from top-tier VC GPs about portfolio construction, and analysis of fund return
distribution in academic studies.

## Strengths

- **Term sheet literacy is genuinely empowering.** Before this book, the
  information gap between a first-time founder and a first-time term sheet was
  effectively insurmountable without expensive legal counsel. This book makes
  the gap navigable without requiring a law degree.

- **The economics/control framework is a durable mental model.** The simplicity
  of the split — "who gets the money and who makes decisions" — is powerful
  because it works at all stages of company development. It is as useful for
  evaluating a Series B term sheet as it is for a seed-round convertible note.

- **Concrete worked examples in every chapter.** The liquidation preference math
  tables, the anti-dilution calculation walkthroughs, the cap table progression
  across rounds — these are not abstract legal theory. They are numerical
  demonstrations that founders can apply directly to their own cap tables.

- **Honest acknowledgment of the book's bias.** Feld and Mendelson are VC
  partners at a firm that is actively investing. They include entrepreneur
  commentary (Matt Blumberg) specifically as a counterweight. This meta-awareness
  is unusual in books written by industry participants.

- **Practical process guidance (Chapter 2).** Most books about fundraising
  discuss term sheets and skip the 12-week process that produces the term sheet.
  Chapter 2 covers preparation, materials, VC selection, and closing mechanics
  — the full arc from first meeting to wire transfer.

- **The fund economics chapter (Chapter 8) was pioneering.** When it was
  published in 2011, no other public resource explained VC fund economics with
  this clarity. It remains the most cited chapter for understanding why VCs
  behave the way they do.

---

## Weaknesses

- **Densely technical.** The book's examples require concentration and sometimes
  backtracking. Founders report reading the liquidation preference and anti-dilution
  sections multiple times (often with a calculator) before internalizing them.
  The density is a feature for reference use but a bug for casual reading.

- **Legal references are US-specific (Delaware corporate law, California
  securities law).** International founders, particularly those incorporated in
  the UK, Germany, India, or Singapore, need country-specific supplements. The
  book acknowledges this limitation in a single paragraph but does not address
  it.

- **Outdated on seed-stage instruments.** The 2011 edition pre-dates the SAFE
  (Y Combinator, 2013) and the widespread adoption of rolling funds and SPVs.
  Convertible notes receive full treatment, which is appropriate given the era,
  but founders in 2024+ will need to supplement with current seed-stage guidance.

- **Defensive of VC orthodoxy.** The phrase "this is standard" appears throughout
  the book. While the authors almost always explain *why* terms are standard,
  the cumulative effect positions VC industry practice as broadly fair and
  reasonable. Critics argue that "standard" is a negotiating position, not an
  objective description, and the book treats it more like the latter.

- **Light on emotional intelligence.** Fundraising is among the most stressful
  experiences for founders — the book frames it as an optimization problem.
  There is no guidance on managing rejection, impostor syndrome, or the personal
  toll of months of investor meetings.

- **Assumes the founders will sign the term sheet.** The book is fundamentally
  a guide to negotiating within the VC framework, not an exploration of whether
  founders should raise VC at all. Founders considering bootstrapping, revenue-
  based financing, or crowdfunding will find little validation for those paths.

## Criticism

### "This Is Marketing for Foundry Group"
*Marc Andreessen* (a16z co-founder, writing in a 2011 blog post context): "The
book is useful, but it's also positioning for the firm. By writing the definitive
guide to term sheets, Feld positions Foundry Group as the most founder-friendly
firm around. That's smart business."

**Response:** Andreessen's charge is structurally correct. The book does generate
reputational value for its authors and their firm. But the self-interested origin
does not negate the output's value. A rising tide lifts all boats — the book's
terminology and framework have been adopted industry-wide. Information asymmetry
was reduced for the entire ecosystem, not just founders who end up at Foundry
Group. Andreessen himself has since funded and recommended the book.

### "The True Standard Is What Successful Founders Do, Not What This Book Says"
*Marc Suster* (Upfront Ventures, writing in his "Both Sides of the Table" blog,
2011): "I read a draft of this before publication. I told Brad and Jason that
their characterization of 'standard' terms didn't match what we see in most LA
and New York deals. The book describes the ideal, not the reality that most
founders face — especially outside Silicon Valley."

**Response:** This critique is partially accurate but frames the problem backward.
The book describes what founders *should* know, not what they *will* encounter.
Suster is right that many VCs outside the top-tier ecosystem use aggressive terms.
The correct response to that observation is: every founder should know what
standard is so they can recognize when they're getting a non-standard deal. The
book's value increases, not decreases, when the market reality is worse than the
described standard.

### "The Book Sanctifies VC Governance Models Inadequately"
*Cory Doctorow* (writer, activist, and technology critic, writing in Boing Boing,
2012): "The book's frame assumes founders who want to build companies that last
should eagerly accept VC terms including board majorities, protective provisions,
and liquidation preferences that structurally subordinate common stock. There is
no treatment of alternative governance models: dual-class structures that founders
actually use to retain control, revenue-based financing that doesn't require equity,
or cooperative ownership models."

**Response:** The book's narrow frame — "this is how VC deals work, here's how
to navigate them" — is a choice, not a failure. Doctorow is correct that the book
does not explore governance alternatives, and that omission does implicitly
legitimate the VC model as the default. However, *Venture Deals* was never
positioned as a guide to whether to raise VC. It is a manual for the most common
path — and that's a legitimate scope.

### "It's Boring and Unreadable for Anyone Not About to Raise Money"
*David Pogue* (technology columnist, writing in a personal blog context, 2011):
"When a task is urgent and specific — 'I have a term sheet in my inbox and need
to understand it by Thursday' — this book is a godsend. As general reading, it's
rough going."

**Response:** Accurate. The book is a reference manual, not narrative non-fiction.
The authors would likely agree: their stated goal is not to entertain but to
inform. Founders who read it before their first fundraise, put it on a shelf,
and return to it when a term sheet arrives are using it correctly.

## Comparison to Similar Books

| Book | Author(s) | Key Difference |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| *Venture Deals* (2011) | Feld & Mendelson | The first-term-sheet founder's manual. Covers priced rounds, entrepreneur narrative, and term sheet mechanics clause by clause |
| *Secrets of Sand Hill Road* | Scott Kupor | a16z insider perspective, broader VC firm strategy, less granular clause-by-clause coverage |
| *The Hard Thing About Hard Things* | Ben Horowitz | Post-fundraising CEO management. Covers culture, emotional leadership — what *Venture Deals* omits |
| *Venture Capital and the Finance of Innovation* | Andrew Metrick | Yale SOM academic treatment. Option pricing, portfolio theory, mathematical rigor. Not a founder's guide |
| *Angel* | Jason Calacanis | Angel investor framing. Good companion for founders who want to compare how angels vs. institutional VCs think |
| *The Business of Venture Capital* | Mahendra Ramsinghani | VC fund raising, LP marketing, and fund structure. From the fund manager's perspective; complements *Venture Deals* founder focus |

## Final Assessment

| Dimension | Rating | Notes |
|------------|--------|-------|
| Practical Utility | 9/10 | The single most actionable book on VC mechanics. Every clause explained with a worked example |
| Originality | 8/10 | The economics/control framework, the fund economics chapter, and the option-pool-as-price-adjustment insight were novel at publication |
| Readability | 5/10 | Dense, reference-like. Not a page-turner; best read with a highlighter and notepad |
| Objectivity | 6/10 | VC authors being transparent about bias, but bias is present. Entrepreneur commentary helps |
| Cross-Applicability | 5/10 | US-centric, first-edition-era instrument focus. SAFE founder world needs supplementation |
| Lasting Impact | 9/10 | Defined the vocabulary of VC term sheet literacy for an entire generation of founders |

**Overall: 8.5/10**

*Venture Deals* (first edition, 2011) did something no prior book had done: it
made the most important financial document in a founder's professional life legible
without requiring a law degree. Its 288 pages are not philosophy, and they are
not business strategy. They are the operating manual for the specific contract
that determines who owns what after you take institutional investment. That skill
is a prerequisite for everything else in funded entrepreneurship.

The first edition is narrower than what followed — 9 chapters, no SAFE chapter,
no crowdfunding, no venture debt — but the core is intact, coherent, and more
accessible than any subsequent treatment. Founders should read it before their
first priced round, keep it as a reference through subsequent rounds, and
recognize it for what it is: not the final word on VC, but the indispensable
first one.
