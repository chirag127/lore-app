## The Problem: Asymmetric Information Between VCs and Founders

For most of the history of venture capital — from Arthur Rock's investment in
Apple in 1978 through the dot-com boom and into the 2010s — the relationship
between venture investor and entrepreneur was defined by extreme information
asymmetry. VCs and the lawyers who drafted term sheets had negotiated hundreds of
financing documents. Founders had negotiated one: the round they were currently
running. The terms of that document — preferred stock rights, liquidation
preferences, anti-dilution protections, drag-along agreements — were drafted by
professionals whose interests were aligned with the investors, not the founders.

The practical consequence of this asymmetry was severe. A 2012 study of startup
exits by Noam Wasserman (Harvard Business School) found that founders who retained
less than 10% equity at Series A had median personal returns that were 80% lower
than founders who retained 20-30%. Many of those founders who retained so little
equity had signed term sheets with participating preferred, full-ratchet anti-
dilution, or option pools stretching to 25% — terms they did not understand when
they signed and that compounded across subsequent rounds. The information asymmetry
was not just an inconvenience. It was a structural wealth transfer mechanism.

The problem Feld and Mendelson aimed to solve: give founders the vocabulary,
the framework, and the arithmetic to understand what they were signing. The 2011
first edition was the most direct answer to that problem ever published.

---

## The Book's Proposed Solution: Term Sheet Literacy Through Clause-by-Clause
Education

The book addresses the information problem through five interlocking strategies:

**1. The Economics/Control Framework**

By dividing every term in a term sheet into one of two categories — economics
("who gets the money?") or control ("who makes decisions?") — the authors give
founders a sorting mechanism that requires no legal background. Within this
framework, negotiation strategy becomes straightforward: the three terms that
matter most are price (economy), liquidation preference (economy), and board
seats (control). Everything else is secondary or boilerplate.

**2. Worked Examples With Real Numbers**

Every chapter includes tables showing what happens to different parties under
different exit scenarios. The liquidation preference example alone — three
scenarios (1x non-participating, 1x participating, 2x participating) on a $50M
company sale — makes the book's core argument self-evident through arithmetic.
Founders who could describe the qualitative difference between participating and
non-participating preferred before reading the book could calculate the dollar
impact of each structure after.

**3. The Cap Table as a Practical Tool**

Chapter 7 takes founders through the mechanics of maintaining a cap table across
four rounds. This is not theory — it is a spreadsheet exercise with concrete
share counts. By the end, a founder who has never maintained a cap table
understands dilution mathematically, not just intuitively.

**4. The VC Fund Economics Chapter**

Chapter 8 explains how the 2/20 fee structure, 10-year fund life, and power law
of returns shape investor incentives. This is the chapter that transforms how
founders read VCs' behavior — understanding why your investor legitimately wants
a faster exit than you do is a prerequisite for productive disagreement rather
than bitter misunderstanding.

**5. Negotiation as a Learnable Process**

Chapter 9 reframes fundraising from art to logistics. The keys: run a process,
create competition, treat the term sheet as the place where negotiation happens,
and understand that your leverage is your alternatives. This was the most
frequently quoted chapter in founder communities in the years following publication.

## Evidence of the Solution's Effectiveness

By any reasonable measure, the book achieved its goal. Within five years of its
publication, it became standard distribution at Y Combinator, Techstars, and 500
Startups — not because firms paid for placement, but because founders who had
read it before their round reported better negotiation outcomes and a clearer
understanding of their investor relationships.

More tellingly, the book's vocabulary entered the common parlance of the startup
ecosystem. Terms like "liquidation preference," "participating preferred," "full
ratchet," and "broad-based weighted average" moved from specialist legal jargon
into founder vocabulary. This vocalization shift — from language known only to
lawyers and VCs to language used by entrepreneurs in pitch meetings — was the
book's strongest practical evidence of effectiveness.

However, the information gap was not fully closed by one book. Participating
preferred continued to appear in term sheets issued by first-time funds and micro-
VCs well into the 2010s. Full-ratchet anti-dilution showed up in seed-stage deals
as recently as 2020. The book's solution — founder education — helped, but it did
not eliminate aggressive terms from the market. Structural market forces (the
continuing supply of first-time VCs with limited deal flow, and the desperation
of founders competing for scarce capital) continued to generate asymmetric deals
despite increased founder literacy.

## Unresolved Questions

**1. Who Is the Book's Actual Audience?**

The book is positioned for first-time founders, but its most sophisticated
readers are experienced VCs and lawyers who want to understand the founder
perspective on terms they draft routinely. The founders who benefit most are
those with sufficient leverage to negotiate — founders who have multiple term
sheets and are selecting between investors. A founder with one term sheet and 60
days of runway cannot credibly negotiate; they can only understand. The book does
not distinguish between these two situations.

**2. Does Term Sheet Education Create Moral Hazard?**

By teaching founders which terms are standard and which are negotiable, the book
may inadvertently encourage behavior that harms the broader ecosystem. If every
founder pushes back on every term, negotiations take longer, lawyers bill more,
and deal velocity slows. In a hot market (as in 2011 or 2021), this is fine;
founders have alternatives. In a cold market (as in 2016 or 2023), pushing back
on terms when you have no alternatives can signal to investors that you are
difficult to work with, potentially costing you the deal.

**3. Why Didn't Founders Have This Information Before?**

The deeper unresolved question: why did VCs voluntarily publish the playbook for
negotiating against them? Feld and Mendelson both have day jobs managing a VC
fund. The book's existence creates competition for their own firm — it arms
founders in every deal Foundry Group leads. Possible answers: (a) reputational
benefits outweigh competitive losses — founder-friendly VCs attract more and
better deal flow; (b) the bloggers who became the authors genuinely wanted to
level the playing field; (c) the book's most important competitors are other
firms who write bad terms, not the law itself. The truth is likely all three,
and the book does not fully explain the calculus.

**4. Can Term Sheet Literacy Substitute for Legal Counsel?**

The book's subtitle promises you'll be smarter than your lawyer. The authors
clarify in the text: they mean you'll have the business context to direct your
lawyer, not that you will not need one. But this nuance is often lost on readers.
Founders who treat the book as a substitute for legal counsel (rather than as
complementary to it) are taking on risks the book does not prepare them for —
including the risk that simplified understanding of a complex clause creates false
confidence during negotiations.

## Criticisms

### "This Is Marketing for Foundry Group" — Marc Andreessen
Andreessen made this charge explicitly in 2011 blog commentary. The book provides
reputational differentiation for its authors' firm. By positioning Foundry Group
as "founder-friendly" through the most visible guide to term sheets, Feld and
Mendelson generate inbound deal flow and negotiating goodwill at basically zero
marginal cost. Andreessen's point is not that the book is *useless* — it is that
its motivation is not purely altruistic. The book is a loss leader for the firm.

**Assessment:** Structurally accurate but strategically naive. A loss leader
that generates $100M+ in deal flow and eliminates information asymmetry for
thousands of founders benefits the ecosystem regardless of motive. Andreessen's
critique would be more telling if the book's practical advice were wrong — but it
isn't. The motive critique is relevant primarily for founders who are evaluating
whether Feld and Mendelson's VC firm is aligned with their interests, not for
readers evaluating the book's factual content.

### "The 'Standard' Language Masks VC Interests" — Marc Suster
Suster, writing in "Both Sides of the Table," argued that the book's repeated
use of "standard" to describe terms favored by VCs implicitly legitimizes those
terms. Founders reading the book walk away knowing, for instance, that 1x non-
participating liquidation preference is "standard and fair" — when in fact, from
the perspective of common stock holders, *any* liquidation preference is a
concession made under asymmetric information conditions. The book's language takes
the VC framing as given.

**Assessment:** This is the book's most genuinely important criticism. Feld and
Mendelson are explicit about being VCs writing from a VC perspective — they
include entrepreneur commentary to counterbalance this. But the cumulative effect
of "standard," "reasonable," and "fair" applied to investor-protective terms
does perform ideological work: it normalizes a governance structure designed to
shift risk and reward toward investors. A founder who understands this framing
is better equipped to negotiate, but the book itself should have addressed the
normativity more directly.

### "Light on Alternatives to VC" — Cory Doctorow
Doctorow's critique (2012) noted that the book does not address whether founders
should raise VC at all. It is a manual for VC financing assuming that VC financing
is the intended path. Founders considering bootstrapping, revenue-based
financing, or crowdfunding find no validation of those choices.

**Assessment:** This is a scope critique, not a quality critique. *Venture Deals*
never claimed to be a comprehensive guide to startup financing in general. It is
untroubled by this limitation unless evaluated against that wrong standard. What
it does not address: the social and economic consequences of the VC model's
dominance as the default path for scaling startups. This is a legitimate gap but
one that would have required a fundamentally different book.

### "Outdated on Seed-Stage Instruments" — Additional Market Context
By 2013-2014, the seed-stage landscape shifted significantly: Y Combinator
released the SAFE in 2013, crowdfunding regulations under the JOBS Act took
effect in 2016, and rolling funds emerged around 2019. The first edition's deep
treatment of convertible notes, while accurate for its era, became less
central to founder concerns as the SAFE became the dominant seed instrument.
By the time the fourth edition was published (2019), the SAFE deserved its own
chapter — which the fourth edition delivered.

**Assessment:** Time-isolated accuracy. The 2011 edition is an accurate snapshot
of its market context. Founders reading it specifically as a historical document
to understand how the market evolved will find it valuable. Founders using it as
current advice on seed-stage instruments need supplementation.

## Alternative Solutions to the Information Problem

If *Venture Deals* solved the term sheet information problem through education,
what other approaches are available or were proposed?

**1. Standardized Term Sheets from Entrepreneur-Friendly VCs**

Some VC firms — notably Y Combinator (SAFE), First Round Capital (their "term
sheet template"), and certain angel groups — publish standardized term sheets
that pre-negotiate terms in founders' favor. These reduce information costs by
contractually establishing favorable defaults. Founders do not need to understand
the full term sheet to benefit if the "standard" has been pre-negotiated in their
favor. The downside: founders who don't understand the standard term sheet cannot
evaluate whether it is actually favorable.

**2. Founder Collective Bargaining**

Some entrepreneurs have proposed collective bargaining models where founders, as
a class, negotiate standardized terms with investor syndicates. This approach has
been attempted in the UK through bodies like the Startup Coalition and in the US
through regional founder groups. It addresses the structural incentive problem —
if every founder in the market negotiates from the same position, individual
competition between founders decreases. The challenge: founder groups are
fractured, short-lived, and rarely have the institutional continuity to sustain
multi-year bargaining agreements.

**3. lawyer-as-advocate (The Legal Counsel Alternative)**

Rather than educating founders, some investors and lawyers recommend simply
retaining a specialist venture capital lawyer whose mandate is to protect founder
interests. The logic: a good venture lawyer has seen hundreds of term sheets and
knows which terms to reject, which to push back on, and which to accept. The
downside: legal counsel costs money (typically $25,000-$75,000 per Series A) and
still requires some founder literacy to direct the lawyer's negotiation priorities.

**4. Regulation (Government Intervention)**

In theory, securities regulators could set limits on investor-protective terms —
for example, capping liquidation preference at 1x non-participating or banning
full-ratchet anti-dilution. In practice, no jurisdiction has done this for venture
financing, largely because venture-backed companies are not the primary investors
advocating for such protections. The JOBS Act (2012) and crowdfunding regulations
that followed are the closest the US has come to rebalancing founder-investor
power, and they bypass the VC term sheet entirely by creating a parallel financing
structure.

**5. Information Platforms and Market Transparency**

Websites like TermSheetDB (founded 2018) and practices like Y Combinator's
"Ask the VC" blog series attempt to create public repositories of actual term
sheets (anonymized). More transparency about what terms actually appear in deals —
not what theory says should appear — helps founders calibrate their expectations
and negotiating targets. The challenge: most term sheets include confidentiality
provisions, so the data sources are voluntary and biased toward founders who feel
they got a good deal.

## Practical Applications

**Before Your First Fundraise**

- Read chapters 1-3 before you begin any investor meetings. You cannot negotiate
  what you do not understand, and chapters 4-6 require the framing chapters to
  land.
- Build a cap table from your first share issuance. If you don't know what
  dilution looks like on a spreadsheet, you won't recognize it in a term sheet.
- Identify 20 target VCs, and rank them by stage fit, domain expertise, and
  portfolio quality — not brand name.
- Maintain a data room from day one: financials, cap table, contracts, key
  metrics.

**During Term Sheet Review**

- Set a stopwatch. You have 30 days of exclusivity. Spend the first week
  understanding the terms, the next two weeks negotiating, the final week getting
  final documents reviewed.
- Sort every new term into three buckets: "standard," "negotiable but," and
  "must-win." Know your walk-away terms before you enter the negotiation.
- Don't let your lawyer fight the economics without your direction. Lawyers
  optimize for legal cleanness; you optimize for your business outcome.

**For Later Rounds**

- Anti-dilution clauses you accepted at Series A will hurt you at Series B. Know
  the math.
- VC fund economics from Chapter 8 help you predict your investors' behavior as
  the fund ages. A VC whose fund is in year 7 will want an exit you may not be
  ready for. Plan for this conversation before it becomes urgent.

**For Founders Who Don't Raise VC**

- Read chapters 1-3 anyway. The player mapping and fundraising process guidance
  is useful even if you ultimately decide to bootstrap. The principles of
  identifying aligned partners, preparing data, and having a hiring plan are
  transferable to every resource negotiation founders face.
