_Misbehaving: The Making of Behavioral Economics_ (2015) is Richard H. Thaler's intellectual memoir of the revolution he helped lead. Spanning four decades of research, it recounts how a small band of economists and psychologists challenged the dominant paradigm of rational Homo economicus and built a more realistic account of human decision-making. Part autobiography, part history of ideas, part polemic, the book traces how concepts like the endowment effect, mental accounting, and self-control problems moved from the margins of economic thought to the center — culminating in Thaler's 2017 Nobel Prize and the widespread adoption of behavioral insights in business, finance, and public policy around the world.
