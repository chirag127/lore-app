## Strengths

- **Brilliant framework.** The seen/unseen distinction is one of
  the most powerful analytical tools in economics.
- **Clear and accessible.** Written for a general audience with no
  math or jargon.
- **Persuasive writing.** Hazlitt builds each chapter from a common
  fallacy to its refutation with relentless logic.
- **Enduring relevance.** The fallacies Hazlitt exposed in 1946
  (tariffs, rent control, inflation) are still debated today.
- **Logical structure.** Each chapter stands alone as a case study
  in applying the one lesson.

---

## Weaknesses

- **Lacks nuance.** Modern macroeconomics recognizes legitimate
  roles for monetary and fiscal policy that Hazlitt dismisses.
- **No empirical evidence.** Hazlitt argues from logic, not data.
- **Polemical tone.** The book advocates rather than educates.
- **Ignores market failures.** Externalities, public goods, and
  information asymmetries are not addressed.
- **Dated examples.** 1940s references require some historical
  context for modern readers.

---
