## Introduction

Welcome to BookAtlas. Today: *Economics in One Lesson* by Henry
Hazlitt. Published 1946. The most popular introduction to
free-market economics ever written.

Hazlitt was a journalist, not an academic. He wrote for the New
York Times, the Wall Street Journal, and Newsweek. And he had
a gift: he could explain economics more clearly than almost
anyone.

---

## The One Lesson

The book's title is literal. There is one lesson. And Hazlitt
states it in the first chapter:

The art of economics consists in looking not merely at the
immediate but at the longer effects of any act or policy; it
consists in tracing the consequences of that policy not merely
for one group but for all groups.

That is it. If you understand this, you understand economics
better than most politicians and journalists.

---

## The Broken Window

Hazlitt's most famous example: a boy breaks a baker's window. The
crowd gathers and says: at least the glazier will get work. He
will spend his earnings at the tailor. The tailor will buy shoes.
The broken window creates jobs.

What the crowd does not see: the baker was going to spend that
$50 on a suit. Now he must spend it on a window instead. The
glazier gains. The tailor loses. Net effect: one window's worth
of wealth is destroyed.

This seems obvious. And yet this fallacy appears in almost every
economic debate.

---

## The Method

Each chapter follows the same structure. Hazlitt presents a
common argument — rent control helps tenants, tariffs protect
jobs, government spending creates growth. Then he traces the
unseen consequences.

The pattern never gets old. Because the fallacy never gets old.
We still hear the same bad arguments seventy years later.

---

## The Verdict

*Economics in One Lesson* is not a complete economics education.
It does not cover Keynesian models, monetary policy, or behavioral
economics. But it gives you something better than facts: a
framework.

**Rating: 8.5/10** — The best introductory economics book ever
written. Read it, then read something with more nuance.
