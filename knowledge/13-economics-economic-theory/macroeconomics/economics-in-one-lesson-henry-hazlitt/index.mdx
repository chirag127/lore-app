## Overview

*Economics in One Lesson* (1946) by Henry Hazlitt is the most widely
read introduction to free-market economics ever written. Based on
Frédéric Bastiat's essay "What Is Seen and What Is Not Seen,"
Hazlitt builds everything around a single idea: good economics looks
at long-term effects on all groups, not short-term effects on one.

---
{}
------|--------|
| Jobs created by a new project | Jobs destroyed by diverting resources |
| Immediate benefits of a subsidy | Long-term costs of taxation |
| Visible destruction creating work | Invisible wealth that is never created |
| Short-term price drop from controls | Long-term shortages and black markets |

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **The broken window fallacy is everywhere.** Destruction does not
   create net prosperity. It destroys wealth, period.

2. **Government spending is not stimulus.** Every dollar the
   government spends must first be taken from someone. The question
   is who spends it better.

3. **Price controls cause shortages.** Rent control reduces housing.
   Wage floors increase unemployment. You cannot repeal supply and
   demand.

4. **Credit expansion causes boom and bust.** Printing money does not
   create wealth. It creates inflation and malinvestment.

5. **Protectionism hurts everyone.** Tariffs protect a few jobs at
   the expense of consumers and other industries.

## Who Should Read

| Reader Type | Why |
|---|---|
| Economics beginners | The clearest introductory text available |
| Voters and citizens | Essential framework for evaluating policy |
| Students | Basis for understanding economic reasoning |
| Journalists and writers | Masterclass in clear economic writing |
| Supporters of intervention | The strongest statement of the opposing view |

---

## Who Should Skip

- Those seeking a modern, nuanced view of macroeconomics
- Readers who dislike polemical or advocacy-style writing
- Anyone expecting coverage of behavioral economics or game theory

---

## Core Themes

| Theme | Description |
|---|---|
| The seen vs. the unseen | All economic actions have hidden consequences |
| Long-term vs. short-term | Good policy considers lasting effects |
| All groups matter | Not just one industry or class |
| Government failure | Intervention creates secondary effects |
| Spontaneous order | Markets coordinate without central direction |

---

## Why This Book Matters

Since 1946, this book has shaped generations of free-market thinking.
It is not subtle. It is not balanced in a modern academic sense. But
it is clear, relentless, and built on a framework that forces readers
to think beyond the obvious.

## Final Verdict

**Rating: 8.5/10** — The best introductory economics book ever
written. Polemical, yes. One-sided, yes. But the framework it
teaches — looking for the unseen — is indispensable.
