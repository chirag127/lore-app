## Strengths

- **Intellectual clarity.** Friedman argues with remarkable precision.
  Every chapter poses a problem, diagnoses the government failure, and
  proposes a market-based alternative. The logic is easy to follow and
  hard to dismiss.
- **Prophetic influence.** Many of Friedman's proposals — school
  vouchers, negative income tax (as the Earned Income Tax Credit),
  deregulation of airlines and trucking — were implemented and worked.
- **Principled consistency.** The book never wavers from its framework.
  Whether discussing education or monetary policy, the same principles
  apply. This gives the argument unusual coherence.
- **Accessible writing.** Complex economic ideas — externalities,
  monetary policy, comparative advantage — are explained in plain
  language without jargon.
- **The central insight.** The link between economic and political
  freedom is real and important. Centralized economic power does tend
  to concentrate political power. This chapter alone justifies the
  book.

---

## Weaknesses

- **Overly optimistic about markets.** Friedman assumes markets are
  naturally competitive and that monopoly is rare and temporary. The
  history of the 20th century — including massive market concentration
  in technology, finance, and healthcare — challenges this.
- **Dismissive of externalities.** The chapter on government's role
  acknowledges externalities but treats them as marginal. Climate
  change alone raises questions about whether markets can handle
  systemic risks without government intervention.
- **Narrow view of freedom.** Friedman defines freedom as absence of
  coercion. A broader view — positive freedom, the capability to act —
  leads to different conclusions about government's role.
- **Underestimates inequality.** The book assumes that market outcomes
  are inherently fair. It does not seriously engage with the argument
  that concentrated wealth undermines political equality.

---

## Criticism

### The Pinochet Problem

Friedman advised the Pinochet regime in Chile, and *Capitalism and
Freedom* was used to justify free-market reforms under a dictatorship.
Friedman's defense — that he was advising on economic matters, not
endorsing the regime — has not satisfied critics who argue that
economic freedom cannot be separated from political freedom in
practice.

### The Assumption of Rational Choice

Friedman assumes individuals are rational actors who make decisions in
their own best interest. Behavioral economics has shown this is false.
If people make systematic errors, the case for leaving all decisions
to markets weakens.

### Impact on the Social Safety Net

Critics argue that Friedman's ideas — particularly the attacks on
Social Security, public education, and welfare — provided intellectual
cover for policies that have increased inequality and reduced social
mobility.

---

## Counterarguments

| Criticism | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| "Markets fail — climate change proves it" | Friedman acknowledges externalities; he would support carbon pricing over regulation |
| "Inequality has increased since 1980" | Friedman's proposals (vouchers, NIT) aimed at the poor; outcomes reflect partial implementation, not the full framework |
| "Behavioral economics undermines rational choice" | The argument for freedom doesn't require perfect rationality; individuals make mistakes but still have better information than central planners |
| "Chile shows economic freedom under tyranny" | Friedman agreed Chile was not free; his argument is that economic freedom creates pressure for political freedom over time |

## Historical Context

Published in 1962, *Capitalism and Freedom* was swimming against the
tide. The postwar consensus favored Keynesian economics, mixed economy,
and the welfare state. Friedman was an academic outsider. The book gave
the small but growing libertarian movement its manifesto.

By 1980, the tide had turned. Reagan and Thatcher both credited
Friedman. The book's proposals — deregulation, tax cuts, school
vouchers — moved from fringe to mainstream. Today, Friedman's ideas are
so embedded that many who disagree with him still use his frameworks.
