## Strengths

- **Exceptional clarity.** The Friedmans explain complex economic ideas
  — comparative advantage, the quantity theory of money, regulatory
  capture — in language accessible to any reader. This is popular
  economics at its best.
- **Concrete examples.** Japan vs. India, the Great Depression, the
  Interstate Commerce Commission, the FDA — every argument is grounded
  in specific cases the reader can evaluate.
- **Principled consistency.** The same framework — voluntary exchange
  through free markets — applies to every domain. The book has genuine
  intellectual coherence.
- **Policy alternatives.** Unlike purely negative critiques, the
  Friedmans propose concrete alternatives: vouchers, negative income
  tax, fixed monetary rule, effluent taxes. The book is constructive,
  not just destructive.
- **Historical influence.** The book changed the course of policy.
  Understanding it is understanding how the Reagan-Thatcher revolution
  was made.
- **Television companion.** As the book of the PBS series, it reached
  an audience no economics text ever could. The visual arguments
  (charts of inflation, money supply) reinforce the written ones.

---

## Weaknesses

- **Overstates market perfection.** The Friedmans treat externalities,
  monopoly, and information asymmetry as marginal problems when they
  are central to modern economic life. Climate change, tech
  monopolies, and algorithmic pricing are not addressed.
- **Understates government's enabling role.** Markets require
  government-enforced property rights, contract law, antitrust,
  banking regulation, and a stable currency. The book treats these as
  minimal but they are in fact substantial preconditions.
- **Dismisses real inequality.** The chapter "Created Equal" assumes
  that market outcomes are roughly fair and that redistribution
  inevitably reduces liberty. Concentrated wealth can also undermine
  political equality.
- **Overconfidence in competition.** The assumption that markets will
  naturally remain competitive has been falsified by a century of
  monopoly, oligopoly, and market concentration — from Standard Oil to
  Google.
- **Ignores behavioral economics.** The Friedmans assume rational
  actors. If people make systematic errors — in retirement saving,
  mortgage borrowing, health insurance choice — the case for leaving
  decisions to markets weakens.
- **Limited empirical rigor.** The book uses illustrative examples
  rather than systematic data analysis. Critics note that some claims
  (e.g., licensing never improves quality) are asserted rather than
  proven.

## Criticism

### Ideological Rigidity

The most common criticism: *Free to Choose* is not an analysis but an
ideological polemic. Every government program fails; every market
solution succeeds. The lack of nuance — the absence of any sustained
engagement with the circumstances where markets fail — undermines the
book's credibility for many readers.

### The Chile Question

Milton Friedman advised the Pinochet dictatorship on economic policy.
While *Free to Choose* does not discuss Chile, the connection between
Friedman's ideas and authoritarianism haunts the book. The argument
that economic freedom produces political freedom is harder to sustain
when the most dramatic free-market reforms of the era occurred under a
dictatorship.

### Social Safety Net

Critics argue that the Friedmans' attack on Social Security, welfare,
and public education provided intellectual cover for policies that
increased inequality and reduced social mobility. The "tide is turning"
chapter predicted a rollback of government that did not occur for the
poor — tax cuts for the rich were enacted while welfare was cut.

### Regulatory Capture Argument

The Friedmans' claim that regulation always serves the regulated
industry is too sweeping. Some regulations — clean air standards,
auto safety regulations, FDA drug approval — produce clear public
benefits. The evidence of regulatory capture is real, but it does not
justify eliminating all regulation.

### Market Failures Ignored

- **Public goods:** National defense, basic research, public health —
  markets undersupply them
- **Externalities:** Pollution, antibiotic resistance, financial
  contagion — markets do not price them
- **Natural monopoly:** Utilities, railways, network infrastructure —
  competition is inefficient
- **Information asymmetry:** Healthcare, used cars, financial products —
  consumers cannot evaluate quality

## Counterarguments

| Criticism | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| "Markets fail — climate change proves it" | The Friedmans support market-based solutions like carbon taxes, not regulation; the failure is political, not economic |
| "Inequality has increased since 1980" | Much of Friedman's agenda was not implemented (school vouchers, NIT); what was implemented (tax cuts) reflects selective adoption |
| "Chile shows Friedman's ideas work under tyranny" | Friedman's argument is that economic freedom creates *pressure* for political freedom over time; Chile eventually returned to democracy |
| "Some regulation clearly works" | The Friedmans distinguish regulation of externalities (supported) from regulation of voluntary exchange (opposed); the distinction is principled |
| "The negative income tax would be too expensive" | It replaces existing welfare spending; the net cost depends on program design, not the idea itself |

## Historical Context

Published in 1980, *Free to Choose* appeared at a pivot point. The
1970s had produced stagflation — high inflation combined with high
unemployment, which Keynesian economics said was impossible. The
postwar consensus was crumbling. Jimmy Carter began deregulating
airlines and trucking. Within a year, Ronald Reagan would be elected.

The PBS series aired in 1980 and was controversial — public television
was seen as a liberal institution airing a libertarian polemic. The
Friedmans appeared in each episode, explaining their views and
debating opponents. The format gave the book a reach far beyond what a
traditional economics text could achieve.

Today, the book's influence is visible in charter schools, the EITC,
deregulation of transportation and telecommunications, inflation
targeting by central banks, and trade liberalization.
