# First Language Acquisition

## Why This Exists

First language acquisition is one of the most remarkable achievements of human cognition. By age four or five, typically developing children have mastered the phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, and pragmatics of their native language — a system of staggering complexity — without explicit instruction, from input that is often imperfect (filled with hesitations, false starts, and ungrammatical utterances) and underdetermined (the poverty of the stimulus argument). No child is formally taught their native language; they acquire it through exposure and interaction. Understanding how they do so is a central question for linguistics, psychology, and cognitive science.

The field has been shaped by two major competing perspectives. Nativism, associated with Noam Chomsky, argues that children are born with an innate Universal Grammar — a biological faculty specifically dedicated to language that constrains the possible forms of human language and guides acquisition. Empiricist alternatives (usage-based approaches, connectionism) argue that children acquire language through general learning mechanisms — pattern recognition, statistical learning, social interaction — and that grammatical knowledge emerges gradually from usage. The debate remains unresolved, though most researchers now acknowledge roles for both innate constraints and environmental input. Major longitudinal studies (Brown's study of Adam, Eve, and Sarah; the CHILDES database) have documented the stages of acquisition in detail.

## What Belongs Here

Topics: stages of acquisition (crying, cooing, babbling, one-word stage, two-word stage, telegraphic speech), phonological development, lexical acquisition (the vocabulary spurt, fast mapping), morphological development (overregularization errors like "goed" and "foots"), syntactic development (question formation, negation, passive), pragmatic development, the poverty of the stimulus argument, Universal Grammar, principles and parameters theory, critical period hypothesis, usage-based approaches (Tomasello), statistical learning (Saffran), connectionist models, bilingual first language acquisition, cross-linguistic studies.

## Essential Books

1. **Constructing a Language: A Usage-Based Theory of Language Acquisition** — Michael Tomasello (2003) — Major alternative to nativist accounts, arguing that children construct grammar through social-cognitive skills and pattern-finding.
2. **First Language Acquisition** — Eve V. Clark (4th ed., 2016) — Comprehensive textbook covering all aspects of children's language development.
3. **The Language Instinct: How the Mind Creates Language** — Steven Pinker (1994) — Accessible argument for the nativist position, with extensive discussion of acquisition.
4. **Language Development: An Introduction** — Erika Hoff (9th ed., 2019) — Balanced textbook covering both nativist and empiricist perspectives.
5. **How Children Learn the Meanings of Words** — Paul Bloom (2000) — Cognitive account of lexical acquisition, emphasizing the role of conceptual understanding.
6. **The Resilience of Language: What Gesture Creation in Deaf Children Can Tell Us About How All Children Learn Language** — Susan Goldin-Meadow (2003) — Study of deaf children who create their own gesture systems, arguing for an innate drive for language.
7. **The Acquisition of Syntax: Studies in Comparative Developmental Linguistics** — Marc-Ariel Friedemann and Luigi Rizzi (eds., 2000) — Papers on syntactic development within the generative framework.
8. **The CHILDES Project: Tools for Analyzing Talk** — Brian MacWhinney (3rd ed., 2000) — Essential resource for researchers using the Child Language Data Exchange System.
