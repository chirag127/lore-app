# Language Learning Methodologies

## Why This Exists

Language teaching has a long and contested history, with each era's methodology reflecting both theoretical assumptions about language and practical demands of the classroom. The grammar-translation method, dominant in 19th-century European education, treated language as a system of rules to be memorized and literary texts to be translated — producing readers but not speakers. The direct method and audiolingual method (the latter influenced by behavioral psychology) emphasized oral repetition and habit formation. The communicative approach that emerged in the 1970s and 1980s, in contrast, prioritized authentic communication over grammatical drills, arguing that language is learned not by memorizing rules but by using it for real purposes.

The central tension in language teaching is between explicit instruction (teaching rules, structures, and vocabulary directly) and implicit learning (acquiring language through meaningful use). Most contemporary methodologies accept that both have a role, but they differ sharply on the optimal balance. Task-based language teaching (TBLT), content and language integrated learning (CLIL), and immersion education all prioritize meaning over form, while the lexical approach advocates explicit attention to multi-word units (chunks) rather than individual words. Developing methodologies like "translanguaging pedagogy" treat learners' full linguistic repertoires as resources rather than problems to be overcome. The field remains divided, and the research consensus is that no single method is universally superior — context, learner characteristics, and teacher skill matter as much as the method itself.

## Essential Books

1. **Approaches and Methods in Language Teaching** — Jack C. Richards and Theodore S. Rodgers (3rd ed., 2014) — The definitive survey of language teaching methods from grammar-translation to the present.
2. **Principles of Language Learning and Teaching** — H. Douglas Brown (6th ed., 2014) — Integrates SLA theory with practical teaching principles.
3. **A Course in Language Teaching: Practice and Theory** — Penny Ur (2nd ed., 2012) — Practical guide covering all aspects of classroom language teaching.
4. **Techniques and Principles in Language Teaching** — Diane Larsen-Freeman and Marti Anderson (3rd ed., 2011) — Demonstration lessons illustrating each major method.
5. **The Practice of English Language Teaching** — Jeremy Harmer (5th ed., 2015) — Comprehensive methodology textbook with extensive practical suggestions.
6. **Task-Based Language Teaching: Theory and Practice** — Rod Ellis, Peter Skehan, Shaofeng Li, Natsuko Shintani, and Craig Lambert (2020) — State-of-the-art treatment of TBLT research and implementation.
7. **Teaching Language in Context** — Alice Omaggio Hadley (2nd ed., 2000) — Proficiency-oriented approach integrating research and practice.
8. **The Lexical Approach: The State of ELT and a Way Forward** — Michael Lewis (1993) — Argues for the primacy of lexical chunks over grammar in language teaching.
