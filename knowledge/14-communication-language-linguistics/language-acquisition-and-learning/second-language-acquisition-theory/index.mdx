# Second Language Acquisition Theory

## Why This Exists

Second language acquisition (SLA) is the study of how people learn a language after they have already acquired their first. Unlike first language acquisition, which succeeds universally under normal conditions, SLA is characterized by wide variation in outcomes — some learners achieve near-native proficiency, most plateau well short of it, and virtually all show effects of their first language (transfer). Understanding why this happens — what cognitive mechanisms are involved, what role instruction and social interaction play, and what accounts for individual differences — is essential for language teaching and for understanding the nature of language itself.

SLA emerged as a distinct field in the 1960s and 1970s, shaped by Stephen Krashen's Monitor Model (the acquisition/learning distinction, the natural order hypothesis, the input hypothesis, the affective filter). Later developments have largely superseded Krashen's framework, but his work established SLA as a research domain. The interaction hypothesis (Long, 1983, 1996) argues that negotiated interaction — when learners and interlocutors work together to achieve mutual understanding — is the key driver of acquisition. The output hypothesis (Swain, 1985) emphasizes that producing language (not just comprehending it) is essential. The concept of interlanguage (Selinker, 1972) captures the idea that L2 learners construct systematic but transitional grammars that are neither their L1 nor the target language.

Topics: interlanguage theory, transfer/cross-linguistic influence, fossilization, Krashen's Monitor Model, the acquisition-learning distinction, the input hypothesis, the interaction hypothesis (Long), the output hypothesis (Swain), the noticing hypothesis (Schmidt), the competition model (Bates & MacWhinney), processability theory (Pienemann), sociocultural theory (Lantolf, Vygotsky's ZPD), complexity-accuracy-fluency (CAF) frameworks, the critical period hypothesis for L2 acquisition, individual differences (aptitude, motivation, working memory), instructed SLA, task-based language teaching, feedback and correction.

## Essential Books

1. **Second Language Acquisition: An Introductory Course** — Susan M. Gass, Jennifer Behney, and Luke Plonsky (5th ed., 2020) — Comprehensive, research-grounded textbook.
2. **Introducing Second Language Acquisition: Perspectives and Practices** — Muriel Saville-Troike and Karen Barto (2nd ed., 2016) — Accessible introduction for beginners.
3. **Understanding Second Language Acquisition** — Rod Ellis (2nd ed., 2015) — Authoritative overview by one of the field's leading figures.
4. **The Study of Second Language Acquisition** — Rod Ellis (2nd ed., 2008) — Detailed reference covering theory, research methods, and findings.
5. **Key Questions in Second Language Acquisition: An Introduction** — Bill VanPatten, Jessica Williams, and Megan Smith (2020) — Organized around central debates in the field.
6. **Second Language Acquisition and the Critical Period Hypothesis** — David Birdsong (ed., 1999) — Edited volume examining the role of age.
7. **Sociocultural Theory and the Genesis of Second Language Development** — James Lantolf and Steven Thorne (2006) — Vygotskyan approach to SLA.
8. **The Cambridge Handbook of Second Language Acquisition** — Julia Herschensohn and Martha Young-Scholten (eds., 2013) — Comprehensive reference covering all aspects.
