# Phonetics & Phonology

## Why This Exists

Phonetics is the study of the physical properties of speech sounds: how they are produced by the vocal tract (articulatory phonetics), how they travel as acoustic signals (acoustic phonetics), and how they are perceived by the ear and brain (auditory phonetics). Phonology, by contrast, studies how sounds function within a particular language — which sound distinctions are meaningful, how sounds combine, and how they change in context. The International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA), developed in the late 19th century, provides a standardized notation for transcribing the sounds of any language, making cross-linguistic comparison possible.

The distinction between phonetics and phonology mirrors the Saussurean distinction between substance and form. Phonetics deals with the raw physical substance of speech; phonology deals with the abstract categories (phonemes, features, syllables) that organize that substance into meaningful systems. Noam Chomsky and Morris Halle's *The Sound Pattern of English* (1968) established generative phonology as a core subfield, while later developments like Optimality Theory (Prince and Smolensky, 1993) reframed phonological patterns as the resolution of conflicting constraints.

Topics: articulatory phonetics, acoustic phonetics, auditory phonetics, IPA transcription, distinctive features, phonemes and allophones, phonological rules, syllable structure, stress and intonation, tone systems, Optimality Theory, autosegmental phonology, feature geometry, speech perception, coarticulation, phonetic universals.

## What Does NOT Belong Here

Speech-language pathology and clinical articulation disorders (belongs in Medicine). Audio engineering and acoustic signal processing (belongs in Engineering). Orthography and writing systems (belongs under writing systems or historical linguistics). Prosody in poetry and versification (belongs in Literature). Phonetic aspects of singing and voice training (belongs in Music).

## Essential Books

1. **A Course in Phonetics** — Peter Ladefoged and Keith Johnson (7th ed., 2014) — The standard textbook, with practical exercises in transcription and articulatory description.
2. **The Sound Pattern of English** — Noam Chomsky and Morris Halle (1968) — Landmark work that established generative phonology.
3. **Phonology: A Formal Introduction** — David Odden (2005) — Rigorous introduction to phonological theory with extensive problem sets.
4. **Introducing Phonetics and Phonology** — Mike Davenport and S.J. Hannahs (4th ed., 2020) — Accessible overview bridging the two subfields.
5. **Optimality Theory** — René Kager (1999) — Clear introduction to constraint-based phonology.
6. **Vowels and Consonants** — Peter Ladefoged and Sandra Ferrari Disner (3rd ed., 2012) — Multimedia introduction with accompanying website for sound samples.
7. **Phonetic Data Analysis** — Peter Ladefoged (2003) — Practical guide to acoustic analysis of speech.
8. **Patterns of Sounds** — Ian Maddieson (1984) — Cross-linguistic survey of phonological inventories.

## Key Concepts

IPA, phoneme, allophone, distinctive feature, minimal pair, syllable (onset/rhyme/coda), stress, tone, intonation, coarticulation, assimilation, neutralization, markedness, constraint ranking (OT), sonority hierarchy, place/manner of articulation, voice onset time (VOT).

## Connections

Phonetics connects to **Psychology (16)** through speech perception and the cognitive processing of auditory signals. It connects to **Biology (08)** through the anatomy and physiology of the vocal tract and auditory system. Phonology connects to **Computer Science (06)** through speech recognition, text-to-speech systems, and phonological parsing algorithms. Both fields inform language teaching (Education 19), where accurate pronunciation instruction depends on understanding sound systems.
