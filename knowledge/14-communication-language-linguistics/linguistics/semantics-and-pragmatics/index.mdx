# Semantics & Pragmatics

## Why This Exists

Semantics is the study of linguistic meaning: what words and sentences mean independently of context. Compositional semantics, following Gottlob Frege and Alfred Tarski, treats sentence meaning as a function of word meanings and the rules for combining them. Lexical semantics examines the meanings of words and the relationships between them — synonymy, antonymy, hyponymy, meronymy. Formal semantics uses the tools of logic and set theory to model truth conditions, quantification, modality, and anaphora. Pragmatics, by contrast, studies how meaning depends on context: what speakers imply beyond what they literally say. H.P. Grice's theory of conversational implicature (1975) showed that communication relies on shared assumptions about cooperativeness, and that speakers can flout these assumptions to convey meanings indirectly.

The boundary between semantics and pragmatics is itself a subject of debate. Semantic minimalists (Borg, Cappelen & Lepore) argue that sentence meaning is relatively stable and context-independent; contextualists (Recanati, Sperber & Wilson) argue that pragmatic processes permeate all meaning. J.L. Austin's speech act theory (1962) and John Searle's systematization of it showed that utterances are not just true or false but are actions — promises, requests, warnings, apologies — that change social reality. This insight connects semantics and pragmatics to the philosophy of language and social interaction.

## What Belongs Here

Topics: truth-conditional semantics, compositionality, lexical semantics, semantic roles (theta roles), thematic relations, quantification, scope, modality, tense and aspect, presupposition, implicature (conventional, conversational), speech acts (locution/illocution/perlocution), relevance theory, politeness theory, deixis, reference, anaphora.

Books: *Semantics* (Heim & Kratzer), *Meaning in Language* (Cruse), *Semantics: A Coursebook* (Hurford, Heasley & Smith), *Pragmatics* (Levinson), *Presupposition and Implicature* (Kadmon), *Speech Acts* (Searle), *Logic and Conversation* (Grice), *Relevance* (Sperber & Wilson).

## What Does NOT Belong Here

Metaphor and figurative language as literary devices (belongs in Literature or Cognitive Linguistics). The philosophy of language as conceptual analysis (belongs in Philosophy 14). Discourse analysis and conversation analysis (belongs in Sociolinguistics or Communication). Stylistics and the aesthetics of language (belongs in Literature). Cognitive semantics and conceptual metaphor (belongs in Cognitive Linguistics).

## Essential Books

1. **Semantics in Generative Grammar** — Irene Heim and Angelika Kratzer (1998) — The standard textbook for formal compositional semantics.
2. **Logic and Conversation** — H.P. Grice (1975) — The foundational essay on conversational implicature and the cooperative principle.
3. **Pragmatics** — Stephen C. Levinson (1983) — Comprehensive introduction to every major topic in pragmatics.
4. **Speech Acts: An Essay in the Philosophy of Language** — John R. Searle (1969) — Systematic development of speech act theory.
5. **Relevance: Communication and Cognition** — Dan Sperber and Deirdre Wilson (2nd ed., 1995) — Influential cognitive approach to pragmatics.
6. **Presupposition and Implicature in Compositional Semantics** — Nirit Kadmon (2001) — Advanced treatment of the semantics-pragmatics interface.
7. **Meaning in Language: An Introduction to Semantics and Pragmatics** — D.A. Cruse (3rd ed., 2011) — Broad, accessible overview of both fields.
8. **Semantics: A Coursebook** — James Hurford, Brendan Heasley and Michael Smith (2nd ed., 2007) — Step-by-step introduction with exercises.

## Key Concepts

Truth conditions, compositionality, reference, sense, denotation, connotation, entailment, presupposition, implicature, cooperative principle, maxims (quantity, quality, relation, manner), speech act, illocutionary force, perlocution, deixis, anaphora, scope, quantifier, modality, possible worlds.

## Connections

Semantics connects directly to **Philosophy (14)** — the philosophy of language, logic, truth, and meaning are shared concerns. Both semantics and pragmatics connect to **Psychology (16)** through the cognitive processes of language comprehension, inference, and theory of mind that underlie pragmatic interpretation. They also connect to **Computer Science (06)** through natural language understanding, semantic parsing, and the challenge of modeling context in dialogue systems. Cross-cultural pragmatics connects to **Anthropology (17)** .
