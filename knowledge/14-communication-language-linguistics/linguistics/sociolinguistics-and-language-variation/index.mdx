# Sociolinguistics & Language Variation

## Why This Exists

Sociolinguistics examines the relationship between language and society. Unlike theoretical linguistics, which often idealizes a homogeneous speech community, sociolinguistics insists that variation is inherent to language and systematically structured by social factors — class, region, ethnicity, gender, age, and context. William Labov's groundbreaking studies in the 1960s (the department store study, Martha's Vineyard, New York City) demonstrated that variation was not random but followed regular patterns correlated with social stratification and speech style. Labov showed, for example, that the presence or absence of postvocalic /r/ in New York City speech varied predictably by social class and formality of context — a finding that established sociolinguistics as a rigorous empirical discipline.

Sociolinguistics also studies multilingualism, code-switching, language attitudes, language policy and planning, and the relationship between language and identity. The work of John Gumperz on interactional sociolinguistics, Dell Hymes on the ethnography of communication, and Deborah Tannen on gender and conversational style have expanded the field to include face-to-face interaction, discourse strategies, and the micropolitics of everyday talk. More recently, third-wave variationist sociolinguistics (Eckert, Bucholtz, Mendoza-Denton) has focused on how speakers actively construct social identities through linguistic practice, rather than simply reflecting pre-existing social categories.

## What Belongs Here

Topics: variationist sociolinguistics, Labovian methods, social stratification of language, style shifting and audience design, regional dialects (dialectology), social dialects, ethnolects, gender and language, age grading and language change, code-switching, diglossia, multilingualism, language attitudes and ideologies, language policy and planning, language and identity, communities of practice, interactional sociolinguistics, discourse analysis, conversation analysis, language and power.

## What Does NOT Belong Here

Theoretical linguistics (phonology, syntax, semantics) without social context (belongs in core linguistics). Clinical assessment of language disorders (belongs in Medicine/Speech Pathology). Literary dialect representation (belongs in Literature). Language teaching methodology (belongs in Education 19). The psychology of language processing (belongs in Psycholinguistics/Psychology 16).

## Essential Books

1. **Sociolinguistics: An Introduction** — Janet Holmes and Nick Wilson (5th ed., 2017) — Clear, well-structured overview with excellent examples from diverse speech communities.
2. **An Introduction to Sociolinguistics** — Ronald Wardhaugh and Janet M. Fuller (7th ed., 2015) — Comprehensive survey covering all major topics.
3. **Sociolinguistic Patterns** — William Labov (1972) — The foundational collection of papers that established variationist sociolinguistics.
4. **Language and Society: An Introduction** — William Downes (2nd ed., 1998) — Integrates sociolinguistics with social theory and discourse analysis.
5. **Principles of Linguistic Change** — William Labov (3 vols., 1994-2010) — Monumental study of the mechanisms and social embedding of language change.
6. **The Handbook of Language and Gender** — Janet Holmes and Miriam Meyerhoff (2003) — Comprehensive reference on gender and language.
7. **Language, Culture, and Society: An Introduction to Linguistic Anthropology** — James Stanlaw, Nobuko Adachi, and Zdenek Salzmann (7th ed., 2018) — Bridges sociolinguistics and anthropology.
8. **You're the Only One I Can Tell: Inside the Language of Women's Friendships** — Deborah Tannen (2017) — Accessible analysis of gendered conversational style.

## Connections

Sociolinguistics connects closely to **Anthropology (17)** — linguistic anthropology and the ethnography of communication are overlapping fields. It connects to **Psychology (16)** through social psychology, language attitudes, and identity construction. It connects to **Education (19)** through the study of classroom discourse, bidialectalism, and the role of language in educational achievement. It connects to **Political Science (15)** through language policy, language rights, and the politics of linguistic standardization. It connects to **Sociology (17)** through the analysis of social stratification and group identity.
