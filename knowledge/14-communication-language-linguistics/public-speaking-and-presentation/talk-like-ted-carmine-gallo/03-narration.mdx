    <heading>
      <s>Part One: Emotional. Three secrets about connecting with your audience's heart.</s>
    </heading>

    <p>
      <s>
        Secret one: unleash the master within. Speak about something you are
        genuinely passionate about.
      </s>
      <s>
        Gallo shows that the most-watched TED speakers share a single trait: they
        are not just knowledgeable. They are obsessed.
      </s>
      <s>
        Aimee Mullins, a double amputee and Paralympic runner, is not passionate
        about prosthetics. She is passionate about unleashing human potential. That
        distinction matters.
      </s>
      <s>
        The science: mirror neurons. When you watch someone who is passionate,
        your brain fires the same neurons as if you were passionate yourself.
        Passion is literally contagious.
      </s>
    </p>

    <p>
      <s>
        Secret two: master the art of storytelling. Our brains are wired for
        narrative.
      </s>
      <s>
        When you hear a story, your brain releases cortisol during tense moments
        and oxytocin during emotional ones. These chemicals help you remember.
      </s>
      <s>
        Gallo breaks down Bryan Stevenson's TED talk. Of its four thousand words,
        sixty-five percent were emotional appeal. Twenty-five percent were logic.
        Ten percent were credibility.
      </s>
      <s>
        The most persuasive talk in TED history was driven almost entirely by
        feeling.
      </s>
    </p>

    <p>
      <s>
        Secret three: have a conversation. The best TED speakers sound like they
        are talking to one person, not a thousand.
      </s>
      <s>
        This is not natural. It is rehearsed. They practice until the script
        disappears and only the conversation remains.
      </s>
      <s>
        Gallo recommends practicing your presentation at least ten times out
        loud. Each time, it will sound a little more natural.
      </s>
    </p>

  <section>
    <heading>
      <s>Part Two: Novel. Three secrets about surprising your audience.</s>
    </heading>

    <p>
      <s>
        Secret four: teach me something new. The human brain craves novelty.
        New information triggers a dopamine release that focuses attention.
      </s>
      <s>
        Gallo advises asking: what will my audience learn that they did not
        know before? If the answer is nothing, your talk will be forgotten.
      </s>
    </p>

    <p>
      <s>
        Secret five: deliver jaw-dropping moments. Bill Gates released mosquitoes
        into a TED audience to make a point about malaria. It took five seconds.
        It is the only thing most people remember about that talk.
      </s>
      <s>
        These moments work because they create what neuroscientists call an
        emotionally charged event. The brain encodes emotional memories more
        strongly than neutral ones.
      </s>
      <s>
        Gallo advises designing one or two such moments per presentation. Not
        more. Each one dilutes the others.
      </s>
    </p>

    <p>
      <s>
        Secret six: lighten up. Humor lowers defenses. When people laugh, they
        release endorphins and become more receptive to your message.
      </s>
      <s>
        You do not need to tell jokes. Gallo says the best humor arises naturally
        from observation and shared experience.
      </s>
      <s>
        Sir Ken Robinson's TED talk, the most-watched of all time, opens with a
        funny story about a shaman. It disarms the audience before he makes his
        serious point.
      </s>
    </p>
  </section>

  <!-- Part 3: Memorable -->
  <section>
    <heading>
      <s>Part Three: Memorable. Three secrets about making your message stick.</s>
    </heading>

    <p>
      <s>
        Secret seven: stick to the eighteen-minute rule. TED's limit is not
        arbitrary.
      </s>
      <s>
        Research by John Medina, author of Brain Rules, shows that attention
        drops significantly after about ten minutes. The eighteen-minute cap
        forces speakers to cut everything that is not essential.
      </s>
      <s>
        Gallo says: what you leave out is more important than what you include.
      </s>
    </p>

    <p>
      <s>
        Secret eight: paint a mental picture with multisensory experiences.
      </s>
      <s>
        Humans process visual information sixty thousand times faster than text.
        The picture superiority effect means that if you hear something and see
        something, you will remember the image.
      </s>
      <s>
        Gallo advocates replacing bullet points with a single powerful image per
        slide. He also recommends props, demonstrations, and vivid language.
      </s>
      <s>
        Hans Rosling's data visualization style is the gold standard. He made
        statistics thrilling by animating them.
      </s>
    </p>

    <p>
      <s>
        Secret nine: stay in your lane. Be yourself.
      </s>
      <s>
        Audiences can detect inauthenticity within seconds. If you try to be
        someone you are not, they will know.
      </s>
      <s>
        Gallo's final advice: practice until you are polished, but never let the
        polish erase the person.
      </s>
    </p>
  </section>

  <!-- Criticism -->
  <section>
    <heading>
      <s>Now, the other side of the story. Talk Like TED has real weaknesses.</s>
    </heading>

    <p>
      <s>
        First, it treats the TED format as universally optimal.
      </s>
      <s>
        A quarterly earnings call, a scientific conference, a classroom lecture,
        and a political speech all require different communication styles. Gallo
        rarely acknowledges this.
      </s>
    </p>

    <p>
      <s>
        Second, the book is repetitive. For a volume that preaches the
        eighteen-minute rule, it has a lot of padding. The same points about
        passion, stories, and authenticity appear across multiple chapters.
      </s>
    </p>

    <p>
      <s>
        Third, the science is cherry-picked. Gallo uses neuroscience to validate
        conclusions he has already reached, rather than letting evidence drive
        the argument.
      </s>
    </p>

    <p>
      <s>
        Fourth, the book is culturally specific. It assumes a Silicon Valley
        worldview: ideas change the world, individual effort is paramount, and
        inspiration is the highest goal. Not every audience or culture shares
        these values.
      </s>
    </p>

    <p>
      <s>
        And fifth, there is an uncomfortable relationship with truth. The book
        celebrates persuasion as an end in itself. It teaches you how to make
        an idea compelling, but it does not help you determine whether that idea
        is worth believing.
      </s>
    </p>
  </section>
