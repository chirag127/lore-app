# Sanskrit, Pali & Classical Languages

## Why This Exists

Sanskrit is to Indian civilization what Greek and Latin are to Europe — the classical language in which the foundational texts of philosophy, religion, science, and literature were composed over a period of more than three millennia. The *Ṛgveda* (c. 1500–1200 BCE) is among the oldest surviving texts in any Indo-European language, and Pāṇini's *Aṣṭādhyāyī* (c. 400 BCE) is one of the most extraordinary intellectual achievements of the ancient world — a complete generative grammar of Sanskrit in 3,959 rules that modern linguists recognize as anticipating many insights of 20th-century linguistic theory. The study of Sanskrit was essential to the development of comparative Indo-European linguistics in the 19th century; Sir William Jones's recognition of the affinity between Sanskrit, Greek, and Latin in 1786 founded the discipline of historical linguistics.

Pali, the language of the Theravada Buddhist canon (the Tipiṭaka), is closely related to Vedic Sanskrit and serves as the liturgical and scholarly language of Buddhism in Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Thailand, Laos, and Cambodia. The Prakrits (including Ardhamagadhi, Maharashtri, and Sauraseni) were the vernacular Middle Indo-Aryan languages that gave rise to the modern languages of North India. Classical languages from other traditions — including classical Greek, Latin, classical Chinese, and Persian — each open a doorway onto a world of thought and expression that remains vital for understanding human civilization.

## What Belongs Here

Topics: Vedic Sanskrit (phonology, accent, grammar), Classical Sanskrit (Pāṇini's grammar, sandhi, declension, conjugation, compounds, nominal derivation), Sanskrit literature (the Vedas, Upaniṣads, epics, Purāṇas, kāvya, drama, philosophical texts), Pali grammar and literature (the Tipiṭaka, Jātaka tales), Prakrit languages and literature, the relationship of Sanskrit to modern Indo-Aryan languages, the Sanskrit grammatical tradition (Pāṇini, Patañjali, Bhartṛhari), classical language pedagogy, comparative Indo-European grammar, classical Greek and Latin grammar, classical Chinese.

Books: *The Sanskrit Language* (Whitney), *Sanskrit Grammar* (Whitney — the standard reference), *A Practical Sanskrit Introductory* (Kashyap), *The Cambridge Encyclopedia of the World's Ancient Languages* (Woodard), *The Oxford Introduction to Proto-Indo-European and the Proto-Indo-European World* (Mallory & Adams).

## What Does NOT Belong Here

Modern Indo-Aryan languages like Hindi, Urdu, Bengali, Marathi (belongs in World Languages or their specific subcategories). The content of Indian philosophical texts rather than their language (belongs in Indian Philosophy). The history of Buddhism rather than its Pali-language texts (belongs in Religious Studies). Sanskrit's role in Indian nationalism and identity politics (belongs in Political Science or South Asian Studies). Epigraphy and paleography of ancient scripts (belongs in Archaeology or Manuscript Studies).

## Essential Books

1. **Sanskrit Grammar: Including Both the Classical Language and the Older Dialects of Veda and Brahmana** — William Dwight Whitney (1879, rev. 1889) — The standard reference grammar, still unsurpassed in its comprehensiveness.
2. **The Sanskrit Language: An Introductory Grammar and Reader** — Walter Harding Maurer (1995) — Modern textbook with graded readings.
3. **A Practical Sanskrit Introductory** — Charles Wikner (1995) — Free, widely used introductory course designed for self-study.
4. **The Aṣṭādhyāyī of Pāṇini** — Translated by Srisa Chandra Basu (1891, or more recent translations by Cardona or Katre) — The foundational text of Sanskrit grammar.
5. **An Introduction to the Grammar of Sanskrit** — Maurer (1995) — Pedagogical introduction with exercises.
6. **Pāli Grammar** — Wilhelm Geiger (1916, rev. 1994) — The standard reference grammar of Pali.
7. **A New Course in Reading Pāli: Entering the Word of the Buddha** — James W. Gair and W.S. Karunatillake (1998) — Modern graded introduction to Pali.
8. **Pāli Literature: Including the Literature of the Abhidhamma** — K.R. Norman (1983) — Comprehensive survey of Pali textual traditions.

## Key Concepts

Vedic vs. Classical Sanskrit, Pāṇinian grammar, sandhi (euphonic combination), declension (vibhakti), conjugation (lakara), compounds (samasa), nominal derivation (kṛt and taddhita affixes), the Sanskrit sound system (varṇa), Sanskrit epigraphy, devanāgari script, Pali vs. Sanskrit, Middle Indo-Aryan (MIA) vs. Old Indo-Aryan (OIA), Prakrit, the Tipiṭaka (three baskets: Vinaya, Sutta, Abhidhamma), *Aṣṭādhyāyī* (Pāṇini's grammar).

## Connections

Sanskrit study connects to **Indian Philosophy (14)** — nearly all classical Indian philosophical texts are in Sanskrit. It connects to **Historical Linguistics (18)** as the foundational evidence for the Indo-European language family. It connects to **Religious Studies (09)** through the languages of Hinduism, Buddhism, and Jainism. Pali connects to **Buddhist Studies (09)** — the Pali canon is the oldest complete Buddhist scripture. The study of classical languages generally connects to **Literature (25)** and **History (10)** through the texts preserved in these languages.
