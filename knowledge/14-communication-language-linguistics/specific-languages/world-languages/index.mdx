# World Languages

## Why This Exists

There are roughly 7,000 languages spoken in the world today, belonging to perhaps 150 distinct language families. Each language is a unique system for organizing and expressing human experience, with its own sound patterns, grammatical structures, semantic categories, and cultural associations. The study of world languages — whether through typology (classifying languages by their structural properties), language family classification (tracing genetic relationships), or individual language description (grammars, dictionaries, and documentation) — is essential for understanding the range and limits of human linguistic capacity.

Language typology, pioneered by Joseph Greenberg and others, seeks to understand what is possible and impossible in human language by comparing structures across languages. Are all languages equally complex? (The dominant view in linguistics: yes, in different ways.) Do all languages have nouns and verbs? (Probably — but the distinction is harder to draw in some languages than others.) Can a language lack tense? (Yes — Chinese, Burmese, and many others do not grammatically mark tense.) Typology has revealed striking patterns: SOV (subject-object-verb) is the most common word order (about 45% of languages), SVO second (about 42%), and VSO, VOS, OVS, and OSV are rare. Descriptive linguistics documents individual languages — especially endangered ones — producing grammars, dictionaries, and text collections that preserve linguistic diversity for future generations.

## What Belongs Here

Topics: language families of the world (Indo-European, Sino-Tibetan, Niger-Congo, Austronesian, Afro-Asiatic, Dravidian, Turkic, Uralic, etc.), linguistic typology (word order, alignment systems — nominative-accusative, ergative-absolutive, active-stative; morphological typology — isolating, agglutinative, fusional, polysynthetic), language universals (absolute vs. statistical), the Greenbergian universals, language endangerment and extinction (Nettle & Romaine, the Endangered Languages Catalogue), language revitalization (Hinton, Hale), major individual languages (Mandarin Chinese, Spanish, Hindi-Urdu, Arabic, Bengali, Portuguese, Russian, Japanese, German, French), sign languages, lingua francas.

Books: *The World's Major Languages* (Comrie, ed.), *An Introduction to Language Typology* (Whaley), *Language Universals and Linguistic Typology* (Comrie), *Vanishing Voices* (Nettle & Romaine), *The Atlas of the World's Languages* (Asher & Moseley), *The Cambridge Encyclopedia of the World's Ancient Languages* (Woodard), *Sign Language and Linguistic Universals* (Sandler & Lillo-Martin).

## What Does NOT Belong Here

Detailed grammars or textbooks for learning individual languages (belongs under each specific language's resources). Language teaching methodology (belongs in Language Learning Methodologies). Language planning and policy (belongs in Sociolinguistics). Historical reconstruction of proto-languages (belongs in Historical Linguistics). Areal linguistics and language contact in specific regions (belongs in Historical Linguistics or Area Studies).

## Essential Books

1. **The World's Major Languages** — Bernard Comrie (ed., 3rd ed., 2018) — Authoritative surveys of the most widely spoken languages, with structural sketches and historical background.
2. **An Introduction to Language Typology** — Lindsay J. Whaley (1997) — Clear textbook covering word order, case systems, alignment, and morphological typology.
3. **Language Universals and Linguistic Typology: Syntax and Morphology** — Bernard Comrie (2nd ed., 1989) — Classic introduction to the typological approach, with rich cross-linguistic data.
4. **Vanishing Voices: The Extinction of the World's Languages** — Daniel Nettle and Suzanne Romaine (2000) — The extent, causes, and consequences of language endangerment.
5. **The Cambridge Encyclopedia of the World's Ancient Languages** — Roger D. Woodard (ed., 2004) — Comprehensive reference for ancient languages and writing systems.
6. **Language: An Introduction to the Study of Speech** — Edward Sapir (1921) — Classic work by one of the founders of linguistic typology and the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis.
7. **The Languages of the World** — Kenneth Katzner and Kirk Miller (3rd ed., 2002) — Concise profiles of hundreds of languages with sample texts.
8. **Sign Language and Linguistic Universals** — Wendy Sandler and Diane Lillo-Martin (2006) — Systematic comparison of sign languages from a typological perspective.

## Key Concepts

Language family, genetic classification, language isolate, typology, morphological type (isolating/agglutinative/fusional/polysynthetic), alignment (nominative-accusative, ergative-absolutive), word order (SOV, SVO, VSO, etc.), head-marking vs. dependent-marking, tone language, stress language, pitch-accent language, endangered language, language vitality (EGIDS scale), lingua franca, national language, official language, minority language, Sprachbund (linguistic area).

## Connections

They connect to **Biology (08)** through the correlation of language families with genetic and archaeological data. Language endangerment connects to **Ethics** and **Environmental Studies** through the parallels between linguistic and biodiversity loss. They connect to **Computer Science (06)** through natural language processing, machine translation, and the challenge of building language technology for low-resource languages.
