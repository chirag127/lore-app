## Front Matter: Preface, Foreword, and Introduction

*Understanding by Design* opens with a collaborative preface coauthored by Grant Wiggins and Jay McTighe, followed by advice for first-time readers. Both Reinventing and Improving Your Teaching—the two words the authors note—they text editors explicitly asks readers whether their current teaching is shaped by a coherent theory of learning, or is merely inherited habit. The challenge is explicit: most teachers plan from what content to cover next, producing what Wiggins and McTighe term "coverage"—marching through a textbook page-late. The introduction launches the book's central question: What is understanding, and how can curriculum be intentionally designed to produce it?

Howard Gardner's foreword places *Understanding by Design* in the context of his own *The Unschooled Mind* (1991), noting that the same cognitive barrier—students' capacity to memorize facts without grasping meaning—demands a design-based solution rather than a motivational one. Enduring understanding, Gardner says, is not something teachers simply hope for; it must be architectured into the curriculum from the start. The foreword also connects backward design to Howard Gardner's broader theory of multiple intelligences, particularly the importance of bringing disciplinary understanding into focus for all students regardless of their entry profile.

## Chapter 1: What Is Backward Design?

The opening chapter introduces the core reformulation of curriculum design. Wiggins and McTighe present two failed models common in American schools. **Model 1** is the coverage model: teachers believe their primary obligation is to "get through" the textbook or state standards list, treating every item as equally important. **Model 2** is the activity-oriented model: teachers design appealing hands-on tasks without clearly linking them to specific learning outcomes, making the rationale for any activity opaque to students and observers alike.

Both models share an ironic flaw: their definitions of "knowledge" and "understanding" remain unexamined, and assessments are typically designed after instruction rather than as evidence of learning goals. The caterpillar metaphor Wiggins and McTighe deploy is vivid: traditional design resembles a wormlike progression (small, sequential, grubbing forward) rather than a metamorphosis. Backward design instead insists on identifying where students should end up first.

The chapter lays out the three-stage design process that structures the entire book:

**Stage 1 — Identify Desired Results** asks what students should know, understand, and be able to do. Designers distinguish between one-dimensional knowledge-and-skill goals and **enduring understandings**: big ideas that transcend any single lesson and have lasting value beyond the classroom. Questions to drive this stage include: What are the "big ideas" of the discipline? What evidence will we accept as proof of understanding? What misunderstandings are likely to arise?

**Stage 2 — Determine Acceptable Evidence** flips the usual sequence: instead of teaching first and testing last, backward design insists that assessments be planned deliberately as authentic evidence of the understandings named in Stage 1. The authors introduce the Six Facets of Understanding (detailed in Chapter 4) and the GRASPS model for constructing performance tasks (Goal, Role, Audience, Situation, Product or Performance, Standards for success).

**Stage 3 — Plan Learning Experiences and Instruction** concerns the question of how students will arrive at the understandings identified in Stage 1. This chapter stops short of giving prescriptive lessons, insisting instead on W.H.E.R.E. (Where are we headed?, Why?, Hook, Explore, Equip, Reflect and revise, Exhibit, Evaluate) as a design sieve to embed motivational and reflective elements into activities.

The chapter emphasizes that backward design is not backward teaching. The design sequence is reversed, but the teaching sequence still moves from orienting students to empowering them with evidence and performances. 

## Chapter 2: Understanding Understanding

Chapter 2 takes on the philosophical heart of the book. Wiggins and McTighe argue that understanding has been systematically confused with knowledge, and that most curricula—and most tests—reward knowing small facts rather than demonstrating understanding. They present cognitive science findings showing that understanding involves seeing connections, grasping purposes, transferring knowledge, and applying insight to new situations. The chapter differentiates between **knowledge** (propositional content, facts, vocabulary, procedures) and **understanding** (the ability to make inferences, interpret, justify, explain implications, and transform).

The authors draw on research by Lauren Resnick, who argued for "habits of mind" rather than rote recall; by David Perkins, whose "performances of understanding" model emphasizes students' hands-on application of ideas; and by Howard Gardner's work on disciplinary understanding. The "smell test" offered in this chapter is simple: ask whether a student can perform in a novel situation requiring the concept. Chapter 2 also introduces the developmental progression of understanding from naïve through conventional to sophisticated. This progression undergirds the book's insistence on teaching for transfer, not merely repetitive application of practiced procedures.

## Chapter 3: Gaining Clarity on Our Goals

Chapter 3 presents the practical machinery of Stage 1. Wiggins and McTighe guide teachers through the process of writing **understandings**, which they describe as open-ended propositions that are generative rather than finished. A good understanding is written from the learner's perspective and resembles an insight rather than a directive. Examples given include "A law can be just in form yet unjust in outcome" rather than "Students will define justice." Distinguishing understandings from knowledge-and-skill facts can be done by checking whether a statement requires a PDF-level worksheet (a fact) or an interpretive essay (an understanding).

The chapter also differentiates related constructs: **essential questions** are debatable, provocative, and return again and again across a unit or course; **topical questions** tend to resolve quickly; **guiding questions** are narrower and organize specific lessons. Wiggins and McTighe recommend a ratio of roughly three to five enduring understandings and four to six essential questions per meaningful unit.

This chapter moves teachers from content standards lists to a coherent written set of design prompts. Wiggins and McTighe emphasize that clarity comes from writing, not thinking alone. Drafts should be vetted against criteria: Is it graspable? Is it counterintuitive enough to prompt inquiry? Is it a big idea relevant beyond a single lesson? Does it require evidence drawn from the subject rather than only common sense?

## Chapter 4: The Six Facets of Understanding

Chapter 4 offers the most distinctive conceptual contribution of the book: a taxonomy of what it looks like when understanding is present. Wiggins and McTighe lateralize Bloom's work by presenting Six Facets that teachers can use as both an assessment framework and a design framework:

**1. Explanation.** Students provide thorough, justified, evidence-based accounts of phenomena, facts, and data. Explanatory power is the hallmark of genuine knowledge.

**2. Interpretation.** Students synthesize and personalize information, making it relevant and retaining it over time. Narrative, metaphor, and historical context illustrate this facet.

**3. Application.** Students transfer understanding to diverse, non-obvious contexts. The ability to connect principles from one context to another is the fundamental recursive index of understanding.

**4. Perspective.** Students critically examine points of view, recognizing assumptions, biases, and partial information. This facet requires studying epistemic commitments rather than accepting received views.

**5. Empathy.** Students perceive what others might find alien or implausible—valuing the experience of those with different direct knowledge. Historical empathy and cultural empathy illustrate this facet.

**6. Self-Knowledge.** Students recognize how their own habits of mind, prejudices, and perceptual filters shape their understanding. Metacognition here is tied to growing awareness of one's knowing.

The key argument: understanding is not unitary or page-bounded; it manifests along these six distinct dimensions. An assessor must attend to all six to gather valid evidence. The chapter includes exercises in facet analysis: teachers rewrite existing assessments to probe multiple facets simultaneously rather than testing only explanation.

## Chapter 5: Essential Questions: Doorways to Understanding

Chapter 5 focuses specifically on the design and selection of essential questions. Wiggins and McTighe argue that questions are the engine of inquiry-based learning but that most questions asked in classrooms are actually factual or procedural—requiring lookup rather than thinking. By contrast, essential questions have three defining characteristics:

**They are arguable.** No single answer is authoritative; evidence and reasoning can support differing conclusions. Examples include "What makes a law just?" in civics or "How do organisms adapt to changing environments?" in biology.

**They recur across time.** While topical questions have a single answer at a given moment, essential questions return again and again throughout a unit, a course, and a program. Students revisit them at increasing levels of sophistication.

**They are central to the discipline.** The question is not trivia; it maps onto what practitioners in the field actually argue about.

The chapter offers practical guidance: questions should be placed on unit walls, introduced at the start of each class, and used to frame culminating performance tasks. Wiggins and McTighe also caution against treating essential questions as topics (e.g., "Civil Rights" is a topic, not a question). Requiring students to address essential questions via argumentative evidence ensures that the assessment does not collapse into mere summary.

## Chapter 6: Crafting Understandings

Chapter 6 continues the Stage 1 focus, turning from "What are enduring understandings?" to "How do you craft quality unit frameworks?" Using sample units from across subjects—from algebra to art—the chapter traces how a headline understanding anchors a coherent unit. The authors stress that **unit** refers to an integrated module of learning lasting two to four weeks, not a textbook chapter.

A major portion of this chapter is devoted to **unpacking standards**. Many state standards use verbs like "describe," "list," and "identify," which at their worst reduce learning to recognition tasks. Wiggins and McTighe show how to identify the big idea behind any standard, then redesign the standard as an operational objective tied to an enduring understanding. The chapter also introduces the **T.E.A.C.H.** framework for building quality instructional units: Targeted, Engaging, Assessable, Coherent, and Hitting the big picture—the five criteria for a unit that resembles an interesting conversation rather than a filing system.

Practical tools include a unit-planning template that helps teachers map understandings, essential questions, knowledge-and-skill goals, and performance tasks on a single page. The chapter underscores that a well-designed unit reduces teacher workload over time because students know where the unit is heading, reducing chronic "Why are we doing this?" behavior management problems.

## Chapter 7: Thinking Like an Assessor

Reflecting the book's emphasis on assessment as design, Chapter 7 focuses on Stage 2: designing acceptable evidence of the understandings named in Stage 1. Wiggins and McTighe introduce six principles for performance assessment including that **understanding is revealed in performance**, not obtainable by simple recall, and that valid assessments require multiple measures across multiple facets.

The chapter uses an extended metaphor: the assessor thinks like a detective, not a rote-test-builder. Just as detectives seek multiple kinds of evidence to establish a case, assessors seek performances—oral, written, constructed, and observed—to triangulate whether a student truly understands. The **6x6 Assessment Exercise** asks teachers to design six performances that cover six different understandings simultaneously, forcing cross-connection rather than isolated testing.

The chapter also includes substantial discussion of rubric design. Effective analytic rubrics must:
- Relate specific task requirements to more general performance goals
- Discriminate among degrees of performance with descriptive anchors
- Keep independent criteria separate (not bunched together)
- Include all performance outcomes from novice to expert

Wiggins and McTighe contrast analytic rubrics with holistic and checklist-based rubrics, explaining when each is appropriate.

## Chapter 8: Criteria and Validity

Chapter 8 deepens the assessment conversation with technical precision on validity and reliability in formative and performance assessment. Rather than dismissing standardized tests, Wiggins and McTighe ask a harder question: even when a test is reliable, does it measure the right construct? They distinguish between **face validity** (the test looks relevant) and **construct validity** (the test actually samples the intellectual ability or understanding being targeted).

Using real examples from schools utilizing UbD, the authors demonstrate how assessment validity is compromised when: tasks demand memorization while claiming to assess understanding; scoring does not distinguish between rote recall and genuine insight; or grades reflect compliance rather than quality. Wiggins and McTighe introduce the concept of **assessment validity audits**—systematic checks where teachers swap assessments and independently evaluate whether the tasks actually measure the stated understandings.

A central contribution of this chapter is its discussion of **transfer tasks**: assessments requiring students to apply principles in non-routine contexts. Multiple-choice questions, Wiggins and McTighe argue, are useful for narrow knowledge checks but are poorly suited to assessment of higher-order understanding. The chapter's appendix provides sample transfer tasks in mathematics, science, language arts, and social studies.

## Chapter 9: Planning for Learning

Chapter 9 moves to Stage 3 and systematizes the WHERE framework in detail. Each letter represents a design question that, taken together, yields instruction that is both engaging and evidence-rich:

- **W**here are we headed? Establish clear learning targets and reasons for learning; orient students to enduring questions and understandings from the first day of a unit.
- **H**ook students through thought-provoking experiences that create cognitive dissonance or emotional investment.
- **E**xplore the big ideas through cyclical inquiry, read-and-discuss cycles, and guided experimentation.
- **E**quip with needed knowledge and skills, then capitalize on formative assessments so instruction adjusts in response to evidence of student understanding.
- **R**eflect and rethink by having students revisit foundational questions and assess their own work against rubrics.
- **R**ehearse, revise, and refine work through multiple drafts or performances and peer critique.
- **E**xhibit understanding via products, debates, or presentations that demonstrate real accomplishment.
- **E**valuate learning by self- and peer-assessment linked to explicit success criteria.

The chapter's strength is its integration of engagement and rigor. Many instructional design texts treat motivation as a sidebar; *Understanding by Design* insists that WHERE planning produces engagement as a byproduct of clarity and moral purpose. Wiggins and McTighe also address the practical constraints teachers face: limited time, mandated pacing guides, and assessment pressure. The response is not to abandon standards but to deepen the rationale for each required element, showing administrators and parents how high-quality design produces better test results, not worse ones.

## Chapter 10: Teaching for Understanding

The final chapter confronts implementation. Having described what backward design is, Wiggins and McTighe now explain how to make it the working culture of a school. They argue that UbD represents a **schoolwide imperative**, not merely a technique individual teachers can try after school. The chapter discusses typical barriers: departments with fragmented curricula, incoherent sequencing across grades, teachers with competing theories of learning, and resistance from educators who fear that prioritizing understanding will sacrifice knowledge.

To counter these barriers, Wiggins and McTighe prescribe **collaborative curriculum review** protocols where interdisciplinary teams examine each other's unit plans for clarity, coherence, alignment, and potential for understanding. They introduce the **UbD Review Guide**, a structured feedback protocol that enables colleagues to critique unit plans constructively without requiring a curriculum specialist lead. Sample sessions are described.

The chapter also addresses the "tight, not rigid" principle: UbD provides a clear framework but leaves discretion to teachers at the unit and lesson level. Professional judgment is not only preserved but genuinely enhanced by the framework. Wiggins and McTighe also provide an expanded set of design templates, sample unit plans, and overviews of common hurdles with suggested workarounds, including how to respond to students who complain that the new approach feels unfamiliar, and how to adapt UbD for standardized-content curricula.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment

This summary captures the full architecture of *Understanding by Design*: central thesis (curriculum should be designed backward from understanding), each chapter's contribution to the design process, and the book's practical tools (backward design stages, six facets, essential questions, WHERE, GRASPS, rubrics, unit templates). What cannot be fully captured in this summary are the illustrative cases, teacher self-assessment exercises, and sample unit plans that dominate pages 100–220. These exercises are essential for genuine mastery of the framework; reading them reproduces the metacognitive experience Wiggins and McTighe insist is necessary.

### Recommended Reading Path

| Reader Type | Time | What to Read |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | ~20 min | This summary + skim recommended 5 chapters |
| Interested | ~3 hr | Ch. 1, 2, 4, 6, 7, 10 |
| Practitioner | ~8 hr | Full book with unit-planning exercises |
| Scholar | ~15 hr | Full book + original 1998 edition + supplementary articles |

### Chapters to Read in Full (if not reading the whole book)

- **Chapter 1** — Clearest statement of the three-stage backward design process and why it matters
- **Chapter 4** — Six Facets are the most applied tool in the book; essential for designing assessments
- **Chapter 7** — Most useful for assessors and instructional coaches designing rubrics and performance tasks
- **Chapter 9** — WHERE framework is the immediate entry point for classroom practice
- **Chapter 10** — Most relevant for curriculum teams building a culture of design

### Chapters to Skim or Skip

- **Front matter (Preface)** — Useful framing but not required to use the framework
- **Chapter 3** — Well done but overlaps significantly with templates and samples in Ch. 6 and 10; reference as needed during design work
- **Chapter 5** — Rich, but the advice on essential questions is best absorbed by trying essential questions in your own unit, then returning

### What You'll Miss by Not Reading the Full Book

The full text supplies dozens of conceptual checklists, designed-for-clarity rubrics, and iterative teacher-reflection prompts that cannot be readily summarized. The practical value of *Understanding by Design* comes from doing the exercises it includes, which replicate the design thinking it advocates. Reading the exercises produces the reflective pauses needed for transfer—the very outcome the book is designed to achieve.
