# AI in Education and Adaptive Learning

## Why This Exists

Artificial intelligence offers the possibility of education that adapts in real time to each learner's knowledge state, learning pace, and preferred mode of interaction. Rather than delivering a fixed curriculum to all students, AI-powered systems can identify what a student knows, diagnose misconceptions, recommend next steps, and provide targeted feedback at scale. This represents a fundamental shift from one-to-many instruction toward personalized learning pathways that were previously only possible through expensive one-to-one tutoring.

The field builds on decades of research in intelligent tutoring systems (ITS), starting with Anderson's ACT* theory-based tutors like Cognitive Tutor for mathematics. Knowledge tracing models (Bayesian Knowledge Tracing, Deep Knowledge Tracing) enable systems to track student mastery of individual skills. More recently, large language models have opened new possibilities for automated essay feedback, dialogue-based tutoring, and content generation. Natural language processing enables automated question answering and conversational agents. These technologies raise important questions about data privacy, algorithmic bias, the role of the teacher, and what it means to learn when an AI mediates the experience.

## What Belongs Here

Research on intelligent tutoring systems, adaptive learning platforms, knowledge tracing models, automated essay scoring and feedback, AI-generated content, dialogue-based tutoring, educational data mining, learner modeling, recommendation systems for education, AI for assessment, and the ethics of AI in education.

## What Does NOT Belong Here

General e-learning without adaptive technology, AI research not applied to educational contexts, hardware and infrastructure, and corporate training system design not grounded in learning science.

## Essential Books

- Kurt VanLehn, *The Relative Effectiveness of Human Tutoring, Intelligent Tutoring Systems, and Other Tutoring Systems* — Definitive comparison of tutoring approaches
- Arthur C. Graesser et al., *Tutoring, Intelligent Tutoring Systems, and AutoTutor* — Dialogue-based AI tutoring research
- John R. Anderson, *Rules of the Mind* — The ACT* theory underlying Cognitive Tutors
- Ryan Baker & Inventado, *Educational Data Mining and Learning Analytics* — Methods for analyzing learner data
- Rose Luckin, *Re-Designing Learning Contexts* — AI and the ecology of learning
- Wayne Holmes et al., *Artificial Intelligence in Education: Promises and Implications for Teaching and Learning* — Comprehensive overview of the field
- Zachary A. Pardos & Neil T. Heffernan, *KT-IDEM* — Knowledge tracing innovations
- Kenneth R. Koedinger & Albert T. Corbett, *Cognitive Tutors: Technology Bringing Learning Sciences to the Classroom* — The Cognitive Tutor project
