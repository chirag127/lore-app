# Learning How to Learn

**Authors:** Barbara Oakley, PhD & Terrence Sejnowski, PhD (with Alistair McConville)  
**Year:** 2018  
**Difficulty:** Easy  
**Reading Time:** ~4 hours

## Overview

"Learning How to Learn" is the youth adaptation of Barbara Oakley's bestselling "A Mind for Numbers," co-authored with pioneering computational neuroscientist Terrence Sejnowski. The book translates cutting-edge cognitive psychology and neuroscience into practical, actionable strategies for young learners aged 10 through teen years.

The book emerged from the legendary Coursera MOOC "Learning How to Learn" — one of the largest online courses in history with over 4 million enrolled students. Where the MOOC and "A Mind for Numbers" addressed adults, this version speaks directly to kids and teens with accessible language, playful illustrations, exercises, and a tone that treats young readers as capable scientists of their own minds.

## Executive Summary

Oakley and Sejnowski's core argument is simple: learning is a skill, not a talent. Anyone can become an effective learner by understanding how the brain works and adopting evidence-based techniques. The book centers on two modes of thinking: focused (intense concentration on a specific problem) and diffuse (relaxed, big-picture wandering). True learning requires alternating between both.

The authors tackle procrastination head-on, explaining that the discomfort of starting a difficult task activates the insular cortex — the brain's pain center. The antidote is the Pomodoro Technique: 25 minutes of focused work followed by a 5-minute break, which lowers the pain threshold over time. They teach chunking (building neural patterns through understanding, practice, and context), active recall (closing the book and retrieving), spaced repetition, interleaving, and the memory palace method.

## Key Takeaways

- **Two Modes of Thinking:** Your brain switches between focused (tight pinball bumps) and diffuse (loose, wide-ranging pinball bumps). You need both. Getting stuck means you need a diffuse break.
- **Chunking is Everything:** Learning means building neural "chunks" — tight information packages your brain can load as a single unit. The process requires focus, understanding, practice in context, and repetition.
- **Procrastination Starts with Pain:** The insular cortex reacts to dreaded tasks like it reacts to physical pain. The Pomodoro Technique bypasses this by asking only 25 minutes of effort.
- **Active Recall Beats Re-reading:** Re-reading feels productive but creates fluency illusion. Closing the book and forcing your brain to retrieve information is far more effective.
- **Spaced Repetition Builds Neural Zap:** Practicing across multiple days strengthens neural connections. Cramming creates fragile, quickly forgotten pathways.
- **Sleep Physically Cleans Your Brain:** During sleep, the glymphatic system clears metabolic waste and your brain consolidates and reorganizes memories. Pulling all-nighters is counterproductive.

## Who Should Read This

- Middle school and high school students struggling with study habits
- College freshmen who never learned how to learn effectively
- Parents wanting to equip their children with learning skills
- Teachers looking for classroom-friendly learning science
- Adult learners who prefer a quicker, simpler treatment than "A Mind for Numbers"
- Anyone who believes they're "bad at math" or "not a good student"

## Who Should Skip This

- Advanced researchers in cognitive psychology (too elementary)
- Adults seeking deep academic citations and rigor (read "Make It Stick" instead)
- Readers who already know the material from the Coursera MOOC
- Anyone looking for subject-specific tutoring techniques rather than general learning principles

## Core Themes

- **Metacognition:** Thinking about your own thinking is the foundational skill. The book constantly asks readers to reflect on how their minds work.
- **Brain Plasticity:** The brain changes with use. Struggling with a subject doesn't mean you lack talent — it means your brain is building the necessary structure.
- **Active Learning:** Passive consumption (re-reading, highlighting) is a trap. True learning demands effortful retrieval and application.
- **Procrastination as Biological Signal:** Procrastination isn't laziness — it's a neural pain response. The solution is environmental and procedural, not character-based.
- **Sleep as Part of Learning:** Rest is not a break from learning. It's when memory consolidation and neural cleanup happen.
- **Toolkit Over Talent:** A fixed set of techniques (Pomodoro, recall, spaced repetition, memory palace) can compensate for any perceived lack of ability.

## Why This Book Matters

"Learning How to Learn" is arguably the most accessible translation of learning science ever written for young people. It has been translated into Spanish, Chinese, Japanese, Hungarian, Italian, Ukrainian, and more. Its parent MOOC has educated millions globally. In an era of information overload and educational inequality, this book democratizes the meta-skill that unlocks all others: the ability to learn effectively.

## Related Books

- **A Mind for Numbers** by Barbara Oakley — The adult version of this book, focused on math and science
- **Make It Stick** by Peter C. Brown et al. — More academic treatment of retrieval practice and spaced repetition
- **Peak** by Anders Ericsson — Deep dive into deliberate practice
- **How We Learn** by Benedict Carey — Similar accessibility, more narrative-driven
- **Moonwalking with Einstein** by Joshua Foer — Memory techniques and the memory palace
- **Why Don't Students Like School** by Daniel Willingham — Classroom-focused cognitive science
- **Learn Like a Pro** by Barbara Oakley and Olav Schewe — Adult-oriented practical techniques

## Final Verdict

"Learning How to Learn" succeeds brilliantly at its mission: making evidence-based learning strategies feel fun, accessible, and immediately useful for young readers. It avoids the twin traps of being too academic (which would lose kids) and being too gimmicky (which would lose efficacy). Oakley's personal story — from flunking math to becoming an engineering professor — gives the book authentic credibility. While adults may prefer the more detailed "A Mind for Numbers," this version is one of the best learning-skills books ever written for its target audience.
