# Strengths

**Scientific rigour.** Two of the three authors — Roediger and McDaniel — are among the most cited researchers in memory and cognitive psychology. The book is not a journalist summarising secondary sources; it is written by the scientists who conducted the foundational studies. Every claim is grounded in peer-reviewed experimental evidence, and the reference list runs to dozens of pages.

**Multi-author expertise.** The combination of a professional writer (Brown) with two academic researchers produces a rare synthesis. The storytelling is accessible without sacrificing precision; the science is rigorous without becoming impenetrable. The book manages to be both a readable popular-science book and a trustworthy reference.

**Counter-intuitive insights.** The book's core strength is its willingness to challenge deeply held assumptions. The finding that strategies which feel productive (rereading, highlighting, cramming) are among the least effective, while strategies that feel frustrating (retrieval, spacing, interleaving) are the most effective, is genuinely paradigm-shifting for most readers. This central paradox is supported by converging evidence across multiple laboratories and decades of research.

**Practical actionability.** Unlike many cognitive science books that describe phenomena without providing implementation guidance, *Make It Stick* dedicates substantial space to concrete techniques. The final chapter distils the entire book into specific recommendations for students, teachers, and trainers. Readers can begin applying retrieval practice and spaced repetition immediately, at no cost, with any subject matter.

**Broad ecological validity.** The research cited spans laboratory experiments, classroom studies in K-12 schools, medical education, pilot training, and professional skill acquisition. The findings are not confined to artificial memory tasks — they have been replicated in authentic learning environments across the educational spectrum.

**The meta-cognitive thread.** The book's treatment of metacognitive illusions — fluency, overconfidence, the Dunning-Kruger effect — provides a framework for understanding why learners persist with ineffective strategies. This is not merely informative but transformative: readers who internalise the calibration lesson acquire a lifelong skill for self-directed learning.

# Weaknesses

**Density and repetition.** The book is intentionally repetitive (the authors explicitly state they are applying spacing and interleaving to the book itself), but this design choice makes for a tedious read. Core ideas are revisited across multiple chapters with diminishing marginal returns. Some readers will find themselves saying "yes, I understood this the first three times" by chapter four.

**College-age bias.** Nearly all the classroom studies and examples involve university or medical students. The book says relatively little about how its principles apply to early childhood education, adult literacy, vocational training, or older learners experiencing cognitive decline. The implicit assumption that the reader is a college-educated learner pursuing formal academic study limits its scope.

**Limited treatment of motivation and emotion.** The book treats learning almost exclusively as a cognitive process. It barely addresses the role of curiosity, interest, identity, anxiety, self-efficacy, or belonging — factors that educational psychology research consistently identifies as critical to sustained learning. A student who knows about retrieval practice will not use it if they are burned out, anxious about testing, or do not see the material as relevant to their lives.

**Inadequate coverage of attention and environment.** Effective learning requires focused attention, yet the book does not discuss distraction, multitasking, sleep, stress, or environmental design. A reader could follow all the cognitive strategies perfectly while studying in a noisy, sleep-deprived, phone-saturated environment and still struggle.

**Overemphasis on testing.** The book's advocacy for frequent, low-stakes testing is well-supported, but it does not acknowledge contexts where testing is inappropriate or counterproductive. Learners with test anxiety may not benefit from the same frequency. Subjects requiring creative synthesis or open-ended exploration may not map neatly onto a quizzing paradigm.

**No treatment of knowledge organisation systems.** The book says almost nothing about note-taking, concept mapping, knowledge management, or building a personal knowledge base. For knowledge workers who learn primarily through reading and writing, this is a significant gap. Methods like Zettelkasten, progressive summarisation, or digital knowledge graphs are not mentioned.

# Criticism

The most sustained criticism of *Make It Stick* comes from educators who argue that desirable difficulties, while empirically supported in controlled settings, are difficult to implement in real classrooms with diverse learners. Students who are already struggling, under-resourced, or lacking foundational knowledge may not experience difficulty as "desirable" — they may experience it as demoralising. Teachers report that students resist interleaving and retrieval practice because they feel less prepared and more anxious, and this resistance can undermine the effectiveness of the methods.

Another line of criticism concerns the book's blanket dismissal of learning styles. While the scientific consensus agrees that matching instruction to preferred learning style does not improve outcomes, critics argue the book oversimplifies a complex issue. There is legitimate research on modality-specific strengths, cognitive load theory, and multimedia learning that the book does not engage with. Sweller's cognitive load theory, for instance, suggests that presenting information in multiple modalities can reduce cognitive load — a finding that is not the same as the learning-styles myth but that the book's rhetoric sometimes conflates with it.

The book has also been criticised for its tone. Some readers find it polemical and dismissive of student and teacher intuition. The opening sentence — "People generally are going about learning in the wrong ways" — sets an adversarial relationship with the reader that persists throughout. While this rhetorical stance may be justified by the evidence, it risks alienating the very audience the book seeks to convince.

Several reviewers have noted the problematic choice of examples in chapter five, which uses police training scenarios to illustrate cognitive biases. The uncritical treatment of use-of-force training — particularly a scenario where a cop must decide whether to shoot a person on a porch — has been criticised as tone-deaf and avoiding the obvious implications about racial bias in split-second decision-making. This section has been called out as the weakest part of the book, both ethically and analytically.

# Counterarguments

The "desirable difficulties" framework has been challenged on the grounds that difficulty is only desirable when the learner can successfully overcome it. If the gap between current competence and task demands is too large, difficulty produces frustration, cognitive overload, and disengagement rather than learning. Bjork himself has acknowledged that there are boundaries on the desirability of difficulty, though the book does not explore these boundaries in depth.

The emphasis on retrieval practice as the primary learning strategy has been questioned by researchers who argue that encoding quality matters at least as much as retrieval frequency. If initial encoding is shallow or incorrect, repeated retrieval of poorly encoded information may consolidate misunderstandings rather than correct them. The book addresses this with a discussion of corrective feedback but does not fully grapple with the implications for self-directed learners who cannot reliably generate their own feedback.

Some cognitive scientists have argued that the spacing effect, while real and robust, may interact with task type in ways the book does not acknowledge. For complex problem-solving tasks, massed initial practice may be beneficial to establish procedural fluency before spacing is introduced. The "block-first, interleave-later" sequence may be more effective than pure interleaving from the start.

# Scientific Support

The core findings cited in *Make It Stick* are supported by a substantial body of research:

- **Retrieval practice (testing effect)** : Karpicke & Roediger (2008), *Science* — the landmark study showing 80% vs 36% retention at one week. Roediger & Karpicke (2006) established the basic paradigm. Dunlosky et al. (2013), *Psychological Science in the Public Interest* — the comprehensive review that rated practice testing as having "high utility" across conditions.

- **Spacing effect**: Cepeda et al. (2006), *Psychological Bulletin* — meta-analysis of 184 studies confirming spacing advantages across ages and materials. Cepeda et al. (2008) established optimal gap ratios: the gap between study sessions should be approximately 10-20% of the desired retention interval.

- **Interleaving**: Rohrer & Taylor (2007), *Applied Cognitive Psychology* — demonstrated 43% improvement for interleaved vs blocked practice in mathematics. Kornell & Bjork (2008), *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review* — showed interleaving benefits for artist style identification, establishing the discrimination-learning mechanism.

- **Generation effect**: Slamecka & Graf (1978), *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Learning and Memory* — the original demonstration. Richland et al. (2005) extended the finding to classroom contexts.

- **Metacognitive calibration**: Dunning et al. (2003), *Current Directions in Psychological Science* — the Dunning-Kruger effect in learning contexts. Koriat & Bjork (2005) showed that learners systematically overestimate future recall due to fluency during encoding.

# Historical Context

*Make It Stick* emerged at a pivotal moment in the science of learning. By 2014, cognitive psychology had accumulated decades of rigorous research on memory and learning, but this research had barely penetrated educational practice. The typical classroom and study strategy in 2014 looked largely the same as in 1914 — lectures, rereading, and high-stakes exams. The book was part of a broader movement (alongside the work of John Dunlosky, Daniel Willingham, and the Learning Scientists) to bridge the research-to-practice gap.

The book drew on research traditions reaching back to Hermann Ebbinghaus (1885) on the spacing effect, through Endel Tulving (1960s) on the distinction between recall and recognition, to Robert Bjork's (1990s) formulation of desirable difficulties, and the explosion of retrieval-practice research in the 2000s. The ten-year collaboration between Roediger, McDaniel, and K-12 researcher Pooja Agarwal provided the classroom evidence that earlier laboratory findings had lacked.

The book's publication coincided with the rise of educational technology and MOOCs, which created new demand for evidence-based learning strategies that could be implemented at scale. It also appeared during a period of growing scepticism toward educational fads and learning-styles theory, which the book helped to discredit.

# Similar Books

- *Learning How to Learn* (Oakley & Sejnowski) — More accessible, focuses on brain mechanisms and practical tips for students. Less depth on the research base but broader audience.
- *How We Learn* (Carey) — Journalistic treatment of similar topics. More narrative, less rigorous. Weaker on practical implementation.
- *Peak* (Ericsson) — Focuses on deliberate practice and expertise acquisition. Complements *Make It Stick* by addressing the skill-acquisition side that the book underplays.
- *Why Don't Students Like School* (Willingham) — Cognitive science applied to K-12 classroom teaching. More focused on curriculum design and student motivation.
- *Small Teaching* (Lang) — Translates learning-science research into small classroom interventions. More practical for instructors than *Make It Stick*.
- *The ABCs of How We Learn* (Schwartz, Tsang, & Blair) — Broader scope covering 26 learning mechanisms. More comprehensive but less focused on the core retrieval-spacing-interleaving triad.

# Long-Term Relevance

The specific examples and technologies in *Make It Stick* will inevitably age, but the core principles — retrieval practice, spacing, interleaving, elaboration, generation — rest on fundamental properties of human memory that are unlikely to be overturned. These effects have been replicated across cultures, age groups, and learning materials for over a century. The neural mechanisms (synaptic consolidation, systems consolidation, reconsolidation) are increasingly well-understood and consistent with the behavioural findings.

If anything, the book's relevance has increased since publication. The rise of AI-powered learning tools (adaptive spaced-repetition algorithms, AI tutors, personalised retrieval practice) has made it easier to implement the principles at scale. The book provides the theoretical foundation that these tools operationalise. The spread of remote and hybrid learning has also increased the demand for self-directed learning strategies — precisely the audience the book serves best.

The main risk to the book's long-term relevance is if subsequent research identifies boundary conditions that significantly limit the effectiveness of its recommended strategies. For example, if retrieval practice proves substantially less effective for certain types of learning (creative problem-solving, open-ended exploration) or certain populations (novices, children, learners with cognitive impairments), the universal recommendations would need qualification. Current evidence suggests these boundary conditions exist but are narrower than the critics claim.

# Final Assessment

*Make It Stick* is the single most important book on the science of learning written for a general audience. Its core claims are well-supported by converging evidence from cognitive psychology, its practical recommendations are actionable and cost-free, and its central insight — that the most effective learning strategies feel counter-intuitively difficult — has the power to fundamentally change how readers approach studying and practice. The book is not without flaws: it is repetitive, its scope is narrow (excluding motivation, attention, emotion, and knowledge management), and some of its examples are poorly chosen. But these weaknesses do not undermine its central thesis. For students, teachers, and lifelong learners, the return on investment from reading and applying this book is exceptionally high.
