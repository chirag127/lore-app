## Key Problem

The fundamental problem that Neurodiversity in the Classroom addresses is the systemic failure of American K-12 special education to serve its core mandate. Though legally required since the Education for All Handicapped Children Act of 1975 and reinforced through IDEA 2004, special education in practice remains dominated by a deficit-based diagnostic paradigm that systematically underidentifies student strengths, generates low teacher expectations, and sustains inequitable outcomes five decades after the legal mandate. Armstrong identifies the root of this problem not as a failure of individual teachers — most of whom genuinely care about their neurodiverse students — but as a structural and conceptual problem embedded in the field itself: special education was founded on a medical model that frames neurological difference as a pathology to be remediated, diagnosed, and accommodated, rather than as a form of human diversity to be recognized, valued, and leveraged. The consequences of this framing are concrete and measurable: high dropout rates among students with disabilities, overrepresentation in disciplinary removals, underrepresentation in advanced coursework, disproportionately low college and career outcomes, and an emotional toll on students who internalize the deficit narrative as an identity. Armstrong's strategy is to attack the paradigm itself rather than simply reform its implementation, arguing that incremental improvements within the disability framework will not close the gap between the promise and practice of inclusive education.

## Proposed Solution

Armstrong proposes replacing the disability paradigm with a neurodiversity framework implemented through his Positive Niche Construction (PNC) model. The solution has two levels: philosophical and operational. At the philosophical level, Armstrong asks educators and schools to adopt biodiversity as an analogical template and to treat neurodivergent students as members of a recognized minority whose brains are wired differently rather than as broken versions of a normal. At the operational level, the PNC template provides a systematic seven-component framework for classroom and school design: (1) modify the physical and social environment to be neurologically inclusive; (2) provide appropriate accommodations for functional challenges identified via evaluation; (3) deploy assistive technology that removes irrelevant processing barriers; (4) enhance human resources through peer support, mentoring, and advocacy relationships; (5) design strengths-based learning strategies that leverage the student's neurological profile rather than remediating deficits; (6) help the student identify career paths aligned with their cognitive strengths; and (7) expose the student to positive role models — both from history and from contemporary professional life — who share their neurotype. Applied across all five disability categories covered in the book, this framework is designed to function as a practical supplement to, rather than a replacement for, existing IEP processes and UDL curriculum design.

## Evidence and Argumentation

Armstrong's argument is primarily integrative and practitioner-synthetic rather than empirically original. Within each of the book's diagnostic chapters, he marshals three distinct bodies of evidence: peer-reviewed research on condition-specific cognitive profiles; case study and anecdotal evidence from adult success stories; and practical implementation evidence from schools that have adopted UDL and inclusive practices. Specific empirical claims cited include: research on visuospatial strengths in dyslexia by Eide and Eide, Norman Geschwind's earlier work on verbal-visual dissociation in dyslexic profile, and entrepreneurship studies linking dyslexia to self-employment outcomes; research by Darya Zabelina and Mark Beeman on divergent thinking in ADHD, coupled with Barkley's longitudinal ADHD studies; Simon Baron-Cohen's systemizing research in autism and Temple Grandin's "thinking in pictures" thesis on visual cognition in autistic individuals; vocational strengths research by Marsha Skillern applied to intellectual disability contexts; and Kay Redfield Jamison's work on creativity and mood disorder. The biodiversity analogy rests on Odling-Smee, Laland and Feldman's niche construction theory in evolutionary biology. The PNC model itself originates with Detroit-based special educator Dr. Wayne P. Cohen, whose work Armstrong cites and extends for each diagnostic category. The chapter vignettes — poor versus positive niche construction — are designed to be structurally analogous to quality improvement cases in education administration, making the argument felt at a practical level rather than abstractly theoretical. Armstrong supports each vignette with specific teacher actions and their consequences, giving the argument a before/after demonstration structure.

## Unresolved Questions

Several important questions remain unresolved in the book. First, the question of eligibility persists: Armstrong's framework is most readily applied to students at the milder end of each condition — those whose strengths are visible and whose challenges can be addressed through environmental modification rather than intensive therapeutic support. What the framework looks like for a student with severe autism and co-occurring intellectual disability, or a student with significant support needs due to complex trauma and behavioral disability, remains largely undertheorized in this book, though Armstrong acknowledges this limitation explicitly in Chapter 5. Second, the funding question is structurally unresolved: the Positive Niche Construction model requires extensive specialist time — for UDL curriculum design, technology procurement, human relationship building, and career counseling — resources that currently flow through disability-label-based funding mechanisms. As Dr. Jonathan Rose pointed out in his critical response, there is a dependency: the disability paradigm's legal protections are what provide the resources to implement the neurodiversity paradigm's recommendations, and it is not clear that schools will continue to provide those resources if the disability framing is substantively weakened without a new funding architecture. Third, the question of measurement: how does a strength-based model demonstrate accountability? If a school documents growth in student well-being, engagement, and career pathway development rather than standardized test scores, it must defend those measures against accountability systems that continue to prioritize narrow academic proficiency. Fourth, the question of teacher preparation: the book assumes that general education teachers are capable of implementing PNC strategies, but most teacher preparation programs do not currently address neurodiversity framework, UDL design, or strength-based assessment, creating a serious capacity gap between what the model requires and what entering teachers possess.

## Criticisms

Several named critics have raised substantive objections to the model as presented in the book.

**Dr. Ginny Russell (2022, University of Exeter, published via Disabled World critique series)**: Russell has argued from a disability studies research perspective that the neurodiversity movement's application to school contexts has sometimes produced a "light" version of the framework — symbolic adoption of neurodiversity language without the structural investment that would make the substantive vision work. She observed that schools that adopt strength-based rhetoric without corresponding increases in staffing, UDL capacity, and support services risk producing a rhetorical shift while leaving substantive inequities intact. Russell does not reject Armstrong's framework but argues it must be coupled with anti-neoliberal analysis of the accountability regimes that constrain it.

**Dr. Elizabeth Pellicano (UCL Institute of Education, various publications 2016–2021)**: Pellicano's critical research on UDL and autism-inclusive education has identified what she calls the "valueducation gap" — a pattern in which teachers report endorsing strength-based values at the policy level while maintaining deficit-oriented instructional practices in day-to-day teaching. Her 2018 *Autism* study found that the rhetoric/practice gap was significant and was maintained not by teacher resistance but by structural conditions: caseload sizes, standardized accountability, and the absence of in-service professional development on UDL implementation. Pellicano's critique is that Armstrong's framework is right in direction but needs to address the structural conditions that perpetuate the gap between aspiration and practice.

**Dr. Jonathan Rose (Ohio State University, 2013–2014, Educational Leadership critical forum)**: Rose's protest is not against the neurodiversity framework itself but against its implications for disability law and policy. He argues in public-reviewed analyses that replacing a disability focus with a neurodiversity focus risks dissolving the very legal category (disability) that IDEA uses to protect students and fund their services. Rose's concern: if a school successfully reclassifies students as simply "neurodiverse" without maintaining disability eligibility, those students may lose access to the resource-intensive services Armstrong's framework actually requires. He contends that the proper sequence is: strengthen the disability-based protections first, then use the neurodiversity framework within those protected spaces — not replace protections with philosophy.

**Dr. Michael Wehmeyer (University of Kansas, Beach Center on Disabilities, 2015 and ongoing)**: Wehmeyer, a leading researcher in self-determination and intellectual disability, has endorsed the strengths-based orientation while cautioning that Armstrong's framework risks conflating conditions of very different support needs. The neurodiversity movement originated in the autistic self-advocacy community, where many self-advocates are verbally fluent and able to articulate a particular conception of identity. That framework does not map neatly onto people with moderate to profound intellectual disability, who may not have the same capacity for self-advocacy and whose support needs are considerably broader than the environmental modifications Armstrong describes. Wehmeyer recommends that the neurodiversity framework be applied selectively rather than universally across all disability categories.

## Alternative Solutions

Several alternative frameworks have been proposed to address similar problems — supporting neurodiverse students in inclusive classrooms — and these represent distinct intellectual lineages with different strengths and limitations.

**Response to Intervention (RTI) and Multi-Tiered Systems of Supports (MTSS)**: RTI, embedded in IDEA 2004 as a tiered intervention framework, addresses the early identification and support problem Armstrong identifies without departing from the disability paradigm. Its advantage is legal grounding and administrative familiarity. Its limitation is that it remains fundamentally deficit-responsive — it targets skills gaps rather than building strengths — and it has not been shown to reduce disproportionality in special education identification rates across racial groups.

**Universal Design for Learning (UDL) as a standalone framework**: UDL, originally developed at CAST by David Rose and Ann Meyer, shares many of Armstrong's commitments and is more directly rooted in cognitive science and learning engineering. Its advantage over Armstrong's PNC approach is its tighter alignment with specific brain function research and its clearer empirical evidence base from implementation studies. Its limitation is that UDL has historically been framed primarily as a curriculum design tool, not an ecology-level framework: UDL addresses the "what" of instruction but is less explicit about the student strengths inventory, career aspiration development, and role-model components of Armstrong's integrated model.

**Social Model of Disability (British advocacy tradition)**: Originating in disabled people's organizing in 1970s Britain, the social model distinguishes impairment (a bodily condition) from disability (social barriers to participation). This model has been enormously influential in policy and advocacy and provides a clear theoretical alternative to the individualistic medical model. Its advantage over Armstrong's framework is its sharper political analysis of structural barriers. Its limitation for classroom practitioners is that it is primarily a policy and advocacy framework, not a practical planning tool — it tells schools what not to do (discriminate) more clearly than what to do (optimize learning environments for neurological diversity).

**The Neurodiversity-Affirming IEP approach**: Emerging from recent advocacy and practitioner work, particularly in occupational therapy and speech-language pathology, this framework treats the IEP document itself as a site of neurodiversity advocacy, writing strength-based present levels of performance alongside deficit-based ones and structuring annual goals around student interests and identity. This approach is more administratively compatible than Armstrong's framework because it works within existing IEP structures, but it is also more limited in scope — it does not address schoolwide culture or curriculum design.

**Trauma-Informed Practice**: A growing movement in special and general education that reframes behavioral difficulties as responses to adverse experience rather than symptoms of neurodivergence. This framework addresses a real gap in Armstrong's analysis — he does not extensively address the overlap between neurodiverse conditions and adverse childhood experiences. Trauma-informed practices work alongside rather than compete with neurodiversity-affirming approaches, and their combination represents a promising emerging synthesis.

## Practical Applications

The book's practical applications span five distinct operational domains:

**Curriculum and Instructional Design**: Armstrong's chapter demonstrates how UDL and strength-based strategies can be integrated into lesson planning. Specific approaches include: offering multiple means of representation (text, audio, video, manipulatives), multiple means of action and expression (written response, oral presentation, visual project, multimedia composition), and multiple means of engagement (choice of topic, choice of format, self-pacing). For students with ADHD, project-based learning structures that allow movement and choice; for students with dyslexia, audiobook and dictation alternatives to decoding barriers; for autistic students, structured routines and visual schedules that reduce anxiety; for students with EBD, trauma-sensitive assignments that provide emotional safety as a precondition for learning.

**Assistive Technology Integration**: The book catalogs and evaluates specific tools across conditions: text-to-speech (NaturalReader, VoiceDream Reader), speech-to-text (Dragon NaturallySpeaking built-in dictation), audiobook platforms (Bookshare, Learning Ally), organizational apps (iCalendars with visual cues, reminder apps), sensory tools (fidget objects, noise-canceling headphones, weighted lap pads), communication supports (picture exchange systems, iPad-based AAC apps), and environmental tools (visual timers, flexible seating, green screen backgrounds for reducing distracting visual input).

**Strength-Based Assessment and Profile Development**: Armstrong recommends that educators — working with students, parents, and specialists — compile an explicit "strength profile" for each neurodiverse student in addition to the deficit-oriented evaluation reports that drive IEP placement decisions. The strength profile asks: what does this student do well? What do they find absorbing? What conditions bring out their best performance? What historical figures share their profile? What careers have been successfully navigated by people with their profile? The profile serves as a counter-narrative to the IEP's deficit catalog.

**Peer-Mediated Support**: Several chapters explicitly recommend structured peer relationships — peer buddy systems, cooperative learning groups with explicit role assignments that play to each member's strengths, and mentorship programs that pair neurodiverse students with neurotypical peers for social and organizational support. The model implicitly draws on contact theory in social psychology: reducing stigma requires meaningful, sustained, reciprocal contact — not casual proximity.

**Schoolwide Systems Change**: Chapter 7 provides a self-audit framework: Does your school have a UDL implementation plan? Are all teachers trained in neurodiversity concepts, not only special educators? Are strength-based practices embedded in professional development? Does the school celebrate neurodiverse contributions publicly? Are twice-exceptional students identified and served? Do behavioral discipline policies account for neurodivergent communication styles? Do IEP meetings begin with strengths rather than deficits? Each question operationalizes the larger philosophical commitment into an administrative checklist.

**Career Development**: Armstrong recommends starting career aspiration conversations early — not in high school only but in elementary school — by presenting students with affirmative role models and stories of adults who share their neurotype and have built successful careers. The career pathways recommended for each condition are designed to match the strength profile: law enforcement and entrepreneurship for ADHD, engineering and data science for autism, creative arts and hospitality for intellectual disability, and journalism and creative writing for students with dyslexia or emotional and behavioral disorders whose emotional depth generates powerful expressive capacity.
