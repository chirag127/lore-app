Neurodiversity in the Classroom argues that the field of special education has been trapped for forty years in what Armstrong calls a "disability paradigm" — a deficit-focused framework that asks "what's wrong with this student?" and responds with remediation, labels, and segregated support. Drawing on the neurodiversity movement that emerged from the autism rights community in the late 1990s, Armstrong proposes replacing it with a "neurodiversity paradigm" that asks "what are this student's strengths?" and seeks to construct positive cognitive niches in which those strengths can flourish. The book covers five categories of special needs — learning disabilities, ADHD, autism spectrum disorder, intellectual disabilities, and emotional and behavioral disorders — dedicating a chapter to the strengths and positive role models associated with each, then closes with a chapter on how to build a strength-based school. Each chapter follows a consistent structure covering background, strengths awareness, positive role models, assistive technologies, enhanced human resources, strengths-based learning strategies, affirmative career aspirations, environmental modifications, and "positive niche construction" scenarios aligned with the Common Core State Standards (though the book predates most standards adoption). Published by ASCD in 2012, the 184-page book is based on Armstrong's 40-plus years as an educator, psychologist, and author, and originated as an expansion of his earlier article for ASCD's Educational Leadership magazine (April 2012, "Neurodiversity: The Future of Special Education?"). The work is notable for being among the first commercially published books to bring the neurodiversity framework from autism activism into mainstream K-12 special education practice.
