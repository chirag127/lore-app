## Key Problem

The core problem *The Interpretation of Cultures* addresses is a crisis of method in mid-century anthropology and the broader social sciences. The positivist paradigm of the 1930s–1950s had produced sophisticated descriptions of particular societies but struggled to generate cumulative, generalisable knowledge. Functionalist anthropology described what institutions did for social systems without seriously examining what the participants themselves thought those institutions meant. Structuralist anthropology sought deep, universal grammars of human thought but abstracted away from history and context in ways that made its findings remote from actual human experience. Against both, Geertz posed a problem: *how can a social science account for meaning?* The answer he proposed — that anthropologists should aim for thick, interpretive description of culturally specific webs of significance — was a direct methodological response to a genuine impasse in the field.

## Proposed Solution

Geertz's solution has three interlocking parts. First, redefine culture symbolically: culture is not a set of behavioural customs or a mechanism for satisfying needs, but a system of inherited conceptions expressed in symbolic forms through which people communicate, perpetuate, and develop their knowledge about and attitudes toward life. Second, redefine the anthropologist's role: not as a scientist discovering empirical laws but as an interpreter reading cultural texts. Third, redefine ethnography as thick description: narratives that layer action within context to convey not only what happened but what it meant to the participants. Taken together, these moves proposed not a new theory of culture but a new *practice* of studying it.

## Evidence and Argumentation

Geertz's argument relies primarily on conceptual argument, philosophical analogy, and exemplary case study rather than systematic empirical demonstration. The philosophical argument — that mind, culture, and meaning are linked through the concept of the symbol — is presented in Chapters 1–3 with reference to Weber, Ryle, and Cassirer. The semiotic programme — religion and ideology as cultural systems — is developed in Chapters 4, 5, and 8 through conceptual definitions and illustrative examples drawn from Morocco, Java, and Bali. The argument is made most compellingly in Chapter 15, "Deep Play," where a single Balinese social practice is read at multiple levels simultaneously: as economic game, as status competition, as symbolic drama, as political negotiation.

The strength of the argument lies in its compelling use of the Balinese cockfight to illustrate what thick description looks like in practice. That essay performs the method it advocates. More questionably, the essays on postcolonial politics (Chapters 9–12) rely on broad generalisation about nationalism, primordial sentiment, and ideology without the close-grained ethnographic evidence that would be required to make those categories rigorous. The philosophical argument is persuasive but leaves open the question of how thick description validates itself as knowledge.

## Unresolved Questions

Geertz leaves several fundamental questions unresolved. The most important is what counts as a *good* interpretive reading of a cultural practice. If anthropology is an interpretive science, there must be some standard for adjudicating between competing readings — but Geertz does not provide one. A second open question concerns power: Geertz's symbolic anthropology shows how meanings are constructed and communicated, but it offers no account of how certain meanings come to dominate while others are suppressed. A third question concerns generalisability: thick description produces rich knowledge about particular cases, but it is unclear how — or whether — findings from one cultural setting travel to another. A fourth question concerns the boundary between interpretation and self-deception or ideology: if culture is a system of symbols, how does the anthropologist know when she is reading genuine cultural meaning rather than projecting her own assumptions onto a situation?

## Criticisms

**Talal Asad** argued in *Genealogies of Religion* (1993) that Geertz's semiotic model of religion systematically excludes the dimensions of power, discipline, and historical constraint that are essential to understanding what religion actually does in human societies. What Geertz presents as a neutral description of religion as a symbol system, Asad reads as a peculiarly modern and Western way of conceiving religion — one that reflects liberal Protestant assumptions about belief and meaning rather than the realities of religious practice across history.

**Roy D'Andrade** (as reported by T. M. Luhrmann in the *Times Literary Supplement*, 2024) regarded Geertz's interpretive turn as a disciplinary regression that moved anthropology away from the comparative empirical programme that might have made it a cumulatively progressive science. D'Andrade's concern was not merely methodological but social-scientific: he believed that anthropology needed to ask testable questions about why cultures differ in the ways they do, not just to produce resonant literary descriptions of individual cases.

**Sherry Ortner** questioned the underlying assumption that cultures are coherent, integrated systems of meaning. Her critique of the "culture-first" paradigm — of which Geertz was the leading exponent — noted that the emphasis on shared meaning tends to flatten cultural conflict and the operation of power within symbolic systems. Ortner's point is that Geertz's method tends to produce harmonious, internally consistent cultural pictures rather than accounts of contested, unequal symbolic fields.

**Ward H. Goodenough**, in his *Science* review of 1974, offered a measured but pointed critique. He worried that Geertz's semiotic approach, while intellectually sophisticated, could not ground cumulatively reliable knowledge about culture because different interpreters might legitimately arrive at incompatible readings. For Goodenough, interpretive anthropology risked substituting literary artistry for scientific rigour.

## Alternative Solutions

The most direct alternative to Geertz's interpretive approach is the cognitive/quantitative tradition represented by Ward Goodenough and Roy D'Andrade, which sought to remedy anthropology's interpretive drift by building comparative, empirically testable models of cultural knowledge. A second alternative is Marxist political economy and dependency theory, which treated symbolic systems more as superstructural epiphenomena determined by economic relations than as autonomous systems of meaning to be interpreted on their own terms. A third alternative is practice theory as developed by Pierre Bourdieu, which retains a role for symbolic systems but anchors them in durable structures of economic and social capital, showing how symbolic classification is itself a form of social power. A fourth alternative within anthropology is the "ontological turn" of the 2010s — represented by scholars such as Bruno Latour and Eduardo Viveiros de Castro — which proposes studying not the meanings that humans attribute to a shared world but the different worlds that different ontologies make possible, a move beyond both Geertzian interpretation and structuralist universals.

## Practical Applications

*The Interpretation of Cultures* has had substantial practical consequences for several fields. In anthropology, thick description became a standard methodological commitment woven into ethnographic training. In education, Geertz's approach informed approaches to understanding how cultural meanings are transmitted and reproduced through schooling. In religious studies, the semiotic definition of religion in Chapter 4 provided a conceptual framework for comparing religious systems across traditions while remaining attentive to internal meaning — a framework taken up by scholars of religion in comparative perspective. In political sociology, the political essays contributed to the study of nationalism, ethnicity, and identity as symbolic formations, particularly influencing analyses of postcolonial politics. In cultural studies, Geertz's approach provided an intellectual bridge between anthropology and literary/cultural theory: it demonstrated that culture could be studied with the same textual sensitivity that literary critics brought to novels and poems, a connection that helped shape the methodological commitments of the Birmingham School. More broadly, the book's influence can be detected wherever scholars attempt to read cultural practices as texts, particularly in ethnography-driven research across the social sciences and humanities.
