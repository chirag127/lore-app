Clifford Geertz’s *The Interpretation of Cultures* appeared in 1973 at a moment when American anthropology was searching for a new identity. The collection of fifteen essays — written between 1957 and 1973 — argued that culture is neither a mysterious collective consciousness nor a catalogue of customs, but rather "a web of significance" spun by human beings that anthropologists have the task of interpreting. The book introduced "thick description" as a methodological attitude and became the founding manifesto of what would be known as symbolic or interpretive anthropology. Awarded the Sorokin Award by the American Sociological Association in 1974 and named one of the hundred most important books published since World War II by the *Times Literary Supplement*, it reshaped how multiple disciplines approach the description and analysis of cultural life.
