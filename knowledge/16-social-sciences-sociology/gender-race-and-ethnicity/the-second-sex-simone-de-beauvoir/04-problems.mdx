## Key Problem

The core problem *The Second Sex* identifies is structural rather than incidental: women are systematically excluded from freedom, not because they lack the capacity for freedom but because a patriarchal civilization has constructed an entire apparatus—biological ideology, religious doctrine, legal institution, custom, myth, and psychology—to prevent them from exercising it. This apparatus produces and reproduces woman as the socially subordinate Other, and it does so because that subordination is structurally useful to the men who occupy the Subject position. The problem is therefore not personal failure (women are not "naturally" passive or vain or domestic) and not lack of knowledge—it is structural, institutional, historical, and ongoing. Beauvoir writes: "One is not born, but rather becomes, woman," precisely to insist that this becoming is not natural but engineered, and that whatever is engineered can be contested and potentially transformed.

---

## Proposed Solution

Beauvoir's solution is existentialist rather programmatic in the narrow political sense. She does not present a list of legislative reforms or a political strategy. Her solution consists of three interlocking demands:

**1. Economic independence.** Women must participate fully in production and earn their own living. The dependent wife is structurally unfree; the working woman has, at minimum, the material basis for freedom. Competition through work "awakens woman's will to power" and forces her to develop capacities that domestic life systematically suppresses.

**2. Rejection of the ideology of femininity.** Women must refuse the roles, scripts, and myths—mother, wife, muse, woman-child—designed to keep them in immanence. This means refusing to seek validation through beauty, marriage, reproduction, or male admiration. It means recognizing that the "feminine" virtues—passivity, self-sacrifice, charm, domesticity—are virtues of subordination, not freedom.

**3. Commitment to genuine projects of transcendence.** Freedom, for Beauvoir, is not abstract but concrete: it is the commitment to a project that outlasts the individual and gives her life a meaning that is not derived from men. Writing, art, politics, science, craftsmanship—any undertaking through which a woman creates rather than exists.

## Evidence & Argumentation

Beauvoir structures her argument evidence by evidence, tradition by tradition, in a methodical suppression of every available explanation for women's condition except social construction. She addresses:

**Biological determinism:** By examining mammalian reproduction, physiology, and endocrine systems and showing that biological facts never logically entail social arrangements. The female reproductive cycle does not justify domestic confinement. Physical strength does not confer moral authority. Biology is factual but not normative; what women are biologically does not determine what they can or should become socially.

**Psychoanalytic theory:** Freud's account of penis envy, castration anxiety, and female psychosexual development is rejected on the grounds that it describes the effects of woman's situation rather than its causes. The girl who envies the boy's penis is not responding to anatomical absence but to the cultural significance attached to possession of the penis—significance that psychoanalysis itself has not explained but merely assumed.

**Historical materialism:** Marx and Engels correctly identify material conditions as primary but stop too soon. The explanation of women's subordination in terms of the rise of private property is true but incomplete; the property relation is itself rooted in a deeper structure of the subject's domination of the female body through reproduction.

**Myth and religion:** The myth of woman-as-Earth-Mother, as Virgin Mary, as Eve, as Muse, as femme fatale—each serves to idealize and thereby immobilize. Myths don't merely describe women; they constitute the available space in which women can appear as subjects. To exist only as myth—as what men project onto women—is to be denied the capacity to define oneself.

## Unresolved Questions

Beauvoir's existentialism leaves several questions open that subsequent feminist theory has struggled to answer.

**Is the category "woman" itself suspect?** If woman is a social construct with no natural essence, can the category "woman"—the very category the book seeks to liberate—serve as a foundation for feminist politics? This is the tension that Judith Butler would later identify and amplify in *Gender Trouble* (1990).

**Where does race enter?** The primary woman Beauvoir describes is the white, educated, middle-class French woman. The conditions, histories, and structures of oppression that shape the lives of Black women (in the United States, France, or Africa), colonized women in Algeria or Indochina, or working-class women in any national context receive only marginal attention. That this is a limitation, not a refutation, does not diminish its significance for feminist theory today.

**What would a genuinely liberatory institution of motherhood look like?** Beauvoir is clear that privatized family motherhood is a trap. She calls for "socialist child-rearing practices" but does not specify what these would be. The tension between bodily particularity and freedom—women must bear children in bodies, yet those bodies should not determine their social destiny—remains unresolved.

**What is the relationship between freedom and love?** Beauvoir's ambivalence about heterosexual love—her sense that love between men and women is almost inevitably structured by the Subject-Other dialectic—leaves a question she only partially answers in her closing pages about the possibility of women loving "in their strength and not in their weakness."

## Criticisms

The criticisms of *The Second Sex* are legion and well-founded in parts.

**Alfred Kinsey** (1894–1956) argued the book was of no scientific value, treating it as literary rather than empirical. This is partly fair: Beauvoir rarely distinguishes sharply between what she knows from evidence and what she infers from observation or philosophical commitment. Her generalizations about female psychology and sexuality are sometimes asserted rather than demonstrated.

**Albert Camus** (1913–1960), who knew Beauvoir well, was personally outraged by what he saw as a caricature of men in the book. His complaint—that Beauvoir "ridiculed the French male"—reflects the book's most provocative rhetorical gesture: its determination to describe male subjectivity not as a neutral standard but as a historical and ideological formation with concrete interests in maintaining women's subordination.

**Francis Jeanson** and **Stevie Smith** accused Beauvoir of "unconscious misogyny"—the charge that for all her critique of patriarchy, she wrote from a position identified with masculine norms of intellectual achievement and independence, and that her own self-image as an exceptional woman required a certain contempt for ordinary women living ordinary domestic lives.

**C. B. Radford** extended this politically, arguing that *The Second Sex* is "primarily a middle-class document," and that its generalizations about women reflect the specific problems of a bourgeois intellectual woman rather than universal female condition.

**Kathryn Sophia Belle** has made the most rigorous systematic extension of this criticism in *Beauvoir and Belle* (2020): Beauvoir's failure to thematize race and colonialism means the book describes a specifically white, Western, cisgender female condition and treats it as universal. For Black women in the United States, the category of woman is not only defined in relation to men but in relation to a white supremacist order in which womanhood itself is racialized.

**Toril Moi** has been critical of the reception of the book in feminist theory, arguing that Butler's influential reading of Beauvoir as offering a clean "sex versus gender" distinction imposes a conceptual framework (derived from 1990s post-structuralism) that Beauvoir's existentialism does not actually provide.

**David M. Halperin** criticizes Beauvoir's idealizing portrayal of relations between women, arguing that she romanticizes the possibility of female erotic community in ways that cut against her own account of women's internalization of patriarchal norms.

## Alternative Solutions

Critics have proposed directions that Beauvoir did not take.

**Intersectionality** (Patricia Hill Collins, Kimberlé Crenshaw, bell hooks) treats gender as analytically inseparable from race, class, sexuality, and colonial position—a direction Beauvoir gestures toward but does not systematically develop.

**Radical lesbian separatism** (Andrea Dworkin, Jill Johnston) argued that women's liberation required rejecting heterosexual relations altogether, an argument Beauvoir resists: she insists on heterosexual possibility as the site of potential equality, not merely oppression.

**Reproductive technology and abolition of the biological family** (Shulamith Firestone's *The Dialectic of Sex*) pushes toward a more radical—some would say science-fictional—transformation: if biology is the root of women's subordination rooted in the biological family, socializing reproduction through technology is the logical extension of Beauvoir's analysis.

**Poststructuralist deconstruction** (Judith Butler, Luce Irigaray) rejects the category of "woman" as a stable referent and argues for the proliferation of genders, sexualities, and identities rather than Beauvoir's insistence on a single universal female subject tied to bodily situation.

Each of these alternatives addresses genuine lacunae in *The Second Sex*. Each also, critics have argued, risks abandoning the universalist claim that made *The Second Sex* politically powerful: that women everywhere, as women, are systematically excluded from freedom, and that this exclusion demands general, cross-cultural feminist response.

## Practical Applications

*The Second Sex* is not an applied text in the conventional sense. It does not offer policy proposals, organizational strategies, or clinical protocols. Its applications are philosophical and political rather than technical.

**For feminist pedagogy:** The book remains the single most important historical prerequisite for understanding modern feminist theory. Any graduate or advanced undergraduate course in feminist philosophy, gender studies, or women's history that does not assign *The Second Sex* has a hole at its center.

**For intersectional theory:** The book's limitations—its failure to address race, colonialism, and global inequality—are themselves productive, as Kathryn Sophia Belle's work demonstrates. Engaging Beauvoir's blind spots is one way of formulating the intersectional project that contemporary feminism has made central.

**For gender policy and advocacy:** The distinction between sex (biology) and gender (social construction) that the book popularized is now embedded in law, policy, and institutional practice across much of the world—in anti-discrimination law, gender mainstreaming, education policy, and workplace regulation. Contemporary gender policy debates about bathrooms, sports, pronouns, and affirmative action all take place within a conceptual space that *The Second Sex* helped create.

**For philosophy of identity:** The book's existential account of freedom—as concrete project, as going beyond one's given situation, as refusal of imposed identity—provides a vocabulary for thinking about gender dysphoria, identity formation, and the political demand for self-determination that remains philosophically rigorous in ways that purely sociological or psychological frameworks are not.

**For organizations and practitioners:** The basic insight—that women are denied not biological difference but institutional freedom—continues to shape programs for women's economic empowerment, educational access, political representation, and legal rights around the world. NGOs working on gender justice operate within a tradition whose philosophical core *The Second Sex* provided.
