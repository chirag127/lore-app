Between the fifteenth and twenty-first centuries, a single historical system has come to organize virtually all of human activity on the planet: the capitalist world-economy. In *World-Systems Analysis: An Introduction*, Immanuel Wallerstein distills four decades of research into a compact, crystalline statement of the intellectual framework he created to understand how this system works, where it came from, and where it may be headed. Published by Duke University Press in 2004 as part of the John Hope Franklin Center series, this 128-page volume is both a primer and a manifesto — it teaches the student while it enlists the scholar in a project of paradigm reconstruction. Wallerstein argues that the nation-state, the firm, the household, the identity group, and the individual are all nodes within a single world-system whose logic of endless accumulation has structured global inequality since 1500 and is now producing its own undoing. The book is indispensable reading for anyone trying to move beyond the ahistorical, nation-state-bound analytical habits inherited from nineteenth-century social science.
