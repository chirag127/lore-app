# The Culture of Fear
## Narration Transcript

---

Barry Glassner: The Culture of Fear: Why Americans Are Afraid of the Wrong
Things.

This is a book about a system. Not a conspiracy. Not a handful of villains
making deliberate choices to deceive the public. A system -- a culture of
fear -- with rules, incentives, and regular routines that consistently
produces public anxiety that bears almost no relationship to actual public
risk.

Glassner, a professor of sociology at the University of Southern California,
set out to answer a simple and alarming question: if Americans are so safe
by any objective measure -- safer than at any point in human history -- why
do so many Americans feel so afraid?

The answer he found is the title of the book. We are afraid because we have
been carefully, systematically, and profitably conditioned to be afraid.

---

The book opens with the most fundamental paradox of modern American life:
Americans have never been safer, and they have never been more afraid. The
crime rate in the United States fell steeply throughout the 1990s, reaching
levels that would have seemed miraculous to Americans in the 1970s and 1980s.
And yet polling data consistently showed that Americans believed crime was
getting worse. They believed it even when it was getting better.

This is the raw data of the culture of fear: a measurable, persistent gap
between risk and perception. Glassner's project is to explain how that gap is
manufactured -- who creates it, why they create it, and what the consequences
are.

---

The first major case study is city crime. In the 1990s, large American cities
experienced historically low violent crime rates. New York City, for example,
saw its homicide rate drop from over 2,200 in 1990 to fewer than 400 by 2000.
This was one of the most significant public safety improvements in American
history. Most Americans were unaware of it.

Meanwhile, news coverage of crime in network television and newspapers
increased. More crime stories. More fear. More panic about releasing
criminals, about urban danger, about "quality of life" in cities. The data
ran one direction; the narrative ran another.

Glassner traces this to a set of mechanisms that he identifies throughout the
book. First, the **dramatic case study**: one vivid story about a single crime
overwrites a graph showing ten years of declining statistics. Second, the
-crime is news; safety is not. Third, politicians amplify fear strategically.
"Tough on crime" was a winning political stance across the political spectrum
in the 1990s, regardless of whether the data supported it.

The consequence was not simply misinformed anxiety. It was policy that ran
counter to the evidence, including mass incarceration on an unprecedented
scale, programs that cost billions of dollars while addressing problems that
were already in decline, and a national conversation about safety that ignored
the actual causes of crime -- poverty, inequality, community disinvestment --
while fixating on punishment.

---

Perhaps the most important case study in the book concerns schools. Glassner
documents that schools in the 1990s were safer than they had been in the
1970s -- safer by every available metric: violent incidents per student,
serious injuries, homicides on school grounds. Schools were the safest place
for an American child to be.

And yet, following highly publicized school shootings in the 1990s and 1999
Columbine massacre, public concern about school safety spiked dramatically.
A Gallup poll found that a majority of parents reported being afraid for their
child's physical safety at school -- even though the statistical basis for
such fear was virtually nonexistent.

The response, predictably, was a wave of security measures: zero tolerance
policies, metal detectors, security cameras, surveillance systems, lockdown
drills. Some of these measures were sensible. Many of them were not. And
critically, almost none of them were evaluated systematically before they were
implemented. The fear came first; evidence followed, if it followed at all.

Glassner does not argue that school shootings are not tragedies. They are.
He does not argue that schools should be above security measures. They should
not. He argues that the *response* should match the *risk*. And that in
America in the 1990s, the response swelled to proportions far beyond the risk,
while genuine systemic issues -- access to mental health care, social
isolation, gun accessibility -- that might have reduced future shootings were
largely ignored.

---

A chapter that has only become more relevant with time is Glassner's analysis
of vaccine scares. He traces the history of vaccine controversy in America,
notably the DPT vaccine scare in the 1970s and 1980s -- when a poorly
designed study and hyperbolic media coverage caused dramatic drops in DPT
immunization rates and a corresponding rise in pertussis deaths -- and the
emerging MMR controversy in the late 1990s.

The pattern, as Glassner identifies it, is always the same. A small,
low-quality, or outright fraudulent study raises concerns. The media covers
the *controversy* -- treating the issue as a genuine scientific debate, even
when the scientific consensus is overwhelming. Public fear rises. Vaccination
rates fall. Outbreaks of previously controlled diseases occur. Eventually the
debunked claim is proven false -- but not before preventable deaths have
occurred.

The Vaccine chapter is Glassner at his sharpest. He identifies a specific
journalistic ritual he calls **the air of controversy**: the practiced habit
of news programs of presenting two sides of every issue as if they are
equivalent, even when one side is a scientific consensus backed by thousands
of studies and the other is a single discredited study. This practice, which
news outlets describe as "balanced," actually generates false balance -- and
in the case of vaccines, false balance generates real death.

---

A chapter that was unusually forward-looking for 1999 is Glassner's analysis
of SARS coverage. The SARS epidemic of 2003 was, in retrospect, a small
epidemiological event compared to the coverage it received in early 2003.
Glassner's 1999 book anticipated how media would cover a future pandemic:
with saturation, fear, and almost no context for base-rate risk. He uses the
SARS example -- though it was not yet a public event when the book was
published -- to show that the culture of fear is not simply reactive to
events. It is anticipatory. It finds fear before events arrive, and amplifies
the fear until the events themselves seem to justify the original panic.

---

One of the most significant sections of the book -- and one that makes it
difficult to classify as merely a media critique -- is Glassner's analysis
of race and fear. He documents a consistent asymmetry: when a Black person
perpetrates a violent act, the coverage is more sustained, more sensational,
more likely to connect that individual's behavior to a broader narrative about
Black criminality. When a white person perpetrates a similar act, the coverage
shifts to individual psychology -- mental illness, political grievance,
personal pathology. The white perpetrator is treated as an anomaly. The Black
perpetrator is treated as evidence of a pattern.

The consequence is measurable: white Americans systematically overestimate the
percentage of crime committed by Black people, and underestimate the percentage
committed by white people. They are more afraid of Black people than statistics
warrant, and less aware of danger from white people than statistics warrant.

Glassner's framing here is careful and important. He is not making a
simple claim that the media is racist. He is making a sociological claim that
the culture of fear operates through pre-existing racial narratives, amplifying
them and being amplified by them. The culture of fear does not invent race
anxiety; it commercializes it.

---

Two conceptual contributions that distinguish *The Culture of Fear* from
simpler media critiques are **prognostic fear** and **tragedy criticism**.

Prognostic fear is the art of predicting danger without evidence. It is
generated by "risk experts," advocacy groups, cable news personalities, and
political consultants who identify potential future threats and amplify them
before any harm has occurred. The advantage is enormous -- a prognostic
fear-monger never has to wait for an actual event. The feared outcome never
has to materialize for the fear to have served its purpose: ratings were
captured, attention was held, political capital was accumulated. The asymmetry
is structural: fear predictions are cheap to make and costly to disprove when
they are wrong. And even when wrong, they generate no consequences for the
fear producer. The anchor who warned of a non-existent threat never apologizes
on primetime.

Tragedy criticism is the practice of narrative response to disaster that
substitutes for actual political engagement. After every school shooting,
terrorist attack, or public health scare, America engages in extraordinary
amounts of collective emotional processing: cable news filled with commentary,
talk show hosts debating meaning, calls for "doing something" escalating
before any diagnosis has been offered. And then, almost without exception,
nothing substantial happens. Glassner's point is not that emotional response
is wrong. It is that emotional response, in the absence of structural change,
functions as a substitute for action. It generates the feeling of civic
engagement without the substance.

---

One of Glassner's most paradoxical findings is that fear changed behavior,
and the changed behavior created new risk. In response to fear of city crime,
Americans drove more -- avoided public transportation, delayed travel to urban
centers, moved further from cities, required cars for trips that distance
made unnecessary. More driving meant more car accidents. More car accidents
meant more traffic deaths. Glassner traces the data: as fear of crime went up,
traffic deaths went up. The feared risk (crime) was declining. The secondary
risk generated by fear of the primary risk (car accidents from avoidance
behavior) was rising.

This is a devastating general insight, and it applies far beyond 1990s crime
fear. Every fear that systematically diverges from risk -- whether of vaccines,
of immigration, of disease, of natural disaster -- produces behavioral changes
that carry their own mortality consequences. Fear is not free. It extracts a
toll even when the feared event never occurs.

---

Glassner closes not with despair but with discipline. His recommendations
for individuals are concrete and practicable: demand base rates before
panicking; distinguish an event from a pattern; notice who benefits from your
fear; resist prognostic fear; demand evidence; check for false equivalence in
media coverage; replace emotional performance with political engagement.

These are not radical prescriptions. They are modest. But they are precisely
the kind of modest, repeatable, evidence-based habits that actually shift
behavior. Glassner's faith is not in revolution. It is in the aggregate effect
of readers who approach news with slightly more skepticism and slightly more
discipline.

---

The legacy of The Culture of Fear is substantial. Glassner's framing --
manufactured fear as a system, the air of controversy as a journalistic
routine, predictive fear as an unaccountable enterprise, race as a key
architect of anxiety -- has become standard analytical vocabulary in media
studies, public health communication, and sociology. Books like Cass
Sunstein's #Republic, which examines filter bubbles and informational
fragmentation, build directly on Glassner's diagnosis. The culture of fear
described in this book has, if anything, accelerated: social media algorithms
optimize for engagement, which drives toward alarm; politicians tweet alarm
directly to followers without media filters; prognostic fear is now a
full-time occupation for thousands of influencers.

What Glassner diagnosed in 1999 was a mechanism. The mechanism has only
become more powerful.
