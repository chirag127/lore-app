## Key Problem: The Lawlessness Charge Against Legal Realism

The central problem The Common Law Tradition addresses is the charge that legal realism, by exposing the indeterminacy of legal rules, had undermined the legitimacy of appellate adjudication. If judges are not constrained by rules, the argument ran, then appellate decisions are merely exercises of will — political preferences dressed up in legal language. This charge was pressed from multiple directions: by formalists who insisted that law really was a closed logical system; by natural lawyers who argued that law without moral foundations was merely power; and by critics within the realist movement itself, like Jerome Frank, who argued that judicial decisions were primarily products of the judge's personality and psychology.

Llewellyn accepted the realist premise — legal rules do not mechanically dictate outcomes in difficult cases — but rejected the conclusion that judging is therefore lawless. The problem, as he framed it, was to explain how appellate courts produce consistent, predictable outcomes despite the indeterminacy of rules. This required an account of constraint that went beyond rules to include institutions, practices, and professional norms.

## Proposed Solution: The Fourteen Steadying Factors and the Grand Style

Llewellyn's solution operates at two levels. At the descriptive level, he identifies fourteen "steadying factors" that constrain appellate judges even when rules are indeterminate. These factors range from the institutional (group decision, the requirement of a written opinion) to the professional (law-conditioned officials, known doctrinal techniques) to the normative (responsibility for justice, the tradition of one single right answer). Together, Llewellyn argues, these factors make the outcomes of appeals "reckonable" — predictable with tolerable accuracy by skilled lawyers.

At the normative level, Llewellyn argues for the Grand Style of judging, which combines respect for precedent with open consideration of purpose, policy, and situation-sense. The Grand Style produces better law — more just, more flexible, and more predictable — than the Formal Style, which hides judicial choice behind a facade of mechanical rule-application. Llewellyn's claim that American appellate courts were returning to the Grand Style gave the book its hopeful, even optimistic tone: the tradition was not broken, but healing itself.

## Evidence & Argumentation

Llewellyn's evidence for the steadying factors is primarily observational and anecdotal. He draws on his own experience as a law professor, appellate advocate, and drafter of the Uniform Commercial Code, supplemented by a deep reading of American appellate opinions. The book does not offer statistical analysis or systematic empirical testing of its claims. The sixty-four precedent techniques are the most concrete empirical contribution — a catalogue compiled from Llewellyn's reading of thousands of cases that demonstrates the range of legitimate techniques available to judges.

The evidence for the return of the Grand Style is weaker. Llewellyn offers exemplary opinions by Cardozo, Hand, and Traynor, but does not provide the systematic survey that would demonstrate that the Grand Style had in fact increased in frequency. The cyclical thesis — that Anglo-American judging oscillates between Grand Style and Formal Style — is asserted rather than proven.

## Unresolved Questions

**The measurement problem**: Llewellyn never specifies how we would determine whether the Grand Style is actually returning, or how we would measure the "reckonability" of appellate outcomes. His concepts are suggestive but not operationalizable, which limits their use for empirical research.

**The skeptical challenge**: A determined skeptic could accept Llewellyn's fourteen factors and still argue that they do not genuinely constrain. Perhaps the factors simply channel discretion in more subtle ways; perhaps they are merely rationalizations that mask the same lawlessness Llewellyn denies. The book does not fully answer this challenge because it relies ultimately on the good faith and professionalism of judges — a reliance that critics find naive.

**The scope of the argument**: Llewellyn's argument is limited to the kind of ordinary appellate litigation — commercial disputes, tort cases, property questions — that formed the bulk of the state-court dockets he studied. It is not clear that the steadying factors operate the same way in constitutional cases, civil rights cases, or hot-button political appeals, where ideological divisions run deeper and public pressure is more intense.

**The indeterminacy of situation-sense**: Situation-sense, Llewellyn's most original concept, remains frustratingly vague. If judges have different situation-senses — different intuitions about what result fits a fact-pattern — then the concept does not constrain at all. It simply redescribes the phenomenon of disagreement without explaining it.

## Criticisms

**Charles D. Breitel** (Columbia Law Review, 1961) argued that Llewellyn overstates the predictability of appellate outcomes and underestimates the extent to which the steadying factors operate differently in different courts. A factor that constrains a federal court of appeals may not constrain a state supreme court, and vice versa. Breitel also questioned whether the Grand Style was actually returning, noting that Llewellyn's evidence for this claim was thin.

**William Twining** (Karl Llewellyn and the Realist Movement, 1973) identified the book's fundamental tension: "a thesis of classic simplicity elaborated in a Gothic structure." Twining argued that Llewellyn never satisfactorily resolves the tension between his descriptive claims (judges have discretion) and his normative claims (the system is nonetheless predictable). The book's ambivalence, Twining suggested, reflects Llewellyn's own ambivalence about legal realism: he could not fully abandon the realist insight about discretion, but he desperately wanted to defend the coherence of the common law.

**Edwin W. Tucker** (Boston University Law Review, 1961) criticized the book for lacking theoretical rigor. He charged that Llewellyn's argument "proves too much" — if the steadying factors are as powerful as Llewellyn claims, then there should be little disagreement among judges, which is obviously false. Conversely, if the factors leave significant room for disagreement, then they do not solve the problem they were designed to address.

**Grant Gilmore**, Llewellyn's colleague at Chicago and himself a leading commercial law scholar, offered a more sympathetic but still critical assessment. Gilmore argued that The Common Law Tradition was ultimately a work of "legal romanticism" — an attempt to find order and meaning in a legal system that was, in fact, far more chaotic than Llewellyn wanted to admit. Gilmore's own The Death of Contract (1974) and The Ages of American Law (1977) can be read as responses to Llewellyn's optimism.

## Alternative Solutions

Llewellyn's account of judicial constraint competes with several alternative approaches. The **legal process school** (Hart and Sacks) located constraint in institutional roles and procedures, arguing that the "reasoned elaboration" of legal materials by courts operating within their proper institutional sphere produced principled decisions. Llewellyn was more skeptical about the power of reason alone to constrain; his emphasis was on craft and tradition rather than institutions.

**H.L.A. Hart** (The Concept of Law, 1961, published one year after Llewellyn's book) offered a more philosophical account of judicial discretion. Hart argued that legal rules have a "core of settled meaning" and a "penumbra of uncertainty"; judges exercise discretion in the penumbral cases, constrained by the shared standards of the legal community. Hart's analysis is cleaner and more systematic than Llewellyn's, but it lacks the concrete detail about how courts actually operate.

**Ronald Dworkin** (Taking Rights Seriously, 1977) argued that judges in hard cases do not have discretion but are bound by "principles" that exist alongside rules. Dworkin's account is both more ambitious than Llewellyn's — he claims judges have a duty to find the single right answer — and more demanding, requiring judges to construct elaborate theories of political morality to justify their decisions. Dworkin explicitly rejected Llewellyn's realism.

**Critical legal studies scholars** (Duncan Kennedy, Roberto Unger) argued in the 1970s and 1980s that law is fundamentally indeterminate and that Llewellyn's steadying factors were no more than "legitimating ideologies" that mask the political character of judicial decision-making. For the critical legal studies movement, Llewellyn's attempt to reconcile realism with legal constraint was a failure — an exercise in bad faith that preserved the illusion of law while politics raged underneath.

**Empirical political scientists** (the "judicial behavior" school) have tested Llewellyn's claims using statistical methods that he never employed. Scholars such as Glendon Schubert, David Rohde, and Harold Spaeth have argued that judicial decisions are best predicted by the judges' ideological attitudes, not by the legal factors Llewellyn emphasized. This research, which uses scaling techniques and regression analysis, has produced mixed results but generally suggests that ideology plays a larger role than Llewellyn's framework would predict.

## Practical Applications

The Common Law Tradition remains directly useful for practicing lawyers. Llewellyn's analysis of the steadying factors provides a framework for predicting how an appellate court will respond to a particular argument. His catalogue of sixty-four precedent techniques is a practical tool for crafting arguments about prior cases — a resource that every appellate advocate should know. His advice about oral argument and brief writing, while dated in some details, remains broadly sound.

For judges, the book offers a normative vision of the judicial role that emphasizes craft, honesty, and the transparent weighing of competing considerations. Llewellyn's Grand Style is an ideal that continues to inspire judges who want to be faithful to law without pretending that judging is mechanical.

For legal scholars, the book remains a rich source of hypotheses about judicial behavior that have not been fully tested. The steadying factors framework, the period-style thesis, and the analysis of precedent techniques all invite empirical investigation using modern methods. A contemporary scholar who could operationalize Llewellyn's concepts and test them against a large dataset of appellate decisions would make a significant contribution to the empirical study of judicial behavior.
