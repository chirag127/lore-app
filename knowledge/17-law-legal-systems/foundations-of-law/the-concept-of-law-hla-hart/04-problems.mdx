# Problems — The Concept of Law

## Key Problem

The core problem Hart addresses is the conceptual disarray that plagued legal philosophy in the mid-20th century: scholars disagreed not only about what law is but about what the question "What is law?" even means. Legal positivism, natural law theory, legal realism, and sociological approaches each offered structurally incompatible accounts, and there was no shared conceptual framework within which these disagreements could be fruitfully pursued. The problem was both philosophical — how to give a coherent account of legal obligation and legal validity — and practical: without a clear understanding of what law is, legal philosophy could not address pressing questions about the proper limits of state power, the role of courts in constitutional democracies, or the relationship between domestic and international law. Hart's thesis is that the philosophical confusion stemmed from a failure to attend carefully to the internal structure of legal systems: law is not a single kind of thing — not merely commands, not merely predictions, not merely norms backed by sanctions — but a complex system of rules of different kinds, each serving distinct social functions. Solving this conceptual problem required distinguishing and relating primary rules (duty-imposing) from secondary rules (power-conferring) and showing how their interaction generates a stable, dynamic social institution.

## Proposed Solution

Hart's solution is the rule-theoretic account outlined above. A legal system exists when (1) officials accept a shared rule of recognition identifying valid primary rules, (2) citizens generally comply with those primary rules, and (3) the combination of primary and secondary rules generates a stable, predictable order. Legal obligation is not explained by coercion alone but by the internal point of view — the fact that participants accept rules as standards for conduct. The rule of recognition provides the conceptual anchor: it is a social fact, not a moral truth or a fictional command, and it grounds the entire system while remaining itself ungrounded within the system. This solution addresses each of the three persistent questions: law differs from orders backed by threats because it includes power-conferring rules that enable as well as direct action; legal obligation differs from moral obligation in that legal obligation does not depend on the content of the rule being morally good — the separation thesis; and law is thoroughly a matter of rules, though rules have an open texture that leaves room for judicial discretion in hard cases.

## Evidence & Argumentation

Hart's argumentative evidence is philosophical and conceptual rather than empirical. His primary method is the counter-diagnosis against Austin: he shows that Austin's command theory generates false implications — that power-conferring rules are not commands, that the habit of obedience cannot explain legal continuity, that the sovereign is a fiction — and that correcting these failures leads naturally toward the primary/secondary rules framework. His second method is non-empirical sociology: he asks what a legal system of only primary rules would be like, what defects it would have, and how secondary rules remedy those defects. This argument is conducted at the level of concept rather than data: Hart does not offer statistical evidence about judicial behavior or historical evidence about the development of rule systems, but constructs a conceptual model that fits the paradigm cases of law that any empirically informed reader will recognize.

The argument's strengths include its conceptual coherence, its fit with ordinary legal practice ("the air of reality" that legal rules are systematically interdependent), and its capacity to generate testable predictions (a legal system can be evaluated by asking whether its officials accept a shared rule of recognition). Its weaknesses are precisely the absence of empirical grounding: the claim that all legal systems have a rule of recognition of the kind Hart describes is plausible but not demonstrable from the conceptual analysis alone, and legal historians have noted that real legal systems have historically operated with less formal rule-of-recognition structures than Hart's model implies.

## Unresolved Questions

Hart's framework leaves several fundamental questions open. First, the composition of the rule of recognition: in complex legal systems, especially those with constitutional bills of rights, the criteria for legal validity are themselves contested. What counts as law when the rule of recognition is indeterminate — when, for example, different judges accept different criteria for constitutional validity? Second, the problem of inclusion: Hart argues that the rule of recognition is accepted by officials, not citizens. But what happens when the practices of officials diverge radically from the expectations of citizens — when, in effect, the internal point of view of the official community is the only one that counts for legal validity? This question becomes especially pressing in authoritarian systems, where a narrow officialdom may accept a rule of recognition that is wholly illegitimate from the perspective of those subject to it. Third, the relationship between the social-fact character of the rule of recognition and its moral evaluation: Hart insists that a regime's rule of recognition can specify gravely unjust laws as valid; is this conceptual separation useful if, in practice, citizens will not regard such a system's law as binding? Fourth, international law: Hart treats it as a primitive legal system, but the decades since 1961 have seen the development of sophisticated international institutions (the WTO, the ICC, the European Court of Human Rights) that do not fit neatly into Hart's model of either fully developed municipal law or primitive customary law. Fifth, the relationship between Hart's theory and legal pluralism: if law is identified by a rule of recognition accepted by officials, what status do non-state legal orders — customary law, religious law, indigenous law, transnational commercial law — have within the conceptual framework?

## Criticisms

**Ronald Dworkin** — Hart's separation of law and morality is conceptually defective. The rule of recognition, in any real legal system that includes constitutional and common-law practice, must incorporate moral criteria. If constitutional due process requires courts to enforce substantive fairness, then moral principles are part of the criteria for legal validity; Hart's account of validity as purely social convention cannot capture this. Furthermore, Hart's account of judicial discretion in hard cases wrongly assumes that judges create new law; on Dworkin's interpretive theory, judges discover the moral principles that already structure the legal system and apply them.

**Lon Fuller** — Hart understates the moral content of legality. The "inner morality of law" — the eight principles of legality (generality, publicity, non-retroactivity, clarity, non-contradictoriness, constancy, feasibility of compliance, congruence between official action and declared rule) — is not merely a set of pragmatic requirements for efficacy but a constitutive condition of law. A system that fails these principles is not merely a bad legal system; it may fail to be a legal system at all. Hart's response — that a poisoner's recipe is also clear and systematic — misses the point. Fuller's principles apply to authority exercised over persons, not to instructions written into a recipe book. The point of generality and clarity in law is that persons subject to law should be able to understand and comply with it; this is not merely instrumentally useful but morally necessary for a system of governance to count as governance by law rather than by arbitrary will.

**Neil MacCormick** — Hart's account of how legal rules are applied in practice is too thin. Hart's focus on the structural logic of legal systems misses the reasoning process by which judges actually reach decisions in hard cases. The kind of argument Hart describes as judicial discretion is actually a form of rational practical reasoning — interpreting the purposes of the rule in light of its context — that has more in common with moral reasoning than Hart's model permits. To defend positivism adequately, one must supplement Hart's social-fact account of the rule of recognition with a richer account of legal reasoning.

**John Finnis** — Hart's descriptive methodology is incoherent. Describing law is not a value-neutral enterprise; it requires selecting among competing social phenomena what counts as law and what does not, and that selection is irreducibly normative. Hart's attempt to do "descriptive sociology" of law silently presupposes the very natural law tradition he wishes to exclude from the concept of law. Finnis argues that we can identify law as a distinctive social institution only by reference to its function in securing the common good — a function grounded in objective moral values.

**A.W. Brian Simpson** — Hart's account of the English legal system rests on a selective and partly inaccurate picture of how English law actually functions. The model of a unified rule of recognition identifying the sources of law is a rational reconstruction that does not reflect the complex, historically contingent, and often contradictory practices of the English legal system in the period when Hart was writing. This is a criticism of method rather than doctrine, but it has significant implications: if Hart's theory is descriptive, it must be judged against real legal systems, and if it is prescriptive, it needs to say so.

## Alternative Solutions

The alternatives to Hart's solution divide broadly along the lines of natural law theory, legal realism, and interpretive legal theory.

**Natural law theory (John Finnis, Robert George).** Natural law theorists argue that valid law must satisfy minimum moral requirements and that a system of norms that systematically violates human goods is not genuinely law. The advantage of this view is that it explains why radically unjust regimes — Nazi Germany, apartheid South Africa — provoke the thought that their laws were "not really law." The disadvantage, from Hart's perspective, is that it makes legal validity depend on moral truth in a way that seems to collapse law and morality into a single sphere, removing the critical distance that legal positivism makes available to the observer.

**Legal realism (Karl Llewellyn, Jerome Frank).** Legal realists reject both Hart's rule-based model and the natural lawyer's moral constraints on law. They argue that what courts actually do — their decisions, their reasoning practices, their institutional incentives — is what studies law should attend to, not the formal structure of legal rules. The realist advantage is empirical realism: judges do exercise discretion, and understanding those factors that influence judicial decisions is more important than mapping the conceptual structure of rules. The disadvantage is the loss of the normative dimension: if law is merely what judges do, there is no critical standpoint from which to say that some judges' decisions are wrong as a matter of law.

**Interpretivism (Ronald Dworkin).** Dworkin's interpretive theory is the most self-conscious alternative to Hart's positivism. On Dworkin's account, law is not a set of rules identified by social convention but a system of principles that justify the political practices of a community. The judge in a hard case does not create new law but discovers the moral principles that best justify the community's legal tradition as a whole. Dworkin's theory captures the sense that legal reasoning is a form of practical reasoning that has genuinely normative force; its difficulty is that it makes legal validity depend on philosophical argument about morality, which may be as contested as political argument itself.

## Practical Applications

Hart's theory has been applied, and continues to be applied, across an extraordinary range of legal and policy contexts. In constitutional theory, the rule of recognition serves as the primary tool for analyzing how constitutional law identifies valid rules and how constitutional change occurs through interpretive practice. The distinction between primary and secondary rules helps clarify the structure of administrative law: delegation of power from legislature to administrator is a process governed by secondary rules; the rules created by administrators are primary rules that affect citizens. In criminal law, the open texture argument underlies debates about statutory interpretation and the proper scope of judicial discretion: if the legislature cannot anticipate every case, judges in hard cases have a creative role that cannot simply be reduced to applying pre-existing rules. In international law, Hart's framework allows legal scholars to ask what sort of secondary rules — if any — international practice has generated, and what the prospects are for developing a more systematic secondary-rule structure in the international legal order. In legal education, Hart's concepts have structured the way legal theory is taught and have shaped how students of law understand the relationship between rules, institutions, and justice. Perhaps most broadly, Hart's insistence that law must be understood from the internal point of view has informed empirical studies of how judges, lawyers, and citizens actually reason about law — research that bridges jurisprudence, sociology of law, and psychology in ways that have significantly enriched our understanding of legal institutions.
