## 1. KEY PROBLEM ADDRESSED

The Constitution of India confronted a singular and historically unprecedented problem: **how to construct a democratic constitutional order in a society that was fundamentally undemocratic in its social structure**. India in 1947 was simultaneously a newly independent nation, a sprawling subcontinent encompassing extraordinary linguistic, religious, caste, ethnic, and regional diversity, and a society in which Brahminical hierarchy, untouchability, and feudal agrarian relations defined everyday life for 85% of the population. The problem was not merely constitutional design—it was the creation of a constitutional order capable of **transforming the society it governed**.

The framers' answer to this problem was to embed **social revolution** directly into the constitutional order—not as aspirational policy to be deferred to ordinary legislation, but as enforceable constitutional commitments.

## 2. PROPOSED SOLUTION

The framers' solution had three interlocking dimensions:

**Dimension 1 — Fundamental Rights as Legal Enforceability:** Rather than leaving rights as the domain of ordinary parliamentary majorities, the Constitution made civil and political rights **justiciable** through Article 32 (original jurisdiction of the Supreme Court). This ensured that even if majoritarian politics eroded rights, the judiciary could act as an independent guardian.

**Dimension 2 — Directive Principles as Democratic Mandate:** Part IV (Articles 36–51) committed future governments to specific redistributive outcomes: abolition of landlordism, equitable distribution of material resources, a living wage, free legal aid, and a Uniform Civil Code. Though non-justiciable, these principles provided a **normative standard** against which state action could be measured by Parliament, media, and civil society.

**Dimension 3 — Reservation and Affirmative Action:** Articles 15(4), 16(4), and Part XVI mandated reservation (preferential treatment) in legislatures, public employment, and educational institutions for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes. This was the Constitution's commitment that **formal equality was insufficient**—substantive equality required active state intervention to correct centuries of structural inequality.

## 3. EVIDENCE AND ARGUMENTATION

The framers marshalled the Constituent Assembly Debates (CAD) as their evidentiary basis: these debates reveal a sustained, deeply serious constitutional deliberation across almost three years. Notably, Ambedkar's own interventions in the CAD on **Fundamental Rights** (CAD Vol. VII, pp. 561–681) and **Directive Principles** (CAD Vol. IX) provide direct evidence of the framers' reasoning: he explicitly invoked the American, Irish, Soviet, and Weimar constitutional traditions in arguing for specific formulations.

The most dramatic evidence of the solution's operational success is the **reservation programme in legislatures**: Scheduled Castes hold approximately 84 reserved seats in the Lok Sabha (out of 543 total), proportionally representing around 15% of the population. Scheduled Tribes hold 47 reserved seats. The 73rd and 74th Amendments (1992) institutionalised reservation for women in local bodies, with one-third of seats reserved for women, creating what is arguably the world's most extensive experiment in **gender-decentralised democracy**.

However, evidence also reveals the solution's incompleteness. **Manual scavenging** (the employment of human beings to manually clean human excreta from dry latrines) remains legally and socially pervasive in India despite constitutional prohibition—a violation Ambedkar specifically targeted. The *Delhi Jal Board v. National Campaign for People's Right to Information* (2020) case reveals that constitutional prohibitions without concomitant material transformation have limited practical force. **Dalit land ownership** remains disproportionately low: the 2011 Census shows that SC households own, on average, less than 5% of agricultural land despite constitutional land reform directives.

## 4. UNRESOLVED QUESTIONS

The Constitution left, or has generated, several pressing questions that remain unresolved:

**What is the scope of "Secularism" as a justiciable constitutional value?** The Supreme Court first declared India a secular state in *S.R. Bommai v. Union of India* (1994), but its content remains contested. Does secularism require the State to positively oppose religious majoritarianism, or merely to refrain from favouring any religion? This question has become acutely political in the 2020s as religious polarisation sharply increases.

**What is the nature of the relationship between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles?** The *Minerva Mills* doctrine (1980) declared the balance between them a basic feature, but in practice, the State has increasingly preferred Directive Part IV goals over Part III rights—particularly denying economic and social rights the justiciability that would force redistribution.

**Can a permanent reservation system remain consistent with Article 14's equality guarantee?** The Supreme Court has placed a 50% ceiling on total reservations and mandated the "creamy layer" exclusion for OBC reservations, but the constitutional logic of continuing reservations indefinitely—even as caste inequality structurally transforms—remains legally contested.

**What is the constitutional status of gender minorities?** The Constitution's binary sex-based language (Articles 14, 15, 16) has been judicially read to protect transgender persons (*NALSA v. Union of India*, 2014; the Transgender Persons (Protection of Rights) Act, 2019), but full constitutional recognition of gender identity and sexual orientation remains contested.

**What constitutional mechanisms constrain Emergency powers?** Article 352's "armed rebellion" threshold (replacing the abolished "internal disturbance") is arguably sufficient for external aggression but remains malleable in practice. Lessons from the 1975 Emergency and from India's declining democracy rankings on Varieties of Democracy (V-Dem) indices since 2017 haunt this provision.

## 5. CRITICISMS

**The Elite-Constituent Assembly Problem:** Scholars including **Arthur W. Macmahon** (reviewing Austin in *Political Science Quarterly*, 1967) and **Marc Galanter** have noted that the Assembly was not directly elected but indirectly elected through provincial legislatures dominated by the Congress Party. Its composition was overwhelmingly male, upper-caste, educated in English, and politically aligned with the nationalist movement. Dalit representation, though symbolically important (Ambedkar himself), was numerically marginal: approximately 7 of the Assembly's 299 elected members were Dalits.

**The Emergency Infrastructure Problem:** Political scientist **Myron Weiner** (noted by Granville Austin) observed that the Constitution contains extensive executive discretionary powers that were specifically designed to enable strong governance during a fragile early republic—but those same powers (Articles 22 preventive detention, 356 President's Rule, 352 Emergency) could be, and were, mobilised for authoritarian purposes in 1975–77. **Upendra Baxi** characterises the constitution as having Janus-faced character—simultaneously enabling democratic governance and containing the tools of its own potential destruction.

**The Colonial-Continuity Problem:** British constitutional scholar **D.D. Karundekar** has argued that key features of imperial governance—ICS cadre continuity, preventive detention, and executive dominance—survived largely intact into the republican Constitution. The Constituent Assembly's decision to retain these structures as transitional measures rooted them permanently. **Anurag Bhaskar**, in *The Foresighted Ambedkar* (2024), revives this argument while arguing that the constitutional framers were more aware of these continuities than most scholarship acknowledges.

**The Social Document Thesis as Incomplete Transformation:** Scholar-gail-omvedt has argued that the Constitution's social provisions (reservations, abolition of untouchability) have produced **partial structural change without transforming the underlying patriarchal and casteist social order**. Spending enormous political energy defending reservation policy has distracted from broader structural reform in agrarian relations, educational infrastructure, and economic redistribution.

**Critique of the Basic Structure as Anti-Democratic:** Constitutional scholar **H.M. Seervai** initially criticised the *Kesavananda* doctrine (1973) for empowering unelected judges to override the amending authority of elected representatives—a fundamentally undemocratic move. While Seervai later accepted the doctrine, his critique remains a live tension between democratic majoritarianism and constitutional limitation that constitutional scholars continue to debate.

## 6. ALTERNATIVE SOLUTIONS

**Panchayat Raj vs. Representative Democracy:** Ambedkar himself warned in the Constituent Assembly that village-based governance in caste-divided rural India would perpetuate, not remedy, caste oppression. His preference was for **decentralised urban municipal democracy** combined with strong centralised redistribution. The 73rd Amendment (1992), which mandated panchayat decentralisation, partially followed this logic but in an unintended form: the one-third women's reservation was not what Ambedkar intended as the primary mechanism. **Gail Omvedt** argues the framers' distrust of village-level governance has been vindicated by persistent caste atrocities in panchayat-dominated rural areas.

**Uniform Civil Code vs. Pluralist Accommodation:** Article 44 included a Directive Principle mandating a Uniform Civil Code (UCC) to replace community-specific personal laws (Hindu, Muslim, Christian, Parsi). **Rajeev Bhargava** argued that a UCC arising from **cross-community dialogue** rather than majoritarian imposition would be appropriate; he and Muslim personal law reformer **Asghar Ali Engineer** proposed a **community-based reform** model rather than a top-down uniform code. The Supreme Court's *Sarla Mudgal v. Union of India* (1995) judgment directed Parliament to enact a UCC but successive governments have deferred. **Flavia Agnes**, legal scholar and women's rights activist, argues that women within minority communities are best served by reformed community law that respects their identities rather than a state-imposed uniform code.

**Keynesian Industrialisation vs. Agrarian Reform:** The Directive Principles (Articles 39, 41, 46) could have been operationalised through **land-to-the-tiller** policies along the lines of successful East Asian redistribution (Korea, Taiwan). Instead, India's land reform achieved modest redistribution (the upper limit ceiling varied from 7 acres in Kerala to 500 acres in erstwhile Hyderabad state) and stalled politically. **Pranab Bardhan**, UC Berkeley economist, has documented how upper-caste landlord dominance of state legislatures systematically blocked meaningful land reform, rendering constitutional land redistribution commitments largely unfulfilled.

## 7. PRACTICAL APPLICATIONS

**For Law Practitioners:**
- Article 32 writ petitions remain the most direct tool for enforcement of Fundamental Rights violations; constitutional lawyers continue developing constitutional doctrine through the Supreme Court's jurisdiction. The Basic Structure doctrine provides doctrinal tools for challenging even constitutional amendments that threaten core commitments.
- The Supreme Court's 2017 *Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India* (9-judge bench) decision, which declared privacy a Fundamental Right under Article 21, illustrates how strategic litigation can expand constitutional interpretation beyond original text.

**For Policy Makers:**
- Directive Principles (Part IV) require government programmes to be constitutionally evaluated—any law that disproportionately harms vulnerable groups should be tested against Articles 14, 15, and 21. **NITI Aayog** reports and the Comptroller and Auditor General's (CAG) audits increasingly use constitutional standards rather than merely administrative efficiency as evaluative criteria.

**For Citizens:**
- Article 19(1)(a) (freedom of speech) has been invoked through RTI (Right to Information) activism—the RTI Act 2005 is a derivative of Article 19(1)(a) and Article 21 (right to know). CitizenRTI applications in cases of governmental corruption or policy failures represent one of the most powerful practical applications of the Constitution by non-lawyers.
- Instructional data on reservations, particularly in educational institutions (Jawaharlal Nehru University, IITs, medical colleges), demonstrates how the reservation clauses (Articles 15, 16) have dramatically expanded access to previously closed professional classes for SC/ST/OBC candidates.
