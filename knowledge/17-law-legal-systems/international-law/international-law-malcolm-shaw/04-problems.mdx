# Problems & Analysis — International Law, Malcolm N. Shaw (8th ed., 2017)

---

## Key Problem

International law, as a system governing relations between sovereign states in an anarchic international system, faces a structural dilemma of enforcement and authority. The field lacks a central legislature, an executive capable of imposing compliance, and a judiciary with compulsory jurisdiction over all states. The problem Shaw's book addresses is therefore not a single problem but a family of interconnected difficulties: how is international law's legitimacy maintained in the absence of coercive enforcement? How are treaty and customary obligations made binding on sovereign states? How do individuals obtain meaningful legal protection when states are the primary subjects? And how can the international legal order evolve to address problems — climate change, mass atrocities, cyber warfare — that its founders in 1945 could not have anticipated?

Shaw addresses these problems comprehensively across 28 chapters, building the case that international law, despite its structural deficits, functions as a complete legal system whose authority is sustained by consent, reciprocity, legitimacy, and institutional practice.

---

## Proposed Solutions

### 1. The Sources Regime as a Foundation for Legitimate Authority

The first solution lies in the sources of international law. Shaw carefully explicates Article 38(1) of the ICJ Statute and the accepted practice that has developed around it: treaties and custom as the primary sources, general principles of law as a bridge between national legal systems, and judicial decisions and the teachings of publicists as subsidiary means. By rooting the law's authority in state consent — expressed through treaty signature and ratification, and through participation in the practice that crystallises customary rules — Shaw establishes the foundational legitimacy of the system. States are bound not because they have submitted to external authority but because they have, through their own conduct, created the rules that bind them.

The VCLT's codification of treaty law (Chapter 16) provides a self-executing framework for how treaties are made, interpreted, and terminated. The ILC's Draft Articles on State Responsibility (Chapter 14) provide a comparable framework for secondary rules governing breach and reparation. Together, these codifications transform what was once a set of amorphous customary practices into a structured, formally articulated legal system.

### 2. Institutional Architecture Providing Limited but Real Enforcement

Shaw describes the UN system — the International Court of Justice, the International Law Commission, the treaty body system under human rights conventions, Security Council Chapter VII enforcement, and regional courts — as providing the institutional mechanisms through which international law becomes practically effective. The ICJ's contentious jurisdiction, Shaw notes, is necessarily based on state consent; but consent once given is binding, and the ICJ has an established record of decisions (Nicaragua, Corfu Channel, the Nuclear Tests cases) that states have complied with, at least formally, even when they initially opposed the Court's jurisdiction.

The Security Council's Chapter VII enforcement powers, Shaw acknowledges, are heavily constrained by the P5 veto — but they are not illusory. The Council has authorised collective action in Korea (1950), the Gulf (1990–91), Libya (2011), and other situations; it has imposed targeted sanctions on individuals and entities; and it has referred situations to the ICC (Darfur, 2005; Libya, 2011). These mechanisms, however imperfectly, provide the enforcement content that pure positivists deny and pure natural lawyers assume.

### 3. The Expansion of International Legal Personality to Individuals

One of Shaw's most significant contributions as a synthesiser of the field has been tracing how the move to recognise individuals as direct subjects of international law — bearers of rights, obligations, and international criminal responsibility — constitutes a structural transformation of the international legal order. By integrating across human rights law, international humanitarian law, and international criminal law (Chapters 9, 23, 24), Shaw shows how the Nuremberg Principles, the Geneva Conventions and their Additional Protocols, the Genocide Convention, the Torture Convention, and the Rome Statute have together erected an edifice of individual accountability that has no precedent in earlier periods of international law.

This expansion partially solves the problem of enforcement that arises from the state-centric structure of international law: even where states are unable or unwilling to hold other states to account, individuals committing the most serious violations of international law can now be prosecuted by international courts or by domestic courts exercising universal jurisdiction.

### 4. The Doctrine of *Jus Cogens* as a Structural Constraint

Shaw treats the category of *jus cogens* — peremptory norms from which no derogation is permitted — as the most powerful structural check within the positivist framework on state freedom to contract. A treaty purporting to permit slavery, genocide, or torture is void under Article 53 of the VCLT, regardless of the quality of state consent. This provides, however imperfectly, a minimal moral floor below which international law cannot sink — and it was this floor that the International Court of Justice invoked in the *Barcelona Traction* dictum (1970) when it distinguished between obligations owed to particular states and obligations of *erga omnes* character owed to the international community as a whole.

Shaw's careful treatment of *jus cogens*, including the considerable controversy about how norms qualify for the category and whether any rules beyond the core prohibitions on aggression, genocide, slavery, and torture have achieved that status, reflects his talent for presenting doctrinal consensus while flagging genuine uncertainty.

## Evidence and Argumentation

Shaw marshals four principal forms of evidence in support of his assertions about the state of international law.

**Primary legal materials**: ICJ judgments (contentious cases and advisory opinions), ICTY and ICTR trial and appellate judgments, arbitral awards (including investment tribunal awards), and Security Council resolutions serve as the principal source material for doctrinal propositions. Shaw cites cases with precision — case name, court, year, paragraph references — and is careful to distinguish between *ratio decidendi* and *obiter dicta*. His treatment of the ICJ's *Nicaragua v. United States* judgment illustrates this: he quotes the Court's careful articulation of the requirement that *opinio juris* may be established through "a very widespread and representative participation" in multilateral conventions, then applies the ICJ's methodology to the question of whether the prohibition on the use of force has acquired customary status independently of the Charter.

**Treaty texts**: Shaw quotes treaty provisions directly and at length, allowing the reader to assess textual meaning rather than relying solely on his paraphrase. This practice is particularly important in the chapters on the law of treaties (Chapter 16), where textual interpretation is the central legal technique, and in the chapters on human rights law, where treaty text and supervisory body practice must be read together.

**State practice**: Shaw's treatment of custom draws extensively on state conduct — diplomatic practice, treaty participation, military manuals, official statements, and domestic court decisions. His citation practice across footnotes reveals a formidable knowledge of state practice in areas ranging from law of the sea to the law of treaties.

**Doctrine and commentary**: Shaw's footnotes comprehensively cite the secondary legal literature across English, French, German, and Spanish. He references English-language monographs (Brownlie, Lauterpacht, Oppenheim/L Jennings, Oppenheim/Lauterpacht/Shearer), law journal articles across the full range of international legal scholarship, and ILC reports and commentary. This is what makes the book simultaneously a textbook and a research roadmap.

## Unresolved Questions

Several fundamental problems of international law that Shaw raises remain live, contested, and largely unresolved as of the 8th edition, reflecting realities that have only intensified since 2017.

**The legality of humanitarian intervention**: The Libyan intervention (2011) — authorised by the Security Council under Chapter VII for civilian protection purposes — provided a partial answer to the question of whether forcible intervention for humanitarian purposes can be lawfully conducted with Council authorisation. But Syria (from 2011) and other situations where P5 members have exercised their veto to block action leave precisely the problem Shaw addresses unresolved: where the Council is deadlocked, does international law have anything to say about unilateral or coalition intervention? The Responsibility to Protect doctrine, adopted in 2005, established a commitment but not a legal entitlement; the gap between moral commitment and legal authority remains the field's most politically urgent unresolved question.

**The fragmentation and coherence of international law**: The ILC Study Group on Fragmentation of International Law (2006), led by Martti Koskenniemi, produced a detailed analysis of how the proliferation of specialised legal regimes creates normative conflict. Shaw acknowledges but does not fully resolve the questions the Study Group raised. The continuing expansion of investment treaty arbitration (with the Energy Charter Treaty cases, the Philip Morris arbitration, and the investment chapters of CPTPP and CETA all generating novel disputes) has intensified concerns about whether the different branches of international economic law are compatible with human rights law and international environmental law.

**Climate change obligations**: The Paris Agreement's nationally determined contributions (NDCs) are not legally binding obligations of result, but the procedural requirements of the Agreement — reporting, review, updating — are legally binding. The question of whether the Paris Agreement, read with the UNFCCC, creates legal obligations that are justiciable before domestic or international courts remains open. Shaw raises but does not resolve this question; the subsequent ICJ advisory opinion request on climate obligations (2023) may partially answer it in the years following his most recent edition.

**Cyber operations as use of force**: International law applies to cyber operations, but the threshold at which a cyber operation constitutes an illegal use of force under Article 2(4) of the UN Charter — rather than a lawful countermeasure or a non-violent-but-harmful operation below the force threshold — has not been authoritatively settled. The Tallinn Manual 2.0 (2017), published the same year as Shaw's 8th edition, provides expert analysis but no binding authority. This is perhaps the single most consequential unresolved boundary question in contemporary international law, and one directly addressed by the ICJ in its treatment of what counts as "force" in the *Nicaragua* case.

**The future of the ICC and universal jurisdiction**: The ICC has survived significant political challenges since 2017, but its reach over situations involving major powers or their allies remains contested. The principle of universal jurisdiction — the capacity of any state to prosecute certain international crimes regardless of the territorial or national nexus — has produced some landmark domestic proceedings (the *Pinochet* case in the UK, the indictment of Chad's former president Hissène Habré in Senegal, proceedings in Germany against Syrian officials) but has also provoked political pushback from African states who see it as selectively applied and neocolonial in practice. Shaw documents both the progress and the resistance without resolving their dialectic.

## Criticisms

Shaw's textbook has been subjected to sustained, substantive, and intellectually diverse criticism that cannot be dismissed as pedantry — it touches on fundamental questions about what international law is, what it should do, and how best to teach it.

**Martti Koskenniemi's "descriptive positivism" critique**: As outlined in the Analysis section, Koskenniemi argues that the doctrinal-descriptive mode adopted by Shaw and other mainstream textbook writers — presenting international law as a coherent body of rules discoverable through neutral legal methods — is itself a rhetorical move that mystifies the interpretive choices and political presuppositions embedded in the act of description. For Koskenniemi, the neat separation between explication and judgement, which Shaw's textbook maintains, is not a methodological virtue but a form of what he calls "managerialism": treating the international legal system as if its problems could be solved by careful application of existing doctrines, without acknowledging that the system's character is itself the problem.

**The TWAIL critique of statecentrism**: Scholars including **Balakrishnan Rajagopal**, **Makau wa Mutua**, and **Antony Anghie** have argued that Shaw's treatment of statehood, sovereignty, and the Westphalian system in Chapters 4 and 5 takes the historical categories of the positivist tradition as neutral analytical starting points, without interrogating how those categories were formed through colonial conquest, racial hierarchisation, and the active exclusion of non-European peoples from the original community of sovereign states. The concept of *terra nullius*, which Shaw treats as a lawful (if now obsolete) mode of acquiring territory, was applied specifically to land inhabited by indigenous peoples in Australia, North America, and other settler-colonial contexts — lands that were never *actually* terra nullius, but which were treated as such by the colonising powers. For TWAIL scholars, a textbook that describes *terra nullius* as a mode of acquisition without foregrounding its colonial history participates in a continuing form of legal mystification.

**Philip Allott's critique of the descriptive project**: Allott argues in *Eunomia* that international law, like all law, is not merely regulatory but constitutive: it shapes the social world it purports merely to reflect. A textbook devoted primarily to describing the rules that currently bind states, in Allott's view, institutionalises precisely the limited, fragmented, state-centric world international law has produced — a world in which structural inequality, poverty, and war remain lawfully possible. Allott's engagement is not directly with Shaw in particular but with the entire positivist-expository tradition; Shaw represents a conservative but intellectually honest version of that tradition.

**The fragmentation critique**: As Jan Klabbers and the ILC Study Group have documented, the proliferation of international legal regimes has generated genuine normative conflict. Treaties in one regime (trade, investment) sometimes produce obligations incompatible with obligations in another (human rights, environmental protection), and there is no hierarchical mechanism for resolving the conflict. Shaw's textbook presents the major sub-fields as complementary parts of a coherent whole, but readers may finish the book with a vision of international law as more unified than it actually is. The 2018 and 2022 framework on investment tribunal reform — the EU's termination of intra-EU BITs, the UNCITRAL Working Group III process, and the Multilateral Investment Court proposal — illustrate the scale of the practical incoherence that Shaw's text does not fully address.

**The absence of economic analysis**: David Riesman and other legal economists have noted that Shaw's account is essentially doctrinal rather than economic, in the sense that it documents what rules states have adopted but declines to address why they have adopted them, what incentives structure compliance and violation, and what institutional designs tend to produce higher compliance. This matters particularly in the investment arbitration and WTO chapters, where the economic logic of the regime is part of its normative justification — and also the principal ground on which critics attack its substantive fairness.

**Conservative treatment of emerging questions**: Some readers have noted that Shaw's treatment of cyber law, autonomous weapons, climate litigation, and emerging technologies is generally limited to noting that the law is uncertain — a technically accurate position but one that represents a missed opportunity to suggest how existing doctrines might be extended to new contexts. Given Shaw's immense knowledge of the field, this restraint is deliberate rather than inadvertent; it reflects his scepticism about theoretical speculation divorced from binding authority, but it leaves students wanting more guidance in newly developing areas.

## Alternative Solutions

### Natural Law Approaches

A distinct tradition of international legal thought — associated with the New Haven School (Myres McDougal and Michael Reisman at Yale Law School) and with figures from Lauterpacht to Hersch Lauterpacht Jr. — argues that international law's authority derives not from state consent but from fundamental principles of justice that exist independently of, and sometimes in contradiction with, state will. These approaches would locate the solution to the enforcement problem in the institutionalisation of these principles — through the ICJ, human rights courts, and other supervisory bodies empowered to enforce rights irrespective of state opposition. The limitation of natural law approaches, from a positivist perspective, is that they must concede that they have no binding legal authority when states explicitly reject the norms being advocated, making them more useful as moral or political arguments than as legal arguments.

### TWAIL Reconstruction

TWAIL scholars propose not merely critiquing the colonial origins of international law but reconstructing it from the perspective of those it has historically marginalised — peoples rather than states, economic and social rights alongside civil and political rights, and structural obligations on wealthy states to address the global economic inequality that shapes the capacity of poorer states to participate in the international legal system. Makau wa Mutua proposes a "normative reconstitution" of international law that centres human flourishing rather than state interests, while **Rajagopal** argues for centring transnational social movements and bottom-up norm creation. The practical objection to TWAIL reconstruction is that the institutions through which it would be implemented — the UN General Assembly, treaty bodies, the ILC — remain dominated by state interests, and that genuinely transformative change requires political power that international law alone cannot generate.

### Critical Legal Studies

Koskenniemi's *From Apology to Utopia* proposes a different response: rather than seeking to strengthen international law's authority through doctrinal elaboration or institutional reform, CLS scholars argue for greater reflexivity about the field's inherent contradictions — between sovereignty and community, between consent and obligation, between universality and particularity — and for using the law's own indeterminacies creatively in service of progressive causes. For Koskenniemi, international law can never be a stable framework because of the contradictory political demands placed upon it; it must rather be understood as a site of ongoing political struggle in which the law's internal contradictions can be mobilised to challenge the status quo.

### Rational Choice Institutionalism

Scholars including **Kenneth Abbott** and **Duncan Snidal** propose applying rational choice methodology — analysing state behaviour as the product of cost-benefit calculation under institutional constraint — to improve the design of international regimes. Their proposal, broadly, is that better-designed institutions (increasing monitoring, reducing verification costs, making compliance more attractive relative to violation) can achieve higher compliance rates without requiring genuine commitment to international law as such. This approach is influential in the international environment and trade literature and provides the intellectual underpinning for much contemporary work on institutional design, but it shares with CLS an underlying scepticism about how far formal legal rules can reshape state behaviour absent deeper institutional change.

## Practical Applications

Shaw's *International Law* is not a self-help manual, and its practical applications are understood in the professional rather than the personal sense. But the book is enormously practical in that sense.

**For law students**: Shaw's systematic exposition of doctrine, combined with the extensive case citations, provides the doctrinal infrastructure for examination success in almost any common law jurisdiction. The chapters on sources of law (Chapter 3), state responsibility (Chapter 14), law of treaties (Chapter 16), use of force (Chapter 21), and the ICJ (Chapter 19) are especially high-yield.

**For practitioners**: Shaw's footnotes contain virtually every significant case and treaty provision relevant to any question of public international law. A practitioner drafting treaty arguments, preparing an ICJ or arbitral submission, or advising a government on jurisdictional questions will find the book a starting reference that reduces the need for extensive independent research — provided the practitioner is aware of developments post-2017.

**For diplomats and foreign ministry lawyers**: The chapters on treaty law, state succession, territory, immunities, and the law of the sea are essential practical references for conducting bilateral negotiations: the Vienna Convention rules on reservations, the maritime delimitation principles, and the state succession framework are all routinely applied in bilateral relations and multilateral negotiations.

**For judges and arbitrators**: Judicial citations of Shaw across ICJ, ICTY, ICTR, and arbitral decisions demonstrate that the book provides an accepted authoritative baseline for articulating the state of international law. Domestic supreme and constitutional courts in common law jurisdictions regularly cite Shaw when applying customary international law and unincorporated treaties in domestic proceedings.

**For NGOs and human rights advocates**: Shaw's treatment of international human rights law (Chapters 7 and 8) — particularly the treaty body system, the European Convention, the Inter-American system, and the African system — provides NGOs with the doctrinal framework for interpreting treaty obligations and holding governments to account before regional human rights courts and treaty bodies.

**For the interdisciplinary scholar**: Shaw's book is an ideal entry point to the field for political scientists, economists, and historians seeking a comprehensive and technically accurate account of public international law's doctrine, institutions, and history. The diplomatic history integrating the Westphalian system, decolonisation, and the Cold War provides material and interpretive context for understanding international law as a historical project, not merely a set of rules.

In everyday professional practice, *International Law* occupies the shelf position that *Oxford English Dictionary* occupies for language: the authoritative reference consulted before any more specialised research begins. This is its primary practical application: not as application in the sense of technique or method, but as the indispensable foundation on which all professional work in the field is built.
