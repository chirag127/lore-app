# Problems — The Justice Game

---

## Key Problem

The core problem Robertson addresses is the systematic and recurrent gap between the formal ideals of English law — equality before the law, the presumption of innocence, the independence of the judiciary — and the actual operation of the criminal and civil justice systems in practice. He documents this gap through a series of connected mechanisms: judicial bias that is structurally oriented toward power and authority rather than toward truth; executive secrecy doctrines that allow governments to suppress evidence of their own misconduct; obscenity law applied inconsistently in ways that systematically disadvantage political, sexual, and cultural minorities; Legal Aid erosion that removes the material precondition for adversarial equality; and the absence of a domestic human rights framework that would give victims of institutional injustice a legally enforceable basis for claiming violations. Each of these mechanisms has produced documented miscarriages of justice in the cases Robertson describes: three men imprisoned for years before the Matrix Churchill prosecution collapsed; *Gay News* editor Denis Lemon convicted and given a suspended prison sentence for publishing a poem; Oz magazine editors convicted on the basis of a schoolgirl's contribution to a countercultural publication; Caribbean defendants executed after British judges made statements used to justify those executions; and countless defendants convicted under the Official Secrets Act on the basis of evidence they were never permitted to examine. The problem is not merely specific injustices but the institutional conditions that reliably reproduce those injustices as features of the normal operation of the system.

---

## Proposed Solution

Robertson's solution is twofold and partially realized. First, and most directly actionable, is the incorporation of the European Convention on Human Rights into domestic English law — achieved in the Human Rights Act 1998, which received Royal Assent on the day *The Justice Game* was published. Incorporation would, Robertson argued, give English courts the obligation to scrutinize government secrecy, judicial deference to the Crown, and legislative restrictions on free expression against a framework of enforceable rights rather than leaving those questions to the discretion of Parliament and the executive. Second, and more broadly, Robertson's solution is cultural and professional rather than merely legal: he argues that advocates must play the justice game rigorously rather than deferentially, that they must treat the jury as a democratic institution capable of reaching independent judgments, that they must press for disclosure of all material evidence rather than accepting the formal symmetry of the adversarial process, and that the profession must invest political energy in defending Legal Aid as the material foundation of equal access to justice. In combination, these reform proposals address both the institutional structures and the professional culture that sustain the gap between law and justice.

---

## Evidence & Argumentation

Robertson's argumentative method is case-based and documentary. In each chapter, he builds from the proposition that a specific documented injustice — a conviction overturned, a trial held in camera, an execution carried out — provides evidence of a systemic failure. The Matrix Churchill chapter is the most explicitly evidentiary: Robertson demonstrates from official disclosed documents, memoranda, and contemporaneous accounts that specific ministers knowingly suppressed evidence that would have exonerated the defendants. The selectivity of his cases — he chooses the most dramatic, most politically consequential, and most well-documented — strengthens the evidentiary base of his argument; the weakness is that cases are not randomly selected, and readers cannot infer prevalence from the cases he presents. Some critics have noted this limitation: as David Marr observed, the memoir form by its nature selects victories and omits failures, and the result is an account that documents the system's worst failures but does not demonstrate their frequency. Robertson's implicit response is that a miscarriage of justice is a miscarriage of justice regardless of its frequency, and that a legal system that produces documented cases of executive perjury, judicial bias, and government-manufactured secrecy cannot claim legitimacy on the basis that most cases proceed without them.

---

## Unresolved Questions

Robertson's analysis leaves at least four fundamental questions open. First, the scope of judicial power under an incorporated human rights regime. Robertson campaigned for incorporation on the basis that Strasbourg would provide external accountability, but he acknowledged from the outset that Strasbourg is a court of last resort with its own tendencies toward European-state deference. The question of whether incorporation would produce a genuinely effective check on judicial or executive abuse — or merely additional appellate delay — remained open in 1998 and has proved live in practice. Second, the future of Legal Aid. Robertson identified declining Legal Aid as the most direct mechanism for destroying adversarial equality, but the reforms required to universalize and adequately fund Legal Aid are politically expensive and have proved electorally unrewarding in Britain. Third, the age of secrecy doctrines. Robertson's critique of public interest immunity anticipated the post-9/11 expansion of closed material procedures and the Special Immigration Appeals Commission; the question raised in the book's later chapters — how to balance genuine national security needs against the danger of executive abuse — was answered, in the bleak direction Robertson feared, by subsequent legislation. Fourth, the relationship between free speech rights of the vulnerable and feminist and anti-racist critiques of pornography. As Julian Petley observed, Robertson's free-speech absolutism in the obscenity chapters did not engage with the genuinely difficult question of how to protect the rights of cultural minorities while protecting the expressive rights of publishers and performers who produce sexually explicit and racially charged material.

## Criticisms

**David Marr — On Representativeness and Genre.** David Marr's *Australian Book Review* review (June 1998) raised the most fundamental objection to Robertson's method: the memoir form selects victories and omits the failures, the compromises, and the cases where the formal system did in some sense work. Marr's point is not that Robertson fabricated specific events but that the structure of legal memoir — thrill to the winning case, skip the losing case — systematically skews the evidence base in ways that make the structural critique more pointed than the actual record supports.

**Julian Petley — Free Speech Absolutism.** Professor Julian Petley's critique of Robertson's obscenity chapters is that free speech, taken as an absolute principle, can conflict with the equality rights of groups who are the targets of hate speech. Petley argues that the Left-liberal consensus of the 1970s — within which the Oz and Gay News prosecutions were clearly abuses — has fractured in contemporary debates about pornography, hate speech, and the regulation of online platforms, and that Robertson's account does not adequately acknowledge this complexity.

**Conor Gearty — On the Limits of Legal Formalism.** Conor Gearty's critique of incorporation, developed more fully in his subsequent writing, is that the Human Rights Act would primarily protect wealthy and corporate litigants with the resources to bring Strasbourg-style claims — not the ordinary Legal Aid clients Robertson claims to represent. Gearty argues that formal rights are incomplete without material guarantees, and that incorporation without accompanying increases in Legal Aid funding and institutional capacity would be a rhetorical rather than a substantive achievement.

**John Mortimer — On Judicial Characterization.** John Mortimer's critique of Robertson's judicial portraits is that they caricature a diverse and internally divided institution. While acknowledging that individual judges were biased in the cases Robertson describes, Mortimer argues that Robertson's method of reading the bench as a single class — the "inner chamber" — elides the important differences between progressive and reactionary judges, between those who genuinely seek to apply the law impartially and those who use judicial office to advance conservative political projects.

## Alternative Solutions

**Legal structural reform (John Griffiths).** Legal sociologist John Griffiths argued in the 1980s that the solution to miscarriages of justice in England lay not in better advocates or human rights legislation but in fundamental reform of the investigative process — particularly the structure of police interrogation and the rules governing plea bargaining. Griffiths' argument was that injustices arise mostly at the investigative stage, before trial, and that changes to courtroom procedure have limited effect if the evidence placed before the court is unreliable. This direction, pursued through the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984 and subsequent codes of practice, addressed some of the systemic conditions Robertson documents without fully resolving the adversarial dynamics he critiques.

**Prosecutorial accountability (Antony Duff).** Philosopher R.A. Duff has argued that the proper focus of criminal justice reform is not the advocate's performance but the prosecutor's duty: the Crown's obligation to present the full evidence, including exculpatory material, and to treat the criminal trial as a search for truth rather than a partisan contest. Duff's argument suggests that Robertson's emphasis on adversarial advocacy, while practically necessary in the cases he describes, may reinforce the partisan structure that produces injustice rather than challenging it at its institutional origin.

**Abolition of archaic offences (campaign in progress).** The campaigns that Robertson joined — for the abolition of the blasphemy laws (finally achieved in the Criminal Justice and Immigration Act 2008), for decriminalization of consensual sexual conduct, for repeal of the seditious libel offence — pursued reform through parliamentary legislation rather than judicial or constitutional mechanism. These campaigns have achieved specific victories, and Robertson's documentary case studies contributed directly to public support for them. The strategy's limitation is that new archaic offences can emerge to replace those abolished, as the history of obscenity prosecutions against gay publishers, rap artists, and protest movements demonstrates.

## Practical Applications

Robertson's analysis has had concrete and documented applications across multiple areas of legal practice. First, in advocacy training: criminal and public-law barristers routinely study the Matrix Churchill cross-examination technique — how to use disclosure failures, documentary inconsistency, and judicial intervention to demonstrate official dishonesty — as a model for disclosure applications. Second, in human rights litigation: Robertson's account of Strasbourg surveillance influenced the drafting of arguments in the *Grand Chamber* cases that established proportionality as a test for privacy interferences in post-H.R.A. Britain. Third, in defamation and media law: Robertson's account of the *Guardian* Reynolds defence informed subsequent press freedom litigation, particularly the development of responsible-journalism standards by the House of Lords and the Supreme Court. Fourth, in death-penalty advocacy: Robertson's documented evidence of British judges' pro-execution statements in extradition proceedings was used by human rights organizations — particularly Amnesty International and JUSTICE — in campaigns for abolition of the death penalty in Caribbean Commonwealth states, contributing to the de facto abolition in several jurisdictions and the formal abolition of the mandatory death sentence in the Bahamas, Belize, and other states. Fifth, in parliamentary and public accountability: Robertson's analysis of Matrix Churchill informed the Scott Inquiry's own methodology and contributed to public support for reforming public interest immunity doctrine, leading to more rigorous judicial scrutiny of executive claims of secrecy in criminal proceedings. Sixth, as a reform document: the book is routinely cited in legal education and law reform materials as the most accessible and reliable popular account of the English criminal justice system's structural problems, and it has shaped public understanding in ways that continue to affect the politics of legal reform in Britain, Australia, and other Commonwealth jurisdictions.
