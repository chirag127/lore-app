_Homo Deus: A Brief History of Tomorrow_ by Yuval Noah Harari asks the most
ambitious question a historian can ask: where is the human species heading?

The book opens with a startling claim. For most of history, humanity lived at
the mercy of three great enemies: famine, plague, and war. They were not
problems to be solved — they were forces of nature, woven into the fabric of
existence. People prayed for deliverance from them because they could not
reasonably expect to defeat them. And then, in the space of a few centuries,
something unprecedented happened. We defeated them. Not perfectly, not
everywhere, but to a degree that would be unrecognisable to any previous
generation.

Today, more people die from obesity than from starvation. More people die from
old age than from infectious disease. More people die from suicide than from
violent conflict. The three horsemen have not vanished, but they have been
downgraded from cosmic forces to technical problems. This is not optimism —
it is a statistical fact that Harari documents with care.

But this success creates a new problem. For seventy thousand years, humanity's
agenda was set by survival. Now that survival is largely assured for most of
the world's people, what do we do with ourselves? Harari's answer is at once
thrilling and terrifying. The new human agenda consists of three goals:
immortality, happiness, and divinity.

Immortality becomes a technical problem. Aging is a biological process.
Biological processes can be understood, intervened in, and potentially
reversed. The question is not whether humans will significantly extend the
human lifespan, Harari argues — it is when, and at what cost, and who gets
access.

Happiness becomes a biochemical problem. Science is learning to manipulate
the neurochemical systems that generate our feelings. In the future, we may
not just treat depression — we may engineer contentment as a baseline state.
But this raises a question that Harari presses hard: if happiness is just
chemistry, what makes a happy life _meaningful_? What happens to the
authenticity of human experience when it is designed rather than lived?

Divinity is the ultimate goal: upgrading _Homo sapiens_ into _Homo deus_ — a
god-like being with powers of creation and self-transformation. This is not
science fiction. It is the implicit goal of the most ambitious research
programmes in biotechnology, artificial intelligence, and brain-computer
interfaces that exist today.

But the path to divinity runs through humanism — and this is where the book
makes its deepest argument. Harari contends that humanism is not a secular
alternative to religion. It is a religion, the dominant religion of the modern
world. It places human experience and individual feeling at the centre of all
value and authority. Liberal humanism believes in the inner voice of each
individual. Socialist humanism believes in the collective welfare of human
society. Evolutionary humanism believes in advancing the human species,
whatever the cost. All three are forms of human-worship that replaced
God-worship with a new centre of meaning.

And all three are under threat from the very science that humanism nurtured.
Neuroscience shows that the inner voice is not a soul but a pattern of firing
neurons — easily manipulated and far from free. AI shows that non-conscious
algorithms can make better decisions than conscious humans. Biotechnology
shows that the boundaries of the human are not fixed. The humanist framework
that justifies democracy, human rights, and individual dignity is being
undermined by the intellectual offspring of the humanist project itself.

These are Harari's most discussed predictions. The first: the "useless class."
Previous technological revolutions destroyed old jobs and created new ones.
The Industrial Revolution turned farmers into factory workers. The information
revolution turned factory workers into service workers. But AI might not
create any new jobs. If algorithms can outperform humans at every cognitive
and physical task, there is no logical reason to hire humans. The result is a
new class of people who are not just unemployed but unemployable — without
economic value, and therefore without political power.

The second prediction follows: a biological divergence. The elite — who can
afford genetic enhancement, brain-computer interfaces, and life extension —
will upgrade themselves into a new species. They will become _Homo deus_ while
the masses remain _Homo sapiens_, creating a biological gap as wide as the gap
between humans and chimpanzees.

The third: the rise of Dataism, a new religion that treats the universe as a
system of data flows. Its highest value is not human welfare or divine
purpose but the free and maximally efficient processing of information.
Privacy becomes a bug. Individual autonomy becomes an obstacle. The "Internet
of All Things" — a planetary network of sensors, processors, and algorithms —
becomes the supreme entity. Humans are not the users or the masters of this
system. They are components within it, and replaceable components at that.

But Harari does not write as a doom-monger. His tone throughout is that of a
historian tracing patterns rather than a prophet issuing warnings. He
emphasises that these are possibilities, not predictions. The future remains
malleable. The decisions made today will shape what emerges tomorrow.

The book closes with a question that lingers long after the final page. "What
will happen to society, politics and daily life when non-conscious but highly
intelligent algorithms know us better than we know ourselves?" Harari does not
pretend to have the answer. But he insists that the question cannot be avoided
— because the technologies that make it possible are already here.
