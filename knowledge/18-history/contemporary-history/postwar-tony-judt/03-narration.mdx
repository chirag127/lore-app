---
title: Narration
---

Tony Judt's Postwar is one of those rare books that changes the way you understand the world you inhabit. It is a nine-hundred-page single-volume history of Europe from the rubble of nineteen forty-five to the uneasy present of the early twenty-first century. And it is, above all, a book about how a continent that destroyed itself twice in thirty years tried, and partially succeeded, in building something worth having from the ruins of its own history.

The book begins not with celebration but with devastation. In nineteen forty-five, Europe lay in physical and moral ruin. Thirty million people were dead. The industrial infrastructure of a continent was obliterated. The Holocaust had destroyed Jewish civilization in Europe in its entirety. And the old political order had been comprehensively discredited — some justly, some not, but all of it equally incapable of offering a credible future. Judt's opening insight, which he never abandons, is that everything that follows in European history must be understood as a response to this catastrophe. Europe's postwar story is not a triumphalist narrative of recovery and progress. It is a story of improvisation, moral compromise, forgetting, and the stubborn, uneven construction of something resembling a shared European project from the wreckage of the old order.

The first phase, from nineteen forty-five through the early nineteen fifties, is defined by division and the beginning of reconstruction. The Marshall Plan provided thirteen billion dollars in American aid to sixteen European nations between nineteen forty-seven and nineteen fifty-two. Judt treats this not as an act of generosity but as a strategically designed containment program. The money came with conditions: European nations had to cooperate in supranational planning mechanisms, open their markets to each other, and align with Washington in the emerging Cold War confrontation. The Soviet Union and its satellite states were formally invited to participate but refused on Stalin's orders. That refusal — that single decision — formalized the division of a continent into two armed camps, a division that would persist for almost half a century.

In the West, the reconstruction was remarkably rapid. West Germany's currency reform of nineteen forty-eight — the introduction of the Deutschmark — ended the black-market chaos and restored economic confidence. Ludwig Erhard's social market economy combined free-market competition with a social safety net, creating what became known as the Wirtschaftswunder, the economic miracle. But the miracle was not magic. It rested on imported labor from southern Europe and Turkey, on suppressed consumption and high saving rates, and on the unresolved moral question of German responsibility for the Nazi past. Across Western Europe, from Britain's Beveridge Report to France's Sécurité Sociale to the Scandinavian comprehensive welfare model, governments built systems of national insurance, universal health care, public education, and unemployment protection. For roughly three decades, from nineteen forty-five to the oil shocks of the early nineteen seventies, Europe experienced what the French called the Trente Glorieuses — the thirty glorious years of full employment and rising living standards. This period was not inevitable. It was made possible by a specific historical conjuncture: a destroyed industrial base needing reconstruction, American capital inflow, cheap energy, favorable demographics, and a working class whose political loyalty had been purchased by wartime sacrifice and the promise of a better future. When those conditions eroded in the nineteen seventies, the consensus could not survive.

In the East, the story was very different. Stalinist totalitarianism was not a temporary deviation from a socialist path — it was the system itself. Show trials, mass deportations, political purges, and the systematic elimination of civil society were features of the regime, not bugs. Judt is particularly careful to document the depth of resistance that survived: the Polish Catholic Church as an alternative institutional center, the Hungarian Writers' Union, Czech intellectual networks that kept alive a culture of dissent even under the most repressive conditions. The collapse of nineteen eighty-nine, when it came, was not inevitable. It was the product of contingent choices — Gorbachev's decision not to use Soviet military force to preserve the Eastern European regimes, the decision of communist party elites to negotiate rather than fight, the courage of ordinary citizens who simply walked across borders that had been closed for decades.

November ninth, nineteen eighty-nine, when the Berlin Wall fell, is the hinge event of Judt's narrative. He reconstructs it not as triumph but as improvisation. The wall came down because East German citizens simply walked out, because the Hungarian leadership opened the border, because no one had the will to stop them. German unification followed — not as a pre-planned project but as a historical accident that required enormous political courage to manage. And in the vacuum left by Soviet collapse, a new European architecture began to emerge. The Maastricht Treaty of nineteen ninety-two established the European Union, transforming the European Community from an economic project into a political one. Judt is cautiously positive about the EU but refuses to celebrate it. Its legitimacy, he argues, rests on economic performance rather than democratic engagement. Its expansion was partly motivated by the desire to export instability to institutions rather than leaving it on the streets. And the single currency created monetary union without political union — a structural instability that would eventually produce crisis.

Among the most powerful sections of the book are Judt's chapters on Yugoslavia and the return of ethnic nationalism to European soil. The Yugoslav wars of the nineteen nineties — in Bosnia, in Kosovo — shattered the comforting illusion that nationalism was a thing of the past in Europe. Judt shows that nationalism is not a primitive residue that disappears under modernization. It is a constructed political identity that can be activated by elites when other sources of legitimacy collapse. European institutions had no mechanisms for managing it when it returned, because they had been built on the assumption that it had been permanently resolved.

Equally significant are Judt's intellectual history sections, where he re-examines the relationship between European philosophy and European politics. Jean-Paul Sartre, who dominated European intellectual life in the two decades after the war, refused to condemn the Soviet Union for decades, preferring to see anti-communism as the greater evil. This moral failure, Judt argues, damaged the credibility of the European left for a generation and poisoned honest political debate. May nineteen sixty-eight in Paris, which seemed to offer the promise of revolution, is re-read by Judt not as a failed revolution but as a signal of exhaustion. The generation of nineteen sixty-eight was less interested in seizing state power than in transforming everyday life. When that failed, it hardened into disillusionment — into structuralism, into postmodernism, into the turn toward personal identity as the only remaining site of political action. Judt treats this intellectual retreat as a tragedy: not because the old certainties were worth preserving, but because the new ones were not adequate to the political challenges facing Europe.

The book ends not with triumphalism but with an unresolved question. Europe had become more unified, more comfortable, and more prosperous than at any point in its history. But it had lost something in the process: a sense of shared political purpose, a genuinely democratic public life, a common conversation about what Europe was for and who it was meant to serve. The question Judt leaves hanging in his epilogue is whether a Europe of markets and bureaucrats can sustain the democratic culture that gave European values their authority in the first place. Readers who engage seriously with Postwar will find no easy answers. They will find, instead, a historian who insists that forgetting is not peace, that neutrality is not objectivity, and that understanding the past is the precondition for making the future worth inhabiting.
