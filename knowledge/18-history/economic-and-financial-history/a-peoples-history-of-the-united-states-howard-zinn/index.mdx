*'A People's History of the United States: 1492 — Present'* by Howard Zinn (1980; updated through 2003, Harper Perennial Modern Classics 2015) is the most widely read work of revisionist history in American history. Spanning from Columbus's 1492 landfall through the early years of the George W. Bush presidency, the book turns traditional American historiography on its head: rather than telling history from the perspectives of presidents, generals, Founding Fathers, and industrial titans, Zinn recovers the stories of the people who built the nation, suffered under it, and repeatedly contested its injustices—Indigenous nations, enslaved Africans, factory workers, dissidents, women, immigrants, and the poor. Since its initial publication, the book has sold over two million copies, been translated into more than twenty languages, been assigned as reading in thousands of high school and college classrooms across the United States, and spawned a companion primary-source volume (*Voices of a People's History*, 2004), a young readers' edition (2007), the Zinn Education Project, and adaptations in film and graphic novel form. Its influence extends far beyond the academy: it has shaped how generations of activists and ordinary readers understand the roots of American inequality and the history of popular resistance.

<BookHeader />

The versopaperback edition (Harper Perennial Modern Classics, 2015) carries ISBN 9780062397348, spans 768 pages, and is illustrated with primary documents throughout. Zinn's stated aim was not objectivity—he rejected the myth that "value-free" history is possible or desirable—but advocacy: a corrective to what he described as the fundamental nationalist glorification of country that had long dominated American schooling. Decades of reviews, scholarly debates, and classroom use have confirmed what Zinn understood from the beginning: the book's power lies in its willingness to take sides and in its uncompromising moral clarity.
