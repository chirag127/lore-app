## Book Context & Background

*Against Method* appeared in 1975, thirteen years after Kuhn's *Structure*. The philosophy of science had been transformed by the Popper-Kuhn debates, and Feyerabend — who had been Popper's student and then Kuhn's colleague — saw an opening to radicalize the anti-positivist position. The book was published by New Left Books, which positioned it as part of a broader critique of authority in Western society. The political context — the aftermath of 1968, the rise of countercultural critiques of science — made the book timely.

## About the Author

Paul Feyerabend (1924–1994) was an Austrian-born philosopher who studied physics and philosophy in Vienna. He worked under Popper at the London School of Economics before moving to Berkeley, where he spent most of his career. His early work on the problem of theoretical entities was orthodox Popperian; *Against Method* marked a decisive break. Feyerabend was known for his flamboyant personal style, his love of opera, and his willingness to upend his own positions.

## Core Thesis & Argument

The central thesis is that no methodological rule has been universally followed in scientific practice; therefore, any universal methodology is doomed to irrelevance. The argument proceeds through a detailed case study of Galileo, showing that every methodological crime — ad hoc hypotheses, appeals to authority, manipulation of data — can be found in the founding moments of modern science. Feyerabend's conclusion is that science should be governed by epistemological anarchism.

## Thematic Analysis

Three major themes recur. **The critique of methodological monism**: there is no one way to do science. **The politics of knowledge**: scientific authority is political authority, and it should be contested. **The defense of diversity**: epistemological pluralism is both more productive and more humane than methodological uniformity.

## Argumentation & Evidence

Feyerabend relies primarily on historical case studies, with the Galileo case being the most developed. He also draws on examples from the history of physics, astronomy, and medicine. The argumentation is rhetorical and polemical rather than formal. Feyerabend's evidence is generally accurate, but critics have charged that he selects cases that support his thesis while ignoring those that do not.

## Strengths

The Galileo case study is a masterpiece of historical reconstruction. Feyerabend's critique of the consistency condition and meaning invariance struck genuine blows against dogmatic empiricism. The political argument for separating science and state anticipated later debates about scientific authority. The book's sheer provocation value remains undiminished — it forces every reader to reexamine their assumptions about science.

## Criticisms & Weaknesses

**Ernest Gellner** (London School of Economics) published a scathing review arguing that Feyerabend's epistemological anarchism was self-refuting: if "anything goes," then the claim that "anything goes" also goes, and Feyerabend has no grounds for preferring his view to any other.

**Imre Lakatos** was Feyerabend's most significant critic. Although they were friends and collaborated on a planned joint volume, Lakatos argued that Feyerabend's position collapsed into subjectivism. Lakatos's own methodology of scientific research programmes was designed to preserve rationality while accommodating historical complexity.

**Maurice Finocchiaro** (University of Nevada, Las Vegas) argued in a detailed study that Feyerabend's account of Galileo is historically inaccurate and philosophically confused. Finocchiaro showed that Galileo's arguments were more rational and less propagandistic than Feyerabend claimed.

**Alan Chalmers** (*What Is This Thing Called Science?*) argued that Feyerabend underestimates the role of shared standards in science and that his historical examples can be reinterpreted to support a more nuanced methodological picture.

## Comparative Analysis

Feyerabend radicalizes themes found in both Popper and Kuhn. From Popper he takes the emphasis on criticism and proliferation; from Kuhn he takes historical sensitivity and incommensurability. But where both Popper and Kuhn attempted to preserve some notion of scientific rationality, Feyerabend argues that the very attempt is misguided. The book stands as the culmination of the historical turn in philosophy of science and as the point beyond which many philosophers were unwilling to follow.

## Impact & Legacy

*Against Method* has been translated into more than fifteen languages. It remains a touchstone for critiques of scientific authority. The slogan "anything goes" has entered popular culture, though usually as a caricature of Feyerabend's actual position. The book influenced science studies, postmodern critiques of science, and the academic left's engagement with science. Feyerabend's argument for the separation of science and state has been taken up by philosophers of science like Philip Kitcher and Helen Longino.
