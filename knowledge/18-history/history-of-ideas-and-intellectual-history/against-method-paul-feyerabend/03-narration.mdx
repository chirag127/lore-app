## Writing Style & Voice

Feyerabend's prose is energetic, irreverent, and deliberately provocative. He writes in the style of an essayist rather than a philosopher: personal, digressive, full of jokes and asides. The voice is that of a brilliant, impatient scholar who has lost patience with academic conventions. He quotes from opera libretti, tells anecdotes about his own life, and addresses the reader directly. Some readers find this style exhilarating; others find it self-indulgent.

## Narrative Structure

The book is not organized as a single linear argument. Feyerabend circles back to the same themes, interrupts himself, and occasionally contradicts himself — deliberately, to make the point that consistency is not a virtue. The Galileo case study is the anchor, but the argument around it is intentionally chaotic. The structure enacts the book's thesis: there is no one correct way to organize an argument.

## Rhetorical Techniques

Feyerabend is a master of several rhetorical devices. **Satire**: he presents the rationalist position in exaggerated form to expose its absurdity. **Reductio ad absurdum**: he pushes methodological rules to their logical extreme. **Appeal to authority**: he deliberately uses the very appeals to authority he criticizes. **Self-deprecation**: he undermines his own position, acknowledging that his view might be wrong. The most famous technique is the slogan "anything goes," which Feyerabend presents not as his view but as the rationalist's description of what his view amounts to — and then he embraces it.

## Readability & Accessibility

The book is far more readable than most philosophy of science. The Galileo chapters are genuinely exciting historical narrative. The philosophical arguments are presented without technical jargon. However, the book's chaotic organization can be frustrating, and readers expecting a systematic treatise may be disappointed.

## Comparative Context

*Against Method* belongs to a tradition of iconoclastic philosophical polemics — works that attack established authority through wit, erudition, and exaggeration. Compare Plato's *Gorgias*, Nietzsche's *Beyond Good and Evil*, and Rorty's *Philosophy and the Mirror of Nature*. Within Feyerabend's own corpus, it is the most famous of a trilogy of works on scientific method, followed by *Science in a Free Society* (1978) and *Farewell to Reason* (1987). Unlike Kuhn, whose later work became more cautious, Feyerabend became more radical. *Against Method* represents the point at which he fully embraced the implications of his critique of rationalism.
