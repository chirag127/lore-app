*Against Method* is the most radical work in the philosophy of science from the post-Kuhn era. Paul Feyerabend — philosopher, physicist, and intellectual provocateur — argues that there is no single scientific method that can account for the actual history of science. All methodological rules, from Popper's falsificationism to Kuhn's paradigms, have been violated at crucial moments in scientific history. The only principle that does justice to scientific practice is "anything goes."

Feyerabend builds his case through a detailed study of Galileo's defense of heliocentrism, showing how Galileo used rhetorical tricks, propaganda, and ad hoc hypotheses — not just evidence and logic — to defeat the Ptolemaic establishment. From this case study, Feyerabend generalizes: science is not the rational enterprise its defenders claim. It is a messy, creative, irrational, and deeply human activity. Unlike Kuhn, who was appalled by this conclusion, Feyerabend embraces it. He argues for epistemological anarchism: no method, no authority, no privileged position for science in society.

<BookHeader />

Half a century after publication, *Against Method* remains the most stimulating and infuriating book in the philosophy of science. It is essential reading for anyone who wants to understand the limits of methodological thinking and the sociology of scientific authority.
