## Book Context & Background

Published in 1985, *Leviathan and the Air-Pump* emerged from the "strong program" in the sociology of scientific knowledge (SSK) at the University of Edinburgh. The strong program held that sociological explanation should apply to all knowledge claims — true and false alike — and that the content of science should be explained sociologically, not just its institutions. This book was the program's most celebrated application.

## About the Authors

Steven Shapin (born 1943) is an American historian of science, now at Harvard. Simon Schaffer (born 1955) is a British historian of science at Cambridge. The collaboration combined Shapin's interest in the social basis of credibility with Schaffer's expertise in the history of physics and the philosophy of experiment. Their partnership produced one of the most influential works in science studies.

## Core Thesis & Argument

The experimental method was not discovered as the correct way to study nature; it was invented as a solution to a political problem. Boyle's experimental program won not because it was epistemologically superior but because it was socially and politically aligned with the Restoration order. The dispute between Hobbes and Boyle was unresolvable by evidence because they disagreed about what constituted evidence — and that disagreement was ultimately political.

## Thematic Analysis

The book develops three interconnected themes. **The social construction of facts**: matters of fact are produced through social protocols, not discovered. **The politics of method**: disputes about scientific method are disputes about political order. **The symmetry principle**: the same sociological explanation should account for both successful and unsuccessful knowledge claims.

## Argumentation & Evidence

The evidence is drawn from an extraordinary range of sources: Boyle's published experimental reports, Hobbes's philosophical works, the records of the Royal Society, correspondence, and material evidence about the air-pump itself. The argument is detailed and cumulative, building from the technical specifics of the air-pump to broad conclusions about the sociology of knowledge.

## Strengths

The detailed reconstruction of Boyle's experimental practice — what Shapin and Schaffer call the "literary technology" of the experimental report — changed how historians think about scientific writing. The rehabilitation of Hobbes as a serious philosopher of science, rather than a reactionary opponent of progress, was genuinely revisionist. The connection between epistemology and political theory was original and powerful.

## Criticisms & Weaknesses

**Mario Biagioli** (Harvard) criticized the book for underestimating the role of court patronage in shaping Boyle's science. Biagioli argued that the experimental method was shaped by courtly protocols of gentlemanly conduct as much as by the Restoration political settlement.

**Larry Laudan** argued that the strong program's principle of symmetry — that true and false beliefs should be explained by the same causes — is methodologically incoherent. If sociological forces explain both success and failure, what distinguishes science from other belief systems?

**Thomas de Zengotita** argued that Shapin and Schaffer overstate the political significance of the Hobbes-Boyle dispute. The Restoration was not at stake in debates about vacuum pumps, and the connection between epistemology and politics is more metaphorical than causal.

## Comparative Analysis

*Leviathan and the Air-Pump* builds directly on the Edinburgh School of SSK (Bloor, Barnes). It also engages with the work of Michel Foucault on power and knowledge, though without Foucault's rhetoric. It anticipates the later "practice turn" in history of science (Pickering, Hacking) by focusing on what scientists do rather than what they believe. It contrasts sharply with older accounts of 17th-century science, which treated experimental method as a natural discovery rather than a social invention.

## Impact & Legacy

The book is one of the most cited works in history of science and science studies. It has been translated into multiple languages. The concept of "matters of fact" as social achievements has become a standard tool in science studies. The rehabilitation of Hobbes as a philosopher of science has been widely influential. The 2011 edition, with a new introduction by the authors, confirmed the book's canonical status.
