*Leviathan and the Air-Pump* is one of the most influential works in the sociology of scientific knowledge. Steven Shapin and Simon Schaffer examine the 1660s debate between Thomas Hobbes (author of *Leviathan*) and Robert Boyle (owner of the newly invented air-pump) over the value and propriety of experimental methods in natural philosophy. Their central claim is that the dispute was not merely about scientific facts but about the proper basis of knowledge and authority — in science and in society.

Boyle argued for experimental method: facts should be manufactured by machines like the air-pump, witnessed by gentlemen, and agreed upon collectively. Hobbes argued that experiments were unreliable, that natural philosophy should be based on deductive reasoning from first principles, and that the social organization of experimental knowledge was an invitation to political division. Shapin and Schaffer show that both men were right — and that the victory of Boyle's experimental program was not a matter of superior evidence but of superior social and political positioning.

<BookHeader />

The book introduced the concept of the "air-pump" as a technology for producing matters of fact — a concept that has become central to science studies. It remains a landmark work at the intersection of history, sociology, and philosophy of science.
