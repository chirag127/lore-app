*Science and the Modern World* is Alfred North Whitehead's most accessible major work and the founding document of process philosophy. Delivered as the Lowell Lectures at Harvard in 1925, it traces the genealogy of the modern scientific worldview from the Middle Ages through the 19th century, diagnoses the philosophical inadequacy of "scientific materialism," and proposes an alternative metaphysics of organism in which events, processes, and relations are fundamental.

Whitehead argues that the astonishing success of modern science has led educated society to mistake its working abstractions — particles, forces, space-time coordinates — for the whole of reality. This "fallacy of misplaced concreteness" generates a bifurcated nature in which the world described by physics is separate from the world of color, value, and purpose. The book's constructive proposal — a "philosophy of organism" — offers a unified cosmology that restores value, aesthetic experience, and religious intuition to their proper place within a rational scheme of thought.
