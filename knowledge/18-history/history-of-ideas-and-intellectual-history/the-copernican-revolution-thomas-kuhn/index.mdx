*The Copernican Revolution* was Thomas Kuhn's first major work, published five years before *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* made him famous. In it, Kuhn traces the slow, halting transition from the ancient geocentric cosmos to the modern heliocentric one — a transformation that took nearly 150 years and required rethinking not just astronomy but physics, philosophy, and theology. Kuhn shows that Copernicus was no heroic revolutionary in the modern sense; he was a conservative canon who preserved many features of the Ptolemaic system he sought to replace.

What makes the book enduringly valuable is Kuhn's method: he treats the Copernican revolution not as a triumph of one theory over another but as a profound shift in the very criteria of what constitutes a good explanation. The technical details of planetary astronomy become a lens through which to examine how knowledge communities change their most fundamental commitments. This is Kuhn's historicist approach in embryo — the approach he would later generalize in *Structure*.

<BookHeader />

The Copernican Revolution remains a masterclass in intellectual history, demonstrating that the most consequential changes in human thought often begin with narrow technical problems and spread outward to transform an entire civilization's self-understanding.
