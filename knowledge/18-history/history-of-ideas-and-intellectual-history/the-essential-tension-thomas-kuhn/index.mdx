*The Essential Tension* collects Kuhn's essays from the 1960s and 1970s, the period immediately after *Structure* changed the landscape of philosophy of science. The title essay — one of Kuhn's earliest programmatic statements — argues that scientific creativity requires both a deep commitment to tradition and willingness to break from it. The scientist must be both conservative (mastering the paradigm) and revolutionary (pushing beyond its limits). This "essential tension" between convergent and divergent thinking is what makes science productive.

The volume shows Kuhn responding to critics, clarifying his positions, and extending his analysis into new territory. He addresses the problem of scientific objectivity, the nature of measurement, the relationship between history and philosophy of science, and — perhaps most importantly — the role of values in theory choice. These essays reveal a thinker less certain and more nuanced than the author of *Structure*. The radical claims about incommensurability are tempered; the social dimensions of science receive more careful treatment.

<BookHeader />

For anyone interested in how Kuhn's thought evolved after his magnum opus, *The Essential Tension* is indispensable. It shows a major philosopher wrestling with the implications of his own revolution, refining his positions, and engaging constructively with critics who had accused him of relativism.
