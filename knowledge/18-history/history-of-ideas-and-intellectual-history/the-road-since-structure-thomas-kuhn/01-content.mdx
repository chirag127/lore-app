## Part I: Philosophical Essays

### The Road Since Structure (1990)

The title essay gives its name to the collection and presents Kuhn's mature view. He argues that the central problem of *Structure* — how rational theory choice is possible across revolutions — can be solved by reconceiving incommensurability as localized taxonomic change. A scientific revolution changes the lexical taxonomy — the network of kind terms and categories — that a scientific community uses. This has implications for what can and cannot be expressed in the post-revolutionary lexicon. Communication across the divide is possible but requires translation, and translation is always imperfect.

### Commensurability, Comparability, Communicability (1982)

Kuhn distinguishes three senses of incommensurability. The first, from the history of mathematics (incommensurable magnitudes), provides the guiding metaphor: as there is no common unit of measurement for incommensurable lines, there is no common standard for comparing paradigms. But Kuhn insists comparability does not require a common standard — we can compare paradigms on multiple dimensions without needing a single metric.

### Possible Worlds in History of Science (1989)

Kuhn engages with the possible-worlds semantics of Kripke and Putnam, arguing that the history of science provides evidence for a version of the causal theory of reference. Scientific terms refer through causal chains, not through descriptions — which explains how scientists working in different paradigms can genuinely refer to the same things even while describing them differently.

### The Trouble with the Historical Philosophy of Science (1991)

A retrospective assessment of the "historical turn" in philosophy of science. Kuhn argues that the initial promise of historical philosophy of science has not been fulfilled, partly because of the difficulty of combining historical sensitivity with philosophical rigor. He calls for renewed attention to the normative dimension of philosophy of science.

## Part II: Comments and Replies

### Reflections on My Critics

Kuhn responds to the criticisms of *Structure* that had accumulated over three decades. He engages seriously with Popper, Lakatos, Feyerabend, and others. He concedes that some of his formulations were misleading, particularly the Gestalt-switch metaphor and the claim that scientists "see different worlds." But he reaffirms the core theses — the existence of normal science, the role of crisis, the non-cumulative character of revolutionary change — while refining the presentation.

### Theory Change as Structure Change

An engagement with structuralist philosophy of science (Sneed, Stegmuller). Kuhn finds the formal apparatus of structuralism useful for expressing some of his ideas about theory change, though he remains wary of excessive formalism.

### Metaphor in Science (1979)

Kuhn argues that metaphor plays a constitutive role in science, not merely a decorative one. Metaphors create new similarities, reorganize categories, and enable scientific imagination. The metaphorical extension of terms from one domain to another is a central mechanism of conceptual change.

### Rationality and Theory Choice (1983)

Kuhn extends the "Objectivity" essay from *The Essential Tension*, arguing that rational theory choice depends on shared values rather than shared rules. The rationality of science is the rationality of a community engaged in ongoing discussion, not of an individual applying an algorithm.

### The Natural and the Human Sciences (1989)

Kuhn considers the applicability of his model to the social sciences. He concludes that the social sciences are in a pre-paradigm stage — they lack the shared exemplars that would enable normal science. But he rejects the sharp natural/human science dichotomy, arguing that all sciences involve interpretation.

## Part III: Autobiographical Interview

The final section is a wide-ranging interview conducted by three of Kuhn's former students. Kuhn discusses his education at Harvard, his wartime work in radar, his conversion from physics to history of science, his relationship with James Bryant Conant, the writing of *Structure*, his response to critics, and his sense of unfinished philosophical business. The interview is candid, sometimes defensive, occasionally self-lacerating. It provides an invaluable window into the personality behind the most influential philosophy of science text of the century.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment
This summary captures the volume's major themes. The technical philosophical arguments — particularly those involving possible-worlds semantics and structuralist theory of science — are compressed.

### Recommended Reading Path
| Reader Type | Time | What to Read |
|---|---|---|
| Advanced student | ~8 hr | Part I essays + autobiographical interview |
| Scholar | ~15 hr | Full volume |
| General reader | ~2 hr | Autobiographical interview + title essay |

### What You'll Miss
- The detailed engagement with formal philosophy of science
- The full development of the lexical taxonomy concept
- Some of the more technical replies to critics
