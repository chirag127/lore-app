## Book Context & Background

*The Road Since Structure* appeared in 2000, four years after Kuhn's death. Philosophy of science had moved on: the strong program in sociology of knowledge, feminist epistemology, and science studies had transformed the field. Kuhn's later work was partly an attempt to distance himself from these developments. The volume shows him trying to construct a position that was neither the naive objectivism of the logical empiricists nor the radical constructivism of his more enthusiastic readers.

## About the Author

Kuhn (1922–1996) was at MIT from 1979 until his retirement. The essays in this volume represent the work of his final philosophical maturity. He was increasingly interested in the relationship between his views and Kantian philosophy, attempting to "naturalize" the Kantian categories by making them subject to historical change. The autobiographical interview reveals a thinker still haunted by the charge of relativism and still struggling to articulate a position that would satisfy both historians and philosophers.

## Core Thesis & Argument

The volume's overarching argument is that incommensurability is best understood as a problem of lexical taxonomy. Scientific revolutions change the kind terms and categories through which scientists describe nature. These changes make some statements untranslatable across the revolutionary divide — not because the old paradigm was false, but because the new lexicon cannot express its claims. This is a more precise, less radical formulation than the one in *Structure*.

## Thematic Analysis

Three themes dominate. **Taxonomy**: the fundamental categories for dividing nature are not given by experience but are constitutive of experience. **Translation**: communication across paradigms is possible but requires learning the other lexicon as a foreign language. **Kantianism with moving categories**: Kuhn reaches toward a position where the categories of understanding are not fixed but evolve historically.

## Argumentation & Evidence

Kuhn's later work is more purely philosophical and less historical than *Structure*. The essays engage extensively with contemporary analytic philosophy — Quine, Putnam, Kripke, Davidson. The argumentation is dense, technical, and sometimes tentative. Kuhn is feeling his way toward a position rather than presenting a finished system.

## Strengths

The clarity with which Kuhn redefines incommensurability as taxonomic change is a genuine philosophical achievement. The autobiographical interview is invaluable intellectual history. The engagement with analytic philosophy shows Kuhn's breadth. The volume as a whole demonstrates intellectual honesty — Kuhn never stopped trying to improve his own work.

## Criticisms & Weaknesses

**Richard Rorty** criticized Kuhn's turn toward Kantianism as a retreat from the more genuinely radical implications of *Structure*. Rorty argued that the lexical concept was a step backward — an attempt to find a foundation for objectivity that the original work had shown to be unnecessary.

**Paul Hoyningen-Huene**, one of Kuhn's most sympathetic interpreters, noted that Kuhn never completed the systematic work he was aiming toward. The essays contain programmatic statements rather than fully worked-out positions.

**Steve Fuller** (University of Warwick) maintained his critique that Kuhn's focus on community consensus was politically conservative, arguing that the later work's emphasis on lexicons as quasi-transcendental frameworks naturalized the authority of scientific communities.

Some critics argue that the lexical taxonomy concept solves the problem of incommensurability only by drastically restricting its scope — local incommensurability is much less interesting than the global incommensurability that made *Structure* so provocative.

## Comparative Analysis

The volume sits alongside Kuhn's earlier collections as the third panel of a triptych: *Structure* (the revolutionary manifesto), *The Essential Tension* (clarifications and extensions), *The Road Since Structure* (mature reflection and unfinished business). It engages with figures like Quine (on translation) and Davidson (on conceptual schemes) that the earlier work had ignored. It also responds implicitly to the strong program in sociology of knowledge, which had turned Kuhn's insights about the social nature of science into a full-blown relativistic constructivism.

## Impact & Legacy

The volume confirmed that Kuhn's later philosophy was more Kantian than relativist. It has influenced work on conceptual change in cognitive science, on translation in philosophy of language, and on the history of categories in anthropology. The autobiographical interview has become a primary source for historians of 20th-century philosophy. The lexical taxonomy concept has been taken up by philosophers of biology (on the species problem) and by scholars of conceptual change in science education.
