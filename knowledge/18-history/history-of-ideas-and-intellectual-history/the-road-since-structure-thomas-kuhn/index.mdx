*The Road Since Structure*, published posthumously in 2000, collects Kuhn's philosophical essays from the last twenty-five years of his life. Edited by James Conant and John Haugeland, the volume reveals a thinker who was never satisfied with his own success. After *Structure* made him the most famous philosopher of science of the century, Kuhn spent the rest of his career trying to clarify, refine, and in some cases rethink its central concepts.

The most important development is Kuhn's reconceptualization of incommensurability. In his later work, he abandons the Gestalt-switch metaphor and the strong relativist implications that critics attacked. Instead, he frames incommensurability as a problem of taxonomic categorization and translation between lexicons. Scientific revolutions involve changes in the basic categories scientists use to classify the world — and when categories change, communication across the revolutionary divide becomes partial and difficult but not impossible.

<BookHeader />

The volume also includes an extensive autobiographical interview in which Kuhn discusses his education, his turn from physics to history, the reception of *Structure*, and his intellectual relationships with figures like Popper, Polanyi, and Quine. For anyone interested in the full arc of Kuhn's thought, *The Road Since Structure* is the necessary final chapter.
