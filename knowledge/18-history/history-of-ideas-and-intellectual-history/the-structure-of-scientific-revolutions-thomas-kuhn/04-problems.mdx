## Key Problem

The core problem Kuhn addresses is the failure of the dominant mid-century philosophy of science — logical empiricism and Popperian falsificationism — to account for the actual historical development of scientific knowledge. Both traditions assumed that science progresses through the rational application of fixed methodological rules and that scientific change is (or should be) a logical process of theory evaluation against neutral observational evidence. Kuhn argues that this picture is historically false: the history of science reveals a pattern of discontinuous paradigm shifts that cannot be reduced to rule-governed decision-making. The deeper problem is epistemological: if the standards of rationality themselves change across paradigms, how can science claim to be a uniquely rational enterprise?

## Proposed Solution

Kuhn's solution is a cyclical model of scientific development that replaces the linear-accumulation picture. The model has five phases: (1) pre-paradigm science, characterized by competing schools with no shared framework; (2) normal science, in which a paradigm guides puzzle-solving; (3) crisis, when anomalies overwhelm the paradigm; (4) revolution, in which a new paradigm replaces the old; and (5) resolution, when the community converges on the new paradigm and normal science resumes. This model preserves the rationality of science — normal science is genuinely productive, revolutions solve genuine crises — while acknowledging that paradigm choice involves irreducibly social and psychological factors. Kuhn insists that science is progressive, but he defines progress in terms of problem-solving effectiveness rather than convergence on final truth.

## Evidence & Argumentation

Kuhn's evidence is drawn from the history of physics, astronomy, and chemistry. He develops four extended case studies: the Copernican revolution (the shift from Ptolemaic to Copernican astronomy, which took 150 years and required changes to physics, not just astronomy), the chemical revolution (Lavoisier's oxygen theory replacing phlogiston, which redefined what counted as a chemical element), the Einsteinian revolution (relativity replacing Newtonian space and time, triggered by anomalies including Mercury's perihelion and the Michelson-Morley experiment), and the quantum revolution (which required rethinking causality and measurement). For each case, Kuhn shows that the transition involved a Gestalt-like reconceptualization, not a simple rational choice. His argumentative strategy is to present these historical narratives and then extract a general model — an approach that has been praised for its explanatory power and criticized for selecting cases that fit the thesis.

## Unresolved Questions

Kuhn's model leaves several deep questions open. First, if paradigms are incommensurable, how exactly do scientists communicate across revolutionary divides? Kuhn gestures at the possibility of translation but does not fully develop it. Second, if paradigm choice is not algorithmically determined, what is the precise role of rational argument in scientific persuasion? The 1977 essay "Objectivity, Value Judgment, and Theory Choice" partially addresses this by listing five shared values (accuracy, consistency, scope, simplicity, fruitfulness), but these values are vague and can conflict. Third, does the model apply to the social sciences? Kuhn suggested they are "pre-paradigmatic," but this claim has been fiercely debated. Fourth, the relationship between scientific progress and truth remains unclear — if progress is just improved puzzle-solving, does science tell us anything about the world as it really is? Fifth, the model's applicability beyond physics and chemistry remains untested in any systematic way.

## Criticisms

Kuhn's model has been criticized on multiple fronts. The most persistent philosophical criticism — from Popper, Lakatos, and Shapere — is that Kuhn reduces scientific change to a non-rational social process. Popper charged that Kuhn's normal scientist is a dogmatist whose work is not genuinely scientific; Lakatos argued that Kuhn offered no way to distinguish progressive from degenerating research programs; Shapere contended that incommensurability is self-refuting (if Kuhn's terms were truly incommensurable with those of logical empiricism, no one could understand Kuhn, let alone argue with him). Margaret Masterman's criticism was internal: the concept of paradigm was so ambiguous it could not do the work Kuhn assigned it. Stephen Toulmin argued that the revolutionary model imposes an artificial discontinuity on a process that is more gradual and evolutionary. Steve Fuller's political critique charged that Kuhn's focus on community consensus insulated science from democratic accountability and served a Cold War agenda. Ian Hacking, in his introduction to the 50th-anniversary edition, observes that Kuhn's incommensurability thesis — while less radical than his critics feared — nonetheless posed a genuine problem that philosophy of science has not fully resolved.

## Alternative Solutions

Several alternative models of scientific change were developed in response to Kuhn. Imre Lakatos's "methodology of scientific research programmes" proposed that scientific change could be rationally evaluated by comparing the progressive or degenerating problem-shifts of competing research programs. Larry Laudan's *Progress and Its Problems* (1977) offered a problem-solving model that retained rationality without full incommensurability. Stephen Toulmin's *Human Understanding* (1972) proposed an evolutionary model of conceptual variation and selection. The Edinburgh Strong Programme (David Bloor, Barry Barnes) pushed Kuhn's insights in a fully sociological direction, applying the symmetry principle (explain true and false beliefs by the same causal mechanisms). Bruno Latour and Steve Woolgar's *Laboratory Life* (1979) and Latour's *Science in Action* (1987) applied actor-network theory to the construction of scientific facts. More recently, Helen Longino's critical contextual empiricism and Philip Kitcher's *The Advancement of Science* (1993) have attempted to reconcile the social dimensions of science with rational evaluation.

## Practical Applications

Kuhn's model has been applied far beyond the natural sciences. In the social sciences, it has been used to analyze paradigm conflicts in economics (the Keynesian vs. neoclassical debate), sociology (the structure-agency debate), and psychology (behaviorism vs. cognitivism). In business and management studies, "paradigm shift" became a buzzword for organizational change. In science education, the concept of conceptual change draws directly on Kuhn's work. In the sociology of knowledge, Kuhn's model provided the theoretical foundation for empirical studies of scientific laboratories and controversies. In literary theory, Stanley Fish applied Kuhn's ideas to interpretive communities. In political science, Kuhn's account of how communities manage paradigm change has been used to analyze policy change and institutional reform. For working scientists, the book offers a framework for understanding why their own fields resist revolutionary ideas and why fundamental breakthroughs so often come from outsiders or the young.
