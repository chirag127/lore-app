First published in 1962 as part of Otto Neurath's International Encyclopedia of Unified Science, Thomas Kuhn's *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* became the most influential academic book of the twentieth century. It introduced concepts — "paradigm shift," "normal science," "incommensurability" — that now permeate not only philosophy of science but business, politics, and popular culture. Kuhn, a physicist-turned-historian, argued that science does not grow by steady accumulation of facts. Instead, it lurches through revolutionary breaks in which entire frameworks of understanding are torn down and rebuilt.

The book's central drama lies in the tension between normal science — the puzzle-solving work that occupies most scientists — and revolutionary science, the rare but transformative episodes when anomalies force a community to abandon its paradigm. Kuhn showed that these revolutions are not merely intellectual but social: they involve generational change, institutional resistance, and deeply personal commitments. The result was a portrait of science as a profoundly human enterprise, neither the purely logical march of positivism nor the irrational free-for-all his critics accused him of endorsing.

<BookHeader />

The Structure of Scientific Revolutions remains essential reading for anyone who wants to understand how knowledge actually develops. Its thesis — that the most important scientific advances come not from following rules but from breaking them — has shaped fields from sociology to literary theory. Six decades after publication, the debates it ignited over truth, progress, and the nature of scientific rationality continue to animate intellectual life.
