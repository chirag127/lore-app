*A People's History of the United States* (1980, updated 2003) by Howard Zinn
is one of the most widely read and controversial American history books ever
written. It has sold over two million copies, is assigned in high school and
college classrooms across the country, and spawned a companion volume
(*Voices of a People's History*, 2004), a young readers edition (2007), and
the Zinn Education Project.

---

## About the Author

Howard Zinn (1922–2010) was an American historian, political scientist, and
social activist. Born to working-class Jewish immigrant parents in Brooklyn,
he served as a bombardier in World War II — an experience that radicalized
him. After the war, he earned a PhD in history from Columbia University and
taught at Spelman College in Atlanta, where he was fired for supporting
student civil rights sit-ins. He spent most of his academic career at Boston
University.

Zinn was not a neutral observer of his subject. He was a participant in the
very movements he wrote about: anti-war protests, civil rights marches, and
labor organizing. He described himself as "a socialist and a democrat and an
anarchist and a pacifist." His goal was not objectivity but a corrective —
to balance what he saw as a nationalist, elite-serving narrative that had
dominated American historiography.

---

## The Book's Place in American Historiography

When *A People's History* appeared in 1980, American history as a
professional discipline was already undergoing a shift. The "consensus
school" — which emphasized shared values and national unity — had dominated
mid-century scholarship (Richard Hofstadter, Daniel Boorstin, Louis
Hartz). But the social movements of the 1960s and 1970s had produced a new
generation of "new social historians" who studied ordinary people: women,
workers, slaves, immigrants. Zinn synthesized this diffuse scholarship into
a single, readable narrative.

The book did not originate the idea of "history from below" — that tradition
runs from the Annales School through E.P. Thompson's *The Making of the
English Working Class* (1963) and Staughton Lynd's intellectual activism.
What Zinn did was popularize it for a mass audience. He wrote in clear,
non-academic prose. He let primary sources speak. He named the dead and the
forgotten.

The result was a book that polarized American historiography. Supporters
called it a necessary corrective to nationalist mythmaking. Critics called
it a one-sided polemic that replaced one bias with another. Both sides
agreed on one thing: it changed the conversation.

---

## Why It Matters

More scholars have engaged with *A People's History* — positively and
negatively — than with almost any other American history book of the last
half century. It forced a reckoning with whose stories get told and whose
get erased. Even its harshest critics concede that post-Zinn, it became
harder to write American history as a triumphalist narrative of
inevitable progress.

The book's influence extends beyond academia. It has been cited in
political speeches, featured in Hollywood films (*Good Will Hunting*), and
become a touchstone for activist movements from Occupy Wall Street to
Black Lives Matter. For millions of readers, it was the first book that
made American history feel vital, contested, and relevant to the present.
