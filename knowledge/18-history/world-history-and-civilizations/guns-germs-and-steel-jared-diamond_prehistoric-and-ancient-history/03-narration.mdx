_Guns, Germs, and Steel: The Fates of Human Societies_ by Jared Diamond
attempts to answer one of the deepest questions in human history: Why
did some societies become rich and powerful while others remained poor
and weak? Not why individual people differ — Diamond is firm on this
point — but why entire civilizations developed along such different
tracks.

The question was put to Diamond in 1972 on a beach in New Guinea. Yali,
a local politician, asked: "Why is it that you white people developed so
much cargo and brought it to New Guinea, but we black people had little
cargo of our own?" By "cargo" Yali meant material wealth: steel axes,
ships, guns, books, medicine. Diamond had spent years living among New
Guineans and knew they were as intelligent, resourceful, and capable as
any European. So why had they not developed writing, industry, or
oceangoing ships?

For the next quarter-century, Diamond pursued that question through
archaeology, linguistics, epidemiology, and evolutionary biology. The
result is a book that sweeps from the last Ice Age to the space age,
tracing the hidden forces that shaped human destiny.

The story begins with a dramatic scene. In 1532, the Spanish conquistador
Francisco Pizarro met the Inca emperor Atahuallpa in the Andean town of
Cajamarca. Pizarro had 168 men. Atahuallpa commanded 80,000 warriors,
deep in his own territory. Within hours, the Inca were massacred, the
emperor was captured, and the largest empire in the Americas began to
collapse. How did 168 Spaniards defeat 80,000 Incas? The obvious answers
— guns, steel swords, horses — are what Diamond calls the proximate
causes. The Spanish had better weapons and armor. They had horses, which
the Inca had never seen. Smallpox had arrived ahead of the Spanish,
killing the previous Inca emperor and triggering a civil war. And the
Spanish had writing — they knew about the Inca from Cortés's conquest of
Mexico, while Atahuallpa knew almost nothing about the Spanish.

But this just pushes the question back. Why did Europeans have guns,
germs, steel, writing, and centralized states, while the Inca did not?
For Diamond, the answer lies in a chain of causation that begins
12,000 years ago with the most decisive event in human history: the
invention of agriculture.

Here is the core argument presented in its simplest form. Farming
produces more food per acre than hunting and gathering. More food means
denser populations. Dense populations mean more potential inventors
and more competition between societies. Farming also requires
sedentary living, which allows people to accumulate possessions and
develop specialized skills. And farming with domesticated animals
brings exposure to animal diseases, which over generations becomes
immunity.

But agriculture did not arise everywhere at the same time. It arose
first in places with the right raw materials: wild plants that could
be domesticated into crops, and wild animals that could be domesticated
into livestock. The Fertile Crescent — spanning modern Iraq, Syria,
Turkey, and Israel — was extraordinarily lucky. It had wild wheat and
barley with large edible seeds. It had wild peas, lentils, and
chickpeas. It had wild goats, sheep, pigs, and cows. These species
already had the genetic potential for domestication. In Mesoamerica,
farmers had to domesticate maize from a wild grass called teosinte —
a process that took thousands of years and produced a plant that was
initially far less nutritious. The Americas had only one domesticable
large mammal, the llama. Africa had zebras, rhinos, and buffalo — all
of which resist domestication for specific reasons that Diamond calls
the Anna Karenina Principle. Domesticable animals are all domesticable
in the same way; non-domesticable animals each fail for their own
unique reason.

The most famous argument in the book concerns the shape of the
continents. Eurasia runs east-west. The Americas and Africa run
north-south. Why does this matter? Because crops are adapted to
specific latitudes — to particular day lengths, temperatures, and
rainfall patterns. A grain that grows in Turkey will grow in France
and China because all are at similar latitudes. But a grain that grows
in Mexico will not grow in Canada or Argentina without centuries of
genetic adaptation. The same applies to livestock and even to human
migration.

So agriculture arose earliest and spread fastest in Eurasia. It arose
later and spread more slowly in the Americas and Africa. This
difference compounded over millennia. Eurasian societies had denser
populations, more advanced technology, and — crucially — stronger
immunity to epidemic diseases.

The germs part of the title may be the most important. Diamond argues
that the major infectious diseases that shaped world history — smallpox,
measles, influenza, tuberculosis — are evolved forms of diseases that
affect domesticated animals. Smallpox came from cattle. Measles came
from rinderpest. Flu came from pigs and ducks. Eurasians, living for
thousands of years in dense settlements surrounded by livestock, were
repeatedly exposed to these diseases. Those with genetic resistance
survived and passed on their immunity. When Europeans reached the
Americas, they carried these diseases with them. An estimated 90 to
95 percent of the pre-Columbian population died from Eurasian germs —
the single greatest demographic catastrophe in human history. The
Inca and Aztec empires were not so much conquered as they were
incubated to death.

The steel part of the title represents the broader technological
advantage. Food surpluses allowed societies to support full-time
specialists: blacksmiths, inventors, soldiers, scribes. The denser
the population and the more interconnected the continent, the faster
technology developed. Eurasia had both advantages.

The final link in the chain is political organization. Hunter-gatherers
lived in egalitarian bands. Food-producing societies developed chiefs,
kings, bureaucrats, and armies. Diamond calls these states
kleptocracies — societies organized to transfer resources from the
many to the few — but he acknowledges that they also provided public
services: roads, irrigation, courts, defense. A centralized state can
mobilize armies, finance ships, and coordinate conquest. The Inca
empire was a centralized state — but it was the product of only a few
thousand years of food production in the Americas. The Spanish state
was the product of ten thousand years of food production in Eurasia.
The difference in development time, Diamond argues, was decisive.

In the final chapters, Diamond applies his framework around the world.
Polynesian islands show how the same cultural group developed
differently depending on the resources and geography of the islands
they settled. China's early unification gave it advantages but also a
vulnerability: when a single emperor decided to stop overseas
exploration in the 1430s, the world's largest navy was scrapped. Europe,
fragmented into competing states, could not afford to stop. If one king
refused to fund exploration, another would.

The Africa chapter is the most controversial. Diamond argues that
Africa's north-south axis, lack of domesticable animals, and tropical
diseases slowed its development. But Africanist historians have detailed
how Diamond's analysis relies on outdated sources, overstates disease
barriers, and understates the military effectiveness of African armies
that repeatedly defeated European invaders.

_Guns, Germs, and Steel_ concludes with a surprising admission:
Diamond acknowledges that his analysis invites the charge of geographic
determinism, and he does not entirely deny it. He argues that knowing
the environmental constraints on history does not mean human choices
are irrelevant — only that they operate within limits set by geography.
A New Guinean society could not, through sheer ingenuity, invent horses
and wheats that did not exist in their environment. But they could
choose how to organize their society, whom to trade with, and what
technologies to adopt.

The book's legacy is complex. It has been praised as a landmark of
popular scholarship and criticized as environmental determinism
repackaged. It inspired an entire generation of big-history writers
while drawing sharp rebuttals from professional historians, especially
Africanists and geographers. On one point, nearly everyone agrees:
the question Diamond set out to answer — Why did development proceed at
different rates on different continents? — is one of the most important
questions we can ask. And even if his answer is not the final word, it
changed the terms of the debate for good.
