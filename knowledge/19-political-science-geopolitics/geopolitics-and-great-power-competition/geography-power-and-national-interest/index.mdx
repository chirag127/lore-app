# Geography, Power & National Interest

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Geopolitics is the study of how geographic factors — location, territory, resources, strategic chokepoints, population — condition the power and interests of states. Classical geopoliticians such as Halford Mackinder (the "Heartland" thesis), Alfred Thayer Mahan (sea power), and Nicholas Spykman (the "Rimland" theory) sought to identify permanent geographic determinants of international power. Their ideas have influenced grand strategy for over a century, from German *Geopolitik* through Cold War containment policy.

Contemporary geopolitical analysis moves beyond environmental determinism to examine how geography interacts with technology (cyber, space, AI), economics (resource competition, supply chains), and politics (national identity, strategic culture). Works by Robert Kaplan, Colin Flint, John Agnew, and Saul Bernard Cohen have modernized the field. The study of resource wars, energy geopolitics, climate-related security risks, and the strategic implications of Arctic melting, sea-lane security, and rare-earth mineral dependence all fall within this sub-subcategory.

## What Belongs Here

- Classical geopolitical theory (Mackinder, Mahan, Spykman, Haushofer)
- Heartland and Rimland theories
- Sea power and maritime strategy
- Land power and continental strategy
- Air, space, and cyber domains
- Resource geopolitics (energy, water, minerals, food)
- Strategic chokepoints (Strait of Hormuz, Malacca, Taiwan Strait)
- Geopolitics of climate change and Arctic
- Demography and power (population decline, youth bulges)
- National interest and grand strategy
- Geopolitical codes and worldviews

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- International relations theory about the structure of the international system (see Realism, Liberalism & Constructivism)
- Great-power rivalry between specific states (see US-China Competition)
- Diplomatic history and foreign policy decision-making (see Diplomatic History & Foreign Policy)
- Economic statecraft and sanctions (see Political Economy of Developing Nations)
- Military strategy and doctrine in operational detail

## Key Books

1. Halford J. Mackinder, *Democratic Ideals and Reality* — The classic statement of the Heartland thesis and its implications for world power.
2. Alfred Thayer Mahan, *The Influence of Sea Power upon History* — The foundational argument for naval dominance in great-power competition.
3. Nicholas J. Spykman, *The Geography of the Peace* — The Rimland theory and its application to American grand strategy.
4. Robert D. Kaplan, *The Revenge of Geography* — A contemporary revival of geopolitical analysis for the 21st century.
5. Colin Flint, *Introduction to Geopolitics* — The leading textbook defining critical and classical geopolitical approaches.
6. John Agnew, *Geopolitics: Re-Visioning World Politics* — Critical geopolitics examining how geopolitical knowledge is produced.
7. Paul Bracken, *The Second Nuclear Age* — The geopolitical implications of nuclear proliferation in the post-Cold War era.
8. Michael T. Klare, *Resource Wars* — The new geopolitics of natural resource competition.
9. Zbigniew Brzezinski, *The Grand Chessboard* — American primacy and its geostrategic imperatives.
10. Tim Marshall, *Prisoners of Geography* — Accessible analysis of how geography constrains state strategy.
