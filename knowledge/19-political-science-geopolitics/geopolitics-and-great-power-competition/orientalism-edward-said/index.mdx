Orientalism is one of the most influential and controversial academic books of the late twentieth century. Edward Said's analysis of how the West has represented, studied, and dominated the "Orient" founded the field of postcolonial studies and permanently changed how scholars think about the relationship between knowledge and power.

<BookHeader />

Said argues that Orientalism is not simply a body of scholarship about the Middle East but a "corporate institution for dealing with the Orient" — a Western style for dominating, restructuring, and having authority over the East. Drawing on Michel Foucault's concept of discourse and Antonio Gramsci's concept of hegemony, Said shows how Western scholarship, literature, and art about the East have been deeply implicated in the project of colonial domination. The book transformed multiple fields — comparative literature, anthropology, history, and political science — and remains essential reading for understanding the politics of cultural representation.
