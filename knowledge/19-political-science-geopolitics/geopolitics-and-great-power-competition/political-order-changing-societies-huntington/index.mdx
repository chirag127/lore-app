Samuel Huntington's Political Order in Changing Societies is a foundational text in comparative politics that fundamentally challenged the dominant modernization theories of the 1960s. Published during the height of the Vietnam War and amid widespread social upheaval, the book argued that the primary challenge facing developing countries was not democratization but the creation of effective political institutions capable of maintaining order.

<BookHeader />

Huntington opens with the celebrated line: "The most important political distinction among countries concerns not their form of government but their degree of government." The book systematically argues that economic development, urbanization, and increased literacy — the hallmarks of modernization — actually produce political instability by creating new demands that existing institutions cannot meet. The key to political development is the creation of strong, adaptable, and autonomous institutions that can channel participation and maintain order. This thesis made Huntington controversial among liberal academics who saw it as legitimizing authoritarian rule, but it also made the book essential reading for generations of scholars and policymakers.
