The End of History and the Last Man is the most famous work of political philosophy from the late twentieth century. Francis Fukuyama's contention that the fall of the Soviet Union marked the endpoint of humanity's ideological evolution — that liberal democracy is the final form of government — became the defining thesis of the post-Cold War era.

<BookHeader />

First published as a 1989 article in The National Interest, the book expands Fukuyama's argument by grounding it in a Hegelian-Marxist philosophy of history, supplemented by Plato's concept of thymos (spiritedness, or the desire for recognition). Fukuyama argues that history is directional and driven forward by two engines: the logic of modern science, which drives economic development and homogenization, and the struggle for recognition, which drives political development toward liberal democracy. The "Last Man" of the title — drawn from Nietzsche — is the comfortable but potentially discontented consumer of liberal democracy, secure and prosperous but lacking the desire for great deeds.
