## Part I: Conjectures About the Great Divergence

Pomeranz opens by establishing the puzzle: why did Europe, and particularly Britain, industrialize first, when other parts of the world — especially China — were at similar levels of development? He challenges the standard narrative that Europe had unique advantages in culture (Protestant work ethic), institutions (property rights, rule of law), or science (the Scientific Revolution). He argues that the most advanced regions of China, particularly the Yangzi Delta, were comparable to the most advanced parts of Europe in living standards, life expectancy, market development, and technological sophistication.

The key methodological move is to compare the most developed regions of Europe and China — Britain and the Yangzi Delta — rather than comparing averages across entire continents. When this comparison is made, the evidence for European superiority in the pre-industrial era largely disappears.

## Part II: Economic Growth in Europe and Asia Before the Great Divergence

Pomeranz provides detailed evidence for economic comparability. He examines population patterns, agricultural productivity, living standards, markets, and technology. In the Yangzi Delta, agriculture was highly productive, markets were sophisticated, and living standards were comparable to those in Britain. Chinese merchants operated across vast distances, credit markets functioned, and property rights were reasonably secure.

He acknowledges differences — Europe had more political fragmentation, which may have fostered competition; China had a unified imperial system that could be less dynamic. But he argues that these differences did not create a decisive advantage for Europe. The key constraints on pre-industrial growth — land scarcity, resource depletion, and ecological limits — were similar in both regions.

## Part III: The Role of Coal

The first of Pomeranz's two central arguments concerns coal. Britain had massive, easily accessible coal deposits, particularly in Newcastle and the Midlands. The ability to shift from wood to coal as an energy source freed Britain from the land constraints that bound pre-industrial economies. Without coal, even the most advanced economies were limited by the amount of land needed for fuel and food.

Pomeranz argues that this was a geographically contingent advantage — China had coal deposits, but they were located in the north, far from the Yangzi Delta where industry would have needed them. Transporting coal across China's vast distances was prohibitively expensive. This accident of geology gave Britain a unique escape from ecological constraints, enabling the Industrial Revolution.

## Part IV: The Role of the New World

Pomeranz's second central argument concerns colonialism. The New World provided Europe with two critical resources: land-intensive products (cotton, sugar, timber) and precious metals. These effectively gave Europe access to "ghost acres" — land that did not exist within Europe's borders but served European needs, relieving ecological pressure.

British cotton textile production depended on American cotton. American sugar plantations provided calories. American timber and land absorbed population pressure. These New World resources, extracted through slavery and colonialism, provided a resource windfall that no other region of the world could access. Without them, Europe would have faced the same ecological constraints that limited Asian growth.

## Part V: Conclusion — The Contingent Divergence

Pomeranz concludes that the Great Divergence was not the product of long-term European superiority but of contingent factors that came together around 1800. Coal and colonies — not culture, institutions, or science — were the decisive factors. This means that the rise of the West was less inevitable and more historically specific than standard narratives suggest.

He notes that this conclusion has implications for contemporary development: if the Great Divergence was driven by contingent factors rather than inherent superiority, then developing countries today face challenges that are fundamentally different from those Europe faced. The "ghost acres" of coal and colonies are not available to latecomers, who must find different paths to development.

- **Introduction** — The entire framework and challenge to existing narratives
- **Chapter 5** — The role of coal
- **Chapter 6** — The role of the New World

- The detailed engagement with alternative explanations
