## Book Context & Background

The Great Divergence was published in 2000 at a moment when the rise of China and East Asia was reshaping the global economy and forcing a reconsideration of world history. The book emerged from the "California School" of world history — a group of historians (including R. Bin Wong, Jack Goldstone, and James Lee) who argued that earlier Eurocentric models of world history were fundamentally flawed. Pomeranz's book became the most influential statement of this revisionist perspective.

## About the Author

Kenneth Pomeranz (b. 1958) is an American historian of China. He received his PhD from Yale and taught at UC Irvine before becoming a professor at the University of Chicago, where he served as University Professor of History. His research focuses on late imperial China, economic history, and the history of global development. The Great Divergence won the John H. Dunning Prize from the American Historical Association and established him as a leading voice in world history.

## Core Thesis & Argument

Pomeranz's central argument is that the most developed parts of Europe and East Asia were at comparable levels in 1750-1800, and that the Industrial Revolution-driven divergence was caused not by European exceptionalism but by two contingent factors: Britain's proximity to coal deposits and Europe's access to New World resources through colonialism. Without these two factors, Europe would have faced the same ecological constraints that limited growth in Asia.

## Thematic Analysis

Three major themes structure the book. First, **comparability** — Pomeranz challenges the assumption of European exceptionalism by demonstrating the sophistication of Chinese economic development. Second, **contingency** — he argues that the Great Divergence was not inevitable but depended on specific geographic and historical circumstances. Third, **ecological limits** — he reframes the Industrial Revolution as an escape from ecological constraints rather than as the triumph of European ingenuity, emphasizing the role of fossil fuels and colonial extraction.

## Argumentation & Evidence

Pomeranz draws on a wide range of quantitative evidence: population data, agricultural productivity estimates, wage comparisons, life expectancy data, and trade statistics. He uses comparative regional analysis rather than continent-wide averages, comparing the Yangzi Delta with Britain and the Lower Yangzi with Northwestern Europe. His method is revisionist economic history, challenging existing estimates and offering new interpretations of the evidence.

## Strengths

1. **Fundamentally challenged Eurocentrism** — The book forced historians to reconsider narratives of Western superiority.
2. **Comparative regional analysis** — His method of comparing most-developed regions rather than whole continents set a new standard.
3. **Coal and colonies thesis** — The combination of these two factors provides a powerful, parsimonious explanation of the Great Divergence.
4. **Quantitative rigor** — The book marshals impressive empirical evidence to support its claims.
5. **Influenced policy debates** — The argument that European success was contingent has implications for contemporary development strategies.

## Criticisms & Weaknesses

1. **David S. Landes** — The eminent economic historian (The Wealth and Poverty of Nations) rejected Pomeranz's thesis, arguing that it underestimates the importance of European cultural and institutional advantages — particularly the Scientific Revolution, property rights, and the rule of law.

2. **Peer Vries** — The Dutch historian argued in essays and his book Escaping Poverty that Pomeranz overstates the comparability of Europe and China. Vries contended that European institutions — particularly the competitive state system and the growth of capitalist markets — gave Europe a genuine structural advantage.

3. **Jan Luiten van Zanden** — The economic historian argued that Pomeranz's claim of comparability is undermined by evidence of higher European productivity in key sectors and more advanced financial institutions, even before the Industrial Revolution.

4. **Jack Goldstone** — While broadly sympathetic to the California School, Goldstone argued in Why Europe? that Pomeranz's focus on coal and colonies is incomplete. Britain's political revolution of 1688 and its culture of scientific innovation were also crucial factors.

5. **Robert Brenner** — The Marxist historian argued that Pomeranz underestimates the importance of class relations and the agrarian capitalist transformation in England, which created a unique dynamic of economic development.

6. **Critics of the comparability thesis** — Some economic historians argue that the evidence for Chinese living standards comparable to Europe is weak or misinterpreted. The debate continues with new evidence from both sides.

## Comparative Analysis

Pomeranz's work is part of the larger revisionist movement in world history that includes Andre Gunder Frank's ReOrient (1998), R. Bin Wong's China Transformed (1997), and Jack Goldstone's Why Europe? (2008). It directly challenges the traditional narrative of European exceptionalism represented by David Landes, Eric Jones (The European Miracle), and Niall Ferguson (Civilization). The "California School" debate has reshaped the field of global economic history.

## Impact & Legacy

The Great Divergence is one of the most influential works of world history published in the last quarter century. It transformed debates about European exceptionalism, the Industrial Revolution, and the origins of global inequality. While its specific claims remain contested, the book's insistence on comparing Europe and Asia on equal terms has permanently reshaped the field. The rise of China in the twenty-first century has given its arguments new relevance.
