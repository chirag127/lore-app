## Writing Style & Voice

Pomeranz writes as a revisionist scholar self-consciously challenging established narratives. His prose is academic but engaged, with a clear argumentative thrust. He is careful and precise with evidence, anticipating objections and addressing counterarguments. The tone is confident but not combative — he presents his arguments as better interpretations of the evidence rather than as definitive refutations of his opponents.

## Narrative Structure

The book builds its argument cumulatively. The early chapters establish the empirical case for comparability between Europe and China. The middle chapters examine and dismiss alternative explanations (culture, institutions, science). The later chapters present Pomeranz's positive argument for coal and colonies. This structure — from problem to evidence to dismissal of alternatives to new explanation — is classic academic argumentation.

## Rhetorical Techniques

Pomeranz's most effective technique is the use of counterfactuals. He asks: what if Britain had not had coal? What if Europe had not had America? These thought experiments isolate the causal importance of his two key factors. He also uses the method of regional comparison to powerful effect, showing that the difference within Europe (between Britain and less developed regions) was as large as the difference between Europe and China.

## Readability & Accessibility

The book is demanding but accessible to a serious general reader. It assumes some familiarity with European and Chinese history but explains technical economic concepts. The quantitative material (tables, statistics) is manageable for non-specialists. The historiographical debates can be dense for readers unfamiliar with the literature. The book's main challenge is its length and complexity rather than its language.

## Comparative Context

The Great Divergence belongs to the revisionist tradition in world history that challenges Eurocentric narratives. It is more empirically rigorous than Andre Gunder Frank's ReOrient but shares its skepticism about European exceptionalism. In the context of this leaf, it connects to Edward Said's Orientalism (which critiques Western representations of the East) and to Thomas Friedman's The World Is Flat (which offers a contemporary perspective on global economic integration). The book can be read as the economic-historical foundation for the broader critique of Western-centric accounts of world development.
