The Lexus and the Olive Tree is Thomas Friedman's first major work on globalization, setting out the framework he would develop further in The World Is Flat. Published in 1999, the book introduces the central metaphor that defines the globalization era: the tension between the Lexus (modernity, economic integration, material prosperity) and the Olive Tree (tradition, identity, roots, community).

<BookHeader />

Friedman argues that globalization has replaced the Cold War as the dominant international system. He introduces vivid concepts — the Golden Straitjacket, the Electronic Herd, the Democratization of Technology — to describe the new global order. The book was widely praised for making globalization accessible to a general audience and widely criticized for its uncritical embrace of neoliberal economics. It remains essential reading for understanding the intellectual case for globalization at its peak.
