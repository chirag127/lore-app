## Book Context & Background

The Soldier and the State was published in 1957, during the Cold War and at the height of the Eisenhower administration's "New Look" defense policy emphasizing nuclear deterrence over conventional forces. The American military had emerged from World War II with unprecedented prestige and power, but the Korean War and McCarthy era had strained civil-military relations. Huntington wrote against a backdrop of anxiety about the "military-industrial complex" — a term yet to be popularized by Eisenhower's 1961 farewell address — and concern that the military's growing influence threatened American liberal democracy.

## About the Author

Samuel P. Huntington (1927-2008) wrote The Soldier and the State at age 30, already an associate professor at Harvard. The book grew out of his PhD dissertation and established his reputation as a major political theorist. Huntington's conservative realism — his willingness to defend military values against liberal individualism — made the book controversial within academia. He would later become known for Political Order in Changing Societies (1968) and The Clash of Civilizations (1996), but The Soldier and the State remains his most theoretically original work.

## Core Thesis & Argument

Huntington's central distinction is between objective and subjective civilian control. Subjective control maximizes civilian power over the military through fragmentation, politicization, and restriction. Objective control minimizes military power by making it professional — that is, autonomous within its sphere, politically neutral, and subordinate to civilian leadership in matters of policy. The key insight: a professional military that controls its own internal affairs is less likely to intervene in politics than one that is constantly interfered with. Paradoxically, giving the military autonomy in its professional sphere produces the most reliable civilian control.

## Thematic Analysis

Three major themes structure the book. First, **officership as a profession** — Huntington argues that the modern officer corps meets the criteria of a profession (expertise, responsibility, corporateness) and should be understood as such, not as a mere instrument of state power. Second, **the military mind** — he identifies a distinctive conservative worldview among professional officers, shaped by the functional requirements of security, that emphasizes the inevitability of conflict, the limits of human reason, and the value of hierarchy. Third, **the tension between security and liberty** — liberal societies inherently distrust military values, creating permanent tension in civil-military relations.

## Argumentation & Evidence

Huntington combines theoretical argument with historical case studies. Part I develops the theoretical framework deductively, deriving the characteristics of military professionalism from the nature of the military function. Part II applies this framework to American history from 1789 to 1940, showing how the military profession developed in tension with American liberalism. Part III examines the crisis of civil-military relations during and after World War II. The case studies of Germany and Japan provide comparative testing of his theory, though the evidence is selectively used to support rather than test the framework.

## Strengths

1. **Foundational theory of civil-military relations** — No previous work had systematically theorized the relationship between military professionalism and civilian control.
2. **Objective vs. subjective control distinction** — This remains the central conceptual framework in the field.
3. **Explained American exceptionalism** — Huntington showed how America's liberal culture created distinctive patterns of civil-military tension.
4. **Historical depth** — The analysis of American military development from the Founding to the Cold War is magisterial.
5. **Enduring relevance** — The framework continues to inform debates about civil-military relations in democracies worldwide.

## Criticisms & Weaknesses

1. **Peter Feaver** — The leading contemporary scholar of civil-military relations argued in Armed Servants (2003) that Huntington's professional model is insufficient. Feaver's agency theory — treating civil-military relations as a principal-agent problem — offers a more nuanced framework for understanding when and why the military obeys civilian authority.

2. **Morris Janowitz** — The sociologist's The Professional Soldier (1960) challenged Huntington's ideal-type of the professional officer. Janowitz argued that the modern military was becoming more civilianized, not more distinct, and that Huntington overemphasized the gap between military and civilian values.

3. **Richard Kohn** — The historian criticized Huntington for underestimating the dangers of military autonomy, arguing that a fully professional military could become a "state within a state." Kohn's historical work on American civil-military relations presented evidence that military professionals are not inherently politically neutral.

4. **Suzanne Nielsen and Don Snider** — These scholars argued that Huntington's conception of military expertise as "the management of violence" is too narrow. Modern military professionalism requires expertise in diplomacy, economics, politics, and culture, not just the application of force.

5. **Feminist and critical scholars** — Critics have noted that Huntington's theory ignores gender dynamics in the military and assumes a masculine model of professionalism that excludes women. Cynthia Enloe's work on militarization challenges the gender-neutral assumptions of Huntington's framework.

6. **Samuel Finer** — The British political scientist argued in The Man on Horseback (1962) that Huntington overstates the constraining power of professionalism. Finer showed numerous cases where professional militaries intervened in politics, suggesting that professionalism alone does not guarantee civilian control.

## Comparative Analysis

The Soldier and the State created the modern field of civil-military relations. It stands alongside Morris Janowitz's The Professional Soldier as the two foundational texts. Later scholars like Peter Feaver, Eliot Cohen (Supreme Command), and Michael Desch have refined, challenged, and extended Huntington's framework. The book also connects to broader debates in political theory about the relationship between liberalism, democracy, and military power — debates carried forward by thinkers like Michael Walzer (Just and Unjust Wars) and Samuel Finer.

## Impact & Legacy

The Soldier and the State is the most cited work on civil-military relations and remains required reading at military academies and in political science departments. Its concept of objective civilian control shaped US defense policy during the Cold War and continues to inform debates about the proper relationship between military and civilian leadership. The book's influence is evident in the post-Vietnam reforms that strengthened military professionalism, the debates about Don't Ask Don't Tell, and contemporary concerns about politicization of the military.
