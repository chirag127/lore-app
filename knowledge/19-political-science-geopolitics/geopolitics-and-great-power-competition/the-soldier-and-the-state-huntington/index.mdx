The Soldier and the State is the foundational text in the field of civil-military relations and remains the most widely cited work on the subject more than six decades after publication. Samuel Huntington developed the theory of "objective civilian control" — the idea that the best way to control the military is not to restrict it but to professionalize it, creating an officer corps that is expert, responsible, and politically neutral.

<BookHeader />

The book is divided into three parts: a theoretical framework defining officership as a profession and the military mind as a distinct worldview; a historical analysis of American civil-military relations from 1789 to 1940; and an examination of the revolution in civil-military relations during and after World War II. Huntington's central insight — that the military profession has its own distinctive values of obedience, hierarchy, and duty that are in tension with liberal individualism — challenged the prevailing view that military values should be subordinated to civilian norms.
