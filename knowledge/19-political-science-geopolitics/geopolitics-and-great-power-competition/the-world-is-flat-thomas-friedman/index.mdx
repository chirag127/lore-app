The World Is Flat is Thomas Friedman's most famous and influential book. Published in 2005, it became an international bestseller and shaped how a generation of business leaders, policymakers, and ordinary readers understood the transformative power of globalization in the digital age.

<BookHeader />

Friedman's central metaphor — the flattening of the world — captures his thesis that digital connectivity, the internet, offshoring, and global supply chains have created a level playing field where individuals and small companies can compete globally. The book identifies ten "flatteners" — from Netscape's IPO to open-source software to work-flow automation — that converged around the year 2000 to create a new global economic landscape. While celebrated for its accessible and optimistic vision of globalization, the book has also been criticized for its technological determinism, its neglect of globalization's discontents, and its overly America-centric perspective.
