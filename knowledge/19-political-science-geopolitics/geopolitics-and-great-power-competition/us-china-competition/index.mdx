# US-China Competition

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

The US-China relationship is the most consequential bilateral dyad in contemporary international politics. The rise of China as a peer competitor challenges the unipolar moment that followed the Cold War and raises fundamental questions about the future of the international order. Scholars and strategists debate whether the competition will lead to war (the Thucydides Trap), peaceful coexistence, or a managed Cold War-like stalemate.

Graham Allison's *Destined for War* popularized the Thucydides Trap framework, applying the pattern of past power transitions to the US-China case. John Mearsheimer's offensive realism predicts inevitable conflict, while liberal and constructivist theorists hold out hope for institutional and ideational convergence. Aaron Friedberg, Michael Beckley, and Elizabeth Economy have analyzed the competition's economic, technological, and ideological dimensions. The field encompasses trade war dynamics, technology decoupling (especially semiconductors, AI, 5G), the Taiwan flashpoint, South China Sea disputes, and the battle for influence in the developing world (Belt and Road Initiative vs competing infrastructure projects).

## Key Books

1. Graham Allison, *Destined for War: Can America and China Escape Thucydides's Trap?* — The defining framework for power transition between the US and China.
2. John J. Mearsheimer, *The Great Delusion: Liberal Dreams and International Realities* — Offensive realist analysis of why China will seek regional hegemony.
3. Aaron L. Friedberg, *Weary Titan: Britain and the Experience of Relative Decline* — Historical analogies for US-China competition (also *Contest for Supremacy*).
4. Michael Beckley, *Unrivaled: Why America Will Remain the World's Sole Superpower* — Counter-argument to China-threat narratives.
5. Elizabeth C. Economy, *The Third Revolution* — Xi Jinping's China and the transformation of the party-state system.
6. Hal Brands & John Lewis Gaddis, "The New Cold War: A New History of the New Cold War" — The emerging consensus on US-China rivalry.
7. Kurt M. Campbell & Jake Sullivan, "Competition Without Catastrophe" (Foreign Affairs) — The Biden administration's framework for managed competition.
8. Susan L. Shirk, *China: Fragile Superpower* — China's internal vulnerabilities as constraints on its external ambition.
9. Orville Schell & Susan L. Shirk (eds.), *US-China Relations: Perilous Past, Uncertain Present* — Comprehensive collection from leading experts.
10. Rush Doshi, *The Long Game: China's Grand Strategy to Displace American Order* — Systematic analysis of China's strategy over three decades.
