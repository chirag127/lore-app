## Book Context & Background

Who Are We? was published in 2004, three years after the September 11 attacks, at a time when American patriotism was at a peak but also when debates about immigration and national identity were intensifying. The book appeared during the second Bush administration, as the Iraq War was underway and as Congress debated immigration reform. It contributed to a broader national conversation about assimilation, multiculturalism, and what it means to be American — a conversation that included works like Samuel Huntington's earlier The Clash of Civilizations and Arthur Schlesinger Jr.'s The Disuniting of America.

## About the Author

Samuel P. Huntington wrote Who Are We? as the culmination of his career-long concern with political order and cultural identity. Aged 77 at publication, he was at the end of his intellectual journey from the institutional analysis of Political Order in Changing Societies through the civilizational framework of The Clash of Civilizations to the national focus of Who Are We?. The book represents his most direct engagement with American domestic politics and his most controversial intervention in public debate.

## Core Thesis & Argument

Huntington's central claim is that American national identity is not primarily ideological (the American Creed) but cultural (Anglo-Protestant heritage). He argues that mass Mexican immigration threatens to bifurcate American society and that cosmopolitan elites are abandoning national identity. The book's most provocative argument is that previous waves of immigration succeeded because immigrants assimilated to a dominant Anglo-Protestant culture, but current immigration — particularly from Mexico — is different in kind, threatening the cultural foundations of American unity.

## Thematic Analysis

Four major themes structure the book. First, **the primacy of culture** — Huntington argues that shared culture, not shared ideology, is what holds nations together. Second, **the distinctiveness of Mexican immigration** — he isolates six factors that make Mexican immigration unique and potentially more dangerous to national unity. Third, **the failure of multiculturalism** — he argues that celebrating diversity undermines the common culture necessary for national cohesion. Fourth, **the role of external threat** — he suggests that confronting hostile forces may be necessary to revitalize national identity.

## Argumentation & Evidence

Huntington draws on historical analysis, demographic data, sociological studies, survey research, and comparative immigration studies. His evidence includes census data on language retention, surveys of ethnic identity, and historical comparisons of assimilation patterns. However, critics argue that his evidence is selective — he cites data suggesting Mexican-American assimilation is slower while downplaying evidence of progress, and his historical comparisons with earlier European immigration waves are not controlled for relevant differences.

## Strengths

1. **Raised important questions** — Huntington forced a conversation about national identity and assimilation that many preferred to avoid.
2. **Historical depth** — His analysis of American identity formation from the colonial period onward is genuinely scholarly.
3. **Identified genuine challenges** — The persistence of linguistic enclaves, the scale of unauthorized immigration, and the transnational orientations of elites are real phenomena.
4. **Provoked productive debate** — Critics and supporters alike refined their positions in response to Huntington's arguments.
5. **Predicted political developments** — The nativist turn in American politics (the Tea Party, Trumpism) confirmed Huntington's thesis that identity concerns would become central.

## Criticisms & Weaknesses

1. **Amitai Etzioni** — The sociologist argued in Contemporary Sociology that Huntington's evidence does not support his claims about the decline of English use or the threat to American values. Etzioni pointed out that data on language retention showed robust English acquisition by the third generation.

2. **Alan Wolfe** — The political scientist writing in The New York Times called Huntington's argument "nativist" and "alarmist," arguing that the book "tells us more about Huntington's fears than about the state of American identity." Wolfe noted that similar fears have accompanied every wave of immigration in American history.

3. **Tamar Jacoby** — The immigration scholar argued that Huntington's analysis ignores the dynamics of assimilation that have historically worked, including among Mexican-Americans. She noted that intermarriage rates, English acquisition, and economic integration were all progressing.

4. **Michele Wucker** — Writing in Foreign Policy, Wucker argued that Huntington's focus on culture rather than policy was misleading. She contended that the real challenges of immigration are about economic integration, legal status, and social mobility, not cultural identity.

5. **Critics of Anglo-Protestant framing** — Scholars of Native American and African American history noted that Huntington's celebration of Anglo-Protestant culture ignores the brutal history of racial oppression. His framing of "settlers" legitimately creating culture versus "immigrants" joining it erases the violent dispossession of indigenous peoples and the forced migration of enslaved Africans.

6. **Methodological criticisms** — Demographers pointed out that Huntington's projections assumed current trends would continue indefinitely without considering how assimilation dynamics change over time and across generations.

## Comparative Analysis

Who Are We? extends Huntington's civilizational framework from The Clash of Civilizations to the domestic sphere. Where the earlier book analyzed global inter-civilizational conflict, this one examines intra-national civilizational dynamics. It connects to broader literatures on nationalism (Benedict Anderson's Imagined Communities, Ernest Gellner's Nations and Nationalism), immigration and assimilation (Nathan Glazer's Beyond the Melting Pot, Richard Alba's Remaking the American Mainstream), and American political development (Rogers Smith's Civic Ideals).

## Impact & Legacy

The book was widely reviewed and debated, becoming a touchstone in the immigration debate. Its arguments anticipated the rise of populist nationalism in American politics, and it is frequently cited by those who advocate for reduced immigration and stronger assimilation requirements. Critics accuse it of providing intellectual cover for nativist policies. The book's influence persists in ongoing debates about national identity, multiculturalism, and immigration reform.
