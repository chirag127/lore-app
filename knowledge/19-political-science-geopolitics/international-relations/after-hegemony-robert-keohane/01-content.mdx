## Part I: Puzzles of Cooperation

### Chapter 1: The Challenge of Cooperation
Keohane frames the central question: can cooperation occur without a hegemon? He distinguishes cooperation from harmony (where interests automatically coincide) and from discord. Cooperation requires active coordination to adjust behavior to others' preferences.

### Chapter 2: The Problem of Cooperation Under Anarchy
Keohane uses game theory, particularly the Prisoners Dilemma, to analyze why cooperation is difficult under anarchy. States have incentives to defect, but they also have incentives to cooperate if they expect reciprocation. Iteration and information are keys to sustaining cooperation.

## Part II: The Emergence of International Regimes

### Chapter 3: Hegemonic Leadership and Regime Creation
Keohane acknowledges that hegemonic leadership facilitated the creation of post-war regimes (Bretton Woods, GATT, IMF). The United States, as post-1945 hegemon, had both the power and the interest to establish liberal international economic order.

### Chapter 4: Regimes as Autonomous Forces
This chapter argues that regimes develop autonomous effects. Once created, they change state expectations, create linkages among issues, and provide focal points for coordination. They become independent factors in the calculation of state interests.

## Part III: A Functional Theory of International Regimes

### Chapter 5: A Functional Theory of Regimes
The theoretical core of the book. Keohane argues that regimes are rational responses to the problems of cooperation under anarchy. They serve states' interests by reducing uncertainty, providing information, lowering transaction costs, and facilitating issue-linkage and reciprocity. This is a functional explanation: regimes exist because of what they do for states.

### Chapter 6: Regimes and the Rational Calculation of Interests
Regimes change calculations of interest by lengthening the shadow of the future, reducing fears of exploitation, and providing forums for negotiation. They make it easier to cooperate today by creating expectations of future cooperation.

## Part IV: Applications and Implications

### Chapter 7: The International Petroleum Regime
Keohane tests his theory against the history of international oil cooperation, from the Seven Sisters through OPEC. The oil regime evolved from private corporate governance to intergovernmental coordination. The case demonstrates both the power of regimes and their dependence on state interests.

### Chapter 8: International Trade and Finance Regimes
The cases of GATT and the Bretton Woods monetary system show how regimes sustained cooperation after American hegemony began to decline in the 1970s. Despite predictions of collapse, trade liberalization continued and monetary coordination was maintained.

## Part V: Conclusions and Implications

### Chapter 9: The Politics of Cooperation
Keohane summarizes the argument: cooperation after hegemony is possible if states share interests that regimes can help realize. Hegemons are useful but not necessary. The book's final argument is a plea for institutionalized cooperation as a rational response to interdependence.

### Chapter 10: Policy Implications
The concluding chapter draws lessons for American foreign policy. The United States should maintain and strengthen international regimes even as its relative power declines. Unilateralism is self-defeating; institutions serve American interests.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment
The summary captures the main theoretical argument and case studies but necessarily simplifies the game-theoretic analysis and regime theory.

| Reader Type | Time | What to Read |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | ~15 min | Introduction, Chapters 1, 5, 9 |

| Scholar/Practitioner | ~10-15 hr | Full book |

- **Chapter 5** — A Functional Theory of Regimes
- **Chapter 3** — Hegemonic Leadership
- **Chapter 9** — The Politics of Cooperation

### Chapters to Skim or Skip
- **Chapters 7-8** (case studies, valuable for specialists)
