## Book Context & Background

Published in 1984, *After Hegemony* was a response to the perceived decline of American power after Vietnam and the oil shocks. The heyday of American hegemony (1945-1970) was over; the question was whether the institutions built under that hegemony would survive. The book also responded to the revival of realist theory in Waltz's *Theory of International Politics* (1979) and Gilpin's *War and Change in World Politics* (1981), both of which argued that international order depends on the distribution of power.

## About the Author

Robert O. Keohane (born 1941) is one of the most influential IR scholars of the late twentieth century. He studied at Shimer College and Harvard, taught at Swarthmore, Stanford, and Duke, and has been a professor at Princeton's Woodrow Wilson School. His earlier work with Joseph Nye, *Power and Interdependence* (1977), established the concept of complex interdependence. *After Hegemony* represents the culmination of the liberal institutionalist research program.

## Core Thesis & Argument

Cooperation can persist after hegemonic decline because international institutions have autonomous effects. Institutions reduce the costs of reaching and enforcing agreements, provide information about state compliance, facilitate issue linkage, and support strategies of reciprocity. Cooperation is rational for states even without a hegemon, provided the expected benefits of cooperation exceed the costs.

## Thematic Analysis

**The Functional Theory of Regimes.** The book's most original contribution. Keohane argues that regimes exist because they perform functions for states, reducing transaction costs and information asymmetries. This is explicitly analogized to the theory of the firm in economics.

**Reciprocity.** The mechanism that sustains cooperation without enforcement. Keohane draws on Axelrod's work to show that strategies of tit-for-tat can sustain cooperation in iterated Prisoners Dilemma games.

**The Shadow of the Future.** Cooperation is more likely when states expect to interact repeatedly. Institutions lengthen this shadow by creating ongoing relationships and linking issues.

## Argumentation & Evidence

Keohane uses rational choice and game theory for deductive analysis, combined with historical case studies. The cases of oil, trade, and monetary regimes test the theory. The method is explicitly social-scientific: generate hypotheses from theory, test against evidence, and refine the theory. This made *After Hegemony* the model for the liberal institutionalist research program.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Precision.** Keohane provides a clear, testable theory of cooperation under anarchy. The functional theory of regimes is logically rigorous.

**Empirical Grounding.** The case studies provide genuine evidence for the theory, not merely illustrative examples.

**Policy Relevance.** The book provides a rationale for maintaining international institutions. It influenced the debate about post-Cold War order.

## Criticisms & Weaknesses

**Stephen Krasner (Stanford).** Krasner argues from a realist perspective that Keohane underestimates the role of power. Institutions reflect the interests of the powerful and cannot operate independently of the distribution of power.

**John Mearsheimer (University of Chicago).** Mearsheimer's "The False Promise of International Institutions" (1994-95) argues that institutions have at most marginal effects on state behavior. States will defect when their security is at stake, regardless of institutional commitments.

**Robert Gilpin (Princeton).** Gilpin argues that the functional theory is circular: regimes explain cooperation but cooperation is used to explain regimes. The theory lacks an independent measure of regime effects.

**Andrew Hurrell (Oxford).** Hurrell argues that Keohane's rational choice framework is too narrow. It cannot explain the role of norms, legitimacy, and socialization in sustaining cooperation.

## Comparative Analysis

*After Hegemony* defines neoliberal institutionalism in opposition to both realism (which denies the independent importance of institutions) and earlier liberalism (which had not grounded its claims in rational choice theory). It explicitly engages with Waltz's structural realism, accepting the anarchic premise while challenging the conclusion about conflict. It also builds on Keohane and Nye's earlier work on complex interdependence.

## Impact & Legacy

The book is the most cited work in the liberal institutionalist tradition. It shaped the study of international regimes and global governance. It provided the theoretical foundation for the research program on international institutions that dominated American IR in the 1980s and 1990s. The debate between Mearsheimer (realism) and Keohane (institutionalism) defined the terms of IR theory in the post-Cold War period.
