## Writing Style & Voice

Keohane writes in the American social science tradition: clear, systematic, and methodologically self-conscious. The prose is direct and functional, prioritizing precision over elegance. Unlike Morgenthau or Bull, Keohane is writing for an academic audience trained in social science methods. The voice is that of a scientist testing hypotheses, not a philosopher reflecting on human nature.

## Narrative Structure

The book is structured like a social science dissertation: problem statement, theory, hypotheses, case studies, conclusions. The argument moves from the abstract (game theory and functional explanation) to the concrete (historical cases of oil, trade, and monetary regimes). This structure reflects the book's origin in Keohane's effort to put liberal institutionalism on a firm theoretical foundation.

## Rhetorical Techniques

Keohane's primary rhetorical strategy is the logical demonstration that cooperation is rational even without a hegemon. He uses game theory to show why cooperation is difficult but possible, then uses case studies to show that it actually occurs. His secondary strategy is the systematic engagement with realist counterarguments. He concedes the realist premise (anarchy) while challenging the realist conclusion (conflict).

## Readability & Accessibility

The book is accessible to graduate students and advanced undergraduates in political science. The game-theoretic sections require some patience but no formal training in game theory. The case studies are clearly presented. The book is less accessible to general readers than Morgenthau or Bull, but the core argument is stated in accessible language in the first and last chapters.

## Comparative Context

*After Hegemony* defines neoliberal institutionalism alongside Keohane and Nye's *Power and Interdependence*. It belongs to the rational choice tradition in IR, alongside the work of Axelrod, Oye, and Snidal. Compared to Waltz's neorealism, Keohane's book is more optimistic about cooperation and more supportive of international institutions. Compared to Bull's English School, it is less philosophical and more social-scientific, less concerned with the society of states and more with rational calculation.
