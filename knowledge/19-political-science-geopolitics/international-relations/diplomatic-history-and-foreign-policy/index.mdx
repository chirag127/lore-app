# Diplomatic History & Foreign Policy

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Diplomacy is the oldest institutionalized form of interaction between political communities. The study of diplomatic history and foreign policy examines how states formulate and execute their external strategies, how negotiations succeed or fail, and how the practice of statecraft has evolved. Foreign policy analysis (FPA) — a distinct subfield within IR — opens the black box of the state to examine the psychological, organizational, and domestic-political determinants of foreign policy decisions.

Classical diplomatic history, represented by works of Henry Kissinger, Paul Kennedy, and A.J.P. Taylor, traces the evolution of the European state system and the balance of power. The modern study of foreign policy decision-making was transformed by Graham Allison's *Essence of Decision* (the bureaucratic politics model) and by cognitive approaches pioneered by Robert Jervis, Irving Janis (groupthink), and Alexander George (operational code). More recent work examines diplomatic practice, digital diplomacy, public diplomacy, and the role of non-state actors in diplomatic processes. Grand strategy — the integration of military, economic, and diplomatic instruments to achieve national objectives — has seen a major revival in scholarship.

## Key Books

1. Henry Kissinger, *Diplomacy* — The magisterial sweep of diplomatic history from Richelieu to the post-Cold War era.
2. Graham T. Allison, *Essence of Decision: Explaining the Cuban Missile Crisis* — The canonical framework of rational actor, organizational process, and bureaucratic politics models.
3. Robert Jervis, *Perception and Misperception in International Politics* — The foundational work on the psychology of foreign policy decision-making.
4. Paul Kennedy, *The Rise and Fall of the Great Powers* — Historical analysis of the relationship between economic strength and strategic overreach.
5. Alexander L. George, *Bridging the Gap: Theory and Practice in Foreign Policy* — The operational code and theory-practice integration.
6. Irving L. Janis, *Groupthink: Psychological Studies of Policy Decisions and Fiascoes* — How group dynamics produce flawed foreign policy decisions.
7. Harold Nicolson, *The Evolution of Diplomatic Method* — Classic history of diplomatic practice from ancient Greece to the 20th century.
8. Andrew Moravcsik, "Taking Preferences Seriously" — The liberal theory of foreign policy rooted in domestic societal interests.
9. Joseph S. Nye Jr., *Soft Power: The Means to Success in World Politics* — The concept of attraction-based influence in foreign policy.
10. John Lewis Gaddis, *Strategies of Containment* — The definitive study of US grand strategy during the Cold War.
