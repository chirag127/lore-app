# International Organizations

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

International organizations (IOs) are the institutional architecture of global governance. The United Nations, World Trade Organization, International Monetary Fund, World Bank, NATO, European Union, and hundreds of other IOs shape state behavior through rules, norms, monitoring, and material assistance. The question of whether and how IOs matter — whether they genuinely constrain state behavior or merely reflect power — is central to the debate between realists, liberal institutionalists, and constructivists.

Robert Keohane's *After Hegemony* (1984) provided the foundational liberal theory: hegemonic power may create IOs, but once established they facilitate cooperation even after the hegemon declines. Scholars such as Michael Barnett and Martha Finnemore (constructivist analysis of IO bureaucracy), Kenneth Abbott and Duncan Snidal (rational design of IOs), and Andrew Moravcsik (liberal intergovernmentalism in the EU) have deepened the field. Contemporary work examines the backlash against IOs, rising authoritarian influence in multilateral institutions, and IO adaptation to new challenges such as climate change, pandemics, and AI governance.

## What Belongs Here

- Theories of IO design and effectiveness
- The United Nations system (General Assembly, Security Council, Secretariat, agencies)
- International financial institutions (IMF, World Bank, regional development banks)
- The WTO and international trade governance
- NATO and collective security organizations
- The European Union as a unique political entity
- Regional organizations (AU, ASEAN, OAS, SAARC)
- G7, G20, BRICS, and informal governance groupings
- IO reform and legitimacy
- Compliance, enforcement, and dispute settlement
- IO bureaucracy and autonomy

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- IR theory abstracted from institutional analysis (see Realism, Liberalism & Constructivism)
- Non-governmental organizations and transnational advocacy (see Civil Society & Social Movements)
- International law as a legal system (see International Law in the Law taxonomy)
- Specific country foreign policies toward IOs (see Diplomatic History & Foreign Policy)
- Global governance of specific issues (see Digital Governance & Technology Policy)

## Key Books

1. Robert O. Keohane, *After Hegemony: Cooperation and Discord in the World Political Economy* — The foundational liberal institutionalist theory of IO-facilitated cooperation.
2. Michael Barnett & Martha Finnemore, *Rules for the World* — Constructivist analysis of IO bureaucracies and their pathologies.
3. Kenneth W. Abbott & Duncan Snidal, "Why States Act Through Formal International Organizations" — The rational design of IOs.
4. Andrew Moravcsik, *The Choice for Europe* — Liberal intergovernmentalist theory of European integration.
5. Lisa L. Martin, *Coercive Cooperation* — The role of multilateral economic sanctions in IO-mediated cooperation.
6. Barbara Koremenos, Charles Lipson & Duncan Snidal, "The Rational Design of International Institutions" — Systematic variation in IO institutional features.
7. John G. Ikenberry, *Liberal Leviathan* — The United States, the liberal international order, and IO architecture.
8. Roland Paris, *At War's End* — IO-led state-building after conflict.
9. Judith Goldstein & Robert O. Keohane (eds.), *Ideas and Foreign Policy* — How ideas and norms embedded in IOs shape state behavior.
10. Ian Hurd, *International Organizations: Politics, Law, Practice* — The leading contemporary textbook on IO politics and law.
