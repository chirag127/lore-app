## Chapter 1: Introduction

Waltz introduces the "levels of analysis" framework. The three images correspond to three levels at which we can locate the causes of war: within the individual human being (first image), within the structure of the state (second image), and within the international system (third image). The book examines major thinkers who represent each image.

## Chapter 2: The First Image: International Conflict and Human Behavior

Waltz examines thinkers who locate the causes of war in human nature: St. Augustine (original sin), Reinhold Niebuhr (human pride and will to power), Hans Morgenthau (animus dominandi), and behavioral scientists who see aggression as rooted in frustration or faulty learning. He finds that while human nature contributes to war, it is a constant factor that cannot explain why war occurs in some times and places but not others.

## Chapter 3: Some Implications of the First Image: Behavioral Sciences and the Reduction of Interstate Violence

Waltz critiques the policy implications of the first image: programs for education, psychological conditioning, and international understanding. He argues that changing human behavior, even if possible, would not eliminate war because the structure of the international system would still generate conflicts even among well-intentioned actors.

## Chapter 4: The Second Image: International Conflict and the Internal Structure of States

Thinkers who locate the causes of war in the internal structure of states: liberal internationalists (democracies are peaceful, autocracies are warlike), Marxists (capitalism causes war, socialism will bring peace), and various reformers who argue that the right form of government would eliminate war. Waltz examines Kant's democratic peace thesis, Lenin's theory of imperialism, and mid-century claims about the peace-promoting effects of international organizations.

## Chapter 5: Some Implications of the Second Image: International Socialism and the Coming of the First World War

Waltz tests second-image theories against the historical record. He examines whether socialist states are more peaceful, whether economic interdependence reduces war, and whether the spread of democracy would eliminate conflict. He finds the evidence mixed and argues that even if all states were democratic or socialist, they would still face security dilemmas generated by anarchy.

## Chapter 6: The Third Image: International Conflict and International Anarchy

The central theoretical chapter. Waltz argues that the condition of anarchy—the absence of a supreme authority above states—is the permissive cause of war. No matter how peaceful the individuals or how benign the states, the security dilemma inherent in anarchy generates competition, suspicion, and occasional war. Nuclear weapons, he suggests, may paradoxically reduce war by making the costs catastrophic.

## Chapter 7: Some Implications of the Third Image: Examples from Economics, Politics, and History

Waltz illustrates the third image with case studies from economic competition, political rivalry, and diplomatic history. He shows how even actors who prefer peace can find themselves in conflict because they cannot be certain of others' intentions and must prepare for the worst.

## Chapter 8: Conclusion

Waltz synthesizes the three images. Each image is necessary but insufficient alone. The first image identifies the behavioral source of war; the second identifies the political organization of the state; but the third identifies the condition—anarchy—that permits these other factors to produce war. A complete theory of war must incorporate all three images and their interactions.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment
This summary captures the three-images framework and Waltz's main conclusions but cannot convey the richness of the philosophical analysis or the depth of engagement with individual thinkers.

### Recommended Reading Path
| Reader Type | Time | What to Read |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | ~15 min | Introduction + Chapter 6 + Conclusion |
| Interested | ~3-4 hr | Chapters 1, 2, 4, 6, 8 |
| Scholar/Practitioner | ~8-10 hr | Full book |

### Chapters to Read in Full
- **Chapter 6** — The third image argument
- **Chapter 8** — The synthesis

### Chapters to Skim or Skip
- **Chapter 3** (behavioral science, somewhat dated)

### What You'll Miss by Not Reading the Full Book
The detailed engagement with philosophers and theorists, the development of ideas that would later become neorealism, and the logical structure of the argument.
