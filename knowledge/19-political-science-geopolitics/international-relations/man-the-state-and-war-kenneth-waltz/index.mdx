*Man, the State, and War* (1959) is Kenneth Waltz's first and most philosophical work. Published when Waltz was a young scholar at Columbia University, the book established the "levels of analysis" framework that would become foundational to international relations theory. Waltz examines how different thinkers across the Western tradition have answered the question "What are the causes of war?" and identifies three distinct images: the first image blames human nature or bad leaders; the second image blames the internal structure of states; and the third image blames the anarchic character of the international system.

Waltz does not argue that any single image is correct. Rather, he argues that the third image provides the "permissive condition" that enables the first two images to operate. Without the condition of international anarchy, the behavioral causes identified by the first two images would not lead to war. The book is a philosophical tour de force, engaging with thinkers from St. Augustine and Hobbes to Kant, Lenin, and contemporary behavioral scientists.
