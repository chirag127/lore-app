*Politics Among Nations: The Struggle for Power and Peace* (1948) is the most important single book in the classical realist tradition. Hans J. Morgenthau, a German-Jewish emigre who fled Nazi persecution and became the preeminent American IR scholar of his generation, wrote the book to provide a systematic framework for understanding international politics. It became the dominant textbook in American IR for two decades and shaped a generation of foreign policy practitioners.

The book is organized around Morgenthau's "Six Principles of Political Realism," which argue that politics is governed by objective laws rooted in human nature; that states act in their national interest defined as power; that the concept of interest is universally valid; that universal moral principles cannot be applied to state action; that moral aspirations of nations differ from universal moral principles; and that the political sphere is autonomous from other spheres of life.
