# Realism, Liberalism & Constructivism

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

International relations theory provides the conceptual tools for understanding the anarchic international system, the behavior of states, and the possibilities for cooperation and conflict. Three paradigms dominate the field. Realism, with roots in Thucydides and Machiavelli and modern articulation by Hans Morgenthau and Kenneth Waltz, emphasizes power, security competition, and the constraints of international anarchy. Liberalism, drawing on Kant and modernized by Robert Keohane and Andrew Moravcsik, highlights interdependence, international institutions, and domestic politics. Constructivism, pioneered by Alexander Wendt, focuses on how shared ideas, norms, and identities shape state interests and the structure of international society.

The debate among these paradigms is not merely academic. Different theoretical lenses lead to different policy prescriptions: realists counsel prudence and power balancing; liberals advocate institutional engagement and democracy promotion; constructivists emphasize norm diffusion and diplomatic engagement. Contemporary scholarship increasingly seeks synthesis, applying insights from all three to complex problems such as the rise of China, climate governance, and cyber conflict.

## Key Books

1. Hans J. Morgenthau, *Politics Among Nations* — The classical realist statement of power politics and the national interest.
2. Kenneth N. Waltz, *Theory of International Politics* — The foundational text of structural/neorealist theory.
3. Robert O. Keohane, *After Hegemony* — The liberal institutionalist argument for international cooperation.
4. Alexander Wendt, *Social Theory of International Politics* — The constructivist reconceptualization of anarchy as "what states make of it."
5. Robert O. Keohane (ed.), *Neorealism and Its Critics* — The classic debate framing between realism and liberalism.
6. John J. Mearsheimer, *The Tragedy of Great Power Politics* — Offensive realism: states maximize relative power.
7. Andrew Moravcsik, "Taking Preferences Seriously" — Liberal IR theory grounded in domestic societal preferences.
8. Hedley Bull, *The Anarchical Society* — The English School synthesis of realism and liberalism.
9. Martha Finnemore & Kathryn Sikkink, "International Norm Dynamics and Political Change" — Constructivist analysis of how norms evolve.
10. J. Ann Tickner, *Gender in International Relations* — Foundational feminist critique of IR theory.
