## Book Context & Background

Published in 1977, *The Anarchical Society* was a response to both the behavioral revolution in American IR (which Bull saw as excessively scientific) and the radical critiques of the states system from dependency theory and world-systems theory. The English School tradition, developed at the London School of Economics and Oxford, offered a via media between realism and liberalism. Where realists emphasized conflict under anarchy and liberals emphasized cooperation through institutions, Bull argued that states already formed a society with shared norms and institutions, producing order without world government.

## About the Author

Hedley Bull (1932-1985) was an Australian-born international relations scholar who taught at the London School of Economics, Oxford, and the Australian National University. He was the leading figure of the English School, alongside Martin Wight and Herbert Butterfield. Bull's earlier work focused on strategic studies and arms control; *The Anarchical Society* was his magnum opus. His approach combined philosophical analysis, legal reasoning, and diplomatic history.

## Core Thesis & Argument

The central claim is that an international society exists—a group of states that are conscious of common interests and values, bound by shared rules, and cooperating through common institutions. This society produces order without world government. The key institutions are the balance of power, international law, diplomacy, war (as a regulated practice), and great power management. Order is distinct from justice, and the two values may conflict. The states system is stable because no feasible alternative provides a better balance of order and justice.

## Thematic Analysis

**International Society.** The concept that distinguishes the English School from both realism (which denies society) and liberalism (which believes institutions can transform anarchy). For Bull, society is real but limited: states share interests in survival and predictability, but they do not share the dense normative consensus of domestic societies.

**Institutions of Order.** Bull's analysis of the five institutions is his most distinctive contribution. Each institution is analyzed both functionally (how it maintains order) and historically (how it has evolved). The discussion of war as an institution is particularly subtle.

**Order vs. Justice.** The book's most enduringly relevant theme. Bull argues that the pursuit of justice (whether for oppressed peoples, individual rights, or economic equality) may threaten the international order that makes any justice at all possible. This tension is irresolvable in theory and manageable only through prudence in practice.

## Argumentation & Evidence

Bull's method is philosophical and historical. He draws on the history of international relations since the Peace of Westphalia, on international law, and on diplomatic practice. The argument is not systematically tested in the social-scientific sense but is illustrated through reference to historical examples. The book's strength is its synthetic integration of legal, historical, and political analysis.

## Strengths

**Conceptual Sophistication.** The book provides the most nuanced framework for thinking about order, justice, and international society. The distinctions among system, society, and world society are foundational.

**Institutional Analysis.** Bull's analysis of the five institutions remains unmatched in its combination of functional argument and historical evidence.

**Middle Path.** The book offers a compelling alternative to the extremes of realism and liberalism, one that recognizes both the reality of power and the possibility of cooperation.

## Criticisms & Weaknesses

**Andrew Linklater (Aberystwyth).** Linklater, a leading critical theorist, argues that Bull's international society is complicit in the exclusion and oppression of non-European peoples. The society of states that Bull describes is a product of European colonialism; extending it to the global South required suppressing the very norms of justice Bull recognizes as important.

**Robert W. Cox (York University).** Cox argues that Bull's concept of international society naturalizes the existing power structure. The institutions Bull describes serve the interests of the powerful; order without justice is simply the maintenance of inequality.

**Robert Keohane (Princeton).** Keohane argues that Bull's analysis of institutions is too limited. He excludes economic institutions (markets, trade regimes) and underestimates the role of formal international organizations. Bull's five institutions are "constitutive" rather than "regulative" in Keohane's terminology.

**Cynthia Enloe (Clark University).** Feminist critics argue that Bull's conception of international society ignores gender. The institutions of diplomacy, war, and great power management are deeply gendered, and the society of states is a society of men.

## Comparative Analysis

*The Anarchical Society* is the defining text of the English School, standing alongside Martin Wight's *International Theory* and Herbert Butterfield's historical works. It offers an alternative to Waltz's neorealism (which denies international society) and Keohane's neoliberal institutionalism (which emphasizes regime cooperation). The book challenges both American IR paradigms by insisting that international order is a social achievement.

## Impact & Legacy

The book is one of the most cited works in IR. It founded the English School as a distinctive tradition. It has become particularly influential in Europe, Australia, and parts of Asia. The concept of international society is now standard in IR discourse. The book's analysis of order and justice is increasingly relevant in debates about humanitarian intervention, the responsibility to protect, and the challenge to the liberal international order.
