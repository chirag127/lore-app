*The Anarchical Society: A Study of Order in World Politics* (1977) is the defining text of the English School of international relations theory. Hedley Bull, an Australian-born scholar who spent most of his career at Oxford and the Australian National University, argues that sovereign states form not merely an international system (in which they interact strategically) but an international society (in which they share common interests, values, and rules). The book's title captures its central thesis: the international system is anarchic (no world government), but it is nevertheless a society governed by shared norms and institutions.

Bull explores three fundamental questions: What is order in world politics? How is order maintained in the contemporary states system? And do desirable and feasible alternatives to the states system exist? He argues that order is maintained through five key institutions: the balance of power, international law, diplomacy, war (as a regulated practice), and the special responsibilities of great powers. The book has become the foundational text of the English School tradition and a classic of international relations theory.
