## Chapter 1: Introduction

Walt frames the central puzzle: why do states form alliances? The traditional answer—balance of power—is insufficient because it cannot explain why some states bandwagon rather than balance, or why some apparent threats fail to generate balancing coalitions. Walt proposes a refined theory: balance of threat.

## Chapter 2: The Balance of Power and the Balance of Threat

Walt systematically lays out balance-of-threat theory. Threat perception depends on four factors: (1) aggregate power (population, resources, military strength), (2) geographic proximity (nearby states are more threatening), (3) offensive power (the ability to project force and conquer territory), and (4) perceived intentions (states with aggressive ideologies or revisionist aims are more threatening). He distinguishes balancing (allying against the primary threat) from bandwagoning (allying with the threatening state) and derives hypotheses about when each will occur.

## Chapter 3: Alliance Formation in the Middle East: 1955-1979

The empirical heart of the book. Walt examines alliance patterns in the Middle East during a period of intense alignment and realignment: the Baghdad Pact (1955), the Suez Crisis (1956), the Syrian-Egyptian union (1958), the Six-Day War (1967), the Yom Kippur War (1973), the Egyptian-Israeli peace treaty (1979), and the Iranian Revolution (1979). In each case, he maps the threat perceptions and alignment decisions of key states.

## Chapter 4: Balancing and Bandwagoning

Detailed analysis of when states balance and when they bandwagon. Walt finds that balancing is the dominant tendency: states are far more likely to join together against a threatening power than to ally with it. Bandwagoning occurs only under specific conditions: when the threatened state is weak and isolated, when the threatening state appears unstoppable, or when the threatening state offers side payments.

He tests the bandwagoning hypothesis by examining the behavior of small states (Jordan, Lebanon, the Gulf states) in response to threats from larger powers (Egypt, Iraq, Iran). He finds little evidence of voluntary bandwagoning; most apparent cases are better described as coercion or capitulation.

## Chapter 5: Ideology and Alliance Formation

This chapter examines whether ideological affinity drives alliance formation. Walt analyzes the role of Arab nationalism (Nasserism) and pan-Arabism as potential alliance drivers. He finds that ideology matters but is secondary to threat calculations. States with shared ideology do often ally, but only when the ideological alignment corresponds to shared threat perceptions. Ideology without shared threat produces rhetorical solidarity but not meaningful alliance.

## Chapter 6: Foreign Aid and Alliance Formation

Walt tests the hypothesis that foreign aid can buy allies. He examines U.S. and Soviet aid programs to Middle Eastern states. His conclusion: foreign aid can influence alignment at the margins but rarely determines it. States may accept aid without changing their alignment; aid tends to flow to already-friendly states rather than creating new allies. The impact of aid is greatest when the recipient faces a severe security threat and when the aid is well-targeted.

## Chapter 7: Conclusion: The Future of Alliance Theory

Walt summarizes his findings and discusses their implications for theory and policy. He argues that balance-of-threat theory provides a more accurate framework for understanding alliance formation than balance-of-power theory alone. He discusses the prospects for alliance theory in the study of international relations and suggests directions for future research.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment
This summary captures the theoretical framework and empirical findings but condenses the detailed case study evidence that gives the book its persuasive power.

- **Chapter 4** — Balancing vs. bandwagoning analysis
