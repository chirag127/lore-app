## Introduction: The Tragedy Unfolds

Mearsheimer opens with a stark claim: great powers are trapped. They can never be certain of other states' intentions, they operate in an anarchic system with no overarching authority, and they possess some capacity for offensive military action. Under these conditions, the safest course is to become the strongest power in the system. Because all great powers follow this logic, they are condemned to perpetual competition.

## Chapter 1: Anarchy and the Struggle for Power

The theoretical foundation. Mearsheimer identifies five core assumptions: (1) the international system is anarchic, (2) great powers inherently possess some offensive military capability, (3) states can never be certain of others' intentions, (4) survival is the primary goal, and (5) great powers are rational actors. From these, he deduces that states fear each other, seek to maximize relative power, and act aggressively when opportunity permits.

## Chapter 2: Wealth and Power

Mearsheimer argues that wealth is the foundation of military power, but the relationship is not straightforward. Latent power (population and economic capacity) must be converted into military power, especially land power. He distinguishes between great powers (which can fight a conventional war against another major state) and mere regional powers.

## Chapter 3: The Primacy of Land Power

This chapter argues that land power is the most important form of military power. Navies and air forces are important but cannot conquer and control territory. The most powerful states are those with large, well-equipped armies. Mearsheimer uses the distinction between land power and sea power to explain why the United States is a regional hegemon in the Western Hemisphere but not a global hegemon.

## Chapter 4: Strategies for Survival

Great powers have three strategic options: balancing (building up one's own power or forming alliances to counter a threatening state), buck-passing (shifting the burden of containing a threat to another state), and appeasement (making concessions). Mearsheimer argues that balancing is the most common and effective strategy, while buck-passing is tempting but dangerous.

## Chapter 5: Great Powers in Action

Using case studies from 1792 to 1990, Mearsheimer tests his theory. He examines Napoleonic France, Bismarck's Germany, Imperial Japan, and Nazi Germany. In each case, he argues that these powers sought regional hegemony when the opportunity arose, driven by the structural logic of anarchy rather than by the particular ideology or personality of their leaders.

## Chapter 6: The Offshore Balancers

The United States and Great Britain are the paradigmatic offshore balancers. Geographically separated from the Eurasian landmass, they have historically intervened in European and Asian affairs only when a single power threatened to dominate. Mearsheimer argues that the United States should continue this policy rather than pursuing liberal internationalist crusades.

## Chapter 7: Balancing versus Buck-Passing

A detailed historical analysis of how great powers responded to rising threats. Mearsheimer examines the Napoleonic Wars (where balancing succeeded), the two World Wars (where buck-passing failed disastrously), and the Cold War (where the United States and the Soviet Union balanced each other). The chapter includes careful comparisons of military spending, alliance patterns, and territorial outcomes.

## Chapter 8: The Causes of Great Power War

Wars occur when the distribution of power becomes unbalanced, creating opportunities for a rising power to challenge the existing order. Mearsheimer identifies three structural causes: (1) a power transition when the gap between the leading state and a challenger narrows, (2) unbalanced multipolarity where one state is substantially more powerful than the others, and (3) the disappearance of the stopping power of water when a would-be hegemon can project land power across a region.

He tests these hypotheses against the historical record of great power wars from 1792 to 1990, finding that unbalanced multipolar systems are especially war-prone.

## Chapter 9: Great Power Politics in the Twenty-First Century

The concluding chapter applies offensive realism to the post-Cold War world. Mearsheimer predicts that China's rise will inevitably lead to security competition with the United States. He argues that a wealthy China will seek to dominate Asia, and the United States will act to prevent this. He dismisses the hope that globalization, trade, or international institutions can avert this conflict.

The updated edition (2014) expands this chapter substantially, examining the trajectory of US-China relations and reaffirming that the tragedy of great power politics is inescapable.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment
This summary captures Mearsheimer's core theoretical argument and historical evidence, but condenses the rich case study material and the detailed rebuttals of alternative theories. The statistical analysis in Chapter 8 is necessarily simplified.

### Recommended Reading Path
| Reader Type | Time | What to Read |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | ~20 min | This summary + Introduction + Chapter 1 |
| Interested | ~3-4 hr | Chapters 1, 2, 4, 5, 9 |
| Scholar/Practitioner | ~12-15 hr | Full book |

### Chapters to Read in Full
- **Chapter 1** — The entire theoretical framework
- **Chapter 8** — The systematic theory of war causation
- **Chapter 9** — The application to the 21st century

### Chapters to Skim or Skip
- **Chapter 3** (military procurement details may be dense)
- **Chapter 6** (offshore balancing, though important, is sometimes repetitive)

### What You'll Miss by Not Reading the Full Book
The depth of historical case studies, the detailed rebuttals of liberal and defensive realist critiques, and the nuanced discussion of nuclear weapons' effects on great power war.
