*Theory of International Politics* (1979) is the most important work of international relations theory of the second half of the twentieth century. Kenneth Waltz revolutionized the field by insisting that international politics could be explained scientifically by examining the structure of the international system rather than the internal characteristics of states or the nature of human beings. His "neorealism" or "structural realism" argued that the anarchic structure of the international system—characterized by the absence of a central authority—generates enduring patterns of balance-of-power politics regardless of which particular states or leaders occupy the system.

The book was intended as a foundational contribution to IR theory in the same way that classical works of economics provided foundations for their discipline. Waltz distinguished between reductionist theories (which explain outcomes by studying parts) and systemic theories (which explain outcomes by studying the system's structure). His structural theory identified three defining features of the international political structure: the ordering principle (anarchy), the character of the units (functionally similar states), and the distribution of capabilities (polarity).
