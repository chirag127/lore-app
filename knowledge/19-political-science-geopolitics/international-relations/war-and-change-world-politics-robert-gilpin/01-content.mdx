## Part I: Understanding International Political Change

### Chapter 1: The Nature of International Political Change
Gilpin sets out his framework. The international system has three components: the distribution of power among states, the hierarchy of prestige (status), and the rules/institutions governing state interaction. Change occurs when the underlying distribution of power shifts, creating a gap between actual power and recognized status.

### Chapter 2: The Concept of Political Change
Gilpin draws on social contract theory to argue that the international system, like domestic political systems, rests on a bargain: powerful states provide public goods (order, security) in exchange for deference from weaker states. When the bargain no longer reflects the distribution of power, the system faces crisis.

## Part II: The Dynamics of Change

### Chapter 3: Differential Growth and the Law of Uneven Development
The fundamental cause of systemic change is the law of uneven development: states grow at different rates because of differences in economic organization, technological innovation, and resource endowment. Differential growth shifts the distribution of power and creates the conditions for conflict.

### Chapter 4: Equilibrium and Disequilibrium
When the distribution of power and the hierarchy of prestige are aligned, the system is in equilibrium. Disequilibrium arises when rising states demand status and rules commensurate with their growing power, while declining states resist accommodation. The gap between power and prestige creates incentives for rising states to challenge the system.

### Chapter 5: The Response to Disequilibrium
States can respond to disequilibrium in several ways: accommodation (adjusting the rules), retrenchment (reducing commitments), or preventive war (defeating the rising challenger). Gilpin argues that the optimal response depends on the expected costs and benefits of each option. Most systems eventually resolve disequilibrium through hegemonic war.

## Part III: The Dynamics of War and Change

### Chapter 6: The Mechanism of Hegemonic War
Hegemonic war is "the ultimate test of change in the international system." The rising challenger and the declining hegemon fight a war that determines the new hierarchy of power and prestige. Victory establishes a new hegemon, which then creates new rules and institutions reflecting its interests and values.

### Chapter 7: The Consequences of Hegemonic War
Hegemonic wars produce a new international order. The victor establishes the rules, controls the international economy, and enjoys the benefits of its dominant position. The cycle then begins anew: differential growth eventually erodes the new hegemon's position, leading to another hegemonic war.

### Chapter 8: Historical Patterns of Hegemonic Change
Gilpin tests his theory against the historical record: the Peloponnesian War (Athens vs. Sparta), the Hundred Years War, the Thirty Years War, the Napoleonic Wars, and World Wars I and II. In each case, hegemonic war resolved a fundamental disequilibrium in the system.

## Part IV: The Future of the International System

### Chapter 9: The Contemporary System
Gilpin applies his theory to the Cold War system. He argues that the United States experienced relative decline after 1970 due to the recovery of Europe and Japan, the costs of Vietnam, and the rise of economic multipolarity. The Soviet Union faced even more severe decline.

### Chapter 10: The Implications for Change
Gilpin concludes with reflections on the future. He warns against both complacency and alarmism. The decline of hegemony need not lead to war if states manage the transition wisely, but the historical record is not encouraging.
