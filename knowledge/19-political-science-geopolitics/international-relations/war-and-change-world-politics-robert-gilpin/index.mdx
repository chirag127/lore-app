*War and Change in World Politics* (1981) is the definitive statement of hegemonic stability theory and one of the most important works of international political economy. Robert Gilpin, a professor at Princeton, integrates Thucydides with modern social science to explain the cyclical pattern of rise and decline among great powers. The book argues that the international system is shaped by the differential growth of power among states, that this growth creates periodic disequilibrium between the distribution of power and the distribution of prestige (status), and that this disequilibrium is typically resolved through hegemonic war.

The book draws extensively on historical examples from ancient Greece through the Cold War. Gilpin develops a theory of systemic change that explains both stability (when power and prestige are aligned) and transformation (when they diverge). The book has become essential reading for understanding the dynamics of great power competition, particularly in the context of China's rise and the relative decline of American power.
