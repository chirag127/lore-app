# Political Economy of Developing Nations

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Why are some nations rich and others poor? This question is the central concern of development political economy. The field examines how political institutions, colonial legacies, natural resource endowments, international economic structures, and domestic class configurations shape economic development outcomes. The collapse of the post-WWII development project in the 1970s and 1980s — the debt crisis, structural adjustment, the rise of the Washington Consensus — led to a rethinking of the role of the state in development.

Dependency theory, associated with Andre Gunder Frank, Fernando Henrique Cardoso, and Immanuel Wallerstein's world-systems analysis, argued that underdevelopment was a product of the structure of global capitalism. The "developmental state" literature — Chalmers Johnson on Japan, Alice Amsden on South Korea, Peter Evans on embedded autonomy — emphasized the conditions under which state intervention produces rapid growth. Daron Acemoglu and James Robinson's *Why Nations Fail* (2012) synthesized and popularized the argument that inclusive political institutions are the fundamental cause of prosperity. The field also examines the resource curse (how natural resource wealth undermines governance), foreign aid effectiveness, and the political economy of structural transformation.

## Key Books

1. Daron Acemoglu & James A. Robinson, *Why Nations Fail* — The institutional origins of prosperity and poverty across the globe.
2. Peter B. Evans, *Embedded Autonomy: States and Industrial Transformation* — How state-society linkages shaped development in Korea, Brazil, and India.
3. Alice H. Amsden, *Asia's Next Giant: South Korea and Late Industrialization* — The definitive analysis of the developmental state in action.
4. Robert H. Bates, *Markets and States in Tropical Africa* — The political economy of agricultural policy failure in post-colonial Africa.
5. Jeffrey D. Sachs, *The End of Poverty* — Aid-centered approach to ending extreme poverty.
6. William Easterly, *The White Man's Burden* — Searing critique of the aid industry and its incentives.
7. Douglass C. North, *Institutions, Institutional Change and Economic Performance* — The foundational institutional analysis of economic development.
8. Mick Moore et al., *Taxation and State-Building in Developing Countries* — How fiscal capacity and state capacity co-evolve.
9. Atul Kohli, *State-Directed Development* — Political power and industrialization in a comparison of South Korea, Brazil, India, and Nigeria.
10. Paul Collier, *The Bottom Billion* — Why the poorest countries are trapped and what can be done.
