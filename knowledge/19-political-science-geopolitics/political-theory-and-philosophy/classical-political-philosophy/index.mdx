# Classical Political Philosophy

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Classical political philosophy provides the foundational questions and frameworks for the entire Western political tradition. Plato's *Republic* asks what justice is and what the best regime would be; Aristotle's *Politics* offers the first systematic classification of constitutions; Cicero, Augustine, and Aquinas adapt Greek thought to Roman and Christian contexts. These thinkers established the perennial themes of political philosophy: the nature of justice, the best form of government, the relationship between ethics and politics, and the tension between individual and collective goods.

The study of classical political philosophy is essential not only for understanding the history of ideas but for engaging with arguments about natural law, the common good, and virtue that remain influential in contemporary debates. Major interpreters such as Leo Strauss (who emphasized the esoteric dimension of classical writing), Hannah Arendt (who recovered the Greek concept of the political), and Martha Nussbaum (who develops a capabilities approach from Aristotelian foundations) have shown how classical philosophy remains a living resource for political thought.

## What Belongs Here

- Socratic political philosophy and the problem of justice
- Plato's *Republic*, *Statesman*, *Laws*
- Aristotle's *Politics* and *Nicomachean Ethics*
- Cicero's republican theory and natural law
- Augustine's *City of God* and the two cities
- Aquinas's synthesis of Aristotle and Christian theology
- Islamic political philosophy (Al-Farabi, Avicenna, Averroes, Ibn Khaldun)
- The theory of natural law
- The classical theory of regimes (polity, democracy, oligarchy, tyranny)
- Slavery, gender, and exclusion in classical thought

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- Modern social contract theory (see Enlightenment & Social Contract Theory)
- Liberal and post-liberal political philosophy (see Modern Political Philosophy)
- Practical constitutional design (see Democratic Systems & Electoral Theory)
- Ancient Greek or Roman history (see those in History)
- Medieval European history per se

## Key Books

1. Plato, *The Republic* — The foundational dialogue on justice, the ideal city, and the philosopher-king.
2. Aristotle, *Politics* — Systematic classification of constitutions and the conditions for the good life in a political community.
3. Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics* — The ethical basis of political life: virtue, flourishing, and the mean.
4. Cicero, *On the Republic / On the Laws* — Roman republican political thought and natural law theory.
5. Augustine, *The City of God* — The Christian transformation of classical political philosophy: the two cities and the limits of political order.
6. Thomas Aquinas, *Summa Theologica* (selections on law and politics) — The natural law synthesis of Aristotelian and Christian thought.
7. Leo Strauss, *Natural Right and History* — The classic defense of classical natural right against modern historicism.
8. Hannah Arendt, *The Human Condition* — Recovery of the Greek concept of the political as action in the public sphere.
9. Martha C. Nussbaum, *The Fragility of Goodness* — Greek ethical thought and its relevance to modern questions of luck and vulnerability.
10. Ibn Khaldun, *The Muqaddimah* — The foundational work of Islamic historical sociology and political philosophy.

## Key Concepts

Justice, the good regime, natural law, natural right, common good, virtue, citizen, polis, constitution (politeia), regime classification (monarchy, aristocracy, polity, democracy, oligarchy, tyranny), the best regime, the rule of law, mixed constitution, the philosopher-king, the ideal city, the two cities, just war theory, prudence, practical wisdom (phronesis), political animal (zoon politikon), the mean, distributive justice, corrective justice, cosmopolitanism, Stoicism

## Connections

Classical political philosophy establishes the questions that **Enlightenment & Social Contract Theory** and **Modern Political Philosophy** inherit and transform. The natural law tradition connects to **Regulatory Theory & Administrative Law** (constitutional foundations of legality). The typology of regimes is an ancestor of **Democratic Systems & Electoral Theory** and **Presidential vs Parliamentary Systems**. Cicero's mixed constitution influenced **Federalism & Decentralization** and American constitutional design.
