# Enlightenment & Social Contract Theory

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Social contract theory is the intellectual foundation of modern political legitimacy. It holds that political authority is not natural or divinely ordained but derives from the consent of free and equal individuals. The great contractarians — Thomas Hobbes, John Locke, Jean-Jacques Rousseau, and Immanuel Kant — developed competing accounts of what individuals consent to, under what conditions, and with what limits. Their arguments gave rise to modern concepts of rights, consent, constitutional government, and the rule of law.

Hobbes's *Leviathan* (1651), written amid the English Civil War, argued that rational individuals would surrender their freedom to an absolute sovereign to escape the state of nature — a war of all against all. Locke's *Second Treatise* (1689) offered a more optimistic account: individuals consent to limited government that protects natural rights to life, liberty, and property, and may be overthrown if it violates that trust. Rousseau's *The Social Contract* (1762) argued that legitimate authority rests on the general will, through which citizens collectively impose the law on themselves. Kant's political writings grounded the contract in the categorical imperative and envisioned a cosmopolitan federation of republics.

## Key Books

1. Thomas Hobbes, *Leviathan* — The founding text of modern political philosophy: the state of nature, the social contract, and absolute sovereignty.
2. John Locke, *Two Treatises of Government* — The classic defense of natural rights, property, limited government, and the right of revolution.
3. Jean-Jacques Rousseau, *The Social Contract* — The radical democratic alternative: popular sovereignty and the general will.
4. Immanuel Kant, *Political Writings* (ed. Hans Reiss) — Perpetual peace, republicanism, and the foundations of cosmopolitan right.
5. David Hume, "Of the Original Contract" — The skeptical empiricist critique of social contract theory.
6. Carole Pateman, *The Sexual Contract* — The foundational feminist critique revealing the patriarchal basis of classical social contract theory.
7. Susan Moller Okin, *Women in Western Political Thought* — Feminist reading of the exclusion of women from the contractarian tradition.
8. Jeremy Waldron, *The Dignity of Legislation* — How the legislative aspect of social contract theory structures political authority.
9. John Rawls, *Lectures on the History of Political Philosophy* — Rawls's interpretive framework for the social contract tradition.
10. J.W. Gough, *The Social Contract* — Classic historical survey of social contract theory from its origins to the 20th century.
