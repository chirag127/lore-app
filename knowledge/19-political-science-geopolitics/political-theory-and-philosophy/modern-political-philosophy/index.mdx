# Modern Political Philosophy

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Modern political philosophy encompasses the major normative and critical currents of political thought from the 19th century to the present. The field was transformed by John Rawls's *A Theory of Justice* (1971), which revived normative political philosophy with a Kantian social contract approach centered on justice as fairness. Rawls's work generated a rich debate: Robert Nozick's libertarian response, Michael Sandel's communitarian critique, the capability approach of Amartya Sen and Martha Nussbaum, the deliberative democracy theory of Jürgen Habermas, and the critical theory of the Frankfurt School.

The tradition also includes 19th-century foundations: John Stuart Mill's liberalism and utilitarianism, Karl Marx's critique of capitalism, Friedrich Nietzsche's genealogy of morality, and John Dewey's pragmatic democratic theory. Contemporary political philosophy engages with new questions: multiculturalism (Will Kymlicka, Charles Taylor), global justice (Thomas Pogge, Peter Singer), feminism and intersectionality (Iris Marion Young, Nancy Fraser), post-structuralist critiques of power (Michel Foucault, Jacques Derrida), and the politics of race (Charles Mills, Cornel West).

## What Belongs Here

- Rawls's theory of justice and the original position
- Liberal egalitarianism (Dworkin, Scanlon, Sen, Nussbaum)
- Libertarianism and free-market philosophy (Nozick, Hayek, Friedman)
- Communitarianism (Sandel, Taylor, Walzer, MacIntyre)
- Deliberative democracy (Habermas, Cohen, Dryzek)
- Critical theory (Adorno, Horkheimer, Marcuse, Honneth)
- Post-structuralism and power (Foucault, Derrida, Deleuze)
- Multiculturalism and minority rights (Kymlicka, Parekh)
- Feminism, intersectionality, and the politics of gender
- Race, racial justice, and post-colonial theory
- Republicanism (Pettit, Skinner) and its revival
- Global justice and cosmopolitanism

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- Pre-Enlightenment political thought (see Classical Political Philosophy)
- Social contract theory of Hobbes through Kant (see Enlightenment & Social Contract Theory)
- Applied policy questions (see Policy Design, Implementation & Evaluation)
- Psychological or sociological theories of justice
- Marxism as economic theory (see that in Economics or Political Economy)

## Key Books

1. John Rawls, *A Theory of Justice* — The most important 20th-century work in political philosophy: justice as fairness, the original position, the difference principle.
2. Robert Nozick, *Anarchy, State, and Utopia* — The definitive libertarian challenge to egalitarian liberalism.
3. Jürgen Habermas, *Between Facts and Norms* — The theory of deliberative democracy and the role of law in modern societies.
4. Michael Sandel, *Liberalism and the Limits of Justice* — The communitarian critique of Rawlsian liberalism.
5. Michel Foucault, *Discipline and Punish* — Genealogical analysis of power, discipline, and the carceral state.
6. Iris Marion Young, *Justice and the Politics of Difference* — The critique of universalist justice and the politics of group difference.
7. Martha C. Nussbaum, *Women and Human Development* — The capabilities approach applied to gender equality and development.
8. Charles Mills, *The Racial Contract* — The social contract tradition rewritten through the lens of racial domination.
9. Will Kymlicka, *Multicultural Citizenship* — The liberal theory of minority rights and group-differentiated citizenship.
10. Hannah Arendt, *The Origins of Totalitarianism* — The analysis of totalitarianism, the banality of evil, and the conditions of political action.

## Connections

Modern political philosophy draws on **Enlightenment & Social Contract Theory** (Rawls is a neo-Kantian, Nozick a neo-Lockean) and **Classical Political Philosophy** (Arendt and Strauss engage with Plato and Aristotle). Its implications reach every sub-subcategory: deliberative democracy to **Democratic Systems & Electoral Theory**; the capabilities approach to **Taxation, Welfare State & Social Democracy**; Foucault's governmentality to **Policy Design, Implementation & Evaluation** and **Digital Governance & Technology Policy**; multiculturalism to **Federalism & Decentralization** and **Civil Society & Social Movements**.
