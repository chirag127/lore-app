---
title: "Narration"
book: "The Social Contract"
author: "Jean-Jacques Rousseau"
---

Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains. This single sentence, opening Jean-Jacques Rousseau's *The Social Contract* in 1762, announces one of the most consequential arguments in the history of political thought. Rousseau, a Genevan philosopher born in 1712, was asking a question that had haunted thinkers for centuries: how can people live together under political authority and still remain free?

To understand what Rousseau was responding to, you have to picture the world he lived in. Europe in the mid-eighteenth century was dominated by absolute monarchs who claimed their authority came from God. Kings ruled by divine right, and to question that right was heresy. Rousseau rejected this entirely. Force, he argued, does not create right. The ability to coerce is not a legitimate power. A state has no right to enslave a conquered people simply because it can. Might does not make right, and the people have no duty to submit to illegitimate authority.

But if divine right is false and force is not a source of legitimacy, then what is? Rousseau's answer was the social contract. People voluntarily agree to live together as a community, surrendering their natural freedom in exchange for civil freedom. The crucial difference from earlier thinkers like Hobbes and Locke is that Rousseau did not believe citizens could transfer their sovereignty to a king or a parliament. The only legitimate contract is one in which the people remain their own rulers, united by what Rousseau called the general will.

The general will is perhaps Rousseau's most important and most debated concept. It is not simply the will of the majority. It is not the sum of individual preferences. It is the collective desire of the citizens for the common good. When people come together as citizens and think about what serves everyone rather than what serves themselves personally, they express the general will. Laws that flow from the general will are legitimate because they apply equally to everyone and aim at the common good. Citizens obey these laws not because they are forced to but because they have prescribed them for themselves. This, for Rousseau, is the highest form of freedom: obedience to a self-imposed law.

Rousseau made a sharp distinction between the sovereign and the government. The sovereign is the collective body of all citizens, united by the general will. The government is merely the executive power that administers the laws the sovereign creates. The government is an agent, not a master. When the government oversteps its mandate and begins to serve private interests instead of the common good, the citizens have every right to dissolve it and start anew. This idea was revolutionary in an age of absolute monarchy, and it was precisely this idea that made the book dangerous.

Rousseau analyzed different forms of government not by who rules but by the structure of the executive. A democracy is one where executive power belongs to half or more of the population. An aristocracy is one where it belongs to a few. A monarchy is one where it belongs to a single individual. Rousseau did not necessarily prefer democracy; in fact, he was skeptical of pure democracy and believed that the ideal form of government depended on the size, culture, and circumstances of the community. Small city-states like his native Geneva, he thought, were best suited to freedom. Large states required stronger executive power.

Equality was essential to Rousseau's vision. He argued that extreme wealth inequality destroys the social contract. If some citizens are so impoverished that they cannot meaningfully participate in civic life, the general will cannot truly represent the common good. The social contract is broken when it produces conditions of extreme inequality. This insight remains strikingly relevant in an era of widening economic gaps.

The immediate reception of *The Social Contract* was explosive. The book was published in April 1762 in Amsterdam. By June, it was banned and burned in both Paris and Geneva. Rousseau became a wanted man in both France and Switzerland, forced to flee and live as a fugitive for the rest of his life. The controversy centered not on his ideas about liberty and sovereignty but on the chapter on civil religion, in which he argued that Christianity, despite its truth, was useless as a republican religion because it directed citizens to the next world rather than inspiring civic virtue in this one.

Yet Rousseau's influence only grew after his death. When the French Revolution erupted in 1789, the revolutionaries claimed Rousseau as the father of republican France. Maximilien Robespierre kept a diary entry calling Rousseau a divine man who taught him to know himself. The Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen echoes Rousseau's principles of popular sovereignty, legal equality, and government by consent. In 1794, the revolutionary government ordered Rousseau's remains moved to the Pantheon in Paris, burying him as a national hero.

The legacy of *The Social Contract* extends far beyond the French Revolution. Immanuel Kant acknowledged a profound debt to Rousseau, crediting him with teaching him to honor human beings. The book influenced Jeffersonian democracy in America, the development of modern democratic theory, and continues to animate debates about legitimacy, consent, and collective governance. Critics from Benjamin Constant to Jacob Talmon have argued that the general will contains the seeds of totalitarianism. Supporters counter that Rousseau's insistence on individual consent and the right to dissolve unjust government is fundamentally liberal.

What makes *The Social Contract* endure is that it identifies the fundamental tension of political life with unmatched clarity. How do we live together under authority while remaining free? Rousseau's answer may be impractical, his general will may be vague, and his system may contain dangers he did not fully anticipate. But the question itself is permanent, and Rousseau posed it with a passion and rigor that no subsequent thinker has surpassed. The book is not easy. It is not always right. But it is always thought-provoking, and it remains, two and a half centuries after its publication, one of the essential texts for anyone who wants to understand what democracy means and what it demands.
