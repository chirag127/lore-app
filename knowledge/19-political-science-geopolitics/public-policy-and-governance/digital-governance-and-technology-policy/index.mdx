# Digital Governance & Technology Policy

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Digital technologies have transformed how economies function, how politics operates, and how societies organize themselves. The governance of these technologies — who sets the rules, how those rules are enforced, whose interests are served — has become one of the most contested policy domains of the 21st century. Issues range from internet infrastructure governance (ICANN, net neutrality) through data protection (GDPR, privacy regulation), platform governance (content moderation, antitrust), and the emerging regulatory frameworks for artificial intelligence.

The field draws on multiple intellectual traditions: information economics (how platforms create value and extract rents), network governance theory (distributed decision-making in multi-stakeholder systems), science and technology studies (the co-construction of technology and society), and legal scholarship on intermediary liability and data rights. Key scholars include Lawrence Lessig (code as law and internet governance), Julie Cohen (information law and privacy), Yochai Benkler (the networked public sphere), Shoshana Zuboff (surveillance capitalism), and Frank Pasquale (the black box society). The EU's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), Digital Markets Act (DMA), and AI Act represent the most ambitious efforts to regulate the digital economy, while China's model of state-controlled digital governance offers a competing paradigm.

## Key Books

1. Lawrence Lessig, *Code and Other Laws of Cyberspace* — The classic argument that code is a form of regulation: architecture as law.
2. Shoshana Zuboff, *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* — The definitive critique of the business model of digital platform capitalism.
3. Yochai Benkler, *The Wealth of Networks* — How networked information economy transforms production, power, and culture.
4. Julie E. Cohen, *Configuring the Networked Self* — Legal and social theory of privacy, data, and networked identity.
5. Frank Pasquale, *The Black Box Society* — The secret algorithms controlling credit, employment, and justice, and the case for algorithmic accountability.
6. Jack Goldsmith & Tim Wu, *Who Controls the Internet?* — Clash between the borderless internet ideal and territorial sovereignty.
7. Laura DeNardis, *The Global War for Internet Governance* — The geopolitics of internet infrastructure control.
8. Karen Yeung & Martin Lodge (eds.), *Algorithmic Regulation* — How algorithms and AI reshape regulatory governance.
9. Anu Bradford, *The Brussels Effect: How the European Union Rules the World* — How EU digital regulations (GDPR, DMA) become global standards.
10. Virginia Eubanks, *Automating Inequality* — How high-tech tools profile, police, and punish the poor.
