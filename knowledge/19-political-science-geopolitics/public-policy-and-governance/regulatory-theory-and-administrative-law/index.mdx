# Regulatory Theory & Administrative Law

## Why This Sub-subcategory Exists

Regulation is the primary mechanism through which the modern state structures private behavior in the public interest. From financial markets and environmental protection to food safety, telecommunications, and pharmaceuticals, regulatory agencies make decisions that affect virtually every aspect of economic and social life. The study of regulatory theory and administrative law asks: why regulate? Whose interests do regulations serve? How should regulatory agencies be designed, empowered, and constrained?

The intellectual foundations draw from multiple traditions. Chicago-school economists (George Stigler's "The Theory of Economic Regulation," Sam Peltzman) developed the economic theory of regulation, emphasizing regulatory capture and private-interest explanations. The public interest theory (Stephen Breyer, Richard Stewart) argues regulation corrects market failures. Cass Sunstein's work on cost-benefit analysis and regulatory impact assessment bridges legal and economic approaches. Administrative law — the body of law governing agency procedures, rulemaking, adjudication, and judicial review — provides the legal architecture. Scholars such as Jerry Mashaw, Peter Strauss, and Richard Pierce Jr. have shaped modern understanding of administrative procedure.

## Key Books

1. Cass R. Sunstein, *After the Rights Revolution* — The public interest theory of regulation and the role of cost-benefit analysis.
2. George J. Stigler, "The Theory of Economic Regulation" — The foundational economic theory of regulatory capture.
3. Stephen Breyer, *Regulation and Its Reform* — Classic analysis of regulatory strategies and their reform.
4. Jerry L. Mashaw, *Greed, Chaos, and Governance* — How public choice theory illuminates the administrative state.
5. Richard J. Pierce Jr., *Administrative Law Treatise* — The definitive modern treatise on American administrative law.
6. Kenneth J. Meier, *Regulation: Politics, Bureaucracy, and Economics* — The political science of regulatory policymaking.
7. Steven P. Croley, *Regulation and Public Interests* — Empirical defense of public-regarding regulation against capture theory.
8. David Epstein & Sharyn O'Halloran, *Delegating Powers* — The political logic of legislative delegation to regulatory agencies.
9. Giandomenico Majone, *Regulating Europe* — How regulation functions as a mode of governance in the European Union.
10. David Vogel, *The Politics of Precautions* — Comparative analysis of risk regulation in Europe and the United States.
