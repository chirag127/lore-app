Carl Sagan's *Cosmos* (1980) is more than a book about astronomy. It is the book that made the universe popular. Published simultaneously with the PBS television series *Cosmos: A Personal Voyage*, it became the best-selling science book in the English language at the time of its release, spending 70 weeks on the *New York Times* Best Seller list and ultimately selling more than five million copies worldwide. It won the 1981 Hugo Award for Best Non-Fiction Book and was later designated by the U.S. Library of Congress as one of eighty-eight books "that shaped America."

Written at the height of the Cold War, when the threat of nuclear annihilation overshadowed every aspect of public life, *Cosmos* used the grandeur of the cosmos to argue for two things: a deeper sense of wonder at the universe as comprehensible through science, and a renewed commitment to the preservation of the single fragile planet we call home. Sagan, who was already one of America's most visible scientists — involved in the Viking Mars missions, the Voyager program, and the design of the Voyager Golden Record — wrote with the authority of a working researcher and the lyricism of a poet.

Thirteen chapters mirror the thirteen episodes of the television series. Together they span 15 billion years of cosmic history, from the Big Bang to the likely futures of human civilization. The book is an argument as well as a survey: that science is the most reliable method we have for knowing the world, that the universe is vast and ancient and beautiful, and that we — the cosmos become conscious — bear a special responsibility to one another and to the future.
