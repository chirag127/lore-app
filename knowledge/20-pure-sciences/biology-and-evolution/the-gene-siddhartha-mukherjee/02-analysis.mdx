## Strengths

**Genuine historical breadth.** _The Gene_ covers four centuries of science
across cultures — Europe, India, Japan, the United States, China — with a
level of archival diligence that matches a scholarly monograph while never
losing its narrative momentum. Mukherjee visits museums, monasteries, and
laboratories across three continents.

**Writing as intellectual architecture.** Mukherjee's prose moves
rhythmically between analogy and precision. He explains the lac operon as a
genetic switch, chromatin as a spool of thread, DNA methylation as a dimmer
switch — without ever losing the scientific content. He may be the most
gifted explainer working in popular science today.

**The emotional integration of memoir and science.** Rather than treating the
Mukherjee family story as decor overlay, Mukherjee shows that the history of
genetics *is* the history of how families have understood their own suffering.
The sections on schizophrenia in his extended family are not sidebar material —
they are the book's ethical backbone.

**A rigorous treatment of eugenics.** Most popular science accounts of
genetics treat eugenics as a chapter of shame to be covered quickly and
moved past. Mukherjee devotes substantial space to the American eugenics
movement, the legal infrastructure of sterilization, and the direct academic
line from American eugenicists to their German counterparts — including
reproducing the chilling language of Supreme Court justice Oliver Wendell
Holmes Jr. in _Buck v. Bell_.

## Weaknesses

**Scope vs. depth tension.** For a 624-page book the treatment of some core
scientific topics is surprisingly thin. The Human Genome Project receives a
chapter but the science of comparative genomics, population genetics, and twin
studies gets less space than they deserve given their centrality to the book's
arguments. Some molecular biology explanations are elevated by eloquence but
do not advance beyond what a good undergraduate course provides.

**The memoir passages are emotionally heavy but analytically thin.** Mukherjee
interweaves his family story powerfully, but the book does not rigorously
explore the genetics of schizophrenia in any technical sense: it makes loose
references to heritability estimates, candidate genes, and GWAS hits without
explaining why schizophrenia remains genetically unresolved. A reader wanting
to understand the science would be better served by a dedicated text on
psychiatric genetics.

**Phylogenetic determinism risks.** In places the book treats the first 4
billion years of life as progress toward human genetics — a classic
recapitulation narrative that modern evolutionary biology has discarded. The
"tree of life" is far more branching, reticulate, and horizontal than the
linear arc Mukherjee sometimes implies.

**CRISPR section is dated rapidly.** Published in 2016, the book couldn't
cover the first recipient of CRISPR therapy (Victoria Gray, 2019), the
approval of Casgevy (exagamglogene autotemcel, 2023 for sickle cell disease
and beta-thalassemia), or the ethical crisis of He Jiankui's CRISPR babies
(announced 2018). The final chapters therefore read as prescient but already
historical — the questions remain, but the answers have moved.

**The "gift of choice" framing ends ambiguously.** Mukherjee positions his
own family choice (not to genetically test his daughters) as a conscious
act of openness — but it can equally be read as an act of determinism:
defining his daughters' futures by what he has decided not to know. The
genuine ambiguity here is philosophically productive but Mukherjee does not
fully explore the tension.

## Specific Criticisms

**Scientific: epigenetics underdeveloped.** The chapter on epigenetics —
heritable changes in gene expression without changes to DNA sequence — is
misleading. The book notes histone modification, DNA methylation, and the
P53 tumour suppressor (which is not an epigenetic mechanism, it is a
protein-coding tumour suppressor gene acting through canonical pathways). The
simplified framing risks reviving the Lamarckian model that has been
debunked in its original form. Some epigenetic findings in humans (e.g.,
Dutch Hunger Winter) are presented with more certainty than the science
currently warrants.

**Historical: the narrative is Western-biased.** Despite opening with an
Indian family, genomics and eugenics histories beyond the Western orbit —
Japan, China, India's own genetic research — receive token coverage. Japan's
post-war genetic research, India's biotech revolution, Africa's genetic
diversity burden and its marginalisation in genomics all deserve more space.

**Philosophical: the gene remains under-defined.** Mukherjee writes about the
gene extensively — as code, as text, as ancestor, as mirror — but the book
never quite settles what "the gene" means. Is it the Mendelian unit of
inheritance? The DNA sequence that codes for a protein? A regulatory region
that controls expression? In the era of alternative splicing, long non-coding
RNAs, and epigenetics, the classical gene concept is genuinely unstable.
Mukherjee's refusal to clean up the ambiguity is artistically productive but
leaves a conceptual gap.

## The Optimist Counterpoint

Matt Ridley's _The Agile Gene_ (2003) and Adam Rutherford's _A Brief History
of Everyone Who Ever Lived_ (2016) represent a more calibrated optimism about
genomics. They acknowledge the ethical risks but emphasise the scientific
achievements and therapeutic promise. Francis Collins' _The Language of God_
(2006) integrates faith and genomics at the opposite philosophical end. For
the pessimistic counterpoint, see James Watson's _DNA: The Secret of Life_
(2003), which champions the power of genetic knowledge with less ambivalence.

---

## Long-Term Relevance

_The Gene_ will likely be remembered as the book that brought the personal
dimension into popular genetics writing at scale — and as one of the clearest
non-academic arguments for serious governance of genetic technology. Its
treatment of the Mukherjee family history is likely to be cited as a landmark
in science memoir.

The scientific sections on CRISPR, published two years before the first child
was born from edited embryos, now read as early warning signs realised. The
ethical framework Mukherjee proposes — pluralism, oversight, public
participant in decision-making — has only become more urgently relevant as
CRISPR therapies move through regulatory approval globally.

---

## Recommendation

This is the single best one-volume introduction to the history and future of
genetics for a general reader. Read it alongside a dedicated textbook if you
want technical depth. Read it alongside _The Emperor of All Maladies_ for
Mukherjee's treatment of cancer genetics at the molecular level. Use it as
the foundation for an informed conversation about CRISPR ethics — a
conversation the book insists we must all have.
