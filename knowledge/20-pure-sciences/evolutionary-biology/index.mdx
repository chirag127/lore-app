# Evolutionary Biology

## Why This Subcategory Exists

Evolutionary biology is the unifying framework of all life sciences. Theodosius Dobzhansky's famous dictum — "Nothing in biology makes sense except in the light of evolution" — captures its centrality. Evolution explains the diversity of life on Earth, the relationship between species, the development of complex organs, the origin of human cognition, and the emergence of antibiotic resistance in bacteria. It is simultaneously a historical science (tracing the actual path life has taken) and an experimental science (testing mechanisms of change under controlled conditions).

The field rests on foundations laid by Charles Darwin in *On the Origin of Species* (1859), which proposed natural selection as the primary mechanism of evolutionary change. The 20th century brought the Modern Synthesis, which integrated Darwinian selection with Mendelian genetics, population genetics (Fisher, Haldane, Wright), and eventually molecular biology. The 21st century has added genomics, evo-devo (evolutionary developmental biology), and a richer understanding of non-selective mechanisms like genetic drift and horizontal gene transfer.

## What Belongs Here

Books about evolutionary theory, natural selection, population genetics, speciation, phylogenetics, evolutionary developmental biology, evolutionary psychology (when grounded in biological evidence), human evolution, and the history of evolutionary thought.

## What Does NOT Belong Here

- Creationism, intelligent design, or any non-scientific alternatives to evolution
- Molecular biology without evolutionary context
- Ecology without an evolutionary dimension
- Philosophy of biology (belongs in Philosophy & History of Science)

## Key Concepts

Natural selection, genetic drift, gene flow, mutation, speciation, adaptation, fitness, inclusive fitness, kin selection, sexual selection, evolutionary arms race, coevolution, phylogeny, common descent, molecular clock, evo-devo.
