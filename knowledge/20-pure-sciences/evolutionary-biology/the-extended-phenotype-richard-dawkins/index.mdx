*The Extended Phenotype* (1982) is the book Richard Dawkins himself considers his most original contribution to evolutionary theory — the one he would save if his works were burning. A direct sequel to *The Selfish Gene*, it is written primarily for professional biologists, yet Dawkins's legendary clarity makes it accessible to any determined reader willing to grapple with abstract argumentation.

The central idea is radical but simple: if genes are the true units of natural selection, then their phenotypic effects cannot be limited by the skin of the organism. A beaver dam, a cuckoo chick's manipulative begging cry, a parasite's alteration of its host's brain — these are all *extended phenotypes*, gene effects reaching into the wider world. Dawkins builds the case systematically over fourteen chapters, clearing away objections, sharpening definitions, and arriving at a unified gene-centered framework for all of biology.
