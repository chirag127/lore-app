Few books have reshaped our understanding of evolution as forcefully as Richard Dawkins' *The Selfish Gene*. Published in 1976, it introduced a gene-centered view of natural selection that explains not only why organisms compete, but why they cooperate, sacrifice, and even die for one another.

The central argument is deceptively simple: natural selection operates at the level of genes, not organisms or species. Organisms are temporary vehicles — "survival machines" — built and programmed by genes that act to maximize their own replication. Altruism, far from being a puzzle for Darwinian theory, emerges naturally when helping others benefits copies of the same gene.
