A music professor with visual agnosia reaches for his wife's head, mistaking it for his hat. A sailor with Korsakoff's syndrome lives permanently in 1945, unable to form a single new memory. A woman loses her entire sense of proprioception and must learn to control her body by sight alone. These are not fictions — they are the real patients of neurologist Oliver Sacks, whose 1985 masterwork *The Man Who Mistook His Wife for a Hat* transformed how the public understands the brain. Written with literary grace and deep compassion, the book's twenty-four case studies in four thematic sections — Losses, Excesses, Transports, and The World of the Simple — explore both the devastating and the strangely generative powers of neurological difference.
