# Problems — Against Method

## Key Problem

The central problem Feyerabend addresses is the discrepancy between the philosophical image of science as a rational, method-governed enterprise and the actual historical record of scientific practice. Philosophers from Bacon to Popper had proposed universal methods — inductivism, falsificationism, Bayesian confirmation — that were supposed to distinguish science from non-science and guide rational inquiry. Feyerabend argues that all such proposals fail when measured against the actual history of science.

A deeper problem underlies this: the problem of **authority**. Why should science have a privileged place in education, policy, and public life? If the standard philosophical justifications of scientific authority are invalid, then the dominance of science in modern society is a political fact, not a rational one — and it can be legitimately challenged.

## Proposed Solution

Feyerabend's solution is **epistemological anarchism**. He does not propose a new method to replace the old ones; he proposes that we abandon the search for method altogether. Science should not be governed by fixed rules but should be a pluralistic, opportunistic, creative enterprise in which researchers are free to pursue whatever approaches seem promising.

The political corollary is the **separation of science and state**. Just as modern liberal democracies do not mandate religious belief, they should not mandate scientific belief. Science should be taught alongside other traditions; research funding should not be monopolized by established scientific institutions; citizens should have the right to choose alternative medical practices, educational systems, and knowledge traditions.

## Evidence & Argumentation

Feyerabend's argument proceeds through historical case study (Galileo) and conceptual analysis. The Galileo case is designed to show that even the most celebrated episode in scientific history — the Copernican revolution — cannot be reconstructed as following any rational method. The conceptual argument is that for any methodological rule, one can construct circumstances in which violating it would advance knowledge.

The book also argues by **reductio ad absurdum**: if we take the best available theories of scientific rationality (Popper, Lakatos, etc.) and apply them rigorously, they either license any behavior (making them indistinguishable from anarchism) or forbid productive research (making them harmful). The only consistent position is to abandon methodological prescriptions altogether.

## Unresolved Questions

Feyerabend never fully resolves the tension between his descriptive claim (science has no method) and his normative claims (science should be separated from the state). If there is no rational way to prefer one form of inquiry over another, on what basis does Feyerabend argue for political change? His critique of method seems to undermine his own political advocacy.

A related question: if "anything goes," does this include going back to the very methods Feyerabend attacks? Can a scientist choose to follow Popperian falsificationism if she finds it useful? Feyerabend says yes — he is not against method but against *forcing* method — but this concession seems to drain his critique of much of its force. If falsificationism is permissible as a local choice, then the disagreement with rationalists becomes much narrower than it first appears.

## Criticisms

**Imre Lakatos** argued that Feyerabend's position is self-refuting: if no methodological judgments can be made, then Feyerabend cannot argue that his account is *better* than rival accounts. Lakatos's methodology of research programs was designed to show that rational evaluation of scientific change is possible without the rigid rules Feyerabend attacks.

**Larry Laudan** argued that Feyerabend conflates "contextual" or "local" methodological judgments (which depend on specific circumstances) with the complete absence of rational standards. Laudan's "research traditions" approach showed that theories can be evaluated by their problem-solving effectiveness even in the absence of a universal algorithm.

**John Watkins** argued that Feyerabend's historical scholarship was tendentious. The Galileo case, Watkins claimed, was not as irrational as Feyerabend portrayed it: the telescope had been tested terrestrially, Copernicanism addressed known problems with Ptolemy, and the choice between them was more reasonable than Feyerabend admitted.

**Ernest Gellner** charged that Feyerabend's relativism was a luxury that members of stable, scientifically-minded societies could afford but that had disastrous consequences when applied in the real world. If all knowledge traditions are equal, Gellner asked, why should we prefer modern medicine over witch-doctoring?

## Alternative Solutions

**Karl Popper** offered falsificationism: science progresses through bold conjectures and severe tests. Feyerabend's objection is that scientists do not and should not abandon theories at the first sign of counterevidence. Popperians reply that falsificationism is a normative standard, not a descriptive account, and that Feyerabend's historical examples show only that scientists are sometimes irrational — not that irrationality is scientifically productive.

**Imre Lakatos** offered his methodology of scientific research programs: theories are evaluated not individually but as parts of ongoing research programs; a program is "degenerating" if it becomes increasingly ad hoc. Feyerabend's response is that Lakatos's standards are empty — they do not forbid any behavior because any program can be portrayed as progressive with enough ingenuity.

**Larry Laudan** offered a problem-solving model: science progresses by solving empirical and conceptual problems. Unlike Lakatos and Popper, Laudan's model does not require universal standards of theory choice but still provides criteria for rational evaluation. Laudan's approach has been influential in the sociology of scientific knowledge and avoids the relativism Feyerabend seems to embrace.

**Helen Longino** offers a social-epistemological approach: scientific method is not a set of rules for individuals but a set of social norms — criticism, peer review, transparency, responsiveness to evidence — that govern scientific communities. This account preserves rationality while acknowledging the social and political dimensions of science that Feyerabend emphasizes.

## Practical Applications

Feyerabend's arguments have been applied in debates about science education (should schools teach creationism alongside evolution? alternative medicine alongside biomedicine?), science funding (should the state fund only peer-reviewed research?), and scientific expertise (should experts have the final say in policy disputes?). These remain live questions in contemporary political and philosophical debates.

The most direct practical application is the push for epistemic diversity in scientific institutions: including more voices, methodologies, and perspectives in research. Whether this requires the radical separation of science and state that Feyerabend proposed remains an open question.
