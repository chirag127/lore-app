# Narration — The Structure of Scientific Revolutions

Thomas Samuel Kuhn was born in Cincinnati in 1922. He studied physics at Harvard, earning his bachelor's degree in 1943, his master's in 1946, and his doctorate in 1949. His dissertation was on the theory of solids — a standard piece of normal science within the quantum mechanical paradigm.

The book that would make him famous began not as philosophy but as a pedagogical experiment. In 1947, Harvard president James Bryant Conant asked Kuhn to design a course in the history of science for undergraduates in the General Education program. Kuhn, who had never studied the history of science, began reading Aristotle's *Physics* with his students. What he found there changed his intellectual trajectory.

Reading Aristotle had always frustrated modern physicists: how could such a brilliant mind have gotten so much wrong? But Kuhn realized that the problem was not Aristotle's intelligence but the reader's assumptions. When Kuhn learned to read Aristotle on his own terms — within the conceptual framework of Aristotelian physics — the arguments made perfect sense. This experience planted the seed for the concept of incommensurability.

Kuhn left Harvard in 1956 for the University of California, Berkeley, where he taught history of science. *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* was published in 1962 as a monograph in the *International Encyclopedia of Unified Science* — an unlikely venue for what would become the most famous philosophy book of the century. The University of Chicago Press picked it up as a standalone book later that year.

The reaction was explosive. Philosophers accused Kuhn of undermining the rationality of science. Popperians saw him as a threat to the very idea of scientific progress. Sociologists and historians embraced him. The book became a Rorschach test: what you saw in it said more about your own commitments than about Kuhn's argument. For the next three decades, Kuhn was the central figure in the philosophy of science, constantly clarifying, defending, and sometimes retreating from his original claims.

Kuhn's writing style is remarkable for its accessibility. Unlike most philosophy of science, *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* contains no formal logic, no symbolic notation, and almost no technical jargon. The arguments proceed through historical example and vivid analogy. This accessibility is partly responsible for the book's wide influence — but also for its many misinterpretations.

The political implications were never far from the surface. The book appeared at the height of the Cold War, when the authority of science was a matter of national prestige. Kuhn's argument that science was not a simple accumulation of objective truths seemed, to some critics, to weaken the West's claim to rational superiority over Soviet ideology. Kuhn insisted this was not his intention — but the debate reflected how much was at stake in how we understand science.

Kuhn died in 1996 at the age of 73. He spent his final decade at MIT, working on a book about the philosophy of language and perception that he never completed. His influence has only grown since his death. *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* is now considered not just a classic of philosophy but one of the most consequential books of the 20th century — a work that fundamentally changed how we understand the enterprise of science and, by extension, how we understand human knowledge itself.
