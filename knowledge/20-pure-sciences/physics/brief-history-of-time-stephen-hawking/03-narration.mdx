---
title: "A Brief History of Time — Narration"
book: "A Brief History of Time"
author: "Stephen Hawking"
---

There is a question that has haunted humanity since the first conscious minds looked up at the night sky and wondered what they were seeing. Where did all of this come from? The stars, the planets, the vast and silent darkness between them—did it always exist, or did it begin? And if it began, what happened before? Stephen Hawking takes up this question not as a philosopher or a theologian but as a physicist, and his answer is as startling as it is elegant.

Hawking begins with a story about an elderly woman at a public lecture who, upon hearing the astronomer explain that the Earth orbits the Sun, declared that the whole thing was nonsense because it was obvious the Earth was flat and stationary. The point is not that the woman was foolish. The point is that our everyday intuitions about the universe—intuitions shaped by a lifetime of living on what feels like a flat, still surface—are systematically wrong. Every major advance in cosmology has required us to abandon what seems obvious. The Earth moves. Space curves. Time stretches. And the universe itself is expanding, rushing apart in all directions from a moment of inconceivable density and heat.

This is the central narrative of the book: the progressive uncovering of a reality that defies common sense. Hawking traces the journey from Copernicus, who displaced the Earth from the center of the cosmos, through Galileo, who paid for his convictions with house arrest, to Newton, who unified the laws of falling apples and orbiting moons in a single mathematical framework. Then comes Einstein, who shattered the Newtonian edifice and replaced it with something stranger and more beautiful. Space and time, Einstein showed, are not the fixed stage on which the drama of the universe unfolds. They are part of the drama—dynamic, flexible, warped by the presence of matter and energy. The planet Mercury wobbles in its orbit not because of some invisible tug but because the Sun's mass bends the fabric of spacetime around it. Light itself, the fastest thing in the universe, follows curved paths through this warped geometry.

But general relativity, for all its elegance, is not the whole story. At the smallest scales—the scale of atoms and subatomic particles—a different set of rules governs reality. Quantum mechanics, born in the early twentieth century, replaced the smooth certainties of classical physics with a world of probabilities and uncertainties. You cannot simultaneously know where a particle is and how fast it is moving. This is not a limitation of our instruments; it is a fundamental feature of nature. The universe, at its most basic level, is not a precise machine. It is a shimmering field of possibilities, collapsing into definite outcomes only when we look.

The tension between these two frameworks—the smooth curvature of general relativity and the jittery uncertainty of quantum mechanics—is the great unresolved problem of modern physics. Hawking is honest about this. He does not pretend that science has all the answers. What it has are partial theories, each brilliant in its domain, none complete. The dream of a unified theory, a single set of equations that would encompass everything from the Big Bang to the behavior of quarks, remains elusive. String theory, with its extra dimensions curled up beyond the reach of any experiment, is the most promising candidate, but Hawking acknowledges that it may be forever beyond our ability to test.

Into this landscape of uncertainty, Hawking introduces his own great contribution: the idea that black holes are not entirely black. A black hole, as traditionally understood, is a region of spacetime where gravity is so overwhelming that nothing—not even light—can escape. It is a cosmic trap, a one-way door. But Hawking showed that quantum effects near the event horizon—the boundary of no return—allow pairs of virtual particles to form, with one falling in and the other escaping. The escaping particle carries energy, and so the black hole slowly loses mass. It radiates. It evaporates. Given enough time, a black hole will vanish entirely, leaving behind only the radiation it emitted and the mystery of what happened to everything that fell in.

This is the black hole information paradox, and it remains one of the deepest puzzles in physics. If a black hole evaporates completely, does the information about everything it consumed disappear forever? Quantum mechanics says no—information cannot be destroyed. But general relativity seems to say yes. The resolution of this paradox may hold the key to a unified theory. Hawking does not pretend to have solved it, but he frames it with the clarity and precision of someone who helped create it.

The book also addresses the origin of the universe itself. Hawking, working with James Hartle, proposed the no-boundary proposal: that if we describe the universe using imaginary time rather than real time, the universe becomes finite but has no edge or boundary, much like the surface of a sphere. There is no moment of creation, no first instant, no need for a cause preceding an effect. The universe simply exists, self-contained and eternal in imaginary time. This is a deeply counterintuitive idea, but it is mathematically coherent, and it dissolves the ancient question of what came before the Big Bang by showing that the question itself may be meaningless.

Hawking also tackles the arrow of time—the puzzling fact that time seems to flow in only one direction. The fundamental laws of physics are time-symmetric; they work just as well backward as forward. Yet we remember the past and not the future, eggs break but do not unbreak, and the universe is expanding rather than contracting. Hawking argues that this arrow arises from thermodynamics: the second law says that entropy, or disorder, always increases. The universe began in an extraordinarily low-entropy state at the Big Bang, and everything since has been the inevitable increase of disorder. Time flows in the direction of increasing entropy. Our psychological sense of time's passage is a consequence of this thermodynamic arrow, not its cause.

Throughout the book, Hawking's voice is distinctive—measured, precise, and occasionally wry. He writes without pretension, acknowledging complexity without hiding behind jargon. His humor is dry and understated, as when he notes that the discovery that the universe is expanding means that an expanding universe does not preclude a creator, but it does place limits on when he might have carried out his job. This is a scientist who has thought deeply about the largest questions and is sharing his thinking with candor and humility.

The book's cultural impact is difficult to overstate. It sold over twenty-five million copies in more than sixty languages. It spent nearly four years on the London Sunday Times bestseller list. It made cosmology a topic of conversation in households around the world. And it made Stephen Hawking, a man confined to a wheelchair by a disease that should have killed him decades earlier, one of the most recognized and beloved figures on Earth. His achievement was not just scientific but deeply human: he showed that the life of the mind can triumph over the limitations of the body, and that the universe, for all its vastness and indifference, is knowable.

Today, the specific measurements Hawking cited have been refined. The age of the universe is known with greater precision. The existence of dark energy has been confirmed. Gravitational waves have been detected. But the questions Hawking raised—Where did the universe come from? What is the nature of time? Can we find a theory of everything?—remain as urgent and unanswered as ever. The book is a snapshot of a moment in the history of human understanding, written by the person who did more than anyone else to expand that understanding. It is not a textbook. It is an invitation to wonder.
