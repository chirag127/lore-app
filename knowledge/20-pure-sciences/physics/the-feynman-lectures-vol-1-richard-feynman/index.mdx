The Feynman Lectures on Physics, Volume I: Mainly Mechanics, Radiation, and Heat is the first of three volumes based on Richard Feynman's two-year introductory physics course delivered at Caltech from 1961 to 1963. Co-authored with Robert B. Leighton and Matthew Sands, the book treats physics not as a collection of formulas but as a living intellectual adventure, progressing from atoms in motion through Newton's laws, conservation principles, relativity, optics, electromagnetic radiation, quantum behavior, kinetic theory, thermodynamics, waves, and symmetry. More than a textbook, it is a masterclass in physical reasoning from one of the 20th century's greatest physicists.

Feynman approached the course with a mandate to excite Caltech's brightest undergraduates, and the result is widely considered the greatest physics lectures ever recorded. In 2013, Caltech released the full text online for free, ensuring universal access. Rob Phillips, reviewing the book in *Nature* that year, described its "simplicity, beauty, unity presented with enthusiasm and insight." Yet as co-author Matthew Sands later noted, the lectures were never quite suitable as a standalone textbook — they work best as a supplement that transforms understanding.
