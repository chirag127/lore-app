Silent Spring, published on September 27, 1962, is the foundational text of the modern environmental movement — a meticulously researched work of investigative science journalism and moral philosophy that exposed the catastrophic ecological and human health consequences of indiscriminate synthetic pesticide use. Rachel Carson, already celebrated as the author of *The Sea Around Us* (1951) and a distinguished aquatic biologist with the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, turned her prodigious talent for translating complex science into lyrical, accessible prose toward a subject she had been tracking since the late 1940s: the postwar chemical assault on the American landscape.

The book assembled hundreds of case studies and peer-reviewed findings into a unified argument so compelling that it triggered a presidential review, earned the enmity of a multi-billion-dollar chemical industry, and ultimately reshaped federal environmental policy. Within a decade, DDT was banned in the United States, the Environmental Protection Agency was created, and a global environmental consciousness was born. *Silent Spring* has been translated into more than twenty languages, remains in continuous print, and was named one of the 25 greatest science books of all time by the editors of *Discover* magazine in 2006.
