## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

### Part I: Groups and Group Theory

**Chapters 1-4: Introduction and Examples.** Fraleigh begins with an inviting introduction to groups through symmetries of geometric figures. Defines groups and provides examples: the integers modulo n, the symmetric group S_n, matrix groups GL(n,R) and SL(n,R), and the quaternion group. The pace is gentle with extensive motivation.

**Chapters 5-8: Subgroups and Quotients.** Covers subgroups, cyclic groups, cosets, Lagrange's theorem, and normal subgroups. Fraleigh's treatment of quotient groups is careful and builds intuition before abstraction. The isomorphism theorems are presented clearly with applications.

**Chapters 9-11: Group Actions and the Sylow Theorems.** Group actions are introduced through concrete examples (stabilizers, orbits, the class equation). The Sylow theorems are proved with applications to classifying groups of small order. Direct and semidirect products are covered.

### Part II: Rings and Ring Theory

**Chapters 12-14: Introduction to Rings.** Rings are introduced through familiar examples (Z, Q[x], M_n(R)). Covers subrings, ideals, quotient rings, ring homomorphisms, and the isomorphism theorems. The emphasis is on building intuition through computation.

**Chapters 15-17: Factorization.** Treats integral domains, Euclidean domains, PIDs, and UFDs. Includes Gauss's lemma, Eisenstein's criterion, and polynomial rings. Fraleigh's treatment is accessible without sacrificing rigor.

### Part III: Fields and Galois Theory

**Chapters 18-20: Field Extensions.** Introduces field extensions, vector space structure of field extensions, degree of an extension, and algebraic elements. The geometry of ruler-and-compass constructions is used to motivate the theory.

**Chapters 21-23: Galois Theory.** Develops the fundamental theorem of Galois theory. Fraleigh uses the tower law, the correspondence theorem, and the primitive element theorem to build toward the crowning result: the insolvability of the quintic. The chapter includes sections on finite fields, cyclotomic fields, and applications to classical Greek construction problems.

### Part IV: Special Topics

**Chapters 24-26:** Additional topics including modules (a brief introduction), algebraic coding theory, and the structure of finite abelian groups.

## Reading Guide

Fraleigh is designed for a one-year undergraduate course. Chapters 1-11 (groups) typically occupy the first semester, and Chapters 12-23 (rings and fields) the second. The book assumes only a standard calculus sequence and mathematical maturity. Each section ends with a substantial set of exercises, divided into "Computations" and "Proofs" — students should attempt both categories. The historical notes throughout provide context and motivation. Instructors often supplement Fraleigh with applications from coding theory or cryptography to maintain student interest.
