## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

### Part I: Groups

**Chapters 1-3: Group Theory.** A compact but rigorous treatment of group theory. Rotman covers subgroups, normal subgroups, quotient groups, the isomorphism theorems, group actions, the Sylow theorems, and the structure of finite abelian groups. The presentation assumes some prior familiarity and moves quickly.

**Chapters 4-5: Solvable and Nilpotent Groups.** Covers the commutator subgroup, the derived series, solvable groups, nilpotent groups, and the Jordan-Holder theorem. Free groups and presentations are treated with clarity.

### Part II: Rings and Modules

**Chapters 6-7: Rings and Modules.** Rings, ideals, polynomial rings, and the classical factorization theory (Euclidean domains, PIDs, UFDs). Module theory including exact sequences, free modules, projective and injective modules, and the structure theorem for finitely generated modules over PIDs.

**Chapter 8: Categories.** A thorough introduction to category theory: categories, functors, natural transformations, limits and colimits, adjoint functors, and the Yoneda lemma. This chapter lays the groundwork for the homological algebra to follow.

### Part III: Homological Algebra

**Chapters 9-10: Homological Algebra.** The heart of the book. Covers complexes, homology, projective and injective resolutions, derived functors (Ext and Tor), the Kunneth theorem, and group homology and cohomology. Rotman's treatment is comprehensive and includes many computational examples.

**Chapter 11: Homological Dimension.** Dimension theory for rings, the global dimension, and the Auslander-Buchsbaum formula. Connections to commutative algebra.

### Part IV: Commutative Algebra

**Chapters 12-13: Commutative Algebra.** Noetherian rings, the Hilbert basis theorem, primary decomposition, integral extensions, Dedekind domains, and the Hilbert Nullstellensatz. The geometric motivation is emphasized throughout.

### Part V: Algebraic Number Theory

**Chapter 14: Algebraic Number Theory.** An introduction to the algebraic theory of numbers. Covers algebraic integers, the ring of integers of a number field, class groups, and Dirichlet's unit theorem. The connections to Galois theory and commutative algebra are highlighted.

### Part VI: Additional Topics

**Chapters 15-17:** Additional advanced topics including the representation theory of finite groups, an introduction to algebraic geometry, and the theory of associative algebras.

## Reading Guide

Rotman's book is best suited for a second course in algebra or for self-study after a standard graduate algebra sequence. The homological algebra chapters (9-11) are the most distinctive feature and could form the basis of a semester course. Parts I and II can be used for review or covered quickly. The later chapters (12-14) connect algebra to number theory and geometry, making the book valuable for students planning to work in those areas. The exercises are extensive and include many that extend the theory presented in the text. A unique feature is the "Notes and References" section at the end of each chapter providing historical background and pointers to the literature.
