## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

### Part I: Groups

**Chapters 1-3: Basic Group Theory.** Isaacs begins with a review of group fundamentals: subgroups, cosets, normal subgroups, quotient groups, and the isomorphism theorems. The treatment is efficient and assumes prior exposure to group theory. The pace is faster than Dummit and Foote or Lang.

**Chapters 4-6: Group Structure and Actions.** Group actions, the Sylow theorems, and applications to the classification of groups. Isaacs's treatment of the Sylow theorems is elegant and includes several nontrivial applications. Semidirect products and the classification of groups of small order.

**Chapters 7-9: Solvable and Nilpotent Groups.** A particular strength of the book. Isaacs was an expert in group theory, and these chapters reflect his deep understanding. Covers the commutator subgroup, the lower and upper central series, the Fitting subgroup, and the Frattini subgroup. Includes the theorem of P. Hall on solvable groups and the Schur-Zassenhaus theorem.

**Chapters 10-12: Further Group Theory.** Free groups, presentations, the Burnside problem (an introduction), and transfer theory. Isaacs includes material rarely found in graduate texts at this level, such as the Schur multiplier and covering groups.

### Part II: Rings and Modules

**Chapters 13-15: Rings and Ideals.** Rings, ideals, prime and maximal ideals, the field of fractions, and polynomial rings. The treatment is brisk and assumes familiarity from undergraduate study.

**Chapters 16-18: Modules.** Modules, submodules, quotient modules, direct sums, free modules, and the structure theorem for finitely generated modules over PIDs. The treatment of the structure theorem emphasizes the invariant factor and elementary divisor formulations.

**Chapters 19-21: Artinian Rings.** The Jacobson radical, semisimple modules, the Artin-Wedderburn theorem, and the structure of Artinian rings. Nilradicals and prime radicals. Isaacs's treatment is clean and well-organized.

### Part III: Fields and Galois Theory

**Chapters 22-24: Field Extensions.** The fundamentals of field theory: algebraic elements, splitting fields, the degree of an extension, separable and normal extensions, finite fields.

**Chapters 25-27: Galois Theory.** The fundamental theorem, the Galois group of a polynomial, cyclotomic fields, and the insolvability of the quintic. Isaacs's presentation is efficient and emphasizes the logical structure of the theory.

### Part IV: Selected Topics

**Chapters 28-30:** Representation theory of finite groups (characters, orthogonality relations, induced representations), an introduction to homological algebra (Ext and Tor), and the structure of Hopf algebras.

## Reading Guide

Isaacs is designed for a two-semester graduate course. The book's focus on non-commutative algebra distinguishes it from other graduate texts. Groups receive the most extensive treatment (Chapters 1-12), reflecting the author's expertise. Instructors may choose to cover the ring and field theory sections (Chapters 13-27) in the second semester, possibly omitting some of the more specialized group theory topics. The exercises are among the best in any algebra text—carefully crafted to build technique and extend understanding. Each chapter contains both routine exercises and "challenge" problems.
