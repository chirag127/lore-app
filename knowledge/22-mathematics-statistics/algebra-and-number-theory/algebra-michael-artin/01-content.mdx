## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

### Part I: Linear Algebra

**Chapters 1-2: Matrices and Linear Equations.** Artin begins with linear algebra — the algebra that arises naturally from geometry. Covers systems of linear equations, matrices, row reduction, and determinants. The treatment is computational but with an eye toward deeper theory.

**Chapters 3-4: Vector Spaces and Linear Transformations.** Vector spaces, bases, dimension, linear transformations, eigenvalues and eigenvectors, the characteristic polynomial, and canonical forms. Artin's approach emphasizes geometric intuition: eigenvalues are introduced through the problem of finding invariant lines, canonical forms through the action of a single linear transformation on a vector space. The spectral theorem for symmetric matrices is proved.

### Part II: Groups

**Chapters 5-6: Groups and Symmetry.** Groups are introduced through the study of symmetries. The symmetric group S_n, the dihedral groups, and matrix groups (GL_n, O_n, SL_n) are primary examples. Covers Lagrange's theorem, normal subgroups, quotient groups, and the isomorphism theorems. Group actions are given a central role.

**Chapter 7: More Group Theory.** The Sylow theorems with applications, the classification of finite abelian groups, and an introduction to the structure of finite groups. Free groups and presentations are covered briefly.

### Part III: Rings

**Chapter 8: Rings.** Rings are introduced through the examples of Z, polynomial rings, and matrix rings. Covers subrings, ideals, quotient rings, ring homomorphisms, and the isomorphism theorems. The Chinese remainder theorem and the field of fractions of an integral domain.

**Chapter 9: Factorization.** Euclidean domains, principal ideal domains, and unique factorization domains. Artin's treatment emphasizes the parallels with the integers and polynomials. Gauss's lemma, Eisenstein's criterion, and polynomial factorization.

### Part IV: Fields

**Chapter 10: Fields.** Field extensions, the degree of an extension, algebraic and transcendental elements, splitting fields, and finite fields. Artin's treatment is concise and elegant.

**Chapter 11: Galois Theory.** The fundamental theorem of Galois theory, cyclotomic fields, the insolvability of the quintic, and applications to ruler-and-compass constructions. Artin's presentation is notably clear and geometric in spirit.

### Part V: Modules and Advanced Topics

**Chapter 12: Modules.** An introduction to module theory over a PID, leading to the structure theorem and its application to canonical forms. The treatment is briefer than in other texts but sufficient for the book's purposes.

**Appendix:** Additional topics including the classification of semisimple Lie algebras (a preview) and the connection to algebraic groups.

## Reading Guide

Artin's book is designed for a one-year undergraduate or beginning graduate course. Its most distinctive feature is the emphasis on linear algebra and geometry. The typical sequence would be: linear algebra (Chapters 1-4) followed by groups (Chapters 5-7) in the first semester, and rings and fields (Chapters 8-11 plus Chapter 12) in the second. The book is suitable for advanced undergraduates who have completed a standard linear algebra course. Artin's style is clear and motivated, making it accessible for self-study. The exercises range from computational to theoretical and include many that connect algebra to other areas of mathematics. The geometric perspective makes this book particularly valuable for students planning to work in algebraic geometry or Lie theory.
