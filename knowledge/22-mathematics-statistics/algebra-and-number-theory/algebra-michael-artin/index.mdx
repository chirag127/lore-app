Michael Artin's *Algebra* offers a distinctive approach to introductory abstract algebra, informed by Artin's perspective as a Fields Medalist and his deep contributions to algebraic geometry. First published in 1991, the book emphasizes the geometric origins and applications of algebraic ideas.

The text covers groups, rings, fields, and modules, but the order and emphasis differ from traditional treatments. Artin begins with linear algebra — the algebra that arises naturally from geometry — and uses it as a springboard into more abstract territory. Group theory is introduced through symmetries and matrix groups (the "linear" groups that give the subject much of its power). Throughout, Artin shows how algebraic structures arise from geometric problems, keeping the subject anchored to concrete intuition even as the level of abstraction rises. His treatment of Galois theory, in particular, is noteworthy for its clarity and elegance.
