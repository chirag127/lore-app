## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

### Part I: Basic Concepts

**Chapter 1: Sets and Mappings.** Lang establishes the set-theoretic foundations, covering sets, mappings, equivalence relations, and Zorn's Lemma. The treatment is brisk and assumes familiarity with basic set theory. Includes cardinality and the axiom of choice.

**Chapter 2: Groups.** A thorough introduction to group theory. Begins with definitions and examples (symmetric groups, matrix groups), moves through subgroups, cosets, Lagrange's theorem, normal subgroups, quotient groups, and the isomorphism theorems. Concludes with group actions, Sylow theorems, and the classification of finite abelian groups. Lang's treatment of group actions is particularly crisp and provides the foundation for later applications.

**Chapter 3: Rings.** Introduces rings, ideals, homomorphisms, and the isomorphism theorems for rings. Covers polynomial rings, factorization, Euclidean domains, principal ideal domains, and unique factorization domains. The chapter includes the Chinese remainder theorem and a discussion of the prime spectrum of a ring, foreshadowing algebraic geometry.

**Chapter 4: Modules.** Defines modules over a ring and develops their theory in parallel with vector spaces. Covers direct sums, free modules, projective and injective modules, and the structure theorem for finitely generated modules over a PID. This chapter is one of the strongest in the book and provides the conceptual bridge between linear algebra and ring theory.

**Chapter 5: Linear Algebra.** A substantial chapter covering vector spaces, linear transformations, matrices, determinants, and the theory of a single linear transformation (rational and Jordan canonical forms). Includes multilinear algebra, tensor products, and exterior algebras. Lang treats linear algebra as a special case of module theory while maintaining computational accessibility.

### Part II: Algebraic Equations

**Chapter 6: Galois Theory.** The high point of the book. Covers field extensions, algebraic and transcendental extensions, splitting fields, normal and separable extensions, the fundamental theorem of Galois theory, and applications including cyclotomic fields, finite fields, and the insolvability of the quintic. Lang's treatment is notably clean and conceptual.

**Chapter 7: Valuation Theory.** Introduces valuations, completions, and p-adic fields. Covers absolute values, Hensel's lemma, and extensions of valuations. This chapter connects algebra to number theory.

**Chapter 8: Commutative Rings.** A more advanced treatment of commutative algebra. Covers Noetherian rings, primary decomposition, integral dependence, Dedekind domains, and the Hilbert Nullstellensatz. This chapter prepares the reader for algebraic geometry.

### Part III: Algebraic Structures

**Chapter 9: Representation Theory.** Introduces representations of finite groups, characters, induced representations, and the Frobenius reciprocity theorem. Includes the Burnside theorem and applications to group theory.

**Chapter 10: Homological Algebra.** The most infamous chapter in Lang's book. Covers categories and functors, exact sequences, projective and injective resolutions, derived functors (Ext and Tor), and group cohomology. The first edition of this chapter is legendary for its difficulty; later editions softened it somewhat but it remains challenging.

**Chapter 11: Further Algebra.** A collection of advanced topics including algebraic groups, Lie algebras, and an introduction to the theory of algebraic numbers.

## Reading Guide

Lang's *Algebra* is not a book to be read passively. Each section should be worked through with pencil and paper. The exercises are essential — many contain results used in later chapters. Readers new to the book should start with Chapters 2-5 (groups, rings, modules, linear algebra) regardless of their background. The Galois theory section (Chapter 6) is the payoff for the earlier material and should not be rushed. The homological algebra chapter (Chapter 10) can be deferred to a second reading or to accompany a course in algebraic topology. A typical two-semester sequence covers Chapters 1-8 in the first semester and Chapters 9-11 in the second.
