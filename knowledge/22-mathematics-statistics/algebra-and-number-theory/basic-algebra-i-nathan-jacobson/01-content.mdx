## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

### Part I: Preliminaries

**Chapter 0: Set Theory and Number Theory.** Jacobson begins with a concise review of set theory, including cardinal numbers, Zorn's Lemma, and the well-ordering principle. A brief treatment of the integers covers induction, divisibility, and the Euclidean algorithm. This chapter establishes the language used throughout the text.

### Part II: Groups

**Chapter 1: Groups.** Defines groups and provides an extensive array of examples: symmetric groups, dihedral groups, matrix groups (GL(n,F), SL(n,F)), and the quaternion group. Covers subgroups, cosets, Lagrange's theorem, normal subgroups, quotient groups, and the isomorphism theorems. Includes the group action concept and its applications.

**Chapter 2: Group Theory Continued.** Delves deeper into group structure. Treats the Sylow theorems with complete proofs, the Jordan-Holder theorem for compositions series, and the theory of solvable and nilpotent groups. Concludes with free groups and presentations via generators and relations.

### Part III: Rings

**Chapter 3: Rings.** Introduces rings, subrings, ideals, and ring homomorphisms. Covers polynomial rings, matrix rings, and the field of fractions of an integral domain. The isomorphism theorems are developed in parallel with the group theory treatment.

**Chapter 4: Factorization Theory.** A comprehensive treatment of factorization in integral domains. Covers Euclidean domains, principal ideal domains, and unique factorization domains. Includes Gauss's lemma, Eisenstein's criterion, and the polynomial ring case. Jacobson's exposition of UFDs is notably thorough.

**Chapter 5: Modules.** Introduces modules over a ring as a generalization of vector spaces and abelian groups. Covers submodules, quotient modules, direct sums, free modules, and the structure theorem for finitely generated modules over PIDs. The treatment of the structure theorem is particularly detailed and includes applications to linear algebra (canonical forms of matrices).

### Part IV: Vector Spaces and Linear Algebra

**Chapter 6: Vector Spaces.** A review and extension of linear algebra from the module perspective. Covers bases, dimension, linear transformations, dual spaces, and the relationship between linear transformations and matrices.

**Chapter 7: Linear Transformations.** Develops the theory of a single linear transformation in detail. Includes the minimal polynomial, characteristic polynomial, invariant subspaces, and the rational and Jordan canonical forms. The treatment uses the module-theoretic approach (F[x]-modules) to unify the theory.

### Part V: Fields and Galois Theory

**Chapter 8: Field Extensions.** Introduces field extensions, algebraic elements, minimal polynomials, degree of an extension, and the classification of finite fields. Covers ruler-and-compass constructions as a classical application.

**Chapter 9: Galois Theory.** The culminating chapter of the volume. Jacobson develops the fundamental theorem of Galois theory, relating intermediate fields to subgroups of the Galois group. Covers cyclotomic extensions, finite fields, and the insolvability of the quintic by radicals. The treatment is authoritative and draws on Jacobson's deep understanding of the subject.

## Reading Guide

*Basic Algebra I* can be used for a two-semester sequence. A one-semester course might cover Chapters 1-4 and selected topics from Chapters 5 and 8. The book assumes mathematical maturity but no prior abstract algebra. Jacobson's style is direct and theorem-proof, but he provides ample motivation and historical context. The exercises range from routine to quite challenging and are essential for mastery. Readers moving on to Volume II should ensure a solid grasp of Galois theory and module theory.
