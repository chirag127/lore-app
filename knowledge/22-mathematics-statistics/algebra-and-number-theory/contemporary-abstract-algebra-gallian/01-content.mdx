## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

### Part I: Groups

**Chapters 1-2: Preliminaries and Groups.** Gallian begins with modular arithmetic and the concept of binary operations. Groups are introduced via symmetries, with careful definitions and numerous examples including Z_n, U(n), and the dihedral groups.

**Chapters 3-5: Subgroups and Cyclic Groups.** Covers subgroups, centralizers, normalizers, and cyclic group structure. Gallian's treatment emphasizes the classification of subgroups of cyclic groups and the Euler phi function.

**Chapter 6-7: Permutation Groups and Cosets.** The symmetric group S_n receives extensive treatment with applications to puzzle-solving. Cosets and Lagrange's theorem are covered with crystal clarity. Cayley's theorem is proved.

**Chapters 8-10: Normal Subgroups and Factor Groups.** Isomorphism theorems, normal subgroups, and quotient groups. The class equation is introduced through group actions. Applications to counting arguments in finite groups.

**Chapters 11-12: Sylow Theorems.** The Sylow theorems with applications to group classification. Direct products and the fundamental theorem of finite abelian groups.

**Chapter 13-14: Homomorphisms and Further Topics.** Group homomorphisms, the kernel-image relationship, and applications. Introduction to free groups and presentations.

### Part II: Rings

**Chapters 15-17: Introduction to Rings.** Rings, subrings, ideals, and quotient rings. Polynomial rings, integral domains, and the field of fractions. Gallian emphasizes the parallels between group and ring theory.

**Chapters 18-19: Factorization.** Euclidean domains, PIDs, UFDs, and polynomial factorization. Irreducibility criteria including Eisenstein's criterion and reduction mod p.

### Part III: Fields and Galois Theory

**Chapters 20-21: Field Extensions.** Vector space structure of field extensions, algebraic elements, degree calculations. Geometric constructions are used to provide concrete motivation.

**Chapters 22-23: Galois Theory.** The fundamental theorem, the Galois group of a polynomial, solvability by radicals, and the impossibility of the quintic. Gallian's treatment is accessible for advanced undergraduates.

### Part IV: Applications and Special Topics

**Chapters 24-32:** A remarkable array of applications including cryptography (RSA, ElGamal), coding theory (linear codes, BCH codes), Polya counting theory, and the algebraic structure of Rubik's Cube. The applications chapters set Gallian apart from other undergraduate texts.

## Reading Guide

Gallian is designed for a one-semester or full-year undergraduate course. A one-semester course typically covers Chapters 1-15 (groups and rings). A full-year course adds Chapters 16-23 and selected applications from Chapters 24-32. The book is exceptionally well-suited for self-study: each chapter begins with a historical vignette, includes worked examples, and ends with a "Biography" section. The "Historical Notes" and "References" sections at chapter ends provide context and further reading. The exercises are extensive and graded by difficulty. Gallian's writing style is engaging and conversational, making the material accessible to students who may find other texts intimidating.
