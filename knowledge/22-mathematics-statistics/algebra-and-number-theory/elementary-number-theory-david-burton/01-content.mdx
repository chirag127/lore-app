## Chapter 1: Preliminaries

Burton opens with mathematical induction and the binomial theorem, establishing the proof techniques used throughout the book. The treatment of induction is thorough, with multiple examples and variations (strong induction, backward induction). The binomial theorem is proved and applied to number-theoretic problems. This chapter sets the tone: rigorous but accessible, with historical notes woven into the mathematical exposition.

## Chapter 2: Divisibility Theory in the Integers

This chapter covers the foundational material: the division algorithm, greatest common divisors, the Euclidean algorithm, and the Diophantine equation ax + by = c. Burton's treatment of the Euclidean algorithm is particularly clear, with worked examples and geometric interpretations. The chapter includes biographical sketches of key figures, including Euclid and Diophantus. The exercises range from computational to theoretical, building both skill and understanding.

## Chapter 3: Primes and Their Distribution

Burton discusses prime numbers, the fundamental theorem of arithmetic, the sieve of Eratosthenes, and the Goldbach conjecture. The chapter includes Euclid's proof of the infinitude of primes and a discussion of the distribution of primes, leading up to the prime number theorem (stated but not proved). The historical notes cover Marin Mersenne, Christian Goldbach, and their contributions to number theory.

## Chapter 4: The Theory of Congruences

This chapter introduces congruences, beginning with a biographical sketch of Carl Friedrich Gauss, whose Disquisitiones Arithmeticae established the modern foundations of number theory. Burton covers the basic properties of congruences, linear congruences, the Chinese remainder theorem, and applications to decimal representations. The treatment is systematic and includes numerous examples showing how congruences simplify computational problems.

## Chapter 5: Fermat's Theorem

Burton proves Fermat's little theorem and Wilson's theorem, with biographical material on Pierre de Fermat. The chapter includes Euler's generalization of Fermat's theorem, the Euler phi function, and applications to the computation of large powers modulo primes. The treatment emphasizes the historical development: Fermat's original statements, Euler's proofs and generalizations, and later refinements.

## Chapter 6: Number-Theoretic Functions

The Mobius function, the divisor functions, and the Euler totient function are studied in detail. Burton proves the Mobius inversion formula and uses it to derive relationships among arithmetic functions. The chapter includes a discussion of perfect numbers and amicable pairs, with historical notes on the Pythagoreans and their number mysticism.

## Chapter 7: Euler's Generalization of Fermat's Theorem

This chapter extends the results of Chapter 5 to composite moduli. Euler's theorem is proved and applied to the computation of powers modulo composite numbers. The chapter includes the concept of reduced residue systems and Carmichael numbers. The historical focus is on Leonhard Euler, whose prodigious output transformed number theory.

## Chapter 8: Primitive Roots and Indices

Burton develops the theory of primitive roots modulo primes and prime powers. The existence theorem for primitive roots is proved, and the concept of indices (discrete logarithms) is introduced. The chapter includes applications to the solution of polynomial congruences and the construction of multiplicative tables.

## Chapter 9: Quadratic Reciprocity

The law of quadratic reciprocity is stated and proved. Burton gives Gauss's third proof, which uses the Gauss lemma. Applications include the determination of quadratic residues and the solution of quadratic congruences. The chapter includes biographical material on Gauss and his many contributions to number theory.

## Chapter 10: Introduction to Cryptography

Later editions include a chapter on the RSA cryptosystem, showing how number-theoretic concepts apply to modern cryptography. Burton covers the basic algorithms, including modular exponentiation, primality testing, and the Euclidean algorithm as applied to key generation.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment

This summary covers the main topics but cannot convey Burton's distinctive historical approach, which is the book's main pedagogical contribution.

### Recommended Reading Path

| Reader Type | Time | What to Read |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | ~15 min | This summary |
| Interested | ~3-5 hr | Summary + Chapters 2, 4, 5, 9 |
| Undergraduate | ~15-25 hr | Full book |

### Chapters to Read in Full

- **Chapter 4** — The core of elementary number theory
- **Chapter 9** — Quadratic reciprocity, the deepest result

### Chapters to Skim or Skip

- **Chapter 1** — Review material for most readers

### What You'll Miss by Not Reading the Full Book

The historical biographies, the extensive exercise sets, and the gradual development from simple computations to abstract reasoning.
