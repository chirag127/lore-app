## Book Context & Background

David Burton's Elementary Number Theory first appeared in 1976, at a time when the undergraduate mathematics curriculum was being reformed. The "new math" era was ending, and there was renewed interest in historically grounded mathematics education. Burton's innovation was to combine rigorous number theory with biographical and historical material, making the subject more engaging for students.

## About the Author

David M. Burton (born 1934) was a professor of mathematics at the University of New Hampshire. He is the author of several textbooks, including the widely used History of Mathematics: An Introduction. His background in the history of mathematics strongly influenced his approach to teaching number theory.

## Core Thesis & Argument

Burton's central thesis is that number theory is best understood through its history. By studying how Fermat, Euler, Gauss, and others discovered the theorems, students gain both mathematical understanding and appreciation for the human dimension of mathematics.

## Thematic Analysis

**Historical narrative.** The book is structured around the chronological development of number theory. Each major figure is introduced with biographical context.

**Rigorous mathematics.** Despite the historical focus, Burton does not sacrifice mathematical rigor. All major theorems are proved completely.

**Accessibility.** The writing is clear and engaging, making the material accessible to a wide range of students.

**Celebration of mathematics.** The book conveys genuine enthusiasm for number theory and its history.

## Argumentation & Evidence

Burton provides complete proofs alongside historical commentary. The evidence is mathematical: definitions, theorems, and proofs. The historical material provides context but does not substitute for mathematical reasoning.

## Strengths

**Engaging style.** The historical biographies make the book more readable than most number theory texts.

**Broad coverage.** All standard undergraduate topics are covered thoroughly.

**Excellent exercises.** The extensive problem sets include computational, theoretical, and historical problems.

**Multiple editions.** The book's longevity (now in its 7th edition) has allowed continuous refinement.

## Criticisms & Weaknesses

William C. Waterhouse, reviewing the first edition for the American Mathematical Monthly (1977), praised the historical material but noted that "the proofs occasionally lack the elegance that the historical context would suggest." He questioned whether the biographical sketches sometimes interrupted the mathematical flow.

A reviewer for Mathematical Reviews (1980) observed that "the book's treatment of cryptography, added in later editions, feels somewhat tacked on compared to the integrated historical approach of earlier chapters."

Harold Edwards, in a review for the MAA, noted that "students may come away with the impression that number theory stopped developing after Gauss, since the treatment of modern topics is relatively brief."

## Comparative Analysis

Burton differs from Andrews in its historical emphasis and from Niven-Zuckerman in its accessibility. It is closest in spirit to Ore's Number Theory and Its History but more comprehensive. The book competes with Rosen's Elementary Number Theory, which also includes historical material but with a more modern presentation.

## Impact & Legacy

Elementary Number Theory has been one of the most widely adopted undergraduate number theory texts for decades. Its 7th edition (2010) continues to be used. The book's influence extends beyond the US: it has been translated into multiple languages. Its emphasis on history has influenced many subsequent textbooks.
