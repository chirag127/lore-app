David Burton's Elementary Number Theory has been a classroom standard since its first edition in 1976. What sets it apart is its integration of history: each chapter opens with biographical sketches of figures like Fermat, Euler, Gauss, and Lagrange, placing mathematical discoveries in their human context. The seventh edition continues this tradition while updating exercises and references.

The book covers all the classical topics — divisibility, primes, congruences, quadratic reciprocity, Diophantine equations, and number-theoretic functions — at a level accessible to undergraduates who have completed calculus. Burton's prose is clear and engaging, and the extensive exercise sets range from routine to challenging, making the book suitable for a wide range of students.
