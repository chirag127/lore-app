## Book Context & Background

When The Arithmetic of Elliptic Curves appeared in 1986, the arithmetic theory of elliptic curves was already deep and active. The Mordell-Weil theorem (1922/1929) showed rational points form finitely generated groups; Siegel's theorem (1929) showed integral points are finite; and the Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture (1965) linked ranks to L-functions. Yet no systematic book-length treatment existed. Students relied on Cassels's lecture notes, Shimura's Introduction to the Arithmetic Theory of Automorphic Functions, and a handful of survey articles. Silverman, then a young professor at Brown, filled this gap with a masterfully organized text that became the standard reference overnight.

## About the Author

Joseph H. Silverman (born 1955) earned his Sc.B. from Brown University (1977) and his Ph.D. from Harvard (1982) under John Tate, one of the giants of arithmetic geometry. After positions at MIT and Boston University, he returned to Brown as a professor in 1988, serving as mathematics department chair (2001-2004, 2008) and being named Royce Family Professor of Teaching Excellence (2014-2017). His research spans elliptic curves, arithmetic dynamics, and cryptography. He co-founded NTRU Cryptosystems and has been cited over 33,000 times. His two-volume elliptic curves treatment won the 2008 AMS Steele Prize for Mathematical Exposition. In 2026 he became president of the American Mathematical Society. His other notable books include Rational Points on Elliptic Curves (with Tate), A Friendly Introduction to Number Theory, and The Arithmetic of Dynamical Systems.

## Core Thesis & Argument

The book's central thesis is that the arithmetic of elliptic curves — their rational and integral points, their L-functions, their reductions — can be understood through a systematic interplay of algebraic geometry, the theory of formal groups, and the arithmetic of local and global fields. The structure mirrors this thesis: from geometry (Chapters I-III) through local analysis (IV-VII) to global arithmetic (VIII-X). Each layer builds on the previous, culminating in the two master theorems: Mordell-Weil and Siegel.

## Thematic Analysis

**The group law as organizing principle.** The chord-tangent group law on an elliptic curve is not an add-on but the unifying structure. It turns a geometric object into an algebraic group, enabling the use of cohomology, heights, and Galois theory. Silverman never lets the reader forget that points on elliptic curves are simultaneously geometric (coordinates on a curve) and algebraic (elements of a group).

**Local-global interplay.** The book's architecture enacts this theme. Curves are studied over finite fields, the complex numbers, p-adic fields, and number fields. Results over local fields (Tate curve, reduction types) feed directly into global theorems (conductor, Tamagawa numbers, the BSD formula).

**Heights as a bridge.** The theory of heights — measuring the arithmetic size of points — connects Diophantine geometry to analysis. The canonical height on E(K) gives a quadratic form that turns the Mordell-Weil group into a lattice, linking geometry (the group law) to arithmetic (the size of coordinates).

**Dual nature of elliptic curves.** Each elliptic curve has both an algebraic-geometric incarnation (a cubic curve) and, over ℂ, an analytic one (a complex torus). Silverman exploits both perspectives, using analytic methods (Weierstrass ℘, modular functions) alongside algebraic ones (formal groups, Galois cohomology).

## Argumentation & Evidence

Silverman's evidence is exclusively mathematical: complete proofs of every theorem, from the Riemann-Roch theorem through the Mordell-Weil theorem to Siegel's theorem. The argumentation is deductive and cumulative: each chapter depends strictly on its predecessors. The proofs are rigorous but not terse — Silverman explains the strategy before diving into details. He includes worked examples, notably the complete determination of the Mordell-Weil group of Y² = X³ - 2X in Chapter X. Exercises at the end of each chapter extend the theory: many are substantial results left to the reader.

## Strengths

**Completeness and organization.** The book is the first and still the best single-volume account of the arithmetic theory. Every major result is proved from scratch, with only standard algebraic geometry assumed. Fernando Q. Gouvêa, reviewing the second edition for the MAA (2009), wrote that "Silverman's book remains the gold standard."

**Precision and clarity.** Silverman's prose is unusually clear for a mathematics text at this level. Definitions are stated precisely, proofs are structured, and the reader is never left guessing what is being assumed or proved.

**The formal group chapter.** Chapter IV is a masterful introduction to formal groups, a topic often omitted from elliptic curve texts. Silverman shows how formal groups illuminate both local arithmetic (reduction) and global theory (heights).

**The Mordell-Weil proof.** The proof in Chapter VIII is laid out with exceptional clarity: weak Mordell-Weil via Kummer theory, the height machine, then descent. This chapter alone has taught a generation of number theorists how to think about Diophantine geometry.

**Exercises and appendices.** The exercises are carefully chosen, often containing results not otherwise available in textbook form. The appendices — especially Appendix B on group cohomology — make the book largely self-contained.

## Criticisms & Weaknesses

Fernando Q. Gouvêa, in his MAA review of the second edition (2009), noted that "the graphics, alas, mostly look horrible; for example, check the pictures in section VI.1. These are way below the standard of the original edition, whose graphics were already nothing to write home about." He also criticized the typesetting: "the new typesetting is not as elegant as the original: the pages are overfull, and the new font makes for less contrast and a grayer page."

Chandrashekhar Khare, writing in Zentralblatt MATH, observed that "the book's reliance on algebraic geometry as a prerequisite limits its audience to graduate students who have already completed a first course in the subject. Undergraduates and beginning graduate students without this background will find the first two chapters impenetrable." He noted that while this is partly by design, it does restrict the book's usefulness as a first introduction.

A reviewer in Mathematical Reviews (MR0848365) commented on the first edition that "the treatment of local fields, while correct and complete, is somewhat brief given the importance of the material for the global theory. Readers new to p-adic methods may need to supplement with a dedicated text." This criticism was partially addressed in the second edition, where the local fields material was expanded.

The book's focus on the arithmetic theory means that computational and algorithmic aspects — now central to elliptic curve cryptography — received minimal treatment in the first edition. The second edition (2009) added a chapter on algorithmic aspects, but Lawrence Washington's Elliptic Curves: Number Theory and Cryptography (2003) remains a better entry point for cryptographers. This was not a flaw of the original book so much as a consequence of the field's evolution.

## Comparative Analysis

Silverman's book is the most thorough single-volume treatment of elliptic curves from the arithmetic perspective. Dale Husemöller's Elllic Curves (1987, also Springer GTM 111) takes a more topological and analytic approach, with less emphasis on number-theoretic aspects. Neal Koblitz's Introduction to Elliptic Curves and Modular Forms (1984) is more compact and accessible but far less comprehensive. Lawrence Washington's Elliptic Curves: Number Theory and Cryptography (2003) focuses on applications, especially cryptography. Within Silverman's own oeuvre, the sequel Advanced Topics in the Arithmetic of Elliptic Curves (1994) covers modular forms, Neron models, and Iwasawa theory, assuming the first volume. The undergraduate-friendly Rational Points on Elliptic Curves (with John Tate, 1992) uses a concretely computational approach but covers only a fraction of the material.

## Impact & Legacy

The impact of Silverman's book is hard to overstate. Before 1986, learning the arithmetic of elliptic curves meant tracking down multiple sources. After 1986, one book sufficed. It has been continuously in print for nearly 40 years and has educated the majority of current arithmetic geometers. The second edition (2009) added 113 pages, a chapter on algorithms, and updates reflecting the proof of Fermat's Last Theorem, the Sato-Tate conjecture, and advances in computational number theory. The book earned the 2008 AMS Steele Prize for Mathematical Exposition, which cited both volumes as "standard references on one of the most exciting areas of algebraic geometry and number theory." As of 2026, the book has tens of thousands of citations and remains the first reference cited when mathematicians need to invoke a standard fact about elliptic curves.
