## Historical Introduction

Stewart opens with a vivid account of Évariste Galois's short, tumultuous life (1811-1832). Born in Bourg-la-Reine, Galois showed early mathematical brilliance but faced repeated rejection from the École Polytechnique. His father committed suicide after a political controversy, and Galois himself became politically active as a republican during the July Monarchy. The night before his fatal duel at age 20, Galois frantically wrote down his mathematical discoveries, scribbling "I have no time" in the margins. He was shot in the abdomen the next morning, died of peritonitis, and was buried in a common grave. His mathematical papers, originally dismissed by Cauchy and Fourier, would eventually be recognized as founding one of the most profound theories in mathematics. Stewart sets the stage by noting that Galois was solving a problem that had baffled mathematicians for centuries: when can a polynomial equation be solved by radicals?

## Chapter 1: Classical Algebra

Stewart reviews the classical solutions of cubic and quartic equations. He presents the quadratic formula as the starting point, then develops Cardano's method for the cubic (complete with the casus irreducibilis where real roots emerge from complex intermediate expressions) and Ferrari's solution of the quartic. The key observation is that these solutions all proceed by reducing the degree: solving a cubic requires solving a quadratic (the resolvent), and solving a quartic requires solving a cubic. This pattern of degree reduction through auxiliary equations motivates the question: can the same be done for quintics? Stewart introduces symmetric functions of the roots, noting that the coefficients of a polynomial are (up to sign) the elementary symmetric functions of its roots. This connection between coefficients and roots is the thread that will lead to Galois theory.

## Chapter 2: The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra

Stewart provides a proof that every non-constant polynomial with complex coefficients has at least one complex root. The proof uses the fact that a continuous function on a closed disk attains a minimum, combined with the observation that if a polynomial p(z) never vanishes, then 1/p(z) would be bounded and analytic, contradicting Liouville's theorem. This chapter establishes that the complex numbers are algebraically closed, so every polynomial of degree n has exactly n roots (counting multiplicities) in C. The FTA guarantees that when working over C, we never need to leave it to find roots — a fact that simplifies much of the early development. Stewart also uses the FTA to show that irreducible polynomials over R are either linear or quadratic, which connects to the later classification of finite field extensions.

## Chapter 3: Factorization of Polynomials

This chapter develops the theory of polynomial rings and factorization. Stewart defines irreducible polynomials and proves unique factorization in K[x] for any field K. He introduces the concept of the greatest common divisor of two polynomials, the Euclidean algorithm for polynomials, and properties of irreducible factors. The chapter covers the relationship between roots and linear factors: if alpha is a root of f(x) in some extension field, then (x - alpha) divides f(x). Stewart proves that a polynomial of degree n has at most n roots (in any field), and that over an algebraically closed field it has exactly n roots. The chapter closes with a discussion of multiple roots and their characterization via the formal derivative: a polynomial has repeated roots iff it shares a common factor with its derivative.

## Chapter 4: Field Extensions

Stewart introduces the concept of extending a field by adjoining elements. If K is a field and alpha is algebraic over K (satisfies some polynomial equation with coefficients in K), then K(alpha) denotes the smallest field containing both K and alpha. Stewart proves that K(alpha) is isomorphic to K[x]/(f(x)) where f is the minimal polynomial of alpha over K — an isomorphism that bridges between concrete computation and abstract algebra. The chapter develops the Tower Law: if L/K and M/L are finite extensions, then [M:K] = [M:L][L:K]. Examples include adjoining sqrt(2) to Q to obtain Q(sqrt(2)), which has degree 2, and adjoining a primitive cube root of unity to obtain Q(omega) of degree 2 over Q.

## Chapter 5: Simple Extensions

Simple extensions are those generated by a single element. Stewart proves the primitive element theorem: any finite separable extension can be generated by a single element. This result is crucial for Galois theory because it guarantees that the field generated by all roots of a polynomial can be written as K(alpha) for some alpha. The chapter distinguishes between algebraic and transcendental extensions and between simple and finitely generated extensions. Stewart shows that every finite extension is algebraic, but the converse is false — Q(pi) is algebraic? No, Q(pi) is transcendental — the algebraic closure of Q is an infinite algebraic extension. The chapter also discusses the degree of a simple extension in terms of the degree of the minimal polynomial.

## Chapter 6: The Degree of an Extension

Stewart develops methods for computing degrees of field extensions, particularly for composite fields and iterated extensions. If L/K and M/K are subfields of some larger field, their compositum LM is the smallest field containing both. The degree [LM:K] satisfies [LM:K] <= [L:K][M:K], with equality when the extensions are linearly disjoint. Stewart proves that if L = K(alpha1, ..., alphan) where each alphai has degree di over K, then [L:K] <= d1 ... dn, with equality if successive minimal polynomials remain irreducible. Examples include constructing the cyclotomic field Q(zeta_n) and computing its degree phi(n) via Euler's totient function.

## Chapter 7: Ruler-and-Compass Constructions

Stewart applies the theory of field extensions to one of the classic problems of Greek geometry: which geometric constructions are possible using only an unmarked straightedge and compass? He proves that a length is constructible iff it belongs to a field obtained from Q by a finite sequence of quadratic extensions. This impossibility theorem yields elegant proofs that the three classical problems — doubling the cube, trisecting an arbitrary angle, and squaring the circle — are unsolvable. Doubling the cube requires constructing the cube root of 2, which generates an extension of degree 3, not a power of 2. Trisecting a 60-degree angle requires solving a cubic with no rational root. Squaring the circle requires constructing pi, which is transcendental. The chapter is a beautiful demonstration of how abstract algebra settles ancient questions.

## Chapter 8: The Idea Behind Galois Theory

This pivotal chapter introduces the central concept: the Galois group. Stewart defines the Galois group of a polynomial f over a field K as the group of all field automorphisms of the splitting field of f that fix K elementwise. Each such automorphism permutes the roots of f, and different automorphisms give different permutations. Thus the Galois group is isomorphic to a subgroup of the symmetric group S_n, where n is the degree of f. Stewart illustrates with examples: the quadratic x^2 - 2 has Galois group of order 2 (complex conjugation), while x^2 + 1 over Q has Galois group of order 2 (sending i to -i). The chapter also introduces the concept of the fixed field of a subgroup and previews the Fundamental Theorem: there is a bijection between subgroups of the Galois group and intermediate fields of the extension.

## Chapter 9: Normality and Separability

Two technical conditions are needed for the Galois correspondence to work perfectly. A finite extension L/K is normal if every irreducible polynomial over K that has one root in L splits completely in L. It is separable if every element's minimal polynomial has distinct roots. Stewart proves that every extension of a field of characteristic 0 is separable, so separability is mainly an issue in positive characteristic. Normal extensions, however, are not automatic: Q(cuberoot(2)) is not normal over Q because the minimal polynomial x^3 - 2 has only one root in this field. Stewart proves that the splitting field of any polynomial is both normal and separable (over Q), so the Galois correspondence applies to splitting fields. A finite extension that is both normal and separable is called a Galois extension.

## Chapter 10: Counting Principles

Stewart develops the counting arguments that underpin the Galois correspondence. For a finite extension L/K, the size of the automorphism group Aut(L/K) is bounded above by [L:K], with equality iff L/K is a Galois extension. The Artin Lemma states that if G is a finite group of automorphisms of a field L, then [L:L^G] = |G|, where L^G is the fixed field. This lemma, due to Emil Artin, provides the critical bridge between group theory and field theory: given a finite group of automorphisms, you can recover the fixed field, and its degree over the base equals the group order. Stewart uses this to show that for a Galois extension, the Galois group has order exactly equal to the degree of the extension.

## Chapter 11: Field Automorphisms

Stewart deepens the study of automorphisms of field extensions. He proves that any K-automorphism of a field extension L/K sends each element to a conjugate (another root of the same minimal polynomial). The set of all K-automorphisms forms a group, and its structure reflects the symmetry of the roots. For a finite extension, the group of automorphisms is finite. Stewart proves the important result that if L is the splitting field of a separable polynomial f over K, then |Aut(L/K)| = [L:K]. The chapter also discusses embeddings of one field into another and the concept of the Galois group as the group of permutations of the roots that preserve all algebraic relations among them.

## Chapter 12: The Galois Correspondence

The heart of the book: the Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory. Stewart proves that for a finite Galois extension L/K, there is a bijection between the set of intermediate fields M (with K ⊆ M ⊆ L) and the set of subgroups H of the Galois group G = Gal(L/K). The correspondence sends an intermediate field M to the subgroup Gal(L/M) (automorphisms fixing M), and a subgroup H to its fixed field L^H. The correspondence reverses inclusions: larger subgroups correspond to smaller intermediate fields, and vice versa. Moreover, the degree [L:M] equals the index [Gal(L/M):1], and [M:K] equals [G:Gal(L/M)]. The intermediate field M is Galois over K iff Gal(L/M) is a normal subgroup of G, in which case Gal(M/K) is isomorphic to G/Gal(L/M).

## Chapter 13: A Worked Example

Stewart works through the computation of a Galois group in complete detail. He considers the polynomial f(x) = x^4 - 2 over Q. Its splitting field is Q(cuberoot(2), i) — actually Q(fourthroot(2), i). Wait, for x^4 - 2 the splitting field is Q(2^(1/4), i). The Galois group is the dihedral group D_4 of order 8. Stewart explicitly computes the action of each automorphism on the generators, lists all subgroups and their corresponding fixed fields, and verifies the Galois correspondence in this concrete case. This example illustrates all the components of the theory: finding the splitting field, computing its degree, identifying the Galois group as a subgroup of S_4, listing subgroups, and finding their fixed fields. The chapter also includes a discussion of the quartic's resolvent cubic and how the Galois group determines solvability.

## Chapter 14: Solubility and Simplicity

Stewart connects Galois groups to the classical problem of solvability by radicals. He defines a solvable group: a finite group G is solvable if it has a chain of subgroups {1} = H_0 ⊴ H_1 ⊴ ... ⊴ H_k = G where each quotient H_i/H_{i-1} is abelian. The key theorem: a polynomial equation is solvable by radicals iff its Galois group is solvable. Stewart proves that S_n is solvable for n <= 4 but not for n >= 5, using the fact that A_n is simple for n >= 5. The alternating group A_5 of even permutations on 5 symbols is simple (has no nontrivial normal subgroups), hence not solvable. Since there exist quintic polynomials whose Galois group is S_5 (or A_5), such polynomials cannot be solved by radicals. Stewart gives an explicit example: f(x) = x^5 - 6x + 3 has Galois group S_5.

## Chapter 15: Solution by Radicals

The final chapter of the first edition's core material formalizes the concept of solution by radicals and proves the main impossibility theorem. An extension L/K is a radical extension if it can be obtained by successively adjoining roots of elements (n-th roots). A polynomial f over K is solvable by radicals if there is a radical extension of K containing all roots of f. Stewart proves that if f is solvable by radicals, then its Galois group is solvable. The converse also holds when K contains appropriate roots of unity. The chapter gives a complete proof of the Abel-Ruffini theorem: the general polynomial of degree n >= 5 is not solvable by radicals. Stewart also discusses the specific quintic x^5 - 6x + 3, proving its Galois group is S_5 by reducing modulo primes and applying Dedekind's theorem on the factorization of polynomials mod p.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment

This summary captures the complete logical arc of Stewart's first edition: from classical algebra through the Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory to the insolubility of the quintic. It covers all 15 chapters plus the historical introduction. The summary conveys the key theorems and examples but cannot reproduce the full proofs or the many exercises that develop the reader's technique.

### Recommended Reading Path

| Reader Type | Time | What to Read |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | ~20 min | This summary |
| Motivated undergrad | ~3-5 hr | Summary + Historical Intro, Chapters 1, 4, 7, 8, 12, 14, 15 |
| Full course student | ~30-50 hr | Complete book with exercise work |

### Chapters to Read in Full

- **Historical Introduction** — The story of Galois's life is essential motivation
- **Chapter 8** — The central idea of Galois groups, the conceptual heart
- **Chapter 12** — The Fundamental Theorem, the book's main result
- **Chapter 14** — The connection to solvability and the role of A5's simplicity
- **Chapter 15** — The payoff: proof that the quintic is unsolvable

### Chapters to Skim or Skip

- **Chapter 2** — FTA can be taken as known; the proof is dense and somewhat tangential
- **Chapter 10** — Counting principles are important but the proofs are technical

### What You'll Miss by Not Reading the Full Book

The full book provides complete proofs with all details, numerous exercises that build intuition, the worked example in Chapter 13 that cements understanding of the Galois correspondence, and Stewart's engaging prose style that makes abstract ideas feel concrete. The historical introduction alone is worth the price of admission.
