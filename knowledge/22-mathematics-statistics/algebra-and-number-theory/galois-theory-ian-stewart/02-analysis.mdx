## Book Context & Background

Galois Theory appeared in 1973 as part of Chapman and Hall's Mathematics Series, at a time when the "New Math" movement was reshaping mathematics education. Abstract algebra had become a standard part of the undergraduate curriculum, following the influence of Bourbaki and the success of texts like Lang's Algebra (1965) and Herstein's Topics in Algebra (1964). However, there was no textbook that presented Galois theory at a level appropriate for advanced undergraduates — most treatments assumed graduate-level sophistication. Stewart's book filled this gap. It appeared in the same era as Artin's Galois Theory (1942, republished by Notre Dame) and was roughly contemporary with Edwards's Galois Theory (GTM 101, 1984). Stewart's approach was distinctive in combining a historical narrative with rigorous modern algebra.

## About the Author

Ian Stewart (born 1945) is Emeritus Professor of Mathematics at the University of Warwick and a Fellow of the Royal Society (elected 2001). He has written over 200 research papers on pattern formation, chaos theory, network dynamics, and biomathematics. Stewart is also one of the most successful popularizers of mathematics, authoring more than 120 books including Does God Play Dice?, Nature's Numbers, and the Science of Discworld series with Terry Pratchett. He wrote the Mathematical Recreations column for Scientific American from 1990 to 2001. His awards include the Royal Society's Faraday Medal, the IMA Gold Medal, the Zeeman Medal, the Lewis Thomas Prize, and the Euler Book Prize from the MAA. His undergraduate textbook on Galois theory, first published when he was a young lecturer at the University of Warwick, established him as a mathematical expositor of the first rank.

## Core Thesis & Argument

The book's central thesis is that Galois theory constitutes a unification of two major branches of mathematics — group theory and field theory — producing a machine of extraordinary power for solving problems about polynomial equations. The argument proceeds in three stages: first, the algebraic prerequisites are developed (field extensions, polynomial factorization, automorphisms); second, the Galois correspondence is established, showing that the structure of intermediate fields mirrors the structure of subgroups of the Galois group; third, this correspondence is applied to prove that the general quintic equation is unsolvable by radicals because the alternating group A5 is simple and therefore not solvable. Stewart's particular contribution is to include a direct proof that a specific quintic polynomial with integer coefficients (x^5 - 6x + 3) is not solvable by radicals, not merely the general polynomial.

## Thematic Analysis

**Unification.** The dominant theme is mathematical unification: Galois theory shows that group theory (the study of symmetry) and field theory (the study of algebraic structures) are fundamentally connected. This theme of unification recurs throughout Stewart's career as a writer.

**History and mathematics.** Stewart intertwines the historical story of polynomial solving from the Babylonians through Galois with the mathematical development. The historical introduction is not decorative but functional: it explains why anyone would care about solving equations by radicals.

**Impossibility as insight.** The book centers on a negative result — that quintics cannot be solved by radicals — but presents this impossibility as a positive achievement. The insight is that the structure of the Galois group determines solvability, and simplicity of A5 is a deep fact about symmetry.

**Concrete computation meets abstract theory.** Stewart balances abstract theorems with worked examples, particularly the computation of Galois groups for explicit polynomials. The general theory is always grounded in specific calculations.

## Argumentation & Evidence

The book's evidence is entirely mathematical: each theorem receives a complete proof. Stewart's argumentation is deductive, building from definitions through lemmas to major theorems. The reasoning is rigorous but patient: Stewart assumes only linear algebra and basic group theory, developing everything else. The key arguments include: the Tower Law for field extensions, the Artin Lemma relating automorphisms to fixed fields, the proof of the Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory, and the proof that A5 is simple (and therefore the quintic is unsolvable). Stewart's treatment of the specific quintic uses Dedekind's theorem on reduction mod p, connecting algebraic number theory to Galois theory.

## Strengths

**Accessibility.** The book was groundbreaking for making Galois theory accessible to undergraduates. Stewart assumes minimal prerequisites and develops all necessary algebra.

**Historical motivation.** The historical introduction is engaging and provides context that purely mathematical treatments lack. The story of Galois's life and death gives emotional weight to the technical development.

**Clear exposition.** Stewart's writing is lucid, with well-chosen examples and exercises that build understanding. Difficult concepts are introduced gradually and motivated carefully.

**The specific quintic.** Including an explicit polynomial whose unsolvability can be proved is a significant pedagogical advantage over treatments that only address the general polynomial.

**Breadth of applications.** Beyond the quintic, the book covers ruler-and-compass constructions, finite fields, cyclotomic theory, and transcendental numbers, showing the power of Galois-style reasoning.

## Criticisms & Weaknesses

James Wiegold, reviewing the first edition in The Mathematical Gazette (1974), noted that "the compression, while masterly, sometimes leaves the reader wishing for more explanation." Wiegold praised the book's content but suggested that some proofs moved too quickly for the intended audience.

Fernando Q. Gouvêa, reviewing the fifth edition for MAA Reviews (2023), offered a retrospective critique that applies in spirit to the first edition as well. He noted that "the writing is sometimes quite dense with proofs given without a lot of hand-holding." Gouvêa also pointed out that the proof of the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra in Chapter 2 "is one of the hardest in the book" requiring topological ideas that many undergraduates would not have mastered.

The first edition has also been criticized for its assumption that students could handle abstract field extensions from the start. The third edition was restructured specifically to address this criticism, moving to a more concrete approach working inside C for the first 15 chapters. This restructuring itself generated criticism: a MathOverflow discussion (2010) described the third edition as "sometimes harshly criticized for ruining a great book, by doing everything over the complex numbers first (leading to some long-winded proofs), and being full of typos."

Gouvêa's 2023 review identified several specific errors in the fifth edition that had persisted through multiple revisions, including a mischaracterization of Omar Khayyam's Rubaiyat as a "long poem," a non-functional cross-reference in Theorem 2.5, and confusion about the conventional definition of group extensions.

Some critics have noted that the historical introduction, while engaging, does not reflect modern historical scholarship. Gouvêa observed that it "displays no awareness of the work of more recent historians" and does not reference Jens Høyrup on Babylonian mathematics, Wilbur Knorr on Greek geometry, or Caroline Ehrhardt on the Galois myth.

## Comparative Analysis

Stewart's book competes with several other undergraduate texts on Galois theory. Artin's Galois Theory (1942) is shorter, more abstract, and assumes greater mathematical maturity; it is better suited for graduate students. Rotman's Galois Theory (1990) is similar in level but more concise and group-theoretically focused. Cox's Galois Theory (2004) is more comprehensive, covering modern developments, but is longer and more expensive. Bewersdorff's Galois Theory for Beginners (2004) takes a historical approach but is less rigorous. Edwards's Galois Theory (GTM 101, 1984) is notable for developing the theory without assuming abstract algebra, using Lagrange's original approach. Among these, Stewart's book stands out for its balance of historical narrative and modern rigor, its patient development of prerequisites, and its inclusion of the specific quintic example.

## Impact & Legacy

Galois Theory has been continuously in print since 1973 and has gone through five editions (1973, 1989, 2002, 2015, 2022). It has educated generations of undergraduate mathematicians and is widely adopted for advanced undergraduate courses. The book's influence extends beyond its direct readership: Stewart's approach of combining historical narrative with rigorous mathematics in a textbook setting was influential, predating similar approaches in other subjects. The book established Stewart's reputation as a mathematical expositor, leading to his prolific career as a popular science writer. A Zentralblatt MATH review (2004) called it "a valuable textbook for algebra undergraduate students," noting that "after more than 30 years, Ian Stewart's Galois Theory remains" relevant.
