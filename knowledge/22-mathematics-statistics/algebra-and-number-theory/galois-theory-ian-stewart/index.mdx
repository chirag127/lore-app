Galois Theory by Ian Stewart, first published in 1973 by Chapman and Hall, was one of the first textbooks on the subject written specifically for undergraduates. Stewart presents Galois theory as "a showpiece of mathematical unification," bringing together group theory, field theory, and the classical theory of equations to create a powerful machine for studying polynomial solvability. The central arc of the book moves from the historical problem of solving polynomial equations through the construction of the Galois correspondence to the proof that the general quintic is insoluble by radicals.

The book is distinguished by its extensive historical introduction recounting the life and tragic death of Évariste Galois, its patient development of prerequisite algebra, and its direct approach to proving the insolubility of a specific quintic polynomial with integer coefficients. Stewart's writing is clear and engaging, making sophisticated mathematics accessible without sacrificing rigor. The first edition established a blueprint that would sustain the book through five editions over five decades.
