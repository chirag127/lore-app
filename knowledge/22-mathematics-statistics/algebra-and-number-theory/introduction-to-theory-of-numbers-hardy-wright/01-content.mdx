## Chapter I: The Series of Primes (1)

Hardy and Wright begin at the beginning: the integers, divisibility, and prime numbers. They state the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic (unique prime factorization) and prove it in two ways. They introduce the sequence of primes, discuss the sieve of Eratosthenes, and state the Prime Number Theorem: the number of primes not exceeding x is asymptotically x / log x. The logarithmic function is defined and its basic properties established. The chapter closes with a table of primes and a discussion of unresolved questions — twin primes, Goldbach's conjecture — that remain unsettled to this day.

## Chapter II: The Series of Primes (2)

This chapter gives three distinct proofs of Euclid's theorem that there are infinitely many primes: (1) the classical Euclid argument that any finite list can be extended, (2) a proof using Fermat numbers showing that Fermat numbers are pairwise coprime, and (3) a proof using the divergence of the sum of reciprocals of primes (an argument that foreshadows analytic methods). The chapter also discusses primes in arithmetic progressions (Dirichlet's theorem is stated but not proved), Fermat and Mersenne numbers, and the challenge of finding formulae for primes.

## Chapter III: Farey Series and a Theorem of Minkowski

Farey series are sequences of reduced fractions between 0 and 1 with denominators bounded by a given integer. Hardy and Wright prove the two fundamental properties of Farey sequences: that consecutive terms satisfy the mediant property, and that they are generated by the Stern-Brocot tree. They give three proofs of the Farey theorems, each illuminating a different aspect of the structure. The chapter then introduces Minkowski's fundamental theorem in the geometry of numbers: a symmetric convex set of area greater than 4 must contain a non-zero integer lattice point. This theorem, proved using elementary geometric arguments about the fundamental lattice, connects number theory to geometry and foreshadows the deeper material in Chapter XXIV.

## Chapter IV: Irrational Numbers

The authors discuss numbers known to be irrational: sqrt(2) (the classical Pythagorean proof), sqrt(3), sqrt(5), and more generally any irrational square root. They give a general theorem: if a rational number in lowest terms satisfies a monic polynomial with integer coefficients, it must be an integer. This implies that any non-integer root of a monic polynomial is irrational. They discuss the irrationality of e, prove it using the series expansion, and discuss the historical reception of irrational numbers from the Pythagoreans through Dedekind.

## Chapter V: Congruences and Residues

This chapter develops the elementary theory of congruences. The Euclidean algorithm is presented for computing highest common divisors. Congruences and residue classes are defined, and the basic algebraic properties are established — addition, multiplication, and exponentiation modulo m. Linear congruences are solved. Euler's function phi(m) is introduced, counting the numbers less than m that are coprime to m. An elegant application of phi(m) to trigonometric series is given. The chapter closes with Gauss's construction of the regular 17-gon using roots of unity, showing how number theory connects to geometry.

## Chapter VI: Fermat's Theorem and Its Consequences

Fermat's Little Theorem is proved: if p is prime and a is not divisible by p, then a^(p-1) ≡ 1 (mod p). Two proofs are given, one using binomial coefficients and one using group theory of the multiplicative group modulo p. Wilson's theorem is derived as a corollary. The theory of quadratic residues is introduced: an integer a is a quadratic residue modulo p if the congruence x^2 ≡ a (mod p) has a solution. Gauss's lemma and the law of quadratic reciprocity are proved — one of the crowning achievements of elementary number theory. The chapter includes tests for primality and Euler's theorem on factors of Mersenne numbers.

## Chapter VII: General Properties of Congruences

This chapter treats congruences of higher degree. A general theory of polynomial congruences modulo a prime is developed: a polynomial of degree n has at most n roots modulo a prime. Lagrange's proof of Fermat's and Wilson's theorems is given as an application. Wolstenholme's theorem and von Staudt's theorem on the denominators of Bernoulli numbers are proved. The chapter demonstrates the power of working modulo a prime, where the algebraic structure is particularly clean.

## Chapter VIII: Congruences to Composite Moduli

The authors extend congruence theory to composite moduli using the Chinese Remainder Theorem: a system of congruences to pairwise coprime moduli has a unique solution modulo the product. Hensel's lemma (or rather its elementary precursor) is developed for lifting solutions from prime to prime-power moduli. Bauer's identical congruence and Leudesdorf's theorem are proved, giving deeper results about binomial coefficients modulo primes. The chapter shows how problems that are difficult modulo composite numbers can be broken into simpler problems modulo prime powers.

## Chapter IX: The Representation of Numbers by Decimals

Decimal expansions are examined from a number-theoretic perspective. Terminating and recurring decimals are characterized: a fraction a/b terminates iff b has no prime factors other than 2 and 5. The period of a recurring decimal is related to the order of 10 modulo the denominator. Bachet's problem of the weights (weighing any integer amount using a minimal set of weights) is solved using ternary expansions. The game of Nim is analyzed through binary representations. The chapter also includes a remarkable discussion of normal numbers — numbers whose decimal expansions contain every finite sequence of digits with the expected frequency — and proves that almost all numbers are normal.

## Chapter X: Continued Fractions

A masterful exposition of continued fractions, one of the highlights of the book. Finite continued fractions are introduced, their convergents derived, and the relationship with the Euclidean algorithm established. The fundamental inequality governing the accuracy of approximation by convergents is proved. Infinite continued fractions are then developed for irrational numbers. Periodic continued fractions are shown to correspond exactly to quadratic irrationals. The Fibonacci and Lucas sequences appear naturally. The approximation properties of convergents are explored in depth.

## Chapter XI: Approximation of Irrationals by Rationals

Dirichlet's approximation theorem states that for any irrational α and any positive integer N, there exists a rational p/q with q ≤ N such that |α - p/q| < 1/(qN). Liouville's theorem on Diophantine approximation gives a criterion for transcendence: if α is algebraic of degree n, then |α - p/q| > c/q^n for all but finitely many rationals. The existence of transcendental numbers (Liouville numbers) is proved. The chapter culminates in Hermite's proof of the transcendence of e and Lindemann's proof of the transcendence of π, which finally settled the ancient problem of squaring the circle.

## Chapter XII: The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic in k(1), k(i), and k(ρ)

The authors extend the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic to algebraic number fields. The Gaussian integers Z[i] are introduced, and Euclid's algorithm is shown to hold in this ring, establishing unique factorization. The primes of Z[i] are classified: a rational prime p either remains prime (p ≡ 3 mod 4), splits (p ≡ 1 mod 4), or ramifies (p = 2). The ring of Eisenstein integers Z[ρ] (where ρ is a primitive cube root of unity) is similarly treated. This chapter is the book's first foray into algebraic number theory.

## Chapter XIII: Some Diophantine Equations

Fermat's Last Theorem is discussed as the motivating problem. The Pythagorean equation x^2 + y^2 = z^2 is solved completely using the parameterization via Gaussian integers. The equation x^4 + y^4 = z^4 is proved to have no non-trivial solutions using infinite descent — Fermat's own method. The equation x^3 + y^3 = z^3 is treated similarly using the Eisenstein integers. The chapter shows how algebraic number fields provide natural settings for solving Diophantine equations.

## Chapter XIV: Quadratic Fields (1)

A systematic treatment of quadratic fields Q(√m). Algebraic integers in quadratic fields are characterized: they take the form a + b√m with certain parity conditions. The chapter discusses unities (units), primes, and the problem of unique factorization. The real quadratic fields Q(√2) and Q(√5) are treated in detail, along with the complex Euclidean fields Q(√-1), Q(√-2), Q(√-3), Q(√-7), Q(√-11). Examples where unique factorization fails (such as Q(√-5)) are given, motivating the development of ideal theory.

## Chapter XV: Quadratic Fields (2)

The primes of the Gaussian integers Z[i] are classified in detail: 2 ramifies, primes p ≡ 1 mod 4 split as products of conjugate Gaussian primes, and primes p ≡ 3 mod 4 remain inert. Fermat's theorem in Z[i] is proved, giving an arithmetic characterization of Gaussian primes. Similar analyses are given for the Eisenstein integers and the fields Q(√2) and Q(√5). Lucas's test for the primality of Mersenne numbers M_(4n+3) is proved using the arithmetic of quadratic fields. The chapter concludes with an introduction to ideals in quadratic fields and a discussion of other algebraic fields.

## Chapter XVI: The Arithmetical Functions φ(n), μ(n), d(n), σ(n), r(n)

This chapter is a systematic study of the standard arithmetical functions. Euler's totient φ(n) counts numbers coprime to n. The Möbius function μ(n) is defined and the Möbius inversion formula is proved, giving a powerful technique for inverting sums over divisors. Ramanujan's sum is evaluated. The divisor function d(n) and the sum-of-divisors function σ(n) are studied. Perfect numbers — numbers equal to the sum of their proper divisors — are discussed, and the Euclid-Euler theorem characterizing even perfect numbers is proved. The function r(n), counting representations of n as a sum of two squares, is expressed in terms of divisor functions.

## Chapter XVII: Generating Functions of Arithmetical Functions

Dirichlet series are introduced as generating functions for arithmetical functions. The Riemann zeta function ζ(s) is defined and its basic properties established, including the existence of a simple pole at s = 1 with residue 1. The Euler product for ζ(s) is proved, connecting the zeta function to primes. Multiplication of Dirichlet series corresponds to Dirichlet convolution of arithmetical functions. Generating functions for φ(n), μ(n), d(n), σ(n), and r(n) are derived. The chapter also treats ordinary power series generating functions, foreshadowing the theory of partitions.

## Chapter XVIII: The Order of Magnitude of Arithmetical Functions

The average, normal, and maximal orders of key arithmetical functions are determined. The divisor function d(n) has normal order (log n)^(log 2) and average order log n. The sum-of-divisors function σ(n) has average order π^2 n / 6. Euler's totient φ(n) has average order 6n/π^2. The number of squarefree numbers up to x is asymptotically 6x/π^2. The function r(n), counting representations as sums of two squares, has average order π. These results use a combination of elementary estimates and the properties of the zeta function.

## Chapter XIX: Partitions

The theory of partitions — the number p(n) of ways to write n as a sum of positive integers — is developed in depth. Euler's generating function for p(n) is derived: Σ p(n)x^n = Π (1 - x^k)^(-1). Euler's pentagonal number theorem gives a recurrence for p(n). Jacobi's triple product identity is proved and used to derive further identities. The Rogers-Ramanujan identities — among the most beautiful formulas in mathematics — are stated and proved. Ramanujan's congruence properties of p(n) modulo powers of 5, 7, and 11 are discussed. The chapter shows Hardy and Wright at their best: deep combinatorial theorems proved with verve.

## Chapter XX: The Representation of a Number by Two or Four Squares

Waring's problem is introduced: for each integer k, what is the smallest number g(k) such that every positive integer can be expressed as a sum of g(k) k-th powers? The chapter focuses on the cases k = 2. A famous theorem of Fermat states that a prime p ≡ 1 mod 4 can be expressed as a sum of two squares; several proofs are given. Lagrange's four-square theorem — every positive integer is a sum of four integer squares — is proved using quaternions and again using geometry of numbers. The number of representations is studied in both cases.

## Chapter XXI: Representation by Cubes and Higher Powers

Waring's problem for higher powers is treated. The existence of g(3) (cubes) is proved, and bounds for g(3) are established. For general k, a lower bound for g(k) is derived: g(k) ≥ 2^k + floor((3/2)^k) - 2. Upper bounds are discussed (using the Hardy-Littlewood circle method, which Hardy himself developed). The number v(k), representing sums of signed powers, is introduced. The Prouhet-Tarry-Escott problem is discussed.

## Chapter XXII: The Series of Primes (3)

This is the analytic heart of the book. The functions θ(x) = Σ_(p≤x) log p and ψ(x) are introduced. Chebyshev's estimates are proved: there exist positive constants c1, c2 such that c1 x/log x < π(x) < c2 x/log x. Bertrand's postulate (there is always a prime between n and 2n) is proved. Mertens' theorem relating sums over primes to log log x is established. The normal order of the number of prime factors ω(n) and Ω(n) is log log n. Selberg's elementary proof of the Prime Number Theorem is presented — this was a sensational development when added to the third edition (1954), as it eliminated the need for complex analysis. The chapter closes with a discussion of prime pairs.

## Chapter XXIII: Kronecker's Theorem

Kronecker's theorem in one dimension: if α is irrational, then the sequence {nα} (fractional parts) is dense in [0,1]. Three proofs are given. The "problem of the reflected ray" — a billiard ball in a rectangular table — is solved using these ideas. The general multi-dimensional Kronecker theorem is stated and proved using Lettenmeyer's, Estermann's, and Bohr's proofs. The concept of uniform distribution is introduced, connecting to Weyl's criterion.

## Chapter XXIV: Geometry of Numbers

The fundamental theorem of Minkowski is restated and deepened. Simple applications include the existence of non-trivial integer solutions to linear inequalities. The best possible inequalities for quadratic forms are determined: for the form ξ^2 + η^2, the minimal non-zero value is at most √(4/π), and this is best possible. Non-homogeneous forms are considered. Tchebotaref's theorem is proved. A converse of Minkowski's theorem is discussed.

## Chapter XXV: Elliptic Curves (6th edition only, by Joseph H. Silverman)

This new chapter, added in the 2008 edition, introduces elliptic curves. The congruent number problem — determining which rational numbers occur as areas of rational right triangles — provides motivation. The group law on an elliptic curve is defined geometrically and algebraically. The connection to Fermat's Last Theorem is outlined: Wiles's proof relies on the modularity of elliptic curves. The Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture is stated. The chapter is a survey rather than a fully proved treatment, differing in character from the rest of the book.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment

This summary captures all 25 chapters of the 6th edition. For the first edition (1938), chapters 1-24 were present (the elliptic curves chapter was added in 2008). The level of detail here gives a comprehensive overview of the book's scope and structure, but cannot reproduce the elegance of Hardy's proofs or the charm of the authors' commentary in the end-of-chapter notes.

### Recommended Reading Path

| Reader Type | Est. Time | What to Read |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | ~15 min | This summary |
| Curious undergraduate | ~2-4 hr | Summary + Chapters I, II, IV, V, VI, X, XIII, XVI |
| Serious student | ~20-40 hr | All chapters, skimming Chapter XXV |
| Number theory researcher | ~60+ hr | Full book with end-of-chapter notes |

### Chapters to Read in Full

- **Chapters I-II** — The series of primes, essential reading
- **Chapter VI** — Quadratic reciprocity, the crown jewel of elementary number theory
- **Chapter X** — Continued fractions, a model of expository writing
- **Chapter XI** — Diophantine approximation and transcendence
- **Chapter XIX** — Partitions, including the Rogers-Ramanujan identities
- **Chapter XXII** — The elementary proof of the Prime Number Theorem

### Chapters to Skim or Skip

- **Chapter IX** (decimals) — Interesting but less central
- **Chapter XXIII** (Kronecker's theorem) — Specialist material
- **Chapter XXV** (elliptic curves) — Survey chapter, different in style

### What You'll Miss by Not Reading the Full Book

The book's greatest value lies not in the theorems but in the proofs themselves — Hardy's distinctive combination of economy, elegance, and occasional informality. The end-of-chapter notes are treasure troves of historical context, references, and hints of deeper results. The book's voice — urbane, witty, occasionally sharp — is irreplaceable.
