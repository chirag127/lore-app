G. H. Hardy and E. M. Wright's *An Introduction to the Theory of Numbers* is one of the most famous textbooks in all of mathematics. First published in 1938, it grew out of a series of lectures Hardy delivered across several universities over the preceding decade. The book does not pretend to be a systematic treatise — as the authors disarmingly admit in the preface, "it has no very definite plan." Instead, it offers a series of brilliant introductions to almost every side of number theory: the distribution of primes, the theory of congruences, Diophantine equations, continued fractions, partitions, the geometry of numbers, and much more.

What makes the book extraordinary is its voice. Hardy's unmistakable style — elegant, opinionated, occasionally mischievous — permeates every page. The proofs are models of concision and clarity. The book was revolutionary in 1938 for bringing analytic methods into an elementary setting, and it remains essential reading for anyone who wants to understand what number theory is about. The sixth edition (2008, revised by D. R. Heath-Brown and J. H. Silverman, with a foreword by Andrew Wiles) adds a chapter on elliptic curves and updates the end-of-chapter notes to reflect modern developments including the proof of Fermat's Last Theorem.
