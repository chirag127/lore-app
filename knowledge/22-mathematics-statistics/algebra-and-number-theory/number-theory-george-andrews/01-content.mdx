## Chapter 1: The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic

Andrews begins with the Euclidean algorithm, the ancient procedure for computing greatest common divisors. He proves that every integer greater than 1 can be factored uniquely into primes. The proof is classical and complete, establishing the foundation for everything that follows. The chapter includes a discussion of the sieve of Eratosthenes, the infinitude of primes (Euclid's proof), and the standard notation for divisibility. Andrews' approach is notable for its clarity and for the inclusion of numerous computational exercises that build intuition.

## Chapter 2: Arithmetic Functions and Mobius Inversion

This chapter introduces the standard arithmetic functions: the Euler totient function phi(n), the sum-of-divisors function sigma(n), the number-of-divisors function d(n), and the Mobius function mu(n). Andrews proves the Mobius inversion formula and uses it to derive relationships between arithmetic functions. The treatment is systematic: each function is defined, its basic properties are established, and applications are given. The chapter includes the important result that the sum of phi(d) over divisors d of n equals n itself.

## Chapter 3: Congruences

Andrews develops the theory of congruences, beginning with the basic properties of modular arithmetic. The chapter covers linear congruences, the Chinese remainder theorem, and polynomial congruences. Andrews proves Wilson's theorem and Fermat's little theorem, and discusses their applications. The treatment is problem-oriented: each new concept is immediately illustrated with examples, and the exercises are designed to develop computational fluency with modular arithmetic.

## Chapter 4: Quadratic Reciprocity

This is the crown jewel of the book. Andrews introduces the Legendre symbol and proves the law of quadratic reciprocity, the theorem Gauss called the "golden theorem." He gives a complete proof using Gauss's lemma and Eisenstein's lemma. The chapter includes applications to the determination of whether a given integer is a quadratic residue modulo a prime, and a discussion of the Jacobi symbol as a generalization. The treatment is rigorous and complete, with all steps justified.

## Chapter 5: Quadratic Forms and Representations

Andrews studies the representation of integers by quadratic forms, beginning with the sum of two squares. He proves Fermat's theorem on sums of two squares and generalizes to sums of four squares (Lagrange's theorem). The chapter includes a discussion of the theory of quadratic forms, the reduction theory of binary quadratic forms, and the class number. This material connects the elementary theory to deeper questions in algebraic number theory.

## Chapter 6: Diophantine Equations

The chapter covers the classical Diophantine equations: the Pythagorean equation, Pell's equation, and the equation of Fermat (with a discussion of Fermat's Last Theorem as a historical note). Andrews gives a complete solution of the Pythagorean equation using parameterization, and a thorough treatment of Pell's equation using continued fractions. The chapter includes a discussion of the equation x^4 + y^4 = z^4 and its elementary impossibility proof.

## Chapters 7-9: Partitions

The final three chapters are Andrews' distinctive contribution. He introduces partition theory: a partition of a positive integer n is a representation of n as a sum of positive integers, disregarding order. Andrews develops the generating function approach due to Euler, proves the pentagonal number theorem, and discusses the Rogers-Ramanujan identities. This material reflects Andrews' own research expertise and gives the book a unique character among introductory number theory texts. The treatment includes both classical results and modern developments.

This summary captures the main topics and Andrews' distinctive emphasis on partitions. It misses the many computational examples and the problem-solving approach that characterizes Andrews' exposition.

- **Chapter 4** — Quadratic reciprocity, the centerpiece of elementary number theory
- **Chapters 7-9** — Partitions, the book's unique contribution

### Chapters to Skim or Skip

- **Chapter 2** — Important but somewhat technical

Andrews' distinctive problem-solving approach, the combinatorial perspective on number theory, and the extensive exercise sets that build genuine mathematical skill.
