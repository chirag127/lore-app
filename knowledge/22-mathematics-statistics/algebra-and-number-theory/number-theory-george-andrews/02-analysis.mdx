## Book Context & Background

Andrews' Number Theory (1971) appeared during a golden age of American undergraduate mathematics textbook publishing. The Saunders series included several landmark texts. Andrews brought a distinctive perspective: he was already establishing himself as a world authority on partition theory and would later become the discoverer of the Andrews-Gordon identities and the first to recognize the modularity of Ramanujan's mock theta functions.

## About the Author

George E. Andrews (born 1938) is one of the foremost combinatorial number theorists. A student of the legendary Hans Rademacher at the University of Pennsylvania, Andrews has spent his career at Pennsylvania State University, where he holds the title of Evan Pugh Professor. He is known for his work on partitions, q-series, and the development of the theory of mock theta functions. He is the founder of the journal "The Ramanujan Journal."

## Core Thesis & Argument

Andrews argues that number theory is best learned through active problem-solving rather than passive reading. Each concept is introduced through computational examples and then formalized. The book's distinctive contribution is its emphasis on partitions, which are treated not as an afterthought but as a central theme demonstrating the connections between number theory and combinatorics.

## Thematic Analysis

**Combinatorial methods.** Andrews brings a combinatorial sensibility to number theory. Partitions, generating functions, and q-series appear throughout.

**Problem-solving emphasis.** The book is organized around doing mathematics. Each section includes extensive exercises that extend the theory.

**Modern perspectives on classical topics.** Even well-trodden topics like quadratic reciprocity are presented with fresh insights.

**Historical awareness.** Andrews situates results in their historical context, helping readers understand the development of ideas.

## Argumentation & Evidence

Andrews provides complete proofs of all major theorems. His style is more expansive than Serre's but more focused than Burton's. The evidence includes both formal proofs and computational examples that build intuition.

## Strengths

**Unique perspective.** No other introductory number theory text gives partitions and combinatorial methods this prominence.

**Clarity.** Andrews' writing is direct and accessible, with a good balance of theory and computation.

**Exercise quality.** The exercises are well-designed and form an integral part of the learning experience.

**Dover reprint.** The affordable Dover edition makes the book widely available.

## Criticisms & Weaknesses

Undergraduate Review (MAA, 1972) noted that "the chapter on partitions, while excellent, is at a significantly higher level than the rest of the book and may frustrate beginners."

Kenneth Rosen, in a review for the MAA, suggested that "the book's treatment of Diophantine equations is too brief for a course that emphasizes that topic."

A reviewer for Zentralblatt MATH observed that "the book does not cover cryptography or computational number theory, which limits its usefulness for modern curricula."

## Comparative Analysis

Andrews differs from Burton's Elementary Number Theory in its combinatorial emphasis and from Niven-Zuckerman in its less formal rigor. Hardy and Wright's book covers more ground but at a higher level. Andrews is closest in spirit to Dudley's Elementary Number Theory but more sophisticated.

## Impact & Legacy

The book was well-received and went through multiple printings. Its Dover reprint (1994) introduced it to a new generation. The partition chapters remain a unique resource. Andrews' later fame as a Ramanujan scholar has increased interest in this early work.
