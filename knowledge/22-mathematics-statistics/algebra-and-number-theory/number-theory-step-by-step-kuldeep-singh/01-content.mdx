## Chapter 1: The Integers

Singh begins with the natural numbers and the basic properties of integers. The division algorithm is introduced through worked examples, with each step explained. The Euclidean algorithm is presented as both a theoretical tool and a practical computation method. The fundamental theorem of arithmetic is proved with careful justification. The chapter includes sections on prime numbers, the sieve of Eratosthenes, and the infinitude of primes.

## Chapter 2: Congruences

Modular arithmetic is introduced through real-world examples: time calculations, calendar arithmetic, and remainder problems. The basic properties of congruences are proved and immediately applied. Singh includes sections on linear congruences, the Chinese remainder theorem (with step-by-step worked examples), and systems of congruences. The treatment is methodical: each new concept is introduced with a simple example, developed through several worked problems, and then summarized.

## Chapter 3: Fermat's and Euler's Theorems

Fermat's little theorem is discovered experimentally (compute 2^p mod p for various primes p) before being proved. Euler's theorem extends the result to composite moduli. Singh proves both theorems in full detail and provides extensive practice problems. Carmichael numbers are introduced as a cautionary tale: not every number satisfying Fermat's condition is prime.

## Chapter 4: The RSA Cryptosystem

A highlight of the book: a complete, accessible treatment of the RSA cryptosystem. Singh explains key generation, encryption, decryption, and the underlying number theory. The exposition is remarkably clear: the reader learns why RSA works, how to implement the key steps, and why the system is believed secure. The chapter includes worked examples with small primes that the reader can verify by hand.

## Chapter 5: Primitive Roots and Indices

This chapter develops the theory of primitive roots modulo primes and prime powers. Singh proves the existence theorem and shows how primitive roots are used to solve exponential congruences. The concept of discrete logarithms is introduced, and its connection to cryptography is discussed.

## Chapter 6: Quadratic Residues

The Legendre symbol is introduced, and its properties are established through worked examples. The law of quadratic reciprocity is proved using Gauss's lemma. Singh gives multiple examples of applying reciprocity to determine whether numbers are quadratic residues. The chapter includes the Jacobi symbol as a generalization.

## Chapter 7: Diophantine Equations

The final chapter covers linear Diophantine equations, Pythagorean triples (with complete parameterization), and Pell's equation. The treatment emphasizes the connection between number theory and the geometry of integer lattices. The chapter includes an introduction to the method of infinite descent.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment

This summary captures the book's structure but cannot convey the step-by-step pedagogy that is its main value.

### Recommended Reading Path

| Reader Type | Time | What to Read |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | ~15 min | This summary |
| Self-learner | ~5-10 hr | Chapters 1-4 for the core |
| Computer science student | ~8-15 hr | Full book, especially chapters 4-5 |

### Chapters to Read in Full

- **Chapter 2** — Congruences, the essential tool
- **Chapter 4** — RSA cryptography, the most compelling application

### Chapters to Skim or Skip

- **Chapter 1** — Review material for most readers

### What You'll Miss by Not Reading the Full Book

The patient, step-by-step exposition, the fully worked examples, and the carefully scaffolded exercises that make the material accessible to self-learners.
