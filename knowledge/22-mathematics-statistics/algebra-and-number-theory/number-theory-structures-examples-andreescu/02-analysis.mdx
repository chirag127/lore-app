## Book Context & Background

Number Theory: Structures, Examples, and Problems (2009) belongs to a tradition of problem-based mathematics texts originating from Eastern European mathematical culture. Titu Andreescu, as director of the American Mathematics Competitions and leader of the US IMO team, brought this tradition to an American audience. The book reflects the philosophy that deep understanding comes from solving well-chosen problems.

## About the Authors

Titu Andreescu is the most influential figure in American olympiad mathematics. He coached the US IMO team to multiple first-place finishes and has authored dozens of problem-solving texts. Dorin Andrica is a Romanian mathematician known for work in geometry and analysis, and co-author of several competition-focused mathematics books.

## Core Thesis & Argument

The book argues that number theory is best learned through structured problem-solving. By classifying problems by their structure rather than by their difficulty, the authors provide a systematic approach to building problem-solving skills. The emphasis is on recognizing patterns and applying appropriate techniques.

## Thematic Analysis

**Structural classification.** The book organizes problems not by difficulty but by the underlying mathematical structure, a pedagogical innovation.

**Systematic development.** Each chapter builds from fundamentals to advanced applications.

**Competition focus.** The book is designed for olympiad preparation, not general mathematical education.

## Argumentation & Evidence

The authors provide concise theoretical overviews followed by worked examples and problem sets. The emphasis is on efficiency: learn the theory, see it applied, practice it yourself.

## Strengths

**Comprehensive coverage.** All major competition topics are covered.

**Excellent problem selection.** The problems are well-chosen and graded.

**Clear solutions.** The solutions are detailed and instructive.

**Systematic approach.** The structural classification is genuinely useful for learners.

## Criticisms & Weaknesses

Mark Saul, reviewing for the MAA (2010), noted that "the book is more a reference for teachers than a text for students. The theoretical sections are too compressed for beginners."

A reviewer for the Mathematical Olympiad Treasures series commented that "the problem selection is excellent, but some of the solutions are too clever and would benefit from more motivational discussion."

Ravi Vakil, in a review of the problem-solving genre, observed that "books like this are invaluable for training, but they should not be mistaken for a complete education in number theory."

## Comparative Analysis

The book differs from standard textbooks in its problem-centered approach. It is similar in spirit to Andreescu's other works (104 Number Theory Problems, Problems from the Book) but with a more systematic theoretical structure. It complements Niven-Zuckerman by showing how the theory is applied in competition problems.

## Impact & Legacy

The book has become a standard resource for olympiad training. It fills the gap between elementary texts and advanced competition levels. Its systematic classification approach has influenced subsequent problem-solving texts.
