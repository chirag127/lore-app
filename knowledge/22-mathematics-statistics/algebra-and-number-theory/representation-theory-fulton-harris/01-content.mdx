## Part I: Finite Groups

### Chapter 1: Representations of Finite Groups

The book opens with the fundamental definitions. A representation of a finite group G on a finite-dimensional complex vector space V is a homomorphism ρ: G → GL(V). Equivalently, V is a G-module. The dimension of V is the degree of the representation. Fulton and Harris immediately illustrate with the trivial representation, the standard representation of the symmetric group S_n on C^n modulo the line spanned by (1,...,1), and the permutation representation of S_3.

Key foundational results appear rapidly. Schur's Lemma states that any G-linear map between irreducible representations is either zero or an isomorphism; over C, such a map is a scalar multiple of the identity. Maschke's Theorem guarantees complete reducibility: every representation of a finite group is a direct sum of irreducible subrepresentations. This is proved by averaging an arbitrary inner product over the group. The regular representation C[G] — the group algebra acting on itself by left multiplication — is introduced; its decomposition contains every irreducible representation with multiplicity equal to its dimension.

### Chapter 2: Characters

Characters are the single most powerful tool in the representation theory of finite groups. The character χ_V of a representation V is the function χ_V(g) = Tr(ρ(g)). Characters are class functions (constant on conjugacy classes) and completely determine the representation up to isomorphism. The character of a direct sum is the sum of characters; the character of a tensor product is the product.

The orthogonality relations are the central theorem: the irreducible characters form an orthonormal basis for the space of class functions under the inner product ⟨χ, ψ⟩ = (1/|G|) Σ_{g∈G} χ(g) ψ(g). This yields the decomposition formula: the multiplicity of an irreducible representation W in V equals ⟨χ_V, χ_W⟩. The character table of a group summarizes the values of all irreducible characters on all conjugacy classes.

Fulton and Harris compute the character tables of S_3, S_4, the alternating group A_4, and the dihedral group D_10 in full detail, showing how character theory determines the decomposition of representations into irreducibles without constructing them explicitly.

### Chapter 3: Examples; Induced Representations; Group Algebras; Real Representations

This chapter widens the toolkit. Induced representations are defined: given a subgroup H ≤ G and a representation W of H, the induced representation Ind_H^G(W) is the representation of G on the space of functions f: G → W satisfying f(hg) = h·f(g). Frobenius reciprocity gives the adjunction: Hom_G(V, Ind_H^G(W)) ≅ Hom_H(Res_H^G(V), W). This is one of the most used computational devices in representation theory.

The group algebra C[G] is examined in more depth. Its center consists of class functions; the central idempotents correspond to irreducible representations. Real representations are discussed: a representation can be of real type (quaternionic type, or complex type) depending on whether it admits a G-invariant real structure. The Frobenius-Schur indicator distinguishes these cases.

Concrete examples include the complete determination of all irreducible representations of the dihedral groups D_2n and the quaternion group Q_8.

### Chapter 4: Representations of S_d: Young Diagrams and Frobenius's Character Formula

This chapter treats the representation theory of the symmetric group — a subject of extraordinary combinatorial depth and importance for the rest of the book. The irreducible representations of S_d are classified by partitions λ of d, represented as Young diagrams. The Specht module V_λ is constructed explicitly as a subspace of the group algebra.

Frobenius's character formula gives the character of V_λ evaluated on a permutation of cycle type μ as a determinant involving power sums. The hook-length formula gives the dimension of V_λ as d! divided by the product of hook lengths in the diagram. The Murnaghan-Nakayama rule provides a combinatorial algorithm for computing characters.

These results are essential because the representation theory of GL_n C is intimately connected to that of S_d through Schur-Weyl duality: the d-th tensor power of the standard representation of GL_n C decomposes under the commuting actions of GL_n C and S_d, and the irreducible GL_n C-modules correspond to partitions.

### Chapter 5: Representations of A_d and GL_2(F_q)

The alternating group A_d is handled via its relation to S_d: some irreducible representations of S_d remain irreducible when restricted to A_d, while others split into a pair of conjugate representations. The example of A_5 (isomorphic to the icosahedral group) is worked out completely.

The final sections introduce representations of finite groups of Lie type by studying GL_2(F_q). The conjugacy classes are classified, and the character table is constructed using induced representations from the Borel subgroup. This prefigures the much deeper theory of representations of reductive groups over finite fields (Deligne-Lusztig theory), which the book touches only lightly.

### Chapter 6: Weyl's Construction

Weyl's construction provides an explicit way to build all irreducible polynomial representations of GL_n C using Young diagrams and Schur functors. Starting from the d-th tensor power of the standard representation V = C^n, the symmetric group S_d acts by permuting tensor factors. The image of the Young symmetrizer c_λ (an element of C[S_d]) acting on V^{⊗d} is the Schur module S_λ V, which is either zero or irreducible.

This construction is the key link between the combinatorics of Young diagrams and the representation theory of the general linear group. Fulton and Harris develop it in detail, showing how to compute dimensions using the hook-length formula and how restriction rules correspond to the branching of Young diagrams.

## Part II: Lie Groups and Lie Algebras

### Chapter 7: Lie Groups

The book shifts to the continuous realm. A Lie group is a group that is also a smooth manifold, with the group operations being smooth maps. The classical Lie groups are introduced: GL_n R, GL_n C, SL_n R, SL_n C, O_n, SO_n, U_n, SU_n, and Sp_2n. The exponential map exp: T_e G → G is defined, relating the Lie algebra (the tangent space at the identity) to the group.

Closed subgroups of GL_n are matrix Lie groups; the book works almost exclusively with these. The key relationship is that representations of a Lie group give rise to representations of its Lie algebra by differentiation, and for connected groups the correspondence is bijective.

### Chapter 8: Lie Algebras and Lie Groups

A Lie algebra is a vector space g equipped with a bilinear bracket [·,·] that is antisymmetric and satisfies the Jacobi identity. For a matrix Lie group, the Lie bracket is the commutator of matrices. The adjoint representation ad: g → End(g) is defined by ad(X)(Y) = [X,Y]. The Killing form B(X,Y) = Tr(ad(X) ad(Y)) is introduced as a crucial invariant.

The relationship between Lie groups and Lie algebras is spelled out: for a connected Lie group G, a representation of G determines a representation of g by differentiation, and conversely every representation of g integrates to a representation of the simply connected cover of G. The universal enveloping algebra U(g) is introduced to formalize this.

### Chapter 9: Initial Classification of Lie Algebras

The classification of Lie algebras begins. A Lie algebra is abelian if all brackets vanish; simple if it has no nontrivial ideals and is not abelian; semisimple if it is a direct sum of simple Lie algebras. The Killing form is nondegenerate exactly for semisimple Lie algebras (Cartan's criterion).

The classical simple Lie algebras are listed: A_n = sl_{n+1} C, B_n = so_{2n+1} C, C_n = sp_{2n} C, D_n = so_{2n} C. These four infinite families, together with the five exceptional Lie algebras (G_2, F_4, E_6, E_7, E_8), exhaust the simple Lie algebras over C.

### Chapter 10: Lie Algebras in Dimensions One, Two, and Three

Low-dimensional Lie algebras are classified explicitly. The only one-dimensional Lie algebra is abelian. Two-dimensional Lie algebras are either abelian or solvable with bracket [x,y] = x. Three-dimensional Lie algebras include sl_2 C (simple), the Heisenberg Lie algebra (nilpotent), and so_3 R (which is isomorphic to sl_2 C after complexification).

The structure of sl_2 C is examined in detail: it has basis {H, X, Y} with relations [H,X] = 2X, [H,Y] = -2Y, [X,Y] = H. This is the prototypical simple Lie algebra, and understanding its representation theory is essential for everything that follows.

### Chapter 11: Representations of sl_2 C

This is arguably the most important single chapter in the book. The finite-dimensional irreducible representations of sl_2 C are classified by a nonnegative integer n: there is exactly one irreducible representation V_n of dimension n+1 for each n. The construction is explicit: V_n is the space of homogeneous polynomials of degree n in two variables, with X = x ∂/∂y, Y = y ∂/∂x, H = x ∂/∂x - y ∂/∂y.

The representation theory is controlled by the eigenvalues of H (weights). For V_n, the weights are n, n-2, n-4, ..., -n. The highest weight vector is the unique vector annihilated by X. Every finite-dimensional representation is completely reducible. Fulton and Harris work through examples including the decomposition of tensor products using the Clebsch-Gordan formula:

V_m ⊗ V_n ≅ V_{m+n} ⊕ V_{m+n-2} ⊕ ... ⊕ V_{|m-n|}

### Chapter 12: Representations of sl_3 C, Part I

The techniques developed for sl_2 C are extended to sl_3 C. The Cartan subalgebra h consists of diagonal matrices with trace zero, a two-dimensional abelian subalgebra. For any representation, the weight vectors are simultaneous eigenvectors for the action of h; the set of weights forms a lattice in h^*.

For sl_3 C, the roots (nonzero weights of the adjoint representation) form a root system of type A_2: six roots at 60-degree angles. The simple roots are α_1 and α_2. The irreducible finite-dimensional representations are classified by their highest weight, a pair (a,b) of nonnegative integers. The dimension of the irreducible representation Γ_{a,b} is (a+1)(b+1)(a+b+2)/2.

The case of sl_3 C demonstrates the key features of the general theory: weights, roots, the Weyl group (of order 6), and the way that representation theory is controlled by the combinatorics of the root system.

### Chapter 13: Representations of sl_3 C, Part II: Mainly Lots of Examples

This chapter is entirely computational. Fulton and Harris work through tensor products, symmetric powers, exterior powers, and their decompositions for sl_3 C. The plethysm — decomposing symmetric powers of irreducible representations — is treated in detail.

These examples serve a dual purpose: they provide the computational skills needed for later chapters, and they foreshadow the general theory by showing patterns that the highest weight formalism will explain. The chapter includes extensive tables of decompositions for low-dimensional representations.

## Part III: The Classical Lie Algebras and Their Representations

### Chapter 14: The General Setup: Analyzing the Structure and Representations of an Arbitrary Semisimple Lie Algebra

This is the theoretical heart of the book. The general theory of semisimple Lie algebras is developed: Cartan subalgebras (maximal toral subalgebras), the root system, the Weyl group, and the classification of irreducible representations by highest weights.

A Cartan subalgebra h of a semisimple Lie algebra g is a maximal abelian subalgebra consisting of semisimple elements. The adjoint action decomposes g as g = h ⊕ (⊕_{α∈Φ} g_α), where the roots α ∈ h^* are the nonzero eigenvalues. The root system Φ satisfies the axioms of an abstract root system: it spans h^*, is closed under the action of the Weyl group, and for each root α, the only multiples of α in Φ are ±α.

The Weyl group W is generated by reflections through roots and is a finite Coxeter group. The set of positive roots Φ^+ is chosen, and simple roots Δ are the positive roots that cannot be written as sums of other positive roots. The Dynkin diagram encodes the angles and length ratios between simple roots.

Irreducible representations are classified by their highest weight λ: a dominant integral weight (⟨λ, α_i^∨⟩ ≥ 0 for all simple coroots α_i^∨). Every such weight corresponds to a unique irreducible representation V(λ). The Weyl group acts on the set of weights; the character of V(λ) is given by the Weyl character formula.

### Chapter 15: sl_4 C and sl_n C

The general theory is applied to the A_n series. For sl_n C, the Cartan subalgebra consists of diagonal matrices with trace zero. The roots are ε_i - ε_j (i ≠ j). The simple roots are α_i = ε_i - ε_{i+1}. An irreducible representation is specified by an (n-1)-tuple of nonnegative integers (a_1, ..., a_{n-1}), which are the coefficients of the highest weight in the basis of fundamental weights.

The Weyl dimension formula gives the dimension of Γ_{a_1,...,a_{n-1}} as a product. For sl_4 C (type A_3), the dimensions of the fundamental representations are 4 (standard representation), 6 (exterior square), and 4 (dual of standard). The chapter works through examples including the decomposition of tensor products and the branching rule for restricting from sl_n C to sl_{n-1} C.

### Chapter 16: Symplectic Lie Algebras

The symplectic Lie algebra sp_2n C consists of matrices preserving a nondegenerate skew-symmetric bilinear form. The root system is of type C_n: the long roots are ±2ε_i, the short roots are ±ε_i ± ε_j. The simple roots are α_1 = ε_1 - ε_2, ..., α_{n-1} = ε_{n-1} - ε_n, α_n = 2ε_n.

The fundamental representations of sp_2n C include the standard representation C^{2n}, the exterior powers (which are not all irreducible), and the symmetric powers. A key feature is that sp_2n C has a nontrivial fundamental representation of dimension 2n that is symplectic (preserving the form). The Weyl dimension formula and the structure of weight diagrams are worked out.

### Chapter 17: sp_6 C and sp_2n C

The case of sp_6 C (type C_3) is treated in detail. The three fundamental representations have dimensions 6, 14', and 14. The chapter computes decompositions of tensor products and develops the branching rules for the restriction sp_2n C → sp_{2n-2} C. These are parallel to the A_n case but with important differences due to the symplectic form.

### Chapter 18: Orthogonal Lie Algebras

The orthogonal Lie algebra so_m C consists of matrices preserving a nondegenerate symmetric bilinear form. The root systems depend on the parity of m. For so_{2n+1} C (type B_n), the roots are ±ε_i and ±ε_i ± ε_j. For so_{2n} C (type D_n), the roots are ±ε_i ± ε_j. The difference in root systems leads to different Dynkin diagrams.

The fundamental representations of so_m C include the standard representation C^m, the exterior powers (which are irreducible up to the middle dimension), and the spin representations (discussed in Chapter 20). A key subtlety is that so_{2n} C has two half-spin representations of equal dimension.

### Chapter 19: so_6 C, so_7 C, and so_m C

Low-dimensional isomorphisms among the classical Lie algebras are exhibited: so_3 C ≅ sl_2 C (type A_1), so_4 C ≅ sl_2 C × sl_2 C (type A_1 × A_1), so_5 C ≅ sp_4 C (type C_2), so_6 C ≅ sl_4 C (type A_3). These isomorphisms allow representation-theoretic information to be transferred between the classical series.

The chapter works out explicit decompositions for so_6 C and so_7 C, showing how the exterior powers decompose and how the spin representations appear. The branching rules for restricting so_m C → so_{m-1} C are also given.

### Chapter 20: Spin Representations of so_m C

The spin representations are among the most subtle objects in representation theory. For so_m C, the universal cover of the special orthogonal group SO_m is the spin group Spin_m, which has one or two irreducible representations not factoring through SO_m when m is odd or even respectively.

The construction uses the Clifford algebra Cl(V), the algebra generated by V with relations v·w + w·v = 2⟨v,w⟩. The spin representation is constructed from the exterior algebra of a maximal isotropic subspace. For so_{2n} C, there are two half-spin representations of dimension 2^{n-1}; for so_{2n+1} C, there is one spin representation of dimension 2^n.

## Part IV: Lie Theory

### Chapter 21: The Classification of Complex Simple Lie Algebras

The classification theorem is stated and sketched: every complex simple Lie algebra belongs to one of the four classical families A_n (n≥1), B_n (n≥2), C_n (n≥3), D_n (n≥4) or is one of the five exceptional types G_2, F_4, E_6, E_7, E_8. The classification is achieved through the Dynkin diagram, which encodes the angles and relative lengths of the simple roots. The possible Dynkin diagrams of connected type are classified by an elementary combinatorial argument.

The exceptional Lie algebras are introduced briefly: G_2 has rank 2 and dimension 14; F_4 has rank 4 and dimension 52; E_6 has rank 6 and dimension 78; E_7 has rank 7 and dimension 133; E_8 has rank 8 and dimension 248.

### Chapter 22: G_2 and Other Exceptional Lie Algebras

The exceptional Lie algebra G_2 is treated in some detail. G_2 can be realized as the derivation algebra of the octonions O, or equivalently as the automorphism group of O. Its root system consists of six short roots and six long roots in a plane, with the simple roots at a 150-degree angle. The fundamental representations have dimensions 7 and 14 (the adjoint representation). The decomposition of tensor products for G_2 is worked out, showing the pattern of multiplicities characteristic of exceptional groups.

The other exceptional algebras F_4, E_6, E_7, E_8 are described more briefly, with their Dynkin diagrams and fundamental representation dimensions listed.

### Chapter 23: Complex Lie Groups; Characters

This chapter returns to the Lie group side of the correspondence. For a complex semisimple Lie group G, the irreducible representations are in bijection with dominant integral weights. The character of a representation V is the function on a maximal torus T given by the sum of the weights, counted with multiplicities.

The Weyl group acts on the torus and on the character; the character is W-invariant. Fulton and Harris develop the formalism of characters for the classical groups, connecting the character formulas of Part I with the Lie-theoretic approach of Part III.

### Chapter 24: Weyl Character Formula

The Weyl character formula is the crowning theorem of the representation theory of compact Lie groups. For an irreducible representation V_λ with highest weight λ, its character is

χ_λ(t) = Σ_{w∈W} (-1)^{ℓ(w)} e^{w(λ+ρ)} / Σ_{w∈W} (-1)^{ℓ(w)} e^{w(ρ)}

where ρ is half the sum of the positive roots and ℓ(w) is the length of w. The denominator (the Weyl denominator) factors as a product over positive roots of (e^{α/2} - e^{-α/2}).

The Weyl dimension formula is derived as a corollary: the dimension of V_λ is the product over positive roots of ⟨λ+ρ, α⟩ / ⟨ρ, α⟩. These formulas are applied to the classical groups, reproducing the dimension formulas obtained earlier by explicit computation.

### Chapter 25: More Character Formulas

The Weyl character formula is specialized to each classical series. For GL_n C, the formula becomes a ratio of determinants involving exponentials of the eigenvalues. This is equivalent to the Jacobi-Trudy identity for Schur polynomials, making the connection between representation theory and symmetric function theory explicit.

Tensor product decompositions (the Clebsch-Gordan problem) are approached through character multiplication. The Littlewood-Richardson rule gives the decomposition of tensor products for GL_n C and the symmetric group in terms of Young diagrams. Branching rules — decomposing representations when restricting from G to a subgroup H — are given for the classical groups, including the restriction GL_n → GL_{n-1} and the restriction from SO_m to SO_{m-1}.

### Chapter 26: Real Lie Algebras and Lie Groups

The final chapter treats real forms of complex Lie algebras. A real Lie algebra g_0 is a real form of a complex Lie algebra g if g ≅ g_0 ⊗_R C. The real forms of the classical simple Lie algebras are classified by the Cartan involution. Compact real forms are particularly important because the representation theory of a compact Lie group is equivalent to that of its complexified Lie algebra.

The real representation theory of real Lie groups is discussed: for a compact real Lie group, every finite-dimensional representation can be made unitary. The classification of irreducible unitary representations is given for the classical compact groups.

The chapter closes with a brief discussion of the applications of representation theory to physics, including the classification of elementary particles via the representations of SU(3) (the Eightfold Way) and the representation theory of the Lorentz group.

## Appendices

The six appendices provide essential background material. Appendix A covers symmetric functions, including power sums and Schur polynomials. Appendix B gives a concise treatment of multilinear algebra: tensor products, exterior and symmetric powers, and contractions. Appendix C covers semisimplicity: the Killing form, Cartan's criteria, complete reducibility, and the Jordan decomposition. Appendix D treats Cartan subalgebras, the structure of semisimple Lie algebras, and the Weyl group. Appendix E proves Ado's theorem (every finite-dimensional Lie algebra has a faithful finite-dimensional representation) and Levi's theorem (every finite-dimensional Lie algebra is a semidirect product of a semisimple and a solvable ideal). Appendix F connects representation theory to invariant theory for the classical groups, including the first and second fundamental theorems.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment
This summary captures all 26 chapters, the six appendices, and the central conceptual arc of the book: from finite groups through Lie theory to the classification of irreducible representations of the classical groups via highest weight theory and the Weyl character formula. It includes the key examples (sl_2 C, sl_3 C, sp_6 C, G_2) and all major theorems (Schur's lemma, Maschke's theorem, Frobenius reciprocity, the Weyl character and dimension formulas, the classification of simple Lie algebras). What is inevitably lost is the book's extensive collection of exercises (over 300), the detailed worked examples in every chapter, and the specific computations that build the reader's computational fluency.

### Recommended Reading Path

| Reader Type | Time | What to Read |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | ~30 min | Index + Chapters 1-2, 11, 14 summaries |
| Interested | ~4-8 hr | Chapters 1-4, 11-14, 24 (Weyl Character Formula) |
| Scholar/Practitioner | ~40-80 hr | Full book with all exercises |

### Chapters to Read in Full (if not reading the whole book)

- **Chapters 1-2** — Representations and characters of finite groups are the foundation; Schur's lemma, orthogonality, and the regular representation are used throughout.
- **Chapter 4** — Young diagrams and Frobenius's character formula; essential for Schur-Weyl duality and everything involving the symmetric group.
- **Chapter 11** — Representations of sl_2 C; the prototype for all semisimple representation theory. The weight space decomposition and the classification by highest weight first appear here in their simplest form.
- **Chapter 14** — The general theory of semisimple Lie algebras; highest weight theory in full generality. This is the intellectual high point of the book.
- **Chapter 24** — Weyl character formula; the most powerful theorem in the subject.

### Chapters to Skim or Skip
- **Chapter 5** — Representations of A_d and GL_2(F_q) are interesting but less central to the main narrative.
- **Chapters 13, 17, 19, 22** — Heavily computational; useful as reference but skimmable on first reading.
- **Chapter 26** — Real forms are an advanced topic; can be deferred.

### What You'll Miss by Not Reading the Full Book
The book's defining strength is its concrete, computational approach. The exercises develop genuine facility with the machinery of representation theory — reading summaries alone cannot provide the ability to decompose a tensor product, compute a character, or apply the Weyl dimension formula. The full book also contains extensive tables of decompositions, character formulas for all classical groups, and the subtle arguments that make the theory of spin representations and exceptional groups comprehensible. The appendices alone (particularly on symmetric functions and invariant theory) are valuable reference material that a summary cannot adequately convey.
