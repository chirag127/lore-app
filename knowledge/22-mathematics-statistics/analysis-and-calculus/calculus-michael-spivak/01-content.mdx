## Complete Chapter Summary — *Calculus* by Michael Spivak (4th Edition)

The 4th edition contains 30 chapters organized into five parts plus a prologue and epilogue. What follows is a detailed summary of every chapter, including key theorems, examples, and distinctive features.

---

### Part I: Prologue

**Chapter 1 — Basic Properties of Numbers**

Spivak begins at the foundation: the properties of real numbers as an ordered field. He states the field axioms (associativity, commutativity, distributivity, identities, inverses) and the order axioms (trichotomy, transitivity, preservation under addition and multiplication by positives). From these axioms alone, he proves familiar algebraic facts: that a·0 = 0, that (−a)(−b) = ab, and that if ab = 0 then a = 0 or b = 0. The chapter is remarkable for proving everything — nothing is taken for granted. Spivak's characteristic wit appears early: "Mathematicians like to pretend that they can't even add, but most of them can when they have to" (page 179 of the 3rd edition).

**Chapter 2 — Numbers of Various Sorts**

Natural numbers, integers, rational numbers, and irrational numbers. Spivak proves that √2 is irrational (the classic proof by contradiction). He introduces the concept of countability and shows that the rationals are countable while the reals are not, giving Cantor's diagonal argument. This chapter establishes why the real numbers are necessary: the rationals have "gaps" (like √2), and calculus requires a number system without gaps — the completeness property.

---

### Part II: Foundations

**Chapter 3 — Functions**

The modern definition of a function as a set of ordered pairs. Spivak covers domain, range, composition, identity functions, and inverse functions. The appendix introduces ordered pairs and the Cartesian product. The treatment is precise but accessible, preparing students for the rigorous work ahead.

**Chapter 4 — Graphs**

Graphs of functions in the coordinate plane. Spivak covers symmetry, shifting, scaling, and the relationship between algebraic and geometric properties. Three appendices cover vectors (for understanding parametric curves), the conic sections (parabolas, ellipses, hyperbolas), and polar coordinates. These appendices provide the geometric toolkit needed for later chapters.

**Chapter 5 — Limits**

The heart of calculus begins here. Spivak introduces the epsilon-delta definition of limits with extraordinary care. He proves basic limit theorems (sum, product, quotient of limits) directly from the definition. The chapter includes the precise definition of lim(x→a) f(x) = L and shows how to construct delta given epsilon for specific functions like f(x) = x² and f(x) = 1/x. The Squeeze Theorem is proved. Spivak warns against the common misconception that limits are about "approaching" — they are about precision.

**Chapter 6 — Continuous Functions**

Definition of continuity at a point and on an interval. Spivak proves that sums, products, and compositions of continuous functions are continuous. The chapter includes examples of discontinuous functions (the Dirichlet function, the step function) to clarify the concept. Algebraic functions, trigonometric functions, and rational functions are shown to be continuous on their domains.

**Chapter 7 — Three Hard Theorems**

This chapter is legendary. The three theorems are: the Intermediate Value Theorem (a continuous function on [a,b] takes every value between f(a) and f(b)), the Maximum Value Theorem (a continuous function on a closed interval attains its maximum and minimum), and the Uniform Continuity Theorem (a continuous function on [a,b] is uniformly continuous). Spivak proves them all rigorously, showing that each depends on the completeness of the real numbers. These are the first theorems students encounter that are not "obvious" — they require real work.

**Chapter 8 — Least Upper Bounds**

The completeness of the real numbers is formalized via the least upper bound (supremum) property. Spivak proves that the least upper bound property is equivalent to the theorems of Chapter 7. The appendix proves that a continuous function on a closed interval is uniformly continuous. This chapter is the logical core of the book: everything that makes calculus work traces back to the fact that the real numbers have no gaps.

---

### Part III: Derivatives and Integrals

**Chapter 9 — Derivatives**

The derivative as a limit of difference quotients. Spivak gives the precise definition and computes derivatives from first principles for polynomials, rational functions, and trigonometric functions. The derivative as instantaneous rate of change is discussed, but the emphasis is on the derivative as a limit.

**Chapter 10 — Differentiation**

The basic rules: sum rule, product rule, quotient rule, and the chain rule — all proved from the definition. Derivatives of trigonometric functions (sin and cos) are computed using the limit sin(h)/h → 1. Implicit differentiation and derivatives of inverse functions are introduced.

**Chapter 11 — Significance of the Derivative**

The Mean Value Theorem and its consequences: a function with positive derivative is increasing, a function with zero derivative is constant, the relationship between local extrema and critical points. Spivak proves Cauchy's Mean Value Theorem and L'Hôpital's Rule. The appendix covers convexity and concavity, relating the second derivative to the shape of the graph.

**Chapter 12 — Inverse Functions**

When does a function have an inverse? Spivak proves the Inverse Function Theorem: if f is continuous and strictly monotone on an interval, it has an inverse, and if f is differentiable with f'(x) ≠ 0, then the inverse is differentiable with a specific derivative formula. The appendix covers parametric representation of curves, giving a taste of multivariable geometry.

**Chapter 13 — Integrals**

The Riemann integral defined rigorously via upper and lower sums. Spivak proves that continuous functions are integrable, that monotone functions are integrable, and that the integral is linear and order-preserving. The appendix introduces Riemann sums, connecting the abstract definition to the computational method students expect.

**Chapter 14 — The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus**

The crown jewel. Spivak proves both parts of the FTC: (1) if f is continuous on [a,b] and F(x) = ∫ₐˣ f(t) dt, then F'(x) = f(x); (2) if F' = f and f is integrable, then ∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx = F(b) − F(a). The proof is complete and rigorous, showing exactly where continuity is needed and why the theorem is not trivial.

**Chapter 15 — The Trigonometric Functions**

A rigorous development of sin and cos starting from their geometric definitions and proving their analytic properties. Spivak proves the addition formulas, the derivatives of sin and cos, and the periodicity properties. The chapter is notable for its careful handling of the geometric intuition that underlies the formulas.

**Chapter 16 — π is Irrational**

Starred (optional) chapter. Spivak presents a proof that π is irrational, using an argument based on integrals and properties of the function f(x) = xⁿ(a−bx)ⁿ/n!. The proof is elementary but clever, typical of the gems Spivak includes for motivated readers.

**Chapter 17 — Planetary Motion**

Starred (optional) chapter. Spivak derives Kepler's laws of planetary motion from Newton's law of universal gravitation, using vector calculus in polar coordinates. This is calculus applied to its first great triumph. The mathematics is demanding but the payoff is spectacular: the reader sees why calculus was invented.

**Chapter 18 — The Logarithm and Exponential Functions**

Spivak defines ln x = ∫₁ˣ (1/t) dt, then derives all properties: ln(ab) = ln a + ln b, ln(xʳ) = r ln x for rational r. The exponential function exp is defined as the inverse of ln. The number e is defined as exp(1) = e. The function aˣ = exp(x ln a) completes the treatment.

**Chapter 19 — Integration in Elementary Terms**

Which functions have antiderivatives expressible in elementary form? Spivak discusses integration by substitution, integration by parts, and the standard techniques. The appendix, "The Cosmopolitan Integral," discusses the limits of these methods — showing that many simple-looking functions (like e^{−x²}) have no elementary antiderivative. Problem 7 in this chapter is famously difficult, described as "Potpourri. No holds barred."

---

### Part IV: Infinite Sequences and Infinite Series

**Chapter 20 — Approximation by Polynomial Functions**

Taylor polynomials and Taylor's theorem with remainder (in Lagrange and Cauchy forms). Spivak shows how polynomials approximate functions, proves the Lagrange remainder formula, and discusses when approximations converge to the function as the degree increases.

**Chapter 21 — e is Transcendental**

Starred chapter. A proof that e is transcendental (not the root of any polynomial with integer coefficients). The proof uses the same integral technique as Chapter 16 but is considerably more involved. This chapter is a reward for readers who have mastered the material.

**Chapter 22 — Infinite Sequences**

Formal definition of convergence, limit laws, Cauchy sequences, monotone sequences, and the Bolzano-Weierstrass Theorem (every bounded sequence has a convergent subsequence). Spivak connects sequences to the completeness of the real numbers.

**Chapter 23 — Infinite Series**

Convergence of series, geometric series, the harmonic series (diverges), the comparison test, the ratio test, the root test, and absolute vs. conditional convergence. The alternating series test and rearrangements of conditionally convergent series (Riemann's rearrangement theorem). The harmonic series divergence proof is particularly elegant.

**Chapter 24 — Uniform Convergence and Power Series**

Pointwise vs. uniform convergence — the crucial distinction. Spivak proves that uniform convergence preserves continuity, integrability, and differentiability. The Weierstrass M-test for uniform convergence. Power series are introduced, and their interval of convergence is determined via the ratio test. Differentiation and integration of power series term-by-term is justified.

**Chapter 25 — Complex Numbers**

The complex number system defined, arithmetic operations, the complex plane, polar form, De Moivre's Theorem, and the fundamental theorem of algebra (stated without proof). This chapter provides the algebraic foundation for the three chapters that follow.

**Chapter 26 — Complex Functions**

Limits, continuity, and derivatives of complex-valued functions of a complex variable. The Cauchy-Riemann equations. Spivak shows that complex differentiability is much stronger than real differentiability.

**Chapter 27 — Complex Power Series**

Power series of a complex variable, radius of convergence, and the exponential, sine, and cosine functions extended to complex arguments. Euler's formula e^{iθ} = cos θ + i sin θ appears as a consequence of the power series definitions, tying together the entire book.

---

### Part V: Epilogue

**Chapter 28 — Fields**

The algebraic structure of fields: definition, examples (Q, R, C, finite fields), and properties. Spivak discusses ordered fields and the additional structure needed for calculus.

**Chapter 29 — Construction of the Real Numbers**

Dedekind cuts: a rigorous construction of the real numbers from the rationals. Every real number is defined as a set of rational numbers. Spivak shows that the set of all Dedekind cuts satisfies all the field axioms and the least upper bound property, proving that the real numbers exist.

**Chapter 30 — Uniqueness of the Real Numbers**

Any complete ordered field is isomorphic to the real numbers. Spivak proves that there is essentially only one complete ordered field up to isomorphism, which justifies calling it "the" real numbers.

The book concludes with a Suggested Reading list (annotated, personal, idiosyncratic), Answers to selected problems, a Glossary of Symbols, and an Index.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency for Purpose

Spivak's *Calculus* is **not** a standard calculus textbook. It is a rigorous introduction to real analysis disguised as a calculus book. Spivak himself acknowledged in the preface to the second edition that the work is arguably an introduction to mathematical analysis rather than a calculus textbook. This means:

**What it covers well:** Everything in single-variable calculus. By the end of the book, a diligent reader understands why every theorem of calculus is true, what the real numbers are, and how to construct proofs.

**What it does not cover:** Multivariable calculus (that is the subject of Spivak's *Calculus on Manifolds*), differential equations, Fourier series, or applications to physics/engineering beyond Chapter 17.

**Recommended path:** Read chapters sequentially through Chapter 15, then skip starred chapters (16, 17, 21) on first reading, continue through Chapters 22-24 (the series material is essential), and return to the starred chapters later. The Epilogue (Chapters 28-30) can be read in any order but is best saved for after the rest is understood.

**Chapters to read first:** 1-15 (the core of calculus), then 22-24 (sequences and series).

**Chapters to skim on a first pass:** 19 (integration techniques — these are computational), 25-27 (complex numbers — important but tangential).

**Essential exercises:** Problems in Chapters 5, 7, 8, 11, 13, 14, and 23 are essential. The starred problems in each chapter are where the real learning happens.
