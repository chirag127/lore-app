## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary


### Chapter 1: Complex Numbers


Ahlfors opens by constructing the complex numbers from first principles with characteristic rigor. Section 1.1 defines complex numbers as ordered pairs of real numbers (a,b) with componentwise addition (a,b)+(c,d) = (a+c,b+d) and multiplication (a,b)(c,d) = (ac-bd, ad+bc). The imaginary unit i = (0,1) satisfies i^2 = -1. This purely algebraic construction requires no geometric intuition, establishing the subject on a firm logical foundation as a field extension of the real numbers.

Section 1.2 solves square roots explicitly: for any complex number a+bi, the equation z^2 = a+bi yields two solutions obtained by extracting real square roots. The derivation is purely algebraic: setting z = x+iy, expanding (x+iy)^2 = (x^2-y^2)+i(2xy) = a+ib, and solving the resulting system of real equations. Section 1.3, titled "Justification," provides the formal demonstration that C = R[i] modulo i^2+1 = 0 is a field. Ahlfors proves that every quadratic equation with complex coefficients has a solution in C, motivating the deeper algebraic completeness of C (the fundamental theorem of algebra, proved later in Chapter 4 via Liouville's theorem).

Section 1.4 introduces conjugation (a+bi maps to a-bi), absolute value (|a+bi| = sqrt(a^2+b^2)), and their algebraic properties. The conjugate of a sum/product equals the sum/product of conjugates. The absolute value is multiplicative: |z1 z2| = |z1||z2|. The triangle inequality |z1+z2| <= |z1|+|z2| is proved, along with its corollary ||z1|-|z2|| <= |z1-z2|. Section 1.5 establishes the Schwarz inequality for complex numbers.

The geometric representation (section 2) presents the complex plane: addition as vector addition via the parallelogram law, multiplication as combined rotation and scaling. In polar form z = r e^{i theta}, multiplication corresponds to multiplying radii and adding angles, revealing the geometric essence of complex arithmetic. The binomial equation z^n = a is solved: z = |a|^{1/n} e^{i(arg a + 2pi k)/n} for k = 0,1,...,n-1, showing the n-th roots as vertices of a regular n-gon on the circle of radius |a|^{1/n}.

Section 2.3 treats analytic geometry in complex form: lines through points a and b satisfy Im((z-a)/(b-a)) = 0; circles with center c and radius r satisfy |z-c| = r. The chapter culminates with the spherical representation (2.4): stereographic projection maps the complex plane onto the Riemann sphere S^2 (minus the north pole). A point (x,y) in C maps to (2x/(1+x^2+y^2), 2y/(1+x^2+y^2), (x^2+y^2-1)/(1+x^2+y^2)) on the unit sphere. The north pole (0,0,1) corresponds to the point at infinity, compactifying C into C-hat = C U {infinity}, homeomorphic to S^2. This construction is essential for understanding the extended complex plane and returns crucially in later chapters.


### Chapter 2: Complex Functions


Chapter 2 introduces the central concept of analyticity. Part 1 covers limits and continuity in the complex plane via epsilon-delta definitions. The crucial definition: a function f is analytic (or holomorphic) at z0 if the limit of (f(z0+h)-f(z0))/h exists as h approaches 0 in C. This is far more restrictive than real differentiability because h can approach 0 from any direction in the complex plane, and all paths must yield the same limit.

The Cauchy-Riemann equations are derived: if f(z) = u(x,y) + iv(x,y) is analytic at z, then the partial derivatives satisfy u_x = v_y and u_y = -v_x. Conversely, if u and v are continuously differentiable and satisfy Cauchy-Riemann, then f is analytic. Polynomials are entire functions (analytic everywhere in C); rational functions are analytic except at their poles. The exponential and trigonometric functions are introduced, and their differentiability is established.


Part 2 presents power series: sequences, series, uniform convergence. The radius of convergence is R = 1/limsup |a_n|^{1/n} (Cauchy-Hadamard formula). Within |z-a| < R, the series converges absolutely and uniformly on compact subsets and represents an analytic function that can be differentiated termwise. Abel's limit theorem: if a power series converges at a boundary point, the sum is continuous along the radius to that point.


Part 3 defines the exponential via power series: e^z = sum_{n=0}^{infinity} z^n/n!, entire and non-vanishing. Trigonometric functions follow: cos z = (e^{iz}+e^{-iz})/2, sin z = (e^{iz}-e^{-iz})/(2i). Periodicity: e^{z+2pi i} = e^z, with fundamental period 2pi i. The logarithm: log z = ln|z| + i arg z, inherently multi-valued because arg z is multi-valued. A branch of the logarithm must be chosen; the principal branch has -pi < arg z <= pi. The general power z^a = e^{a log z} inherits this multi-valuedness.


### Chapter 3: Analytic Functions as Mappings


The geometric viewpoint takes center stage. Part 1 provides a self-contained introduction to point-set topology: metric spaces, open/closed sets, connectedness (a set is connected if it cannot be written as the union of two disjoint non-empty open subsets), compactness (every open cover has a finite subcover; in C this is equivalent to closed and bounded by Heine-Borel), continuous functions, and topological spaces. This material was innovative for a 1953 complex analysis text and reflects Ahlfors's commitment to rigorous foundations.


Part 2 introduces conformality: an analytic function f preserves angles wherever f'(z) != 0. If two smooth arcs intersect at z with angle alpha, their images under f intersect at f(z) with the same angle and orientation. The derivative f'(z) acts as a linear transformation (multiplication by the complex number f'(z)), which is a rotation-dilation and hence conformal. Area magnification is |f'(z)|^2; length magnification on infinitesimal scales is |f'(z)|. The condition f'(z) != 0 is necessary and sufficient for conformality at a point.


Part 3 treats Mobius (linear fractional) transformations: w = (az+b)/(cz+d) with ad-bc != 0. These form the group PGL(2,C). The cross ratio (z1,z2;z3,z4) = (z1-z3)(z2-z4)/((z1-z4)(z2-z3)) is invariant under Mobius transformations and provides a complete set of invariants. Mobius transformations map circles and lines to circles and lines, preserve symmetry with respect to circles, and map the upper half-plane to itself iff a,b,c,d are real with ad-bc > 0. Families of circles and oriented circles are discussed.


Part 4 surveys elementary conformal mappings: power functions z maps to z^n (n-fold angle multiplication), exponential z maps to e^z (strips to half-planes), logarithm z maps to log z (annuli to strips), the Cayley transform z maps to (z-i)/(z+i) (upper half-plane to unit disk), and z maps to (z-1)/(z+1) (right half-plane to unit disk). The Joukowsky map z maps to (1/2)(z+1/z) and its role in airfoil theory is noted. Level curves (Re f(z) = constant, Im f(z) = constant) are used to understand mappings. Elementary Riemann surfaces for sqrt(z) and log z are introduced, foreshadowing Chapter 8.


### Chapter 4: Complex Integration


The heart of the book, from which the entire power of complex analysis flows. Part 1 defines complex line integrals: integral_gamma f(z) dz = integral_a^b f(gamma(t)) gamma'(t) dt for a smooth curve gamma. Rectifiable arcs (finite length) are introduced for greater generality. The integral is independent of the parametrization up to orientation. Cauchy's theorem for a rectangle: if f is analytic inside and on a rectangle, the integral around the rectangle is zero. Goursat's proof subdivides the rectangle into smaller rectangles, uses the definition of the derivative to bound the integral, and passes to the limit. Cauchy's theorem in a disk follows by approximating any closed curve by a polygon and subdividing into rectangles.


Part 2 presents Cauchy's integral formula. The winding number n(gamma,a) = (1/(2pi i)) integral_gamma dz/(z-a) is an integer constant on each connected component of the complement of gamma, measuring how many times gamma winds around a. Cauchy's formula: f(a) = (1/(2pi i)) integral_gamma f(z)/(z-a) dz. This is a stunning result: the values of an analytic function on a closed curve determine its values everywhere inside. Higher derivatives follow: f^{(n)}(a) = n!/(2pi i) integral_gamma f(z)/(z-a)^{n+1} dz, showing that analytic functions are infinitely differentiable and that each derivative is itself analytic. This implies that analyticity implies infinite differentiability, a stark contrast with real analysis.


Part 3 explores local properties deduced from Cauchy's formula. Removable singularities (Riemann's theorem): if f is bounded near an isolated singularity, the singularity is removable and f extends analytically. Taylor's theorem: every analytic function has a convergent power series expansion. Zeros of analytic functions are isolated unless the function is identically zero (identity theorem). Poles are isolated singularities where |f(z)| approaches infinity. The local mapping theorem: if f'(a) != 0, f is a conformal bijection from a neighborhood of a onto a neighborhood of f(a). The maximum principle: a non-constant analytic function cannot attain its maximum modulus in the interior of its domain, one of the most important tools in the subject.


Part 4 generalizes Cauchy's theorem using homology. Chains are formal sums of curves with integer coefficients; cycles are closed chains (the sum of the endpoints vanishes). A cycle Gamma is homologous to zero in a region Omega if n(Gamma,a) = 0 for all a not in Omega. Cauchy's theorem: if Gamma is homologous to zero in Omega, then integral_Gamma f(z) dz = 0 for f analytic on Omega. This elegantly handles multiply connected regions and provides the natural setting for the general theory.


Part 5 develops the calculus of residues. The residue theorem: if Gamma is a cycle homologous to zero and f is analytic on Omega except for isolated singularities at points a_k, then integral_Gamma f(z) dz = 2pi i sum_k n(Gamma,a_k) Res(f; a_k). The argument principle: (1/(2pi i)) integral_Gamma f'(z)/f(z) dz = N - P (number of zeros minus poles, counted with multiplicity). Rouche's theorem: if |f-g| < |f| on a closed curve, f and g have the same number of zeros inside. Applications to evaluating definite integrals include trigonometric integrals integral_0^{2pi} R(cos theta, sin theta) d theta via z = e^{i theta}, improper integrals integral_{-infinity}^{infinity} R(x) dx via semicircular contours, integrals with branch cuts such as integral_0^{infinity} x^{a-1}/(1+x) dx using keyhole contours, and Fresnel integrals integral_0^{infinity} sin(x^2) dx using a sector contour.


Part 6 covers harmonic functions (solutions of Laplace's equation nabla^2 u = 0). The real and imaginary parts of an analytic function are harmonic and form a harmonic conjugate pair. The mean-value property: the value of a harmonic function at a point equals its average over any circle centered at that point. Poisson's formula recovers a harmonic function in a disk from its boundary values. Schwarz's theorem guarantees the existence of a harmonic conjugate locally. The Schwarz reflection principle allows analytic continuation across a real interval.


### Chapter 5: Series and Product Developments


Part 1 presents Weierstrass's theorem on uniformly convergent series of analytic functions: uniform convergence on compact subsets implies the limit is analytic and the derivatives converge uniformly. Taylor series represent analytic functions locally. Laurent series represent functions analytic in an annulus as sums of a power series in positive powers and a power series in negative powers (the principal part). The coefficient formulas involve contour integrals.


Part 2 covers partial fractions and infinite products. The Mittag-Leffler theorem constructs meromorphic functions with prescribed principal parts. The Weierstrass factorization theorem represents entire functions as products over their zeros. The gamma function is defined via the Euler product Gamma(z) = e^{-gamma z}/z prod_{n=1}^{infinity} e^{z/n}/(1+z/n) and extended meromorphically to the whole plane with simple poles at non-positive integers. Stirling's formula gives the asymptotic behavior: Gamma(z) ~ sqrt(2pi) z^{z-1/2} e^{-z} as z approaches infinity.


Part 3 treats entire functions of finite order. Jensen's formula relates the growth of an entire function (measured by log|f(re^{i theta})|) to the distribution of its zeros. Hadamard's factorization theorem: an entire function of finite order rho can be written as z^m e^{P(z)} prod_{n=1}^{infinity} E_q(z/a_n) where P is a polynomial of degree <= rho, E_q are elementary factors, and q <= rho.


Part 4 is devoted to the Riemann zeta function zeta(s) = sum_{n=1}^{infinity} n^{-s} for Re s > 1. The Euler product zeta(s) = prod_p (1-p^{-s})^{-1} over all primes connects the zeta function to number theory. Analytic continuation to the whole plane is achieved via the functional equation relating zeta(s) to zeta(1-s). The trivial zeros at negative even integers, the non-trivial zeros in the critical strip 0 < Re s < 1, and the Riemann hypothesis. This is one of the few places in the book where Ahlfors touches on research-level open problems.


Part 5 introduces normal families. Equicontinuity and the Arzela-Ascoli theorem. Montel's theorem: a locally uniformly bounded family of analytic functions is normal (every sequence has a subsequence converging uniformly on compact subsets or tending uniformly to infinity). Normal families are essential for the proof of the Riemann mapping theorem in Chapter 6.


### Chapter 6: Conformal Mapping, Dirichlet's Problem


Part 1 proves the Riemann mapping theorem: every simply connected proper subdomain of C can be conformally mapped onto the unit disk. Ahlfors uses the normal families method: consider the family of injective analytic maps f: Omega -> D with f(a)=0 and f'(a)>0; the supremum of |f'(a)| over this family is attained; the extremal function is the desired bijective conformal map. Boundary behavior: if the boundary of Omega is a Jordan curve, the Riemann map extends continuously to a homeomorphism of the closures (Caratheodory's theorem).


Part 2 develops the Schwarz-Christoffel formula: if f maps the upper half-plane onto a polygon with interior angles alpha_k pi, then f'(z) = C prod_k (z-x_k)^{alpha_k-1}. Applications include mapping onto rectangles (giving elliptic integrals) and the triangle functions of Schwarz (relating to the hypergeometric function).


Part 3 revisits harmonic functions: functions with the mean-value property are harmonic. Harnack's principle: a pointwise increasing sequence of harmonic functions converges either to a harmonic function or uniformly to infinity on compact subsets.


Part 4 treats the Dirichlet problem: given a region Omega and a boundary function phi, find a harmonic function u on Omega that equals phi on the boundary. Ahlfors presents Perron's method using subharmonic functions. A function u is subharmonic if its Laplacian is non-negative (equivalently, it satisfies the sub-mean-value property). The Perron family: all subharmonic functions w on Omega that are <= phi on the boundary. The supremum of this family is the harmonic solution of the Dirichlet problem, provided the boundary is sufficiently regular. This material was a distinctive feature of Ahlfors's book and one of its most praised sections.


Part 5 treats canonical mappings of multiply connected regions. Harmonic measures, Green's function G(z,zeta) = log|z-zeta| + h(z,zeta) where h is chosen to make G vanish on the boundary, and parallel slit regions into which every finitely connected region can be conformally mapped.


### Chapter 7: Elliptic Functions


Part 1 covers simply periodic functions: f(z+p) = f(z). Representation by exponentials f(z) = g(e^{2pi i z/p}) and Fourier series development. Functions of finite order.


Part 2 introduces doubly periodic functions with period module Omega = {m omega_1 + n omega_2}. The fundamental parallelogram: any point in C is equivalent modulo Omega to a unique point in the parallelogram. Unimodular transformations of the basis correspond to changes of basis in the lattice. Liouville's theorem: a doubly periodic entire function is bounded on the compact fundamental parallelogram and hence constant by Liouville's theorem. Thus non-constant elliptic functions must have poles. The number of poles in a fundamental parallelogram (counted with multiplicity) is called the order; it is at least 2 for non-constant elliptic functions.


Part 3 presents the Weierstrass theory. The Weierstrass p-function: p(z) = 1/z^2 + sum_{omega != 0} (1/(z-omega)^2 - 1/omega^2), which converges absolutely and uniformly on compact subsets. It is doubly periodic with periods omega_1, omega_2 and has a double pole at each lattice point. The functions zeta(z) = -integral p(z) dz and sigma(z) = exp(integral zeta(z) dz) are also defined, each with specific transformation properties. The fundamental differential equation: p'(z)^2 = 4p(z)^3 - g_2 p(z) - g_3, showing that (p, p') parametrizes the elliptic curve y^2 = 4x^3 - g_2 x - g_3. The modular function lambda(tau) maps the upper half-plane onto C\{0,1} and is used critically in the proof of Picard's theorem.


### Chapter 8: Global Analytic Functions


Part 1 develops analytic continuation via the Weierstrass theory. A function element is a pair (f,D) where f is analytic on a disk D. Two elements are direct analytic continuations if their disks overlap and the functions agree on the overlap. The sheaf of germs of analytic functions: a germ is an equivalence class of function elements under the equivalence relation generated by direct analytic continuation. Sections of this sheaf give single-valued analytic functions on the base space. Analytic continuation along an arc proceeds by covering the arc with overlapping disks. Homotopy invariance: analytic continuation depends only on the homotopy class of the arc. The monodromy theorem: if a function element can be continued along every curve in a simply connected region, the result is a single-valued analytic function. Branch points arise when continuation around a closed loop changes the value.


Part 2 treats algebraic functions defined by polynomial equations P(z,w) = 0. The resultant of two polynomials eliminates one variable. An algebraic function has finitely many branches, each analytic except at branch points (where the discriminant vanishes).


Part 3 proves Picard's theorem: a non-constant entire function omits at most one complex value (little Picard). The proof uses the modular function lambda(tau) and the monodromy theorem: if f misses two values, compose with the inverse of lambda to get a bounded entire function, which is constant by Liouville. Big Picard: near an essential singularity, a function assumes every value with at most one exception infinitely often.


Part 4 covers linear differential equations in the complex domain. Ordinary points have analytic solutions. Regular singular points have solutions with at worst algebraic or logarithmic singularities. The hypergeometric equation z(1-z)w'' + (c-(a+b+1)z)w' - abw = 0 and its solution F(a,b;c;z) (the Gauss hypergeometric series). Riemann's geometric viewpoint: linear differential equations are characterized by their monodromy groups.


## Reading Guide


Ahlfors is designed for a rigorous first-year graduate course. The standard two-semester sequence covers Chapters 1-3 (foundations), Chapter 4 (integration -- the heart of the course), Chapter 5 (series), and Chapter 6 (conformal mapping). Chapters 7 (elliptic functions) and 8 (global theory) are typically reserved for a second course or independent study. The book demands active reading: work through every proof, fill in omitted steps, solve at least half the exercises in each section. Supplement with Needham's Visual Complex Analysis for geometric intuition and with a solution manual for checking understanding. The AMS Chelsea reprint (2021) is the affordable edition at approximately $60; the McGraw-Hill edition is overpriced at nearly $300. For students, the combination of Ahlfors for rigor and Needham for intuition is ideal.
