## Complete Chapter Summary — *Fourier Analysis: An Introduction* by Stein & Shakarchi

The book contains 8 chapters plus an appendix. It is organized into three natural parts: Fourier series (Chapters 1--4), the Fourier transform (Chapters 5--6), and finite Fourier analysis with number-theoretic applications (Chapters 7--8). The appendix reviews Riemann integration.

---

### Chapter 1 — The Genesis of Fourier Analysis

This chapter is deliberately non-rigorous, serving as a historical and physical motivation for everything that follows. Stein and Shakarchi begin with the problem that launched Fourier analysis: the vibrating string. They derive the one-dimensional wave equation

\[
\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = c^2 \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}
\]

from physical principles (Newton's second law and the small-displacement approximation). Using separation of variables, they find standing wave solutions of the form \( u(x,t) = \sin(kx) \cos(ckt) \). The key insight is linearity: any superposition of these basic solutions is also a solution. This leads to the representation of an arbitrary initial displacement as a Fourier sine series.

The solution of the wave equation for the plucked string is worked out explicitly, showing how the Fourier series coefficients are determined by the initial shape. The chapter then turns to the heat equation:

\[
\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \kappa \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}
\]

and its steady-state version in the disc, which leads to Fourier series in angular coordinates. Joseph Fourier's revolutionary conviction — that any function can be expressed as a trigonometric series — is presented in its historical context. The chapter closes with exercises and a single challenging problem.

### Chapter 2 — Basic Properties of Fourier Series

Here rigor begins. The Fourier series of a function \( f \) on \([-\pi,\pi]\) is defined as

\[
f(\theta) \sim \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} a_n e^{in\theta}, \quad a_n = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(\theta) e^{-in\theta}\,d\theta.
\]

The authors prove the uniqueness theorem: if two continuous functions have the same Fourier coefficients, they are identical. This relies on the fact that a continuous function with all Fourier coefficients zero must be identically zero — a result proved using the Weierstrass approximation theorem.

Convolutions are introduced: \((f * g)(x) = \int f(x-y)g(y)\,dy\). The crucial concept of **good kernels** (families of functions that approximate a Dirac delta) is developed. A kernel family \(\{K_n\}\) is good if: (1) \(\int K_n = 1\), (2) \(\int |K_n| \leq M\) uniformly, and (3) for every \(\delta > 0\), \(\int_{|x|>\delta} |K_n(x)|\,dx \to 0\). The authors prove that convolution with a good kernel converges uniformly to the original function for continuous functions.

Cesàro and Abel summability are introduced as methods to handle the divergence of Fourier series at points of discontinuity. Fejér's theorem — that the Cesàro means of the Fourier series of a continuous function converge uniformly to the function — is proved using Fejér's kernel (a good kernel). The Poisson kernel is introduced for Abel summability, and the solution of the Dirichlet problem on the unit disc (finding a harmonic function with prescribed boundary values) is presented as an application.

### Chapter 3 — Convergence of Fourier Series

This chapter tackles the subtler question of when Fourier series converge in the usual (pointwise or mean-square) sense, not just in the Cesàro sense.

Mean-square convergence is proved first. The authors introduce vector space concepts (inner products, orthogonality, the \(L^2\) norm) and prove that the partial sums of the Fourier series converge to \(f\) in the mean-square sense. This is equivalent to Parseval's identity:

\[
\frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} |f(\theta)|^2\,d\theta = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} |a_n|^2.
\]

The proof uses the fact that the trigonometric system is orthonormal and complete in \(L^2[-\pi,\pi]\).

For pointwise convergence, the story is more delicate. The partial sums \(S_N(f)(\theta)\) are expressed as a convolution with the Dirichlet kernel \(D_N(\theta) = \frac{\sin((N+1/2)\theta)}{\sin(\theta/2)}\). Unlike Fejér's kernel, the Dirichlet kernel is **not** a good kernel — its absolute integral grows like \(\log N\). The Riemann-Lebesgue lemma is proved: if \(f\) is integrable, then its Fourier coefficients tend to zero.

A local version of pointwise convergence is established: if \(f\) satisfies a Dini-Lipschitz condition at a point, the Fourier series converges at that point. The chapter culminates in a construction — due to Fejér and Lebesgue — of a continuous function whose Fourier series diverges at a point. This dramatic result shows that the convergence of Fourier series is a genuinely subtle issue, motivating the summability methods of Chapter 2.

### Chapter 4 — Some Applications of Fourier Series

Four substantial applications are presented.

**The isoperimetric inequality:** Among all closed curves with a given length, the circle encloses the maximum area. The proof uses Fourier series to express the area and length in terms of Fourier coefficients, then applies Parseval's identity to show \(A \leq L^2/4\pi\), with equality only for circles.

**Weyl's equidistribution theorem:** For an irrational number \(\alpha\), the sequence \(\{n\alpha \bmod 1\}\) is equidistributed in \([0,1]\). That is, for any interval \([a,b]\), the proportion of the first \(N\) terms falling in \([a,b]\) approaches \(b-a\) as \(N\to\infty\). The proof uses Fourier series to approximate the indicator function of \([a,b]\) and the fact that exponential sums involving \(e^{2\pi i n\alpha}\) are bounded for irrational \(\alpha\).

**A continuous, nowhere differentiable function:** The authors construct the classic Weierstrass function \(f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a^n \cos(b^n \pi x)\) with \(0 < a < 1\) and \(b\) an odd integer with \(ab > 1 + 3\pi/2\). The proof that this function is nowhere differentiable uses careful estimates of difference quotients and the properties of the Fourier series.

**The heat equation on the circle:** The initial-value problem for the heat equation on a circle is solved completely using Fourier series. Separation of variables gives solutions \(e^{-n^2 t} e^{inx}\), and the general solution is a superposition. The authors show that the solution is smooth for \(t>0\) even if the initial data is merely integrable — illustrating the smoothing property of the heat equation.

### Chapter 5 — The Fourier Transform on R

This chapter develops the Fourier transform for functions on the real line. The Fourier transform is defined as

\[
\hat{f}(\xi) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) e^{-2\pi i x \xi}\,dx.
\]

The Schwartz space \(\mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R})\) (smooth functions all of whose derivatives decay faster than any polynomial) is introduced as the natural setting. On \(\mathcal{S}\), the Fourier transform is a bijection with a simple explicit inverse:

\[
f(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(\xi) e^{2\pi i x \xi}\,d\xi.
\]

The key properties are proved: the Fourier transform converts differentiation to multiplication (\(\widehat{f'}(\xi) = 2\pi i \xi \hat{f}(\xi)\)) and convolution to pointwise product (\(\widehat{f * g} = \hat{f}\hat{g}\)). The Plancherel formula is established: \(\int |f(x)|^2\,dx = \int |\hat{f}(\xi)|^2\,d\xi\), showing that the Fourier transform is an isometry on \(L^2\).

The Fourier transform is extended to functions of "moderate decrease" (bounded by \(C/(1+x^2)\)). The Weierstrass approximation theorem is proved as an application, using convolution with a Gaussian.

Applications to PDEs follow. The time-dependent heat equation on the real line is solved: \(\hat{u}(\xi,t) = \hat{f}(\xi) e^{-4\pi^2 \xi^2 t}\), leading to the fundamental solution (heat kernel) \(u(x,t) = f * H_t\) with \(H_t(x) = (4\pi t)^{-1/2} e^{-x^2/4t}\). The steady-state heat equation in the upper half-plane is solved using the Poisson kernel.

The Poisson summation formula is derived:

\[
\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} f(n) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(n),
\]

connecting Fourier series and Fourier transforms. Applications include the functional equation for the Riemann zeta function and the transformation law for the Jacobi theta function.

The chapter ends with the Heisenberg uncertainty principle: if \(\int x^2 |f(x)|^2\,dx\) and \(\int \xi^2 |\hat{f}(\xi)|^2\,d\xi\) are both finite, then their product is at least \(1/(16\pi^2)\) times \((\int |f|^2)^2\). This is proved using integration by parts and the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality.

### Chapter 6 — The Fourier Transform on R^d

The theory is extended to \(d\)-dimensional Euclidean space. The Fourier transform on \(\mathbb{R}^d\) is defined as

\[
\hat{f}(\xi) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^d} f(x) e^{-2\pi i x\cdot\xi}\,dx.
\]

The symmetries of the Fourier transform under rotations, translations, and dilations are established. Integration on \(\mathbb{R}^d\) is reviewed (including the change of variables formula and spherical coordinates).

The Schwartz space \(\mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R}^d)\) is defined similarly, and all the one-dimensional results (inversion, Plancherel, convolution) generalize naturally.

The main application is the wave equation in \(\mathbb{R}^d \times \mathbb{R}\):

\[
\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = \Delta u, \quad u(x,0) = f(x), \quad \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}(x,0) = g(x).
\]

In \(\mathbb{R}^3\), the solution is given by the Kirchhoff formula:

\[
u(x,t) = \frac{1}{4\pi t^2} \int_{\partial B(x,t)} [g(y) + \nabla f(y)\cdot (y-x) + f(y)]\,d\sigma(y).
\]

The derivation uses the Fourier transform to reduce the PDE to an ODE in \(\xi\), then inverts the transform using spherical symmetry and oscillatory integrals. The solution in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) is obtained from the \(\mathbb{R}^3\) solution by the method of descent. Huygens' principle (sharp propagation of signals in odd dimensions \(\geq 3\)) is discussed.

The Radon transform is introduced: \(R(f)(\ell)\) is the integral of \(f\) over a line (or hyperplane) \(\ell\). The Fourier slice theorem \((R_\theta f)^\wedge(\rho) = \hat{f}(\rho\theta)\) is proved, which is the basis for computed tomography (CT scanning).

### Chapter 7 — Finite Fourier Analysis

The focus shifts from continuous to finite abelian groups. The group \(Z(N) = \mathbb{Z}/N\mathbb{Z}\) is studied first. Characters are defined as homomorphisms \(\chi: Z(N) \to \mathbb{C}^*\), which take the form \(\chi_k(m) = e^{2\pi i k m / N}\). The Fourier transform on \(Z(N)\) is

\[
\hat{f}(k) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{m=0}^{N-1} f(m) e^{-2\pi i k m / N},
\]

with inversion \(f(m) = \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} \hat{f}(k) e^{2\pi i k m / N}\). The Plancherel identity \(\frac{1}{N}\sum_{m} |f(m)|^2 = \sum_k |\hat{f}(k)|^2\) is proved.

The fast Fourier transform (FFT) algorithm is presented: by exploiting the structure of \(Z(N)\) when \(N\) factors, the DFT can be computed in \(O(N\log N)\) operations rather than \(O(N^2)\).

The theory is generalized to arbitrary finite abelian groups. Characters of a product group are products of characters of the factors. The orthogonality relations for characters are proved, and the Fourier inversion theorem is established in this general setting. The chapter establishes that the dual group \(\hat{G}\) (the set of all characters) is isomorphic to \(G\) itself.

### Chapter 8 — Dirichlet's Theorem

The final chapter applies finite Fourier analysis to prove Dirichlet's theorem: there are infinitely many primes in any arithmetic progression \(a, a+q, a+2q, \dots\) where \(\gcd(a,q)=1\).

The proof begins with number-theoretic preliminaries: Euler's product formula \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty n^{-s} = \prod_p (1-p^{-s})^{-1}\) and the fact that \(\sum_{p} 1/p\) diverges (which implies infinitely many primes). The proof of Dirichlet's theorem reduces to showing that for each character \(\chi\) mod \(q\) other than the trivial character, the \(L\)-function \(L(s,\chi) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \chi(n)n^{-s}\) does not vanish at \(s=1\).

Characters mod \(q\) are identified with characters of the group \((\mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z})^\times\). The orthogonality relations from Chapter 7 are used to isolate the progression \(a \bmod q\): \(\frac{1}{\phi(q)} \sum_{\chi} \overline{\chi(a)} \chi(n)\) equals 1 if \(n \equiv a \pmod{q}\) and 0 otherwise.

The core of the proof is showing \(L(1,\chi) \neq 0\) for non-trivial \(\chi\). For complex characters, this follows from a clever product argument. For real (quadratic) characters, the non-vanishing is deeper and uses properties of the class number of quadratic fields. The chapter also introduces logarithms of \(L\)-functions and uses them to show that the sum over primes \(p \equiv a \pmod{q}\) of \(1/p\) diverges — a stronger statement than just infinitude.

### Appendix: Integration

The appendix provides a self-contained review of Riemann integration on R and R^d. It covers the definition of the Riemann integral, basic properties, sets of measure zero and their relation to integrability, multiple integrals, iterated integrals, the change of variables formula, spherical coordinates, and improper integrals. The concept of "functions of moderate decrease" (which decay at least as fast as \(1/x^2\)) is defined and used to justify the integration of the Fourier transform.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment

This summary captures the full scope of Stein and Shakarchi's *Fourier Analysis*. Every chapter, from the physical motivation of Chapter 1 through the number-theoretic payoff of Chapter 8, is represented. Key theorems (Fejér, Plancherel, Poisson summation, Fourier inversion, Dirichlet's theorem) and applications (isoperimetric inequality, Weyl equidistribution, nowhere differentiable function, heat/wave equations, Radon transform, FFT) are all described. The summary captures the book's distinctive arc: starting from physical intuition, building rigorous theory, and culminating in deep applications.
