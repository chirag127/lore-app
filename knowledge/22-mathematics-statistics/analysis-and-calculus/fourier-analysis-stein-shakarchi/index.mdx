**Fourier Analysis: An Introduction** is the first volume of the four-part Princeton Lectures in Analysis series, arising from a radical rethinking of how advanced analysis is taught at Princeton University. Written by Elias M. Stein — a towering figure in harmonic analysis and mentor to Fields Medalists Charles Fefferman and Terence Tao — together with Rami Shakarchi, the book begins with the physical problems that gave birth to Fourier analysis (the vibrating string and heat diffusion) and builds a rigorous mathematical edifice around them.

The book is unique in its organization: it treats Fourier series, the Fourier transform on R and R^d, and finite Fourier analysis as manifestations of a single underlying idea — that functions can be decomposed into fundamental frequencies. Because Lebesgue integration is deferred to Volume 3, the book uses Riemann integration throughout, making it accessible to students with only a standard undergraduate analysis background. The final chapter applies finite Fourier analysis to prove Dirichlet's theorem on primes in arithmetic progressions, a spectacular demonstration of the reach of Fourier methods into number theory.

Fernando Q. Gouvêa, reviewing for the MAA, called it a book that "would make a great book for independent study courses with advanced undergraduates" and noted it is "definitely worth a look." Charles Fefferman and Robert Fefferman, writing in the Notices of the AMS, described the series as "an extraordinary achievement" and praised how the volume establishes "the fundamentals and central questions of Fourier analysis as a base camp for many of their further operations."
