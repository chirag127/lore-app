First published in 1973, Walter Rudin's *Functional Analysis* completes his celebrated trilogy of analysis textbooks. It is the most advanced of the three, presupposing the material of *Real and Complex Analysis* and moving swiftly into the abstract theory of topological vector spaces, distributions, and Banach algebras. Rudin's approach is distinctive: the spectral theorem is derived via commutative Banach algebras (the Gelfand-Naimark theorem) rather than the more common measure-theoretic route, and distribution theory is presented as a natural application of TVS concepts.

The book is renowned for its concision. In under 400 pages, Rudin covers the equivalent of three semester-length graduate courses: general functional analysis (TVS, Banach spaces, duality), distribution theory and Fourier analysis, and Banach algebra spectral theory. Every sentence carries weight, and the proofs are models of efficient exposition. This makes the book demanding but deeply rewarding for the prepared reader. The second edition (1991) added Kakutani's fixed point theorem, Lomonosov's invariant subspace theorem, and an ergodic theorem.
