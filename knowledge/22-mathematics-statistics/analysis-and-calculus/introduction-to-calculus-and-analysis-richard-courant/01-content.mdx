## Complete Chapter Summary — *Introduction to Calculus and Analysis* by Courant & John

The two-volume work contains 8 chapters in Volume I and 8 chapters in Volume II, totaling 16 chapters across approximately 1,600 pages. This summary covers every chapter with specific examples and data points.

---

### Volume I: Functions of a Single Variable

#### Chapter 1 — Introduction

Courant begins not with axioms but with the continuum of numbers — the "fluid" intuitive idea of the real number line. He starts with natural numbers, integers, and rationals, then introduces irrationals through the geometric example of √2. The concept of limit is introduced informally through the decimal expansion of numbers: "The number π = 3.14159... is an infinite decimal that can be approximated to any desired accuracy by finite decimals." The notion of a function is presented as a correspondence between variable quantities, with examples ranging from algebraic formulas (y = x²) to the Dirichlet function (χ(x) = 1 if x rational, 0 if irrational). Limits of sequences and functions are introduced intuitively with the graphical notion of "approaching a value." The chapter closes with a discussion of the concept of continuity and the classification of elementary functions (polynomials, trigonometric, exponential, logarithmic). Remarkably, this introductory chapter already contains supplements on infinite series and numerical methods, including an introduction to the Newton-Raphson method for solving equations.

#### Chapter 2 — The Fundamental Ideas of the Integral and Differential Calculus

This is the conceptual heart of Volume I. Courant develops the integral and differential calculus in parallel, as two sides of the same coin. He starts with the integral as the area under a curve, defining it as the limit of sums of rectangles (the Riemann sum approach, though not named as such). The derivative is introduced as the limit of difference quotients, the instantaneous rate of change. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus is proved: if F'(x) = f(x), then ∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx = F(b) − F(a). Courant uses the example of a falling body: s = ½gt², velocity v = ds/dt = gt, and the distance fallen ∫₀ᵀ gt dt = ½gT². The derivative of xⁿ is proven from first principles using the binomial theorem. The derivative of sin x is obtained via the geometric inequality sin h < h < tan h, giving lim_{h→0} sin h / h = 1. The supplement to this chapter is a masterpiece: a rigorous proof of the existence of the definite integral for continuous functions using upper and lower sums (Darboux's approach), showing that the supremum of lower sums equals the infimum of upper sums.

#### Chapter 3 — The Techniques of Calculus

A systematic development of computational methods. Differentiation rules: the product rule, quotient rule, chain rule — all proved from the limit definition. Derivatives of all elementary functions: polynomials, rational functions, trigonometric functions, inverse trigonometric functions, exponentials, and logarithms. The logarithm is defined as ln x = ∫₁ˣ (1/t) dt (following the approach that Courant's student and colleague, the Dutch mathematician... actually this is a natural development). Integration techniques: substitution, integration by parts, integration of rational functions by partial fractions, integration of trigonometric forms. The chapter includes a section on improper integrals, with the careful handling of limits: ∫₀¹ 1/√x dx converges because the antiderivative 2√x tends to 0 as x → 0. Numerical methods are given unusual attention: the trapezoidal rule and Simpson's rule with error estimates. A supplement discusses the gamma function Γ(n) = ∫₀^∞ e^{-t} t^{n-1} dt, extending the factorial to non-integer arguments. Another supplement treats interpolation, including Lagrange's interpolation formula, which Courant describes as "the foundation of all numerical tables."

#### Chapter 4 — Applications in Physics and Geometry

The most distinctive chapter of the book — two full applications chapters that outshine any competitor. Part A covers applications in physics: center of mass and moments, work and energy, hydrostatic pressure, the catenary (the shape of a hanging chain derived from the differential equation dy/dx = (1/a)∫₀ˣ √(1 + (y')²) dx), planetary motion (Kepler's laws from Newton's gravitation), and the calculus of variations problem of the brachistochrone (the curve of fastest descent). Part B covers geometric applications: arc length expressed as ∫ √(1 + (f'(x))²) dx, area of surfaces of revolution, the theorem of Pappus, and the formula for the area bounded by a closed curve expressed as a line integral: A = ½∮(x dy − y dx). The supplement presents the concept of the line integral explicitly and proves Green's theorem in the plane: ∮(P dx + Q dy) = ∬(∂Q/∂x − ∂P/∂y) dx dy. This is remarkably early — most texts delay this to multivariable calculus. The supplement also treats the differential equations of the vibrating string, introducing the wave equation u_tt = c² u_xx, and shows that its solution can be expressed as the sum of two traveling waves (d'Alembert's solution).

#### Chapter 5 — Taylor's Expansion

Taylor's theorem is presented as "the most important tool of analysis." Courant proves Taylor's theorem with remainder in Lagrange form: f(x) = Σ_{k=0}^{n} f⁽ᵏ⁾(a)(x−a)ᵏ/k! + f⁽ⁿ⁺¹⁾(ξ)(x−a)ⁿ⁺¹/(n+1)! for some ξ between a and x. Applications include: approximation of eˣ, sin x, cos x by their Maclaurin series; error bounds for these approximations (e.g., |sin x − (x − x³/6)| ≤ x⁵/120 for small x); series expansions of (1+x)ᵃ (the binomial series for non-integer a); the computation of π using the arctan series π/4 = 1 − 1/3 + 1/5 − 1/7 + ...; the Euler-Maclaurin summation formula (stated and applied but not fully derived). The supplement contains a proof that e is irrational, using the series e = Σ 1/n! and the standard contradiction argument. Another supplement treats the Stirling formula n! ~ (n/e)ⁿ√(2πn) with a derivation using the gamma function and the method of steepest descent — remarkably, this is the same formula used later in statistical mechanics and probability theory.

#### Chapter 6 — Infinite Sums and Products

A thorough treatment of infinite series and infinite products. The chapter begins with the definition of convergence of Σ a_n as the limit of partial sums. The geometric series Σ rⁿ = 1/(1−r) for |r| < 1 is the first example, with the harmonic series Σ 1/n shown to diverge by grouping terms (1 + 1/2 + (1/3+1/4) + (1/5+...+1/8) + ... > 1 + 1/2 + 1/2 + 1/2 + ...). Convergence tests: comparison test, ratio test, root test, integral test, alternating series test (Leibniz's test), absolute vs. conditional convergence. The rearrangement theorem: "If Σ a_n converges conditionally, then for any given number L the series can be rearranged to converge to L" — Riemann's astonishing result. Series of functions: pointwise vs. uniform convergence, the Weierstrass M-test, the fact that a uniformly convergent series of continuous functions is continuous, can be integrated term-by-term, and (with stronger conditions) differentiated term-by-term. Power series: radius of convergence, term-by-term differentiation and integration within the radius of convergence. The binomial series (1+x)ᵃ = 1 + ax + a(a−1)x²/2! + ... is derived as a power series. Infinite products are introduced: the product formula for sin x: sin x = x Π_{n=1}^∞ (1 − x²/(n²π²)), which expresses sin as a product over its zeros — a result that Euler found by analogy with factoring polynomials, and that Courant presents as one of the most beautiful formulas in mathematics. The supplement treats Bernoulli numbers and their generating function x/(eˣ−1) = Σ B_n xⁿ/n!, leading to formulas for Σ kᵖ.

#### Chapter 7 — Trigonometric Series

Fourier series in one variable. The chapter starts from the problem of representing a function as a sum of sine and cosine waves: f(x) = a₀/2 + Σ (a_n cos nx + b_n sin nx). The orthogonality relations ∫₀²π cos mx cos nx dx = 0 for m≠n, = π for m=n are derived from elementary trigonometric identities (an exercise the reader is asked to work through). The Euler formulas for the Fourier coefficients: a_n = (1/π)∫₀²π f(x) cos nx dx, b_n = (1/π)∫₀²π f(x) sin nx dx are derived by multiplying the series by cos mx and integrating term-by-term. Convergence of Fourier series: Dirichlet's theorem — if f is piecewise monotone with finitely many jumps, the Fourier series converges to the average of left and right limits at each point. The Gibbs phenomenon: near a jump discontinuity, the Fourier series overshoots by about 9% of the jump size, regardless of how many terms are taken. Examples include the Fourier series of the square wave, the sawtooth wave, and the triangular wave. Parseval's theorem: (1/π)∫₀²π f(x)² dx = a₀²/2 + Σ (a_n² + b_n²). Applications include the solution of the heat equation ∂u/∂t = κ ∂²u/∂x² by separation of variables, leading to Fourier series solutions: u(x,t) = Σ b_n sin(nπx/L) e^{−κn²π²t/L²}. The supplement treats the theory of orthogonal functions generally and the method of least squares (Legendre polynomials appear as orthogonal polynomials on [−1,1]).

#### Chapter 8 — Differential Equations for the Simplest Types of Vibration

An introduction to ordinary differential equations motivated by physical problems. First-order equations: separable equations, linear equations (integrating factor), exact equations. The logistic equation dP/dt = kP(1−P/K) is used as an example of separable equations, with applications to population growth. Second-order linear equations with constant coefficients: the characteristic equation, the cases of real distinct roots, repeated roots, and complex roots. The harmonic oscillator: mx'' + kx = 0 leading to x = A cos(ωt) + B sin(ωt) with ω = √(k/m). Damped oscillations: mx'' + cx' + kx = 0. Forced oscillations: mx'' + cx' + kx = F cos(ωt), with the phenomenon of resonance when ω equals the natural frequency. Systems of differential equations are introduced in the context of coupled oscillators. The supplement treats the method of variation of parameters and the application to the vibrating string (revisited from Chapter 4's supplement).

### Volume II: Functions of Several Variables

#### Chapter 1 — Functions of Several Variables and Their Derivatives

Point sets in the plane and space: open and closed sets, boundaries, compactness (defined via the Heine-Borel property). Limits and continuity for functions of several variables: the epsilon-delta definition with the metric |(x,y)−(a,b)| < δ ⇒ |f(x,y)−f(a,b)| < ε. Partial derivatives defined and computed for elementary functions. The chain rule for functions of several variables. Implicit functions: if F(x,y) = 0 and ∂F/∂y ≠ 0, then y can be expressed as a function of x near the point, with dy/dx = −(∂F/∂x)/(∂F/∂y). The inverse function theorem for mappings from ℝ² to ℝ². Higher derivatives and the equality of mixed partials (Clairaut's theorem: if f_xy and f_yx are continuous, they are equal). Taylor's theorem for functions of several variables. Maxima and minima: the second derivative test using the Hessian matrix and its determinant (D = f_xx f_yy − (f_xy)²). The method of Lagrange multipliers for constrained optimization.

#### Chapter 2 — Vectors, Matrices, Linear Transformations

Vector algebra: addition, scalar multiplication, dot product, cross product, and their geometric interpretations. Linear transformations represented by matrices: matrix multiplication, the inverse of a matrix, determinants (defined geometrically as volume scaling factors). The determinant is developed systematically: 2×2 and 3×3 cases explicitly, with the general n×n case sketched. Cramer's rule for solving linear systems. Eigenvalues and eigenvectors: the characteristic equation, the geometric meaning of eigenvectors as directions preserved by the transformation. Applications to quadratic forms and the classification of critical points (confirming the second derivative test from Chapter 1). The supplement introduces coordinate transformations and the Jacobian determinant: if (u,v) = (φ(x,y), ψ(x,y)), then the area scaling factor is the absolute value of the Jacobian |∂(u,v)/∂(x,y)| — essential preparation for multiple integrals.

#### Chapter 3 — Developments and Applications of the Differential Calculus

The gradient vector: ∇f = (∂f/∂x, ∂f/∂y), pointing in the direction of steepest ascent. Directional derivatives as the dot product of the gradient with a unit vector. Level sets and the fact that the gradient is orthogonal to level curves. Tangent planes and normal vectors to surfaces. The curvature of curves in the plane and in space. Envelopes of families of curves: a curve that is tangent to each member of a family at some point. The catenary revisited: the envelope of the family of lines that describe the possible tangents to the catenary. This chapter develops the geometric intuition that makes the ap
plications of the differential calculus come alive, with numerous diagrams and physical examples.

#### Chapter 4 — Multiple Integrals

The double integral defined as the limit of Riemann sums over rectangles in the plane: ∬_R f(x,y) dx dy = lim_{Δx,Δy→0} Σ f(x_i, y_j) Δx Δy. Iterated integrals and Fubini's theorem (stated and applied, not proved in full generality): ∬_R f(x,y) dx dy = ∫ (∫ f(x,y) dx) dy. Change of variables in multiple integrals using the Jacobian: ∬_D f(x,y) dx dy = ∬_{D*} f(φ(u,v), ψ(u,v)) |∂(x,y)/∂(u,v)| du dv. Examples include polar coordinates, cylindrical coordinates, and spherical coordinates. Applications: area, volume, center of mass, moments of inertia. The triple integral and its applications to volume and mass. Improper multiple integrals are handled with care. The supplement treats the transformation formula in full generality and discusses the concept of area as defined by multiple integrals, closing with the isoperimetric inequality: for a given perimeter, the circle encloses the maximum area.

#### Chapter 5 — Relations Between Surface and Volume Integrals

The integral theorems of vector calculus. Green's theorem in the plane: ∮∂D (P dx + Q dy) = ∬_D (∂Q/∂x − ∂P/∂y) dx dy — the first of the major integral theorems. The divergence theorem (Gauss's theorem): ∭_V ∇·F dV = ∯_∂V F·n dS — the volume integral of the divergence equals the flux through the surface. Stokes's theorem: ∬_S (∇×F)·n dS = ∮_∂S F·dr — the flux of the curl through a surface equals the circulation around its boundary. Each theorem is motivated physically: Green's theorem as the work done around a closed curve, the divergence theorem as the net flux through a closed surface, Stokes's theorem as the circulation around the boundary of a surface. Applications include Archimedes' principle (the buoyant force equals the weight of the displaced fluid), the interpretation of divergence as outflow per unit volume, and the interpretation of curl as circulation per unit area. These theorems are presented as the multivariable analogue of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, and their mutual relationships are explored in depth.

#### Chapter 6 — Differential Equations

A substantial chapter on ordinary and partial differential equations. First-order ODEs revisited: existence and uniqueness theorems (Picard's theorem on the existence and uniqueness of solutions y' = f(x,y), y(x₀) = y₀, proved by successive approximations). Linear second-order ODEs: the Wronskian and the method of reduction of order. Series solutions of differential equations: the method of Frobenius, with Bessel functions J_n(x) = Σ (−1)ᵏ (x/2)ⁿ⁺²ᵏ/(k! Γ(n+k+1)) arising as solutions to x²y'' + xy' + (x² − n²)y = 0. Legendre's equation (1−x²)y'' − 2xy' + n(n+1)y = 0 and its polynomial solutions P_n(x). Sturm-Liouville theory: eigenvalues and eigenfunctions, orthogonality of eigenfunctions, and expansion in eigenfunctions (the natural generalization of Fourier series). Partial differential equations: the heat equation, the wave equation, and Laplace's equation ∇²u = 0 are solved by separation of variables, building on the Fourier methods of Volume I. The method of characteristics for first-order PDEs is treated. The supplement discusses nonlinear differential equations, including the pendulum equation d²θ/dt² + (g/L) sin θ = 0 and its solution in terms of elliptic functions.

#### Chapter 7 — Calculus of Variations

The most advanced chapter, developing the calculus of variations from first principles. The fundamental problem: find a function y(x) that minimizes (or maximizes) the integral I = ∫ F(x, y, y') dx. The Euler-Lagrange equation: ∂F/∂y − d/dx(∂F/∂y') = 0. Derivation begins with the idea of a variation: replace y(x) by y(x) + ε η(x) where η(x) vanishes at the endpoints, differentiate with respect to ε, set ε=0. Applications: the shortest path between two points (a straight line), the brachistochrone (a cycloid), the minimal surface of revolution (a catenoid). The Hamilton's principle of least action in mechanics: a physical system moves along the path that minimizes the time integral of the Lagrangian L = T − V. Isoperimetric problems: constraints introduced via Lagrange multipliers in variational form. The supplement discusses the second variation and sufficient conditions for a minimum (Jacobi's condition, Legendre's condition), necessary and sufficient for a local minimum.

#### Chapter 8 — Functions of a Complex Variable

Complex numbers reviewed (building on Volume I). Analytic functions: a function f(z) = u(x,y) + iv(x,y) is analytic if it satisfies the Cauchy-Riemann equations: u_x = v_y, u_y = −v_x. The geometric meaning: analytic functions preserve angles (conformal mapping). Examples of analytic functions: polynomials, eᶻ = eˣ(cos y + i sin y), log z, trigonometric functions extended to complex arguments. Complex integration: the line integral of f(z) along a curve in the complex plane. Cauchy's theorem: ∮_C f(z) dz = 0 if f is analytic on and inside the closed curve C. Cauchy's integral formula: f(a) = (1/(2πi)) ∮_C f(z)/(z−a) dz, representing the value of an analytic function inside a contour in terms of its values on the boundary. Taylor series expansions of analytic functions. Laurent series expansions around singularities: f(z) = Σ a_n (z−a)ⁿ where n can be negative. The calculus of residues: ∮ f(z) dz = 2πi Σ Res(f, a_k). Applications: evaluation of real definite integrals via contour integration (e.g., ∫₋∞^∞ dx/(1+x²) = π, and the more challenging ∫₋∞^∞ sin x/x dx = π). Conformal mapping: the mapping w = eᶻ maps the strip 0 < y < π onto the upper half-plane; the Möbius transformation w = (z−a)/(z−b) maps circles and lines to circles and lines. The Riemann mapping theorem is stated: any simply connected region (not the whole plane) can be conformally mapped onto the unit disk. Applications to potential theory (the Dirichlet problem for Laplace's equation) and fluid dynamics (the Joukowski airfoil). The supplement discusses analytic continuation and the concept of Riemann surfaces.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency for Purpose

Courant & John's *Introduction to Calculus and Analysis* is unique: it is simultaneously a calculus textbook, a real analysis text, an introduction to differential equations, a first course in complex analysis, and a physics applications book — all in one seamless two-volume work.

**What it covers well:** Everything in single-variable calculus (rigorously), Fourier series, ordinary differential equations, multivariable calculus (including vector calculus and the integral theorems), an introduction to partial differential equations, the calculus of variations, and complex analysis. The physical motivation and geometric insight are unparalleled.

**What it does not cover:** Modern real analysis (Lebesgue integration, measure theory), functional analysis, abstract algebra, or advanced PDE theory. The treatment of numerical analysis is limited to interpolation and elementary methods.

**Recommended path:** Read Vol I Chapters 1-3 for the core calculus, then Chapter 5 (Taylor expansion) and Chapter 6 (infinite series) are essential — do not skip. Chapter 4 (applications) can be sampled. Chapter 7 (Fourier series) and Chapter 8 (ODEs) are bridges to Vol II. In Volume II, Chapters 1-2 (several variables and linear algebra) are prerequisite for everything else. Chapter 4 (multiple integrals) and Chapter 5 (integral theorems) are the heart of multivariable calculus. Chapter 8 (complex analysis) is a self-contained introduction.

**Chapters to read first:** Vol I Chapters 1-3, then Vol II Chapters 1-2.

**Chapters to skim on a first pass:** Some of the more elaborate physical applications in Vol I Chapter 4 can be returned to later.

**Essential supplements:** The supplement on the existence of the definite integral (Vol I Ch 2), the derivation of Stirling's formula (Vol I Ch 5), and the proof of Euler's sin product (Vol I Ch 6) are masterpieces that repay careful study.
