## Part I: Representation Formulas for Solutions

### Chapter 1. Introduction

Evans opens with a classification of PDE into three fundamental types: elliptic (Laplace's equation $\Delta u = 0$), parabolic (the heat equation $u_t - \Delta u = 0$), and hyperbolic (the wave equation $u_{tt} - \Delta u = 0$). He introduces the notation of multi-indices, partial derivatives, and linear vs. nonlinear equations, and presents a gallery of examples drawn from physics, geometry, and engineering: Laplace's equation (electrostatics, steady-state heat), the heat equation (diffusion), the wave equation (vibrating strings, acoustics), the eikonal equation $|Du| = 1$ (geometric optics), the $p$-Laplacian, the minimal surface equation, the Monge–Ampère equation $\det(D^2 u) = f$, the Hamilton–Jacobi equation, conservation laws, reaction-diffusion equations, the porous medium equation, the Korteweg–de Vries equation, and the Schrödinger equation. The chapter concludes with an overview of the strategies that will be deployed: representation formulas, the method of characteristics, energy estimates, maximum principles, and the theory of weak solutions in Sobolev spaces.

### Chapter 2. Four Important Linear Partial Differential Equations

This chapter derives **explicit representation formulas** for the four most important linear PDE of mathematical physics.

**2.1 Transport equation.** The linear transport equation $u_t + b \cdot Du = 0$ is solved by the method of characteristics: $u(x,t) = g(x - tb)$, where $g$ is the initial condition. Evans treats the non-homogeneous case and shows how characteristics reduce the PDE to ODE along curves.

**2.2 Laplace's equation.** Evans derives the fundamental solution $\Phi(x) = -\frac{1}{2\pi} \log|x|$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ and $\Phi(x) = \frac{1}{n(n-2)\alpha(n)} \frac{1}{|x|^{n-2}}$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ for $n \geq 3$. He proves the mean-value property, the maximum principle (both weak and strong forms), Harnack's inequality, and uniqueness for the Dirichlet problem. The Poisson integral formula gives an explicit representation for the solution on a ball. Liouville's theorem (bounded harmonic functions on $\mathbb{R}^n$ are constant) is proved, along with derivative estimates.

**2.3 Heat equation.** The fundamental solution $\Phi(x,t) = \frac{1}{(4\pi t)^{n/2}} e^{-|x|^2/4t}$ for $t > 0$ is introduced. Evans proves the mean-value property for the heat equation, the maximum principle, and uniqueness for the initial-value problem on $\mathbb{R}^n \times (0,T]$. He derives regularity estimates and shows that solutions are $C^\infty$ for $t > 0$ even when the initial data are merely bounded.

**2.4 Wave equation.** For the wave equation $u_{tt} - \Delta u = 0$, Evans treats spherical means and derives the Kirchhoff formula in $\mathbb{R}^3$, the Poisson formula in $\mathbb{R}^2$ (method of descent), and the d'Alembert formula in $\mathbb{R}^1$. He discusses finite propagation speed, the domain of dependence, Huygens' principle in odd dimensions, and non-homogeneous equations via Duhamel's principle.

**2.5 Reading.** The chapter ends with a table summarizing the representation formulas for all four equations, noting which techniques work for each type and pointing ahead to why more general equations require the theory of weak solutions.

### Chapter 3. Nonlinear First-Order PDE

**3.1 Complete integrals.** Evans introduces the Hamilton–Jacobi equation $F(Du, u, x) = 0$ and the concept of complete integrals — families of solutions depending on parameters.

**3.2 Characteristics for first-order PDE.** The method of characteristics is developed systematically for quasilinear equations $b(Du, u, x) \cdot Du = c(Du, u, x)$ and fully nonlinear equations $F(Du, u, x) = 0$. The characteristic ODE system is derived, and local existence is proved via the inverse function theorem.

**3.3 Examples.** Applications include the eikonal equation $|Du| = 1$, Burgers' equation $u_t + u u_x = 0$ (leading to shock formation), and the Hamilton–Jacobi equation $u_t + H(Du) = 0$. Evans discusses the geometric interpretation of characteristics as curves along which information propagates.

**3.4 Envelopes and shocks.** The formation of singularities is examined: characteristics cross, leading to multivalued solutions, and the correct physical solution is selected by an entropy condition.

### Chapter 4. Other Ways to Represent Solutions

**4.1 Fourier transform.** Evans gives a rapid but thorough treatment of the Fourier transform on $\mathbb{R}^n$, including its action on derivatives and convolutions, the inversion formula, the Plancherel theorem, and the Fourier transform of the fundamental solution of Laplace's equation. The Fourier transform is then used to solve the heat equation and the wave equation, with the wave equation solution expressed in terms of the Fourier transform of the initial data.

**4.2 Similarity solutions.** Scaling arguments and self-similar solutions are introduced for problems such as the porous medium equation.

**4.3 Poisson integral and potential theory.** Newtonian potentials, volume potentials, and single- and double-layer potentials are discussed as tools for solving the Dirichlet and Neumann problems.

**4.4 Separation of variables.** The classical method of separation of variables is presented, leading to eigenvalue problems for the Laplacian on bounded domains and series expansions in eigenfunctions.

## Part II: Theory for Linear Partial Differential Equations

### Chapter 5. Sobolev Spaces

This is the longest and most technically demanding chapter, providing the functional-analytic foundation for the rest of the book.

**5.1 Definitions and basic properties.** Weak derivatives are defined via integration by parts. The Sobolev spaces $W^{k,p}(U)$ are introduced, with $H^k(U) = W^{k,2}(U)$ as the Hilbert space case. Evans proves completeness and discusses approximation by smooth functions (Meyers–Serrin theorem).

**5.2 Extensions and traces.** Extension operators from $W^{1,p}(U)$ to $W^{1,p}(\mathbb{R}^n)$ are constructed for domains with $C^1$ boundary. The trace theorem — the restriction of a Sobolev function to the boundary lies in $L^p(\partial U)$ — is proved, along with the existence of a bounded linear trace operator.

**5.3 Sobolev inequalities.** The core embedding theorems are proved: Gagliardo–Nirenberg–Sobolev inequality ($W^{1,p} \hookrightarrow L^{p^*}$ where $1/p^* = 1/p - 1/n$ for $p < n$), Morrey's inequality ($W^{1,p} \hookrightarrow C^{0,\gamma}$ for $p > n$), the general Sobolev embedding (Rellich–Kondrachov compactness theorem), and special cases ($p = n$ leads to $BMO$).

**5.4 Poincaré inequalities.** Both the standard Poincaré inequality and the Friedrichs inequality are proved, giving control of the $L^p$ norm of a function by the $L^p$ norm of its gradient when the function vanishes on the boundary or has zero mean.

**5.5 Difference quotients.** As a preparation for regularity theory, Evans develops difference quotient techniques and their relation to weak derivatives.

**5.6 Local approximation by smooth functions.** The chapter concludes with techniques for local smoothing via mollification.

### Chapter 6. Second-Order Elliptic Equations

**6.1 Weak solutions.** The Dirichlet problem for a second-order elliptic operator $Lu = - \sum_{i,j} (a^{ij} u_{x_i})_{x_j} + \sum_i b^i u_{x_i} + cu$ is posed variationally. The Lax–Milgram theorem is used to establish existence and uniqueness of weak solutions in $H^1_0(U)$ for uniformly elliptic operators with bounded coefficients.

**6.2 Energy estimates and Fredholm alternative.** Evans proves energy estimates ($\|u\|_{H^1} \leq C\|f\|_{H^{-1}}$) and discusses the Fredholm alternative for elliptic operators, showing that either the problem has a unique solution or the homogeneous problem has nontrivial solutions.

**6.3 Regularity.** Interior regularity ($u \in H^k_{\text{loc}}(U)$ when data are smooth enough), boundary regularity, and $C^\infty$ regularity for equations with smooth coefficients are proved via the method of difference quotients and bootstrapping.

**6.4 Maximum principles.** The weak maximum principle, the strong maximum principle (Hopf's lemma), and the comparison principle are proved. Applications include uniqueness, a priori bounds, and the method of sub- and supersolutions.

**6.5 Eigenvalues.** The eigenvalue problem $-\Delta u = \lambda u$ in $U$ with $u = 0$ on $\partial U$ is studied. Evans proves the existence of a countable set of eigenvalues $\lambda_1 < \lambda_2 \leq \lambda_3 \leq \cdots \to \infty$, with $\lambda_1$ simple and positive, and the corresponding eigenfunctions forming an orthonormal basis of $L^2(U)$.

**6.6 Linear evolution equations.** This section previews the semigroup approach by discussing the connection between elliptic operators and the generation of contraction semigroups on $L^2$.

### Chapter 7. Linear Evolution Equations

**7.1 Parabolic equations.** Evans treats second-order parabolic equations $u_t + Lu = f$ where $L$ is uniformly elliptic. Existence and uniqueness of weak solutions are proved via the Galerkin method. Regularity estimates — including the fundamental parabolic regularity theorem — are established. Maximum principles for parabolic equations are proved, and the smoothing effect (instantaneous $C^\infty$ regularity) is discussed.

**7.2 Hyperbolic equations.** Second-order hyperbolic equations $u_{tt} + Lu = f$ are treated by a similar Galerkin approach. Energy estimates for the wave equation are derived: conservation of energy $E(t) = \frac{1}{2} \int_U (u_t^2 + |Du|^2) \, dx$ for the homogeneous wave equation with zero boundary conditions. Finite speed of propagation is proved via the energy method.

**7.3 Semigroup theory.** Evans develops the Hille–Yosida theorem and its application to parabolic equations. The solution operator $S(t) = e^{-tL}$ is constructed as a $C_0$ semigroup of contractions on $L^2(U)$, and the abstract Cauchy problem $u' + Lu = 0$, $u(0) = g$ is solved. Analytic semigroups and their smoothing properties are discussed.

## Part III: Theory for Nonlinear Partial Differential Equations

### Chapter 8. The Calculus of Variations

**8.1 Direct method.** Evans introduces the calculus of variations as a tool for nonlinear elliptic PDE: minimize $I[w] = \int_U L(Dw, w, x) \, dx$ over an admissible class. The direct method is presented: take a minimizing sequence, show it is bounded in $H^1$, extract a weakly convergent subsequence, and prove lower semicontinuity to show the limit is a minimizer.

**8.2 Euler–Lagrange equations.** The first variation gives the Euler–Lagrange equation; for $L(Du) = \frac{1}{2}|Du|^2 + F(u)$, this is $-\Delta u + f(u) = 0$. Evans discusses natural boundary conditions and the variational meaning of the Poisson equation.

**8.3 Constraints and Lagrange multipliers.** Minimization with constraints leads to eigenvalue problems (e.g., minimizing $\int |Du|^2$ subject to $\int u^2 = 1$ gives the first eigenfunction of $-\Delta$). The Lagrange multiplier rule is explained.

**8.4 Regularity of minimizers.** The De Giorgi–Nash–Moser theory is summarized: minimizers of convex functionals with $L(Du) = \frac{1}{2}|Du|^2$ are $C^\infty$ in the interior. The section includes the De Giorgi regularity theorem that bounded weak solutions of linear elliptic equations in divergence form are Hölder continuous.

**8.5 Monotonicity and convexity.** The role of convexity of $L$ in $p$ for lower semicontinuity and regularity is explored, with the notion of polyconvexity and rank-one convexity mentioned for vector-valued problems.

### Chapter 9. Nonvariational Techniques

When the PDE does not arise from a variational principle, alternative methods are needed.

**9.1 Fixed-point methods.** The Schauder fixed-point theorem is applied to prove existence of solutions for semilinear elliptic equations $-\Delta u = f(u)$.

**9.2 Degree theory.** Leray–Schauder degree and its application to proving existence: for a family of equations depending on a parameter, if a priori estimates hold at one end and the degree is nonzero, solutions exist.

**9.3 Monotonicity methods.** For operators satisfying $\langle Au - Av, u - v \rangle \geq 0$, Evans proves existence via the Browder–Minty theorem. Applications include the $p$-Laplacian.

**9.4 The Nash–Moser iteration.** This powerful technique for obtaining regularity is introduced, showing how $L^p$ estimates can be iterated to obtain $L^\infty$ bounds (the Moser iteration), with applications to boundedness of weak solutions of semilinear equations.

### Chapter 10. Hamilton–Jacobi Equations

**10.1 Viscosity solutions.** Evans presents the theory of viscosity solutions for first-order Hamilton–Jacobi equations $u_t + H(Du, x) = 0$. The definition uses test functions touching the solution from above and below; the key properties are stability, uniqueness, and the comparison principle. Existence is proved via the vanishing viscosity method $u^\varepsilon_t + H(Du^\varepsilon, x) = \varepsilon \Delta u^\varepsilon$ and passing to the limit.

**10.2 Lax–Oleinik formula.** For $H(Du) = \frac{1}{2}|Du|^2$, the explicit solution formula $u(x,t) = \min_y \{ g(y) + \frac{|x-y|^2}{2t} \}$ is derived and shown to satisfy the Hamilton–Jacobi equation in the viscosity sense.

**10.3 Weak KAM theory.** The connection with dynamical systems is sketched: viscosity solutions of the stationary Hamilton–Jacobi equation $H(Du, x) = c$ are related to invariant tori in Hamiltonian mechanics.

**10.4 Regularizing effects.** Despite the lack of classical smoothness, viscosity solutions are Lipschitz continuous if the Hamiltonian is coercive, and further regularity holds under convexity assumptions.

### Chapter 11. Systems of Conservation Laws

**11.1 Scalar conservation laws.** For $u_t + F(u)_x = 0$, Evans develops the theory of shock waves: Rankine–Hugoniot jump condition, entropy conditions (Lax's entropy condition, Oleinik's E-condition), and the existence of a unique entropy solution via the vanishing viscosity method.

**11.2 Riemann problem.** The Riemann problem — a conservation law with piecewise constant initial data — is solved explicitly. Rarefaction waves and shock waves are constructed, and the entropy condition selects the physically correct solution.

**11.3 Systems in one space dimension.** Evans treats $2 \times 2$ systems and discusses genuinely nonlinear fields, linearly degenerate fields, and the wave breakdown phenomenon. The Glimm scheme (random choice method) for constructing solutions is described.

**11.4 Entropy.** The concept of entropy pairs $(\eta, q)$ where $\eta(u)_t + q(u)_x \leq 0$ in the sense of distributions is introduced. The Lax entropy inequality for shock waves is proved, and the connection with the second law of thermodynamics is explained.

### Chapter 12. Nonlinear Wave Equations

**12.1 Local existence.** For $u_{tt} - \Delta u + f(u) = 0$, Evans proves local-in-time existence of smooth solutions via energy estimates and the contraction mapping principle. The lifespan of solutions depends on the size of the initial data.

**12.2 Finite-time blow-up.** For focusing nonlinearities $f(u) = |u|^{p-1}u$, solutions can blow up in finite time. Evans proves the blow-up criterion via concavity arguments (the Levine–Payne–Todorova method): if the initial energy is negative, the solution cannot exist for all time.

**12.3 Global existence for small data.** For $f(u) = |u|^{p-1}u$ with $p$ larger than the Strauss exponent, small initial data lead to global solutions. The proof uses the decay estimates for the linear wave equation and a bootstrap argument.

**12.4 Wave maps and geometric wave equations.** The chapter closes with a discussion of wave maps $u: \mathbb{R}^{1+n} \to M$ (maps from Minkowski space into a Riemannian manifold), showing how the wave equation extends to curved targets and noting the importance of these equations in general relativity and gauge theory.

## Reading Guide

Evans' book is designed as a graduate text and should be read sequentially, at least for the first six chapters. The dependency flow is: Chapter 1 (motivation) → Chapter 2 (explicit formulas for linear PDE) → Chapter 5 (Sobolev spaces, the functional-analytic core) → Chapter 6 (elliptic equations, the central chapter) → Chapter 7 (evolution equations). Part III (Chapters 8–12) can be taken in any order once Chapters 5–6 are mastered.

**For a first course (one semester):** Chapters 1, 2, 5, 6, 7, and one selection from 8–10.

**For a topics course in nonlinear PDE:** Chapters 8–12, with frequent reference back to Chapters 5–6.

**Suggested reading path for self-study:** Read Chapter 1 for orientation. Skip Chapter 3 on first pass (nonlinear first-order PDE is somewhat independent). Go through Chapters 2 and 4 quickly to build intuition. Invest heavily in Chapter 5 — Sobolev spaces are the language in which all modern PDE is written. Then proceed through 6, 7, and selected topics from Part III.
