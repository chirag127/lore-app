Lawrence C. Evans' *Partial Differential Equations* (1998, AMS Graduate Studies in Mathematics Vol. 19) is the standard graduate text in the field. The book is organized into three parts: **Part I** (Chapters 1–4) develops representation formulas for solutions of the classical PDE — the transport equation, Laplace's equation, the heat equation, and the wave equation — along with the method of characteristics and the Fourier transform. **Part II** (Chapters 5–7) builds the theory of linear PDE through Sobolev spaces, second-order elliptic regularity, and semigroup methods for parabolic and hyperbolic evolution equations. **Part III** (Chapters 8–12) treats nonlinear PDE: the calculus of variations, fixed-point and degree-theoretic methods, viscosity solutions of Hamilton–Jacobi equations, conservation laws with shock waves, and nonlinear wave equations.

Evans' approach is characterized by its systematic use of **energy estimates** and **Sobolev-space techniques** throughout. Rather than presenting PDE as a collection of disconnected tricks, he develops a unified apparatus — weak derivatives, Sobolev embeddings, compactness, a priori estimates — that applies across the entire spectrum of equations. The book also includes five appendices covering notation, inequalities, calculus facts, functional analysis, and measure theory.

Since publication, the book has become the canonical reference. David Jerison of MIT has called it essential reading for every graduate student in analysis, and *SIAM Review* praised it as "extremely well written." The second edition (2010, 9780821849743) added substantial new material on measure-valued solutions, optimal transport, and the Nash–Moser iteration, but the 1998 first edition remains the definitive statement of the modern PDE curriculum.
