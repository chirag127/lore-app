*Visual Complex Analysis* is a radical reimagining of how complex analysis is taught and understood. First published in 1997 and winner of first prize in the National Jesuit Book Award Competition, Tristan Needham's masterpiece develops the entire subject from a purely geometric perspective. There are no epsilons to be found. Instead, the complex derivative is introduced as an **amplitwist** — a local combination of amplification (dilation) and twist (rotation) — and every subsequent result flows from this single geometric insight.

The book features over 500 diagrams, many of them original and beautiful, that replace pages of algebraic manipulation. With a foreword by Roger Penrose — who calls it "a delight, and a book after my own heart" — Needham's work bridges the gap between the physical intuition that originally motivated complex analysis (fluid dynamics, electromagnetism, celestial mechanics) and the rigorous mathematical theory. It is the ideal companion to more formal treatments like Ahlfors, and a revelation for anyone who has ever wondered what complex numbers *really mean*.
