## Strengths

- **The right scope for the hardest gap in ML.** Most ML practitioners
  never took a linear algebra class designed for CS students, never did
  rigorous probability, and never derived the chain rule in more than two
  dimensions. This book fills that gap in a single pass that most other
  resources cannot match.

- **Four pillars, one notation.** Books on linear algebra (Strang),
  probability (Grimmett & Stirzaker), and optimization (Boyd &
  Vandenberghe) each have their own notation, their own conventions, and
  their own pedagogical style. Deisenroth, Faisal, and Ong normalize all
  of it into the notation that ML papers actually use. A reader who works
  through this book can pick up a NeurIPS paper and read the math without
  translation.

- **Chapter 5 is worth the price alone.** The formal derivation of
  backpropagation as a chain rule on a computation graph is one of the
  clearest treatments in any textbook. The authors do not wave at it —
  they derive it, and the reader who follows the derivation will never
  be confused by "autograd" again.

- **The worked-examples chapter is the payoff.** The free PDF chapter
  that derives linear regression, PCA, and Gaussian process regression
  from first principles is the proof-of-concept for the entire book.
  Each derivation calls exactly one tool from each of the four pillars
  in sequence. The result is a feeling of "oh, *that* is what
  backpropagation, eigendecomposition, and the normal equations have in
  common" — which no other book delivers.

- **UCL pedigree, adopted widely.** The book grew from a graduate course
  at the UCL Centre for AI. That pedigree shows: the pacing, the
  exercise selection, the level of rigor, and the professor-side care
  are all evident. It is now used at ETH Zürich, TU Munich, Shanghai
  Jiao Tong, and dozens of other programs.

- **Freely available PDF.** The expanded draft with the extra
  worked-examples chapter is at `mml-book.github.io`. No paywall for
  the content. The CUP hardcover is for people who want a physical
  reference, but the primary text is free to study.

- **Strong reader reception.** 4.5+ stars on Goodreads and Amazon
  across hundreds of reviews. Among people who have actually used it,
  the consensus is near-unanimous: it is the reference most ML
  practitioners reach for first.

- **Authorship breadth.** Having three authors rather than one produces
  a book with no single-person blind spots. Deisenroth brings robotics
  and control; Faisal brings neuroscience and probabilistic modeling;
  Ong brings signal processing and optimization. The three perspectives
  are visible in the choice of examples.

---

## Weaknesses

- **Not book-shaped gentle.** Chapter 2 begins with proofs. There are
  no warm introductions to notation or motivation before the theorems.
  A reader who has not seen a proof in years will feel the cold wind of
  rigor immediately. This is the correct choice for the book's stated
  goal (make the reader fluent in reading ML papers), but it is a
  barrier for practitioners whose last exposure to math was undergraduate
  calculus.

- **No modern ML coverage.** The book predates and deliberately avoids
  the transformer era. There is no attention mechanism, no diffusion
  model, no neural ODE, no normalizing flow. The ML examples are linear
  regression, PCA, and Gaussian processes — clean but dated for
  practitioners whose daily work is deep learning.

- **Exercises are not uniformly good.** The first third of the book has
  strong, well-motivated exercises. The probability chapter, by
  contrast, has several "prove the following identity" exercises that
  test algebraic facility without building intuition.

- **SVD treatment is shorter than it should be.** Given how central
  SVD is to dimensionality reduction, collaborative filtering, and
  image compression, the chapter devotes half the space to
  eigendecomposition that SVD deserves. The Eckart–Young theorem is
  stated without proof.

- **Limited treatment of kernels and function spaces.** The book
  introduces kernels only in the context of Gaussian processes. The
  kernel trick as a general technique — the bridge that connects linear
  algebra to the infinite-dimensional function spaces behind SVMs, RKHS
  theory, and deep kernel learning — is not developed separately.

- **Variable quality of the worked examples.** The linear regression and
  PCA derivations are excellent. The Gaussian process derivation is
  elegant but assumes more background than the earlier chapters have
  provided a reader who has not seen GP literature before.

- **The applied-free style can backfire.** Some readers work through all
  seven chapters and then struggle to translate the results into
  anything that runs in PyTorch or scikit-learn. The book to pair
  with this one — to close the code gap — is
  *Hands-On Machine Learning with Scikit-Learn, Keras & TensorFlow*
  (Géron) or *Designing Machine Learning Systems* (Huyen).
