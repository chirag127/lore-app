## Introduction

Welcome to BookAtlas. Tonight: *Mathematics for Machine Learning* by
Marc Peter Deisenroth, A. Aldo Faisal, and Cheng Soon Ong. Second
edition, 2023, Cambridge University Press, roughly 392 pages. If the
first edition was already the standard mathematical reference for the
ML community, the second edition refines the presentation based on five
years of classroom use at UCL, ETH, and beyond.

We have two voices tonight. On one side, a research scientist who uses
this book as their primary reference every week. On the other, an ML
engineer who came through software engineering and bootcamps and only
encountered the math much later. We have a lot of ground to cover.
Let us begin.

---

## What This Book Is and What It Is Not

**Research Scientist:** I want to be precise about what this book
delivers and what it deliberately omits. It is four subjects in one
volume: linear algebra, analytic geometry, matrix decompositions and
probability theory, organized with a consistent notation and a single
pedagogical arc. It is not a deep learning textbook. It is not a
programming book. It is the math you are assumed to already know when
you open a deep learning book — written out, in full, with no hand
waving.

**ML Engineer:** That distinction is why I bought it. I had been using
PyTorch for a year and realized that when a paper said "the Hessian of
the loss at this critical point has mixed eigenvalues," I did not know
what that meant — not how to compute it, not what mixed eigenvalues
told me about the geometry of the loss surface, not why that mattered
for training.

**Research Scientist:** That is exactly the reader the authors had in
mind. They write in the preface: "We wrote this book because the
mathematical foundations of machine learning are increasingly important
to practitioners, yet they are taught in disconnected fragments." The
whole point of this book is to re-unify those fragments.

---

## The Four Pillars in One Arc

**ML Engineer:** The structure surprised me. I expected a的概率 textbook,
then a linear algebra textbook, then an optimization textbook — three
separate subjects stapled together. What I got instead was a sequence
where each chapter requires the previous ones as active prerequisites.

**Research Scientist:** Yes. Chapter 2 is linear algebra — vectors,
matrices, systems of equations. Chapter 3 adds geometry: norms measure
size, inner products measure angles, projections measure the component
of one vector along another. Chapter 4 uses the geometry to factor
matrices in ways that reveal structure: eigendecomposition, singular
value decomposition.

**ML Engineer:** By the time you reach Chapter 5, the gradient, you
have seen everything the gradient is made of. The partial derivative
operates on one coordinate at a time. The Jacobian generalizes the
gradient to vector-valued functions. The Hessian captures curvature.
The chain rule is the reason backpropagation works. The book does not
announce "now we will derive backpropagation" — it builds toward that
derivation over four chapters of machinery.

**Research Scientist:** The chain rule derivation in Chapter 5 is the
most important page in the book. The authors write it out explicitly:
the Jacobian of a composition is the product of the Jacobians. That
single sentence is the mathematical content of every backward pass in
every deep learning framework ever written.

## Where the Book Actually Goes

**ML Engineer:** I want to say something honest about what caught me
off guard. Some of the chapters in the middle of the book — the
probability chapter, the optimization chapter — are genuinely more
rigorous than I expected. I have taken probability classes. I have read
blog posts on Bayesian inference. The book's treatment of Bayes' rule
is more careful than anything I encountered in my ML bootcamp.

**Research Scientist:** That care is the point. The authors are not
writing a "math for people who find math scary" book. They are writing
a book that, when you are reading the Gauss–Newton section of a Gaussian
process paper at two in the morning, you can open Chapter 6 and find
the derivation written out. This book rewards the reader who is willing
to work through the math.

**ML Engineer:** And the worked examples. The free PDF has a chapter
that derives linear regression, PCA, and Gaussian process regression
using only the tools from the earlier chapters. After seven chapters of
abstract math, reading those derivations and recognizing every step
— "oh, that is where the normal equations come from" — is genuinely
moving. I cannot remember the last time a textbook made me feel that
way.

**Research Scientist:** The three ML examples are deliberately chosen.
Linear regression is the simplest problem where every pillar plays a
role simultaneously. PCA is the simplest problem where eigendecomposition
is the right tool. Gaussian process regression is the simplest problem
where all three ingredients — prior, likelihood, posterior — interact.
The book could have used more modern examples, but these three are
structurally perfect.

## Who Should Read This Book

**Research Scientist:** If you are a graduate student starting an ML
program, this is almost certainly the book your advisor will recommend.
Open it now, not in your second semester when you are trying to read
a paper and missing half the notation.

**ML Engineer:** If you use PyTorch, scikit-learn, or any framework,
and you have ever read a paper or a documentation page that says "the
gradient descent converges when the Hessian is positive definite"
without explaining what that means — this book will be more valuable
than ten more programming books.

**ML Engineer:** And if you are self-taught, from a bootcamp or
equivalent, and your math has gaps — gaps you feel every time you read
a research paper or a library documentation page that assumes
linear algebra — this is the book that closes those gaps. It is hard
work. It takes time. But it is unimpeachably correct and efficient.

---

## What to Watch For

**Research Scientist:** A fair accounting has to include the limitations.
The book deliberately avoids transformers, attention, diffusion models,
flow-based models — the entire post-2017 landscape. It is not that the
authors could not have included them. It is that the math behind those
models is a superset of what the book covers, and the authors chose
completeness and coherence over chronological comprehensiveness.

**ML Engineer:** The exercises are also worth saying something about.
The first two chapters have excellent exercises. The later chapters
have exercises that sometimes test algebraic facility more than
intuition. I recommend working the exercises in Chapters 2 and 3,
reading carefully through Chapters 4 and 5, and using Chapters 6 and 7
primarily to build intuition through the worked examples rather than
through exercises alone.

**Research Scientist:** And one more thing. The book is rigorous, but
it is not self-contained in the way a pure math textbook is. It assumes
you know what a proof is. It assumes you are comfortable with set
notation and basic real analysis. The appendices provide brief reviews,
but if you have not studied math seriously since high school, you will
need to supplement with a gentler linear algebra resource — Strang's
*Introduction to Linear Algebra* or the 3Blue1Brown video series — for
the intuition before you tackle this book's rigor.

**ML Engineer:** My honest advice for practitioners: open to Chapter 5.
Read the backpropagation derivation. Then read Chapter 6 on probability.
Then the worked examples. If you come out of that understanding why
gradient descent converges, why PCA isolates variance, and why Bayesian
methods naturally regularize — you have what you need, and the rest of
the book will deepen it.

## Final Word

**Research Scientist:** This is not a book you read casually. It is a
book you work through. It will take time. It will require pen and paper.
It will occasionally make you feel like you are back in undergraduate
analysis. But when you finish it — or even when you finish Chapter 5 —
you will have a mathematical fluency that most ML practitioners spend
their entire careers without attaining, or only acquire by piecing
together fragments from a dozen disparate sources.

**ML Engineer:** And the free PDF means you can start tonight without
spending anything. The authors put the content out there because they
genuinely believe it should be accessible. That belief shows in every
page.

**Research Scientist:** *Mathematics for Machine Learning*, second
edition, 2023. Required reading for anyone serious about understanding
why machine learning works — or doesn't — from the math up.
