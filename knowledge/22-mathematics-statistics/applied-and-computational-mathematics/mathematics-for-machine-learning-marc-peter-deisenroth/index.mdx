## Overview

*Mathematics for Machine Learning* (2020) by Marc Peter Deisenroth, A. Aldo
Faisal, and Cheng Soon Ong is the most respected one-volume mathematical
foundation for modern machine learning. Published by Cambridge University
Press, ~417 pages, and freely available as a PDF, the book distills the
four mathematical pillars of ML — linear algebra, analytic geometry, matrix
decompositions, vector calculus, probability, and continuous optimization —
into a coherent narrative designed for readers who want to understand ML
from first principles.

The authors are all researchers at University College London (UCL), and
the book grew out of a graduate course they taught there. Their goal is
narrow but ambitious: give a mathematically literate reader the tools
needed to read and write modern ML papers, without the disconnected
fragmentation of a typical undergraduate curriculum.

## The Four Pillars

The book is organized around the two parts of the published edition. The
freely available PDF on `mml-book.github.io` is an expanded draft with
additional worked examples and a chapter on linear regression that the
CUP edition omits for length.

| Part | Chapter | Topic | Why It Matters |
|------|---------|-------|----------------|
| I | 2 | Linear Algebra | Vectors, matrices, systems of equations, the language of data |
| I | 3 | Analytic Geometry | Norms, inner products, orthogonal projections, geometric intuition |
| I | 4 | Matrix Decompositions | Eigen, SVD, Cholesky — the structure-revealing operations |
| II | 5 | Vector Calculus | Gradients, Jacobians, Hessians, the chain rule for backpropagation |
| II | 6 | Probability & Statistics | Random variables, Bayes' rule, MLE, MAP, expectation |
| II | 7 | Continuous Optimization | Gradient descent, Newton's method, constrained problems |

## Why This Book Exists

Before this book, learning the math for ML meant stitching together
Strang for linear algebra, a probability textbook, a calculus reference,
and a separate optimization text — none of them written for ML
practitioners. The notation, the worked examples, and the motivating
problems all came from other fields.

Deisenroth, Faisal, and Ong unified these four subjects around a single
question: *what mathematics do you need to understand why a neural
network trains, why PCA works, why Bayesian methods regularize, and why
gradient descent converges?* The answer is a tight ~400-page book that
covers the essential toolkit without padding or rabbit holes.

A defining feature: the book is application-free until the very end. No
neural networks. No classifiers. No datasets. Just the math. This is a
deliberate pedagogical choice — the authors believe the math must stand
on its own, and only then can it be applied to ML problems with
understanding rather than pattern-matching.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Vectors and matrices are not abstractions.** They are the literal
   data structures flowing through every model. Understanding how a
   matrix multiplies a vector is understanding how a linear layer
   transforms features.

2. **Eigendecomposition and SVD reveal structure.** Eigenvectors are the
   directions in which a linear transformation acts by pure scaling; SVD
   generalizes this to non-square matrices and powers dimensionality
   reduction, recommender systems, and low-rank approximation.

3. **The gradient is the engine of learning.** The gradient of a loss
   function points in the direction of steepest ascent; subtracting it
   is the simplest learning rule. The Jacobian generalizes the gradient
   to vector-valued functions and is the workhorse of backpropagation.

4. **Probability handles uncertainty.** Real-world data is noisy, models
   are approximate, and predictions are uncertain. Random variables,
   Bayes' rule, and likelihood-based estimation are the framework for
   reasoning about what we don't know.

5. **Optimization is where learning happens.** Training a model means
   finding parameters that minimize a loss. Gradient descent, stochastic
   variants, Newton's method, and constrained optimization are the tools
   that make this tractable.

6. **Convex problems are easy; non-convex ones are interesting.** Linear
   regression is convex and has a closed-form solution. Deep learning is
   non-convex and requires sophisticated optimization. The book explains
   both regimes and the theory that distinguishes them.

7. **The math is interconnected.** Linear regression is simultaneously
   a linear algebra problem (solving Ax = b), a calculus problem
   (minimizing ||Ax - b||²), a probability problem (maximum likelihood
   under Gaussian noise), and an optimization problem (gradient descent
   on a convex loss). The book deliberately shows these connections.

## Who Should Read

| Reader Type | Why |
|---|---|
| ML grad students | The book most advisors recommend as a first-year reference |
| Self-taught ML engineers | Fills the math gaps that library tutorials leave behind |
| Researchers entering ML from physics, engineering, statistics | Provides the conventions and notation of the ML community |
| Data scientists needing rigor | Builds the foundation for understanding papers, not just APIs |
| Educators | The book is structured as a one-semester course with exercises |

---

## Who Should Skip

- Anyone looking for hands-on ML tutorials — the book has exactly one
  application chapter (worked examples in the free PDF)
- Practitioners seeking production deep learning recipes — the book
  predates the transformer era and contains no architecture coverage
- Readers uncomfortable with mathematical notation and derivations — the
  book is dense, formal, and requires paper-and-pen work
- Statisticians who already have graduate-level probability and linear
  algebra will find much of the content survey-level
- Anyone needing a quick read — this is a textbook, not a popular
  exposition

---

## Final Verdict

*Mathematics for Machine Learning* is the best single-volume mathematical
foundation for modern ML. It is not a deep learning book, not a coding
book, and not a research survey. It is the book you read *before* those
books — or alongside them — to make the derivations, the proofs, and the
algorithmic choices intelligible rather than mysterious.

The book's greatest strength is its restraint. It covers exactly the
math you need for ML, in the notation ML uses, with the motivating
examples ML produces. It doesn't try to be a complete linear algebra
text, a complete probability text, or a complete optimization text. It
is a curated bridge between undergraduate mathematics and graduate ML
research.

**Rating: 9.0/10** — The standard mathematical foundation for ML.
Essential for serious practitioners and graduate students.
