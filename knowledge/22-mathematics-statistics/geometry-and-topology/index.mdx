# Geometry and Topology

## Why This Subcategory Exists

Geometry and topology study the properties of space and shape. Geometry asks: What is the structure of space? What are the relationships between lengths, angles, areas, and volumes? Topology asks a more fundamental question: What properties of a space remain unchanged when it is continuously deformed — stretched, twisted, or bent, but not torn or glued? A coffee cup and a doughnut are "the same" to a topologist (both have one hole), but different to a geometer (their curvatures differ).

Geometry is one of the oldest branches of mathematics. Euclid's *Elements* (c. 300 BCE) systematized plane geometry through five axioms, the most controversial being the parallel postulate: given a line and a point not on it, exactly one line through the point is parallel to the original line. For two millennia, mathematicians tried to prove this from the other four axioms. The eventual realization (by Gauss, Bolyai, and Lobachevsky, independently around 1830) that the parallel postulate is *independent* — that consistent geometries exist where it fails — was one of the most profound discoveries in the history of thought. It showed that mathematics studies *possible* structures, not just the structure of physical space.

Riemann's *Habilitationsschrift* (1854) generalized geometry further, introducing Riemannian manifolds — spaces equipped with a metric that allows measurement of lengths and angles, but whose curvature can vary from point to point. Einstein used Riemannian geometry (specifically, pseudo-Riemannian geometry) to formulate general relativity: gravity is not a force but the curvature of spacetime.

Topology emerged as a distinct discipline in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, through the work of Poincaré, Brouwer, and others. Algebraic topology assigns algebraic invariants (groups, rings) to topological spaces, allowing topological questions to be answered by algebraic computation. The fundamental group, homology groups, and cohomology rings are the basic tools. The classification of compact surfaces (every such surface is a sphere, a connected sum of tori, or a connected sum of projective planes) was one of the first great achievements.

Differential geometry combines calculus with geometry, studying smooth surfaces and manifolds using the tools of analysis. The Gauss-Bonnet theorem connects the total curvature of a surface to its Euler characteristic (a topological invariant), providing a deep link between local geometry and global topology. The Ricci flow, used by Perelman to prove the Poincaré conjecture (2003), is a differential equation that deforms a manifold's metric, smoothing out its geometry to reveal its topology.

This subcategory holds books on Euclidean geometry, non-Euclidean geometry (hyperbolic, elliptic), projective geometry, differential geometry (curves, surfaces, manifolds), Riemannian geometry, algebraic topology (fundamental group, homology, cohomology), geometric topology (knot theory, 3-manifolds), and the history of geometric thought.

## Why This Is NOT Merged Into Other Subcategories

**Distinct from Algebra and Number Theory:** While algebraic geometry (the study of geometric objects defined by polynomial equations) bridges both fields, the core of geometry and topology — metric properties, curvature, continuous deformation — is fundamentally distinct from the study of abstract algebraic structures. A book on Riemann surfaces belongs here; a book on group representations belongs in Algebra.

**Distinct from Analysis and Calculus:** While differential geometry uses calculus as a tool, its goals (understanding curvature, geodesics, topological invariants) are geometric, not analytic. A book on manifold theory belongs here; a book on Fourier analysis belongs in Analysis.

**Distinct from Foundations:** The geometric axioms (Euclid's postulates, their alternatives) are mathematical structures to be studied, not foundational questions about the nature of mathematical proof.

## What Belongs Here

Books about Euclidean geometry, non-Euclidean geometry (hyperbolic, elliptic, projective), differential geometry of curves and surfaces, Riemannian geometry, algebraic topology (fundamental group, homology, cohomology), geometric topology, knot theory, geometric group theory, symplectic geometry, and the history of geometry.

## Does NOT Belong Here

- Algebraic schemes and varieties → Algebra and Number Theory (when the emphasis is algebraic)
- Numerical geometry / computational geometry → Applied & Computational Mathematics (Category 02)

## Essential Reading: The Most Important Books

1. *Elements* — Euclid (c. 300 BCE) — The foundational text of geometry; axiomatic method applied to plane and solid geometry
2. *Geometry: Euclid and Beyond* — Robin Hartshorne (2000) — Modern treatment of Euclidean and non-Euclidean geometry using Hilbert's axioms
3. *Euclid's Window* — Leonard Mlodinow (2001) — The history of geometry from Euclid to general relativity, accessible to general readers
4. *The Shape of Space* — Jeffrey Weeks (1985) — An intuitive introduction to topology and the geometry of surfaces and 3-manifolds
5. *Differential Geometry of Curves and Surfaces* — Manfredo do Carmo (1976) — The standard undergraduate text; curvature, geodesics, and the Gauss-Bonnet theorem
6. *Riemannian Manifolds: An Introduction to Curvature* — John Lee (1997) — A gentle introduction to Riemannian geometry
7. *Riemannian Geometry* — Manfredo do Carmo (1992) — A more comprehensive treatment for graduate students
8. *A Comprehensive Introduction to Differential Geometry* — Michael Spivak (1970–1975) — Five volumes; the bible of differential geometry, historically informed and deeply pedagogical
9. *Geometry, Topology and Physics* — Mikio Nakahara (1990) — Bridges pure mathematics and theoretical physics; fiber bundles, connections, characteristic classes
10. *Topology* — James Munkres (1975) — The standard reference for point-set topology and introduction to algebraic topology
11. *Algebraic Topology* — Allen Hatcher (2002) — The standard graduate text; fundamental group, homology, cohomology (freely available)
12. *Differential Forms in Algebraic Topology* — Raoul Bott & Loring Tu (1982) — De Rham cohomology and characteristic classes; bridges differential geometry and algebraic topology
13. *Knots and Links* — Dale Rolfsen (1976) — The introduction to knot theory that launched the modern subject
14. *An Introduction to Knot Theory* — W.B.R. Lickorish (1997) — A more modern treatment including the Jones polynomial
15. *Three-Dimensional Geometry and Topology* — William Thurston (1997) — The revolutionary text on 3-manifolds; geometrization conjecture (proved by Perelman)
16. *The Poincaré Conjecture* — Donal O'Shea (2007) — The history and mathematics of the most famous problem in topology
17. *The Road to Reality* — Roger Penrose (2004) — Chapters on geometry and topology in physics; requires mathematical maturity
18. *Flatland* — Edwin Abbott (1884) — A fictional exploration of dimensions that introduced millions to non-Euclidean thinking
19. *Experience of Geometry* — David Henderson & Taimina Daina (2004) — Hands-on approach to hyperbolic geometry
20. *Visual Complex Analysis* (topology chapters) — Tristan Needham — Geometric intuition for topological concepts in complex analysis
21. *Geometry and the Imagination* — Hilbert & Cohn-Vossen (1932) — A visual tour of geometry and topology by one of the great mathematicians
22. *A Panoramic View of Riemannian Geometry* — Marcel Berger (2003) — An encyclopedic survey of Riemannian geometry by a master
23. *The Wild World of 4-Manifolds* — Alexandru Scorpan (2005) — The exotic smooth structures on 4-dimensional Euclidean space
24. *Differential Topology* — Victor Guillemin & Alan Pollack (1974) — An elegant introduction to smooth manifolds, transversality, and intersection theory
25. *Topology from the Differentiable Viewpoint* — John Milnor (1965) — A concise masterpiece; the hairy ball theorem, Sard's theorem, and degrees of maps in 100 pages

## How to Approach This Subcategory

1. **Start with Euclid and non-Euclidean geometry.** Read Euclid's *Elements* (or Hartshorne's modern treatment) and then explore hyperbolic geometry through Weeks's *The Shape of Space* or Henderson & Daina's *Experiencing Geometry*.

2. **Learn point-set topology.** Munkres's *Topology* Part I covers the basics: topological spaces, compactness, connectedness, metric spaces.

3. **Study differential geometry.** Do Carmo's *Differential Geometry of Curves and Surfaces* for intuition, then Lee's *Riemannian Manifolds* for the abstract theory.

4. **Proceed to algebraic topology.** Hatcher's *Algebraic Topology* is the standard; it requires point-set topology as a prerequisite. Focus on the fundamental group and singular homology first.

5. **Specialize.** Depending on interest: knot theory (Rolfsen, Lickorish), 3-manifolds (Thurston), Riemannian geometry (Spivak, do Carmo), or topology in physics (Nakahara).
