## Strengths

- **Narrative architecture that honors the material**. Strogatz has organized a 2,000-year intellectual history around three pillars (slicing, gluing, changing) that are simultaneously his pedagogical device and a genuine structural insight about calculus. The framework is not imposed — it emerges from the mathematics itself, and it makes the diversity of applications feel unified rather than ad hoc.

- **The Archimedes chapter is among the finest popular mathematics writing in existence**. Strogatz takes you inside the method of exhaustion with such care — drawing the inscribed and circumscribed polygons, showing the bounds tightening on pi — that a reader with no mathematical background can feel the intellectual drama of the moment. No other book makes this ancient mathematics feel as alive.

- **Newton as human, not genius-icon**. Strogatz's Newton is a 22-year-old in plague quarantine, writing 18 months' worth of revolutionary mathematics in private, simultaneously inventing calculus and the theory of gravitation. The age, the setting, the simultaneity — these details make the achievement feel accessible without diminishing it. Newton is not deified; he is rendered as unusually capable.

- **The Leibniz-Newton priority dispute handled with genuine fairness**. Strogatz takes a clear-eyed view of a conflict that historians have often treated as evidence of Newton's superiority. He acknowledges that Leibniz published first with superior notation, that Newton's geometric approach was more physically grounded but harder to extend, and that the resulting English–Continental schism harmed mathematics in Britain for a century.

- **GPS as the modern-world calculus moment**. The chapter connecting GPS to general relativity is the book's most effective "why this matters" argument. Strogatz shows that a 38-microsecond-per-day relativistic correction — computed via differential equations — is the difference between your phone knowing your location and being wrong by 10 kilometers daily. The math that Archimedes started in ancient Syracuse is now running inside your pocket every second.

- **Written with genuine warmth and no condescension**. Strogatz's voice is the rare one that speaks to a mathematically anxious reader without being patronizing. He uses analogies from everyday life, he acknowledges when something is genuinely hard, he celebrates the beauty of the mathematics rather than hiding it. His New York Times Opinionator writing shows in the prose: it feels like a good teacher talking to you personally.

- **The three-pillar framework has genuine generative power**. Every major topic in the book — Archimedes's polygons, Descartes's coordinates, Fermat's tangents, the FTC, differential equations, the rigorization of limits — maps onto one or more of slicing, gluing, or changing. The framework is not a gimmick; it is a real insight into the structure of mathematical thinking.

- **Historical range handled with accuracy and judgment**. Two thousand years of history, from ancient Syracuse to modern satellite orbits, compressed to 380 pages, and not oversimplified. Strogatz resists the temptation to turn Archimedes or Newton into flat heroes — he gives context, shows dead ends, acknowledges predecessors.

## Weaknesses

- **The middle chapters are thin relative to the book's ambition**. The period between Newton/Leibniz (1670s) and the Cauchy–Weierstrass rigorization (1820s) — the century and a half of Euler, Lagrange, Laplace, Fourier — is treated in roughly 20 pages. Euler alone could fill a book this size, and his contributions to calculus (function concepts, infinite series, complex analysis) are compressed almost to absence. The narrative gains momentum by skipping this period, but readers interested in the development of analysis between the founding and the rigorization will feel short-changed.

- **The rigorization chapter telegraphs the result**: Strogatz tells us that Cauchy and Weierstrass fixed the logical foundations of calculus by replacing infinitesimals with epsilon-delta limits, but he leaves the reader with only the briefest sketch of how that replacement actually works. A reader coming to epsilon-delta for the first time will not understand it from this chapter alone — they will understand its importance, but not its mechanism.

- **The chapter on differential equations is conceptually rich but mathematically light**. Strogatz gives the SIR model for epidemiology, the logistic equation for population growth, and the Navier-Stokes equations for fluid flow — all excellent choices — but does not actually derive or solve any of them in complete form. The chapter motivates the idea but does not fully satisfy the mathematically curious reader.

- **No worked problems or exercises**: As a narrative rather than a textbook, the book rightly avoids exercises, but some readers — particularly students encountering calculus for the first time — may wish for optional worked examples that bridge the historical narrative and the computational technique.

- **The third pillar (changing) gets less independent treatment than the first two**. Slicing (integration) and gluing (algebraic geometry, rigorization) each get their own rich chapter. Changing as a theme is present throughout but less explicitly developed as a standalone topic. Fermat's tangent method (Chapter 5) is the strongest standalone "changing" chapter, but differential equations (Chapter 8) could arguably have been presented as the "changing" chapter's climax more clearly.

## Criticism

### From Mathematicians

The calculus community has been generally positive about *Infinite Powers*, but some working mathematicians have raised specific objections. The most substantive: Strogatz's treatment of the Cauchy–Weierstrass rigorization, while accurate in broad outline, gives the false impression that the epsilon-delta framework resolved all logical problems with calculus. In fact, the 19th-century rigorization introduced new foundational questions — about the nature of the real number system, about the axiom of choice, about the continuum — that were only resolved in the early 20th century. Strogatz mentions the Weierstrass function (continuous everywhere, differentiable nowhere) in passing but does not develop its unsettling implications. This is a minor historical compression, not an error, but a mathematician reader will notice.

A second objection: Strogatz's treatment of the Leibniz–Newton dispute, while fairer than most popular treatments, still leans slightly toward the view that the dispute was Newton's fault. Modern historians such as Amir Alexander and Michael Nauenberg have argued that Newton had specific grievances — Leibniz's 1675 letter to Oldenburg explicitly described Newton's method, and Leibniz's subsequent publications avoided crediting Newton. Strogatz acknowledges this evidence but does not dwell on it, preferring the broader dramatic arc of a "human tragedy."

### From Historians of Mathematics

The Cambridge historian of mathematics Jeremy Gray has noted that popular books on the history of mathematics inevitably privilege narrative over archival accuracy, and *Infinite Powers* is no exception. Specifically: the claim that Archimedes's method of exhaustion was "integral calculus" is accurate in the modern retrospective reading but would have been confusing — and indeed false — to Archimedes himself, who had no concept of an infinite sum. The book asserts this equivalence confidently; the scholarly literature is more cautious, preferring "anticipation" descriptions. This is a defensible simplification for a popular book but worth flagging.

### From Science Communicators

Some science communicators have noted that Strogatz's warmth and accessibility — his greatest strengths — occasionally lead him to over-simplify in ways that could mislead. The GPS chapter, while factually correct, implies a level of public awareness about the relativistic corrections that does not exist: most people who use GPS daily have no idea it depends on Einstein. Strogatz's retort — that precisely because the math works invisibly in the background, its ubiquity is more impressive than if it were visible — is philosophically sound, but the rhetorical move could be misread as suggesting the public already understands the connection.

### From Educators

Secondary and university mathematics educators have noted that *Infinite Powers* works better as a motivational prelude to calculus than as a supplement for students already studying it. The book's strength is the intellectual why; it does not address the practical how. Students who read it before beginning a calculus course report increased emotional engagement, but those who read it alongside a calculus text may find the historical material competing with the technical material for attention.

## Historical Context

- **~250 BCE**: Archimedes develops the method of exhaustion in Syracuse. The first real ancestor of integral calculus.
- **~1630s**: Fermat develops the method of tangents privately. Descartes publishes *La Géométrie* (1637), embedding geometry in algebra. Analytic geometry is born.
- **~1640s–1650s**: Torricelli, Cavalieri, Roberval, and Fermat (in Italy and France) develop techniques for finding areas and tangents using infinitesimals. The "precursors" period.
- **~1665–1666**: Newton, in plague isolation, invents fluxional calculus. The most productive 18 months in the history of mathematics.
- **~1673–1684**: Leibniz, working independently in Germany, develops his notation and publishes *Nova Methodus* (1684), the first public account of the new calculus. Superior notation survives to the present.
- **~1711–1713**: The Royal Society (controlled by Newton) accuses Leibniz of plagiarism. The priority dispute begins in earnest.
- **~1734**: Bishop Berkeley publishes *The Analyst*, attacking the logical foundation of infinitesimals. The foundational crisis begins.
- **~1820s**: Cauchy and Weierstrass establish the epsilon-delta framework for limits. Calculus is put on rigorous foundations for the first time.
- **~1850s–1870s**: Weierstrass discovers a function that is continuous everywhere but differentiable nowhere — showing that the intuitive link between continuity and differentiability is not logical necessity. Set theory enters analysis.
- **~1900s**: The real numbers are put on rigorous set-theoretic foundations (Dedekind cuts, Cauchy sequences). Analysis, the branch of mathematics built on calculus, is now completely formalized.
- **~1931**: Gödel's incompleteness theorems show that no formal system can capture all mathematical truth. The foundational project begun by Cauchy and Weierstrass hits a hard limit.
- **~1950s–1980s**: Abraham Robinson develops non-standard analysis, showing that infinitesimals can be made logically rigorous after all — using model theory. Newton's "evanescent increments" were neither wrong nor meaningless.

## Long-Term Relevance

*Infinite Powers* is built around ideas that are intellectually permanent. The method of exhaustion is 2,300 years old and still taught. The FTC was proven in the 1670s and remains center-stage in every calculus course. Differential equations model pandemics in 2020 and will model them in 2120. The three-pillar framework is a structural insight into how human beings think about continuous change — it does not depend on any particular technology or cultural moment.

What may age: some of the specific references — the GPS example, the COVID-19 SIR model discussion — are bound to specific years and will date the book mildly. The cultural references and pop-science framing (nods to The Simpsons, etc.) will feel of their moment within a decade. But the central argument — that calculus is the intellectual technology that enabled the modern world — will not date, because it is true independently of any cultural moment.

The more interesting question is whether the book's approach to mathematical history — emphasizing narrative and accessibility over technical precision — will remain the dominant model. There are signs that popular mathematics writing is becoming more sophisticated and willing to include more mathematical detail, following the lead of advanced readers on platforms like LessWrong and 3Blue1Brown. *Infinite Powers* represents the peak of the "accessible narrative" approach. A future book on the same material might combine this warmth with more mathematical apparatus without losing the audience.

| Dimension | Rating | Notes |
|---|---|---|

| Historical Accuracy | 8/10 | Minor compressions for narrative flow; overall scholarly responsible |
| Mathematical Depth (for general readers) | 8/10 | Right level: enough substance to satisfy, not enough to overwhelm |
| Accessibility | 9/10 | Reads like a good TED talk transcribed by a generous teacher |
| Range | 8/10 | 2,300 years compressed to 380 pages — impressive, but some eras get short treatment |
| Originality | 8/10 | The three-pillar framework is genuinely original as a teaching device |
| Relevance to Modern Life | 10/10 | GPS and epidemiology chapters make the case without overclaiming |
| Aesthetic Quality | 9/10 | Strogatz writes mathematics as literature without being precious about it |
| **Overall** | **8.5/10** | The best single-volume popular history of calculus and why it matters |

**It succeeds** at its difficult central ambition: making 2,000 years of mathematical history feel like a single coherent, emotionally engaging story. Strogatz is among a small handful of living writers who could pull this off, and he makes it look effortless.

**It falls short** only where a book like this must — by necessity, the middle centuries (Euler, Lagrange, Laplace) receive compressed treatment, and readers seeking deep mathematical detail will need to supplement with focused study. No single popular book can simultaneously be a work of popular history, a mathematics primer, and a scholarly monograph. *Infinite Powers* knows what it is and does it astonishingly well.

**Net evaluation**: If you read one book to understand where calculus came from and why it matters — and one book that makes you feel, viscerally, that mathematical ideas have a human history with real drama and genuine beauty — this is it. The three-pillar framework alone, once internalized, will change how you see mathematical problems for the rest of your life.
