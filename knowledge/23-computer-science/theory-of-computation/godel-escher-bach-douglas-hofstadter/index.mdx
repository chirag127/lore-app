Gödel, Escher, Bach is one of the most celebrated works of interdisciplinary thought of the twentieth century. Winner of both the Pulitzer Prize for General Nonfiction and the National Book Award, it weaves together the mathematical discoveries of Kurt Gödel, the paradoxical art of M. C. Escher, and the recursive musical structures of Johann Sebastian Bach to build a profound argument about the nature of mind, meaning, and consciousness.

<BookHeader />

At its heart, the book argues that "strange loops" — self-referential hierarches where moving through levels unexpectedly returns you to the starting point — are the fundamental pattern from which consciousness emerges. Through alternating technical chapters and playful dialogues between Achilles and the Tortoise, Hofstadter builds a case that meaning is not a property of isolated symbols but emerges from the isomorphic relationships between levels of a system, whether that system is mathematics, art, music, or the human brain.
