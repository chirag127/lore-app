The Pragmatic Programmer: Your Journey to Mastery, by David Thomas
and Andrew Hunt. Published by Addison-Wesley Professional in 2019.
This is the 20th Anniversary Edition of a book first released in
1999.

Thomas and Hunt are the co-founders of the Pragmatic Bookshelf, a
publishing company that has released hundreds of titles for software
developers. Both were among the 17 original authors of the Agile
Manifesto. Dave Thomas coined the terms Code Kata and Don't Repeat
Yourself. Andy Hunt began programming in the 1980s, writing in 6502
assembler and building manufacturing resource planning systems.

The book is built around 100 tips organized into 53 topics across 9
chapters. Each tip is a short, memorable piece of advice. The authors
call this approach pragmatic: focus on what works, not on what is
fashionable.

The core philosophy is simple. Programming is a craft. You must care
about it. You must think about your work. You have agency over your
career, your tools, and your code. Do not wait for permission to
improve things.

The authors open with the story of a cat eating someone's source
code. The point is not to have a funny excuse but to take ownership.
When things go wrong, offer options, not excuses. If you say you
cannot do something, you are right. But if you say you can, you are
also right. The difference is attitude.

Next comes the broken window theory. Borrowed from criminology, it
says that visible signs of neglect invite more neglect. A building
with one broken window will soon have many. Software is the same.
A bad design choice, a quick hack, a missing error check — these are
broken windows. Fix them immediately. The longer they stay, the more
they signal that nobody cares, and entropy accelerates.

The stone soup story is about being a catalyst. Soldiers convince
villagers to contribute ingredients to a pot of stones and water,
eventually producing a rich soup. Start with something that works.
Make it visible. Let others join in. The boiled frog warns the
opposite: gradual change goes unnoticed until it is too late. Watch
the big picture, not just the immediate task.

The knowledge portfolio is one of the book's most influential
concepts. Treat your skills like an investment portfolio. Invest
regularly — read a book each quarter, learn a new language each year.
Diversify across paradigms and domains. Review and rebalance —
discard what is obsolete, deepen what matters. Critically analyze
everything you read. This is your career, and nobody else will manage
it for you.

Chapter two introduces the design principles. Good design is easier
to change than bad design. That is the measure. Everything else
serves it.

The DRY principle is the most famous. Every piece of knowledge must
have a single, unambiguous, authoritative representation in your
system. DRY is not about typing the same text twice. It is about
knowledge duplication. Two pieces of code that represent the same
concept must be unified. If they represent different concepts, they
should stay separate even if they look similar. The authors classify
duplication into four types: imposed, inadvertent, impatient, and
interdeveloper. Each has a different cure.

Orthogonality means independence. When two things are orthogonal,
you can change one without affecting the other. You can test them
separately. You can reuse them independently. Loss of orthogonality
is loss of control. The book advises designing systems as a set of
self-contained modules with well-defined interfaces.

There are no final decisions. Build systems that can survive change.
Do not commit to a database vendor or a cloud provider as if it
were a marriage. Keep your options open. This is reversibility.

Tracer bullets and prototypes solve different problems. A tracer
bullet is a minimal end-to-end skeleton built with real code. It
validates your architecture, gives you a running system, and lets
you adjust as you fill in the details. You keep the tracer bullet
and build on it. A prototype answers a specific question. You build
it, learn from it, and throw it away. The key is knowing which one
you need.

Program close to the problem domain. Create mini-languages and
vocabularies that mirror how users think about their problems.
This bridges the gap between requirements and implementation.

Estimate to avoid surprises. Break the problem into components.
Build a rough model. Assign values. Validate with real data.
Iterate your estimates with your code. An estimate is a prediction,
not a promise.

Chapter three is about tools. Keep knowledge in plain text. Plain
text is universal, durable, and human-readable. It outlives any
binary format. Master your command shell. Achieve editor fluency.
Always use version control. These were radical recommendations in
1999. They are table stakes today.

Debugging is detective work. Fix the problem, not the blame. Don't
panic. Write a failing test before you fix the code. Read the
error message — it often tells you exactly what is wrong. Do not
assume the framework, the database, or the compiler is broken.
Prove your hypothesis with a test.

Chapter four is pragmatic paranoia. You cannot write perfect
software. Build defenses. Design by Contract means every module
has a clearly defined contract: what it requires, what it guarantees,
and what invariants it maintains. Crash early. A dead program is
better than a corrupt one. Use assertions to prevent the impossible.
Finish what you start — always match resource allocation with
deallocation. Take small steps. You cannot predict the future, so
do not try. Stay within the cone of uncertainty.

Chapter five is about writing code that bends instead of breaking.
Decoupled code is easier to change. Tell, Don't Ask. Do not chain
method calls. Avoid global data. Prefer delegation over inheritance.
Use external configuration. These are the tools of maintainable
software.

Chapter six tackles concurrency. Analyze workflow to find true
parallelism. Shared state is incorrect state. Use actors or
processes to avoid it. Use blackboards to coordinate workflows.

Chapter seven covers the discipline of writing code. Do not program
by coincidence. Know why your code works. Estimate algorithm
complexity. Test your estimates. Refactor early, refactor often.
Testing is not about finding bugs. It is about verifying contracts
and enabling change. A test is the first user of your code. Name
things well and rename when needed.

Chapter eight is about what happens before you write code. No one
knows exactly what they want. Programmers help people discover what
they want. Requirements are learned in a feedback loop. Use a
project glossary. Do not think outside the box — find the box.
Understand your constraints before trying to escape them. Agile
is not a noun. It is how you do things.

Chapter nine is about teams. Maintain small, stable, fully
functional teams. Do what works, not what is fashionable. Beware
of process theater — a coconut is not a nut, and pretending does
not make it so. Use version control to drive builds, tests, and
releases. Test early, test often, test automatically. Code is not
done until all the tests run. Find bugs once. Delight your users,
do not just deliver code. Sign your work.

The book closes with three ethical principles. First, do no harm.
Do not write code that damages people or systems. Do not enable
scumbags. Your craft has power. Use it responsibly.

The Pragmatic Programmer is not the deepest book you will read on
any of these topics. It is not intended to be. It is a map of the
territory, showing you what matters and giving you a vocabulary to
talk about it. The principles — DRY, orthogonality, broken windows,
knowledge portfolio, tracer bullets, design by contract — are now
part of the shared language of software engineering. Understanding
them is understanding your profession.

Read it early in your career to develop good instincts. Re-read it
later to rediscover what you have forgotten. The book changes as
you change. That is the mark of a classic.
