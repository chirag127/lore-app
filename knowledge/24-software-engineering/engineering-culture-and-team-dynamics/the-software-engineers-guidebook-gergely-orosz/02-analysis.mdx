## Strengths

- **The first serious book on the engineering career itself.** There
  are dozens of books on writing code and dozens on managing teams.
  There was, until Orosz, almost nothing on the career between those
  two poles. The book fills a real gap in the literature.

- **Built on primary sources, not theory.** The book draws on
  interviews with hundreds of engineers at named companies —
  Google, Meta, Stripe, Shopify, Uber, Booking.com, and dozens of
  startups. When Orosz says "staff engineers at Google typically
  spend 30-50% of their time on writing," it is grounded in
  observation, not speculation.

- **Specific and actionable.** The book tells you what a promotion
  packet contains, what a tech lead is allowed to delegate, what
  big tech rewards that startups do not, and what salary levers
  exist at each company type. The advice is operational.

- **Honest about the parts of the job that are not technical.** Orosz
  does not pretend performance reviews are purely about merit, that
  promotions are clean, or that big tech is a strictly better place
  to work than startups. He tells you how things work, then lets you
  decide.

- **A clear and useful vocabulary.** The book gives engineers a
  shared language for things they were doing but could not name —
  scope expansion, influence without authority, the IC-vs-manager
  fork, the big-tech-vs-startup behavioral split. Naming things is
  the first step to managing them.

- **A useful book for managers too.** An EM who has never been a
  staff engineer will learn what the IC track actually requires, and
  will be a better partner to their reports for it.

---

## Weaknesses

- **Broad rather than deep.** The book tries to cover the whole
  ladder (mid → senior → tech lead → staff → principal) and both
  worlds (big tech and startups) in 500 pages. The result is that
  any individual level gets maybe 50-80 pages. A focused book on
  just the staff engineer role — say, Sinha's *Staff Engineer* — can
  go deeper. Orosz's book is the map; the focused books are the
  guides to specific regions.

- **Heavily US-centric.** The book draws on US big tech and Western
  European startups. The advice transfers less cleanly to engineering
  careers in Asia, Latin America, Africa, or Eastern Europe — where
  ladders, compensation norms, and what counts as "scope" can be
  quite different. Orosz acknowledges this briefly; the book is
  mostly silent on it.

- **The promotion and negotiation chapters can feel procedural.** The
  promotion packet, the compensation table, the negotiation
  checklist — these are useful as references, but they read like a
  handbook. The book is at its best when Orosz is interpreting
  behavior; he is at his most mechanical when he is listing
  components. Some readers will want one and not the other.

- **Lighter on the technical side.** Orosz is a generalist by
  background and treats staff engineering as a *role*, not as a
  *technical discipline*. A book on the technical depth expected of
  a staff engineer — systems design, technical writing, influence
  patterns — would be a useful complement, and is not in this book.

- **Some repetition of newsletter content.** Readers who follow *The
  Pragmatic Engineer* will recognize sections. This is not a
  complaint — newsletter material is updated and integrated — but
  readers expecting entirely new content will find some familiar
  ground.

## Controversies and Points of Disagreement

### The Manager Fork Is Permanent

Orosz is blunt: the IC-to-manager move is usually a one-way door. This
is a strong claim, and not every company agrees. A handful of
companies (notably some startups and a few non-FAANG large tech
companies) do allow managers to move back to IC, sometimes at the
same level. Orosz is right that this is the exception, not the
norm, and right to flag it as a major decision. He is also right
that the *perception* of the move being permanent, even when it is
technically not, is itself a constraint. An engineer considering the
move should treat it as permanent in practice.

### Big Tech Rewards Different Behaviors

Orosz's claim that big tech rewards visible cross-team impact, written
artifacts, and process — while startups reward shipped product, speed,
and generalism — is supported by his interviews and matches the
experience of most engineers who have worked in both. It is also
overgeneralized. Some big tech teams operate like startups (small
teams, fast iteration, low ceremony). Some startups operate like big
tech (formal reviews, design docs, slow process). The book's
dichotomy is useful as a heuristic, not as a law.

### Senior Engineer "Owns Projects"

The senior engineer's job is to own projects, not tasks. This is
Orosz's claim, and it is broadly right. It is also, in some
companies, a bar that is set too high. A senior engineer at a
mature codebase with stable architecture may have less opportunity
to "own a project" than a senior engineer at a startup building
greenfield code. The book is clearer on what ownership looks like
than on how to demonstrate it when the company's structure does not
naturally create projects to own.

### Performance Reviews Are an Exercise in Packaging

This is the most provocative of Orosz's claims, and the one most
likely to be disagreed with. He argues that effort that is not
visible to the organization does not count, and that the work is
half the job while the documentation of the work is the other half.
Critics argue this is a cynical view that rewards self-promotion
over substance.

Both sides have a point. The work has to be good. But the work also
has to be seen, and the system that decides who is seen is the system
that decides who gets promoted. An engineer who produces excellent
work and nobody outside their team knows about it is, in career
terms, indistinguishable from an engineer who produces mediocre
work. Orosz is right to make this explicit. Engineers who are
uncomfortable with the framing should be careful not to mistake the
framing for the problem.

## Comparison to Similar Books

| Book | Author | Difference |
|---|---|---|
| *The Pragmatic Engineer* (newsletter / book) | Gergely Orosz | The newsletter is ongoing; the book is the consolidated treatment. The newsletter is more current on specific companies; the book is the structured reference. |
| *Staff Engineer* | Tanya Sinha | A personal, narrative account of the staff role. Pairs with Orosz's more structured, multi-source treatment. Sinha is one engineer's story; Orosz is a survey. |
| *The Manager's Path* | Camille Fournier | The counterpart for managers. Where Orosz covers the IC track, Fournier covers the manager track. Both are clear-eyed about the mechanics. |
| *An Elegant Puzzle* | Will Larson | A collection of essays on engineering management, lighter on the career ladder. Larson is closer to a philosopher; Orosz is closer to a reporter. |
| *The Effective Engineer* | Edmond Lau | Focused on the *individual* engineer's productivity. Orosz covers the *career*; Lau covers the day-to-day effectiveness. Complementary, not competing. |
| *The Staff Engineer's Path* | Will Larson | A focused book on the staff-and-beyond track. Narrower and deeper than Orosz. Worth reading alongside. |
| *Designing Your Life* | Bill Burnett | A general career-design book, not engineering-specific. Useful for engineers who are not yet sure whether they want the IC or manager track. |
