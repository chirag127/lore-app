## Introduction

Welcome to BookAtlas. Today: *The Software Engineer's Guidebook* by
Gergely Orosz. Published 2024. Bitfield Insights. About 500 pages.
And it is, in my opinion, the first serious book ever written about
the engineering career itself.

Not about writing code. Not about managing teams. About the space
in between — the ladder from senior engineer to tech lead to staff
engineer, the rules of the game, and the parts of the job that have
nothing to do with your editor.

Let's walk through it.

---

## Who Is Gergely Orosz?

Gergely Orosz is the author of *The Pragmatic Engineer* newsletter,
which is read by hundreds of thousands of engineers and is, in my
view, the most consistently insightful engineering publication in
the industry. He was a software engineer at Uber, Skyscanner,
Microsoft, and Booking.com before he became a full-time writer.

That background matters. Orosz has been on the ladder. He has done
the work. And he has spent the years since interviewing hundreds of
other engineers who are on it too — senior engineers, tech leads,
staff engineers, principals, engineering managers — at companies
from Google and Meta down to 30-person startups. The book is built
on those interviews. When he says "staff engineers at big tech
typically spend 30 to 50 percent of their time writing," he is
reporting, not theorizing.

## The One Big Idea: Scope, Not Skill

The book has one organizing idea, and it is this: **engineering
careers are ladders of scope, not ladders of skill**.

At the junior level, the question is: can you write correct code?
At the mid level, can you ship a feature? At the senior level, can
you own a project? At the staff level, can you set technical
direction across multiple teams, with no formal authority to do so?

Your ability to write code does not change much between levels. What
changes is the radius of the system you can move. A staff engineer
at Google might be a worse coder than the senior engineer two levels
below them. The staff engineer can change the behavior of ten teams.
The senior engineer can change the behavior of one. That is the
difference.

Everything in the book follows from this. The performance review
sections, the promotion chapters, the tech lead versus staff
distinction, the manager fork, the big-tech-versus-startup split —
all of it is downstream of the fact that what changes between levels
is scope, and everything in the work is built around expanding it.

## The Senior Engineer: Ownership

The senior engineer is where most engineers stall, and it is the
level where the ladder starts to feel like a ladder. Orosz's
definition is precise: a senior engineer **owns projects**. Not
tasks, not stories, not features. *Projects*.

What does ownership mean in practice? Four things.

First, defining done. The senior engineer does not wait for a
specification. They figure out what success looks like, write it
down, and align the team around it.

Second, driving unblocking. When the project is stuck on another
team, or on a dependency, or on a tooling issue, the senior
engineer fixes it. They do not write a ticket and wait. They do
not file a Slack message. They fix it.

Third, holding the quality bar. The senior engineer is the one who
pushes back on shortcuts that would compound. They ship, but they
do not ship debt.

Fourth, communicating. Stakeholders know what is happening. Risks
are flagged early. The senior engineer's project is never a
surprise.

The trap: confusing activity with ownership. A senior engineer who
closes many tickets is not necessarily owning projects. A senior
engineer who closes fewer tickets but makes the whole team faster —
*that* is ownership.

## The Tech Lead: A Hybrid Role

The tech lead is the most variable role across companies, which is
why Orosz gives it its own careful treatment. In broad strokes, a
tech lead is a senior engineer who also coordinates a small team's
technical direction. They still write code — often a lot of it —
but they also decide what the team should build, hold the
architectural bar, and unblock the team.

Three flavors, distinguished by Orosz.

The **coding tech lead** spends most of their time writing code and
coordinates through informal conversation and code review. Common at
startups.

The **architect tech lead** spends more time on design, less on code.
Sets the technical direction; delegates implementation. Common at
scale-ups.

The **coordinating tech lead** spends significant time in meetings,
aligning stakeholders, removing dependencies. Still writes some code.
Common in big tech.

These are different jobs. Liking one and ending up in another is a
common source of misery.

A common misconception: the tech lead role is the first step to
engineering management. At some companies it is. At many it is a
parallel track that goes as high as principal engineer. Orosz is
explicit: ask, at your company, what tech lead means. Do not assume.

## The Staff Engineer: Influence Without Authority

If the senior engineer owns a project and the tech lead owns a
team, the staff engineer owns a *team of teams* — with no formal
authority to do so. That is the role in one sentence. The rest of
the job is figuring out how to do it.

A staff engineer usually has no direct reports. They have no
administrative power. They cannot tell other teams what to do. What
they have is **influence**, and they exercise it through three
mechanisms.

First, **writing**. Design docs. RFCs. Vision memos. Postmortems.
Decision records. The staff engineer who cannot write a design doc
that gets five teams to align cannot do the job. Writing is the
scalable way to align multiple teams. Meetings are not.

Second, **relationships**. A staff engineer who shows up on a
project they have never contributed to and asks for help will fail.
A staff engineer who has been helpful on ten previous projects will
succeed. The relationships have to be built before they are needed.

Third, **shipped work**. A track record of getting hard things done.
Influence is a function of trust. Trust is a function of being
right, often. Staff engineers are not always right. But they are
right often enough that people follow.

Orosz's most quoted observation: a staff engineer is essentially a
tech lead of multiple teams, with the key difference that they have
to earn the right to lead rather than being assigned it.

## The Manager Fork: The One-Way Door

The single most consequential career decision Orosz covers is the
choice between staying on the IC track and moving into management.
And the reason it is consequential is that, at most large companies,
it is a **one-way door**.

Once you are a manager for two years, going back to IC is treated as
a demotion. Compensation drops. Respect drops. You are not the only
one who notices. The reverse move — manager to senior IC — is rare
and almost always involves a level reset.

Orosz's framework for the decision: if you like solving technical
problems end to end, stay on the IC track. If you like coaching,
hiring, and team health, move to management. If you like writing
design docs and influencing peers, the staff IC track is the
answer. If you like running one-on-ones and performance reviews,
the manager track is the answer.

The trap — and Orosz warns against this repeatedly — is **drifting
into management** because the IC step looked hard. The IC track to
staff is genuinely harder than the management track in many
companies. But the choice should be made on what you want to do, not
on which path looks shorter at the moment.

## Big Tech, Scale-Up, Startup: Different Games

The same level means different things in different environments.
Orosz devotes significant attention to this, because most
engineers change environments at least once in their career, and
the unspoken rules are not portable.

Big tech rewards visible cross-team impact, written artifacts, and
process. Startups reward shipped product, speed, and generalism.

A senior engineer at Google who is great at writing design docs
and coordinating with peer teams is doing exactly what Google wants.
That same engineer at a 50-person startup is overinvesting in
process. The work that gets them promoted at Google is invisible
at the startup.

The promotion game is different too. At big tech, promotions are
determined by calibration committees — managers argue for their
reports against an objective rubric. Self-promotion, design docs,
and cross-team visibility are how you get promoted. At startups,
promotions happen when the company needs you in a bigger role. The
CEO decides. The signal is the scope you are already operating at,
not the artifacts you have produced.

A startup engineer who has spent two years writing design docs and
asking for cross-team sponsorship has been playing the wrong game.

## Performance Reviews and Promotion Packets

Orosz treats the performance review as a **packaging problem**, not
a work-quality problem. The work is necessary. It is not sufficient.
The work has to be seen, described, and defended by people who are
not you.

A typical staff-engineer promotion packet at a big tech company
includes a self-assessment, a list of projects with scope and
outcome, evidence from peers and stakeholders, and — most
importantly — a narrative. A story of expanding scope over a year
or more.

The narrative is what people remember. The metrics are what they
check. Both are required.

The traps are predictable. Working on invisible things. Owning
everything (which is hard to promote because it is hard to see).
Skipping the self-assessment (which makes the packet read like a
list of tasks, not like an argument for the next level).

## Writing: The Staff-Engineer Multiplier

Orosz makes a strong case that **writing is the single highest-
leverage skill for staff-and-beyond engineers**. The reason is
mechanical: a staff engineer is responsible for aligning multiple
teams, and the only scalable way to align multiple teams is through
text.

The artifacts are concrete. Design docs to align on a technical
approach before building. RFCs to get cross-team input on a shared
change. Postmortems to extract and share lessons from incidents.
Vision memos to set multi-quarter direction for an area. Decision
records to capture the *why* behind a hard choice. Launch reviews
to document what shipped, what worked, what did not.

The staff engineer who writes well multiplies their impact. The
staff engineer who relies on meetings does not. Meetings do not
scale across teams and time zones. Documents do.

## Compensation: The Last Chapter On Purpose

Orosz covers compensation last, deliberately. The reason: **you
negotiate from a position of strength only if you understand the
structure of the offer first**. Going in cold costs real money — at
staff level at a US big tech company, the difference between a
well-negotiated and a poorly-negotiated offer can be a year's
salary over four years of vesting.

The components are base, equity, bonus, signing bonus, and level.
The dominant lever at big tech is the *level* — dropping a level
costs hundreds of thousands of dollars. At startups, the levers
are base, equity percentage, vesting, and signing.

The preparation is universal. Know the market band for the level,
in your geography. Know the company's recent comp changes. Have a
competing offer if possible. Decide in advance what you will and
will not accept. Negotiate in writing. Phone calls are not records.

## The Verdict

**Narrator:** I have been in the industry for over a decade. I have
been a senior engineer, a tech lead, and a staff engineer. I have
worked at a startup and at a large tech company. I have watched
brilliant engineers stall because they did not understand the game
they were playing. I have watched competent engineers get promoted
because they did.

*The Software Engineer's Guidebook* is the book I wish I had at the
start. It is the first book to take the engineering career as
seriously as the engineering work. It is honest about the parts of
the job that are not technical. It tells you what a promotion
packet looks like, what a tech lead is allowed to delegate, what
big tech rewards that startups do not, and what salary levers
exist at each company type. It does not pretend the ladder is
meritocratic, or that promotions are clean, or that big tech is
strictly better than startups. It tells you how things work, then
lets you decide.

The book is not perfect. It is broad rather than deep — the whole
ladder and both worlds in 500 pages means any individual level gets
50 to 80 pages. It is US-centric. It is lighter on the technical
side than some readers will want. But the core argument is
correct, and the specific advice is genuinely actionable.

If you are a mid-level engineer aiming for senior, a senior
engineer picking a path, a tech lead considering staff, or a staff
engineer who feels stuck, this is the book to read.

This has been a BookAtlas narration of *The Software Engineer's
Guidebook* by Gergely Orosz. Thanks for listening.
