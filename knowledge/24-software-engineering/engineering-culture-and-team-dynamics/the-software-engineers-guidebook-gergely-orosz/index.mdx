## Overview

*The Software Engineer's Guidebook* (2024) by Gergely Orosz is a
field-tested map of the engineering career ladder at modern tech
companies and well-funded startups. Orosz is the author of *The
Pragmatic Engineer* newsletter (one of the most widely read engineering
publications in the industry) and the author of *The Pragmatic
Engineer* book. Before becoming a full-time writer he was a software
engineer at Uber, Skyscanner, Microsoft, and Booking.com, and he has
spent the years since interviewing hundreds of senior engineers, tech
leads, staff engineers, principal engineers, and engineering managers
at companies ranging from Google, Meta, and Stripe to high-growth
startups.

The book collects what those engineers reported and turns it into a
practical guide. It is not a memoir. It is not a manifesto. It is a
descriptive, opinionated reference for what the levels actually mean,
what gets people promoted, and how to navigate the parts of the job
that have nothing to do with code.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Scope is the only thing that compounds.** Output scales linearly
   with effort. Scope scales with trust, which scales with consistent
   delivery. Every level is fundamentally a scope expansion.

2. **Senior engineer is about ownership, not autonomy.** The senior
   engineer defines what "done" means for a project, drives it across
   teams, and removes blockers. They do not need to be told what to
   do — they need to be told what to optimize for.

3. **Tech lead is a hybrid role, not a promotion on the same axis.** A
   tech lead still writes substantial code while coordinating the
   technical direction of a small team. It is the role for engineers
   who want influence over a team without giving up the keyboard.

4. **Staff engineer is influence without authority.** Staff-plus
   engineers set technical direction across multiple teams. They
   have no direct reports. They succeed by writing, by relationships,
   and by being right often enough that people follow.

5. **The manager fork is real and usually permanent.** Moving from IC
   to manager is a one-way door at most large companies. Choose it on
   purpose, not because the next IC step looked hard.

6. **Big tech and startups are different games.** Big tech rewards
   visible cross-team impact, written artifacts, and process. Startups
   reward shipped product, speed, and generalism. The same engineer
   can thrive in one and stall in the other.

7. **Performance reviews are an exercise in packaging.** Effort that
   is not visible to the organization does not count. The work is
   half the job; the documentation of the work is the other half.

8. **Promotions need a portfolio.** A single big project rarely
   suffices. You need a multi-quarter story of expanding scope,
   supported by design docs, postmortems, mentorship, and peer
   evidence.

9. **Writing is the staff-engineer multiplier.** Design docs, RFCs,
   vision memos, decision records, and postmortems are how a staff
   engineer multiplies their impact across teams.

10. **Compensation is mostly market-driven.** Understanding leveling,
    bands, and the total-rewards package is the prerequisite to
    negotiating. Going in blind costs real money.

| Reader | Why |
|---|---|

| Senior engineer picking a path | Honest comparison of tech lead, staff, and manager tracks |
| Tech lead considering staff | What changes when scope moves from one team to several |
| Staff engineer feeling stuck | A reference for scope, expectations, and visible artifacts |
| Engineer moving from startup to big tech (or back) | Why the same behaviors get rewarded differently |
| Engineering manager | A clear picture of the IC track you did not take |

## Why This Book Matters

There is no shortage of engineering books. Most of them are about
writing code, designing systems, or running teams. There are very few
that take the career itself — the actual ladder, the actual reviews,
the actual politics — as the subject. Orosz's contribution is to do
exactly that, with the credibility of having been on the ladder and
having interviewed hundreds of people on it.

The book arrives at a moment when the industry has more engineers
than ever, more ladder ambiguity than ever, and more variation between
companies than ever. A senior engineer at one company can be a staff
engineer at another. A tech lead at a startup is a different job than
a tech lead at Google. The book provides the language and the
heuristics to navigate that.

It is also notably honest. Orosz does not pretend the ladder is
meritocratic, that promotions are clean, or that big tech is a strictly
better place to work than startups. He tells you how things actually
work, then lets you decide.

## Final Verdict

*The Software Engineer's Guidebook* is the most useful book on the
engineering career published in years. It is not a programming book.
It is not a leadership book. It is a *career* book — the kind that
should exist and, until Orosz wrote it, mostly did not.

Its strength is specificity. It tells you what a staff engineer does
on Monday, what a promotion packet looks like, what a tech lead is
allowed to delegate, and what big tech values that startups do not.
Its weakness is breadth: the book tries to cover the whole ladder and
both worlds (big tech and startups), so individual sections are
necessarily shorter than a focused book on any one of them.

**Rating: 9/10** — The first book to give the engineering career
itself a serious, structured, honest treatment. Will save you years
of figuring it out the hard way.
