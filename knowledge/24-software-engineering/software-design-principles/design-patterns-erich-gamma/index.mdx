## Overview

*Design Patterns: Elements of Reusable Object-Oriented Software* (1994)
by Erich Gamma, Richard Helm, Ralph Johnson, and John Vlissides —
the "Gang of Four" (GoF) — is the most influential software design
book ever written. It cataloged 23 patterns that became the shared
vocabulary of object-oriented design.

---
{}

| Creational | 5 | Object creation mechanisms | Singleton, Factory Method, Abstract Factory, Builder, Prototype |
| Structural | 7 | Object composition and structure | Adapter, Decorator, Facade, Proxy, Composite, Bridge, Flyweight |
| Behavioral | 11 | Object interaction and responsibility | Observer, Strategy, Command, Template Method, Iterator, State, Visitor, Mediator, Chain of Responsibility, Memento, Interpreter |

## Key Takeaways

1. **Design patterns are solutions to recurring problems.** They are
   not invented — they are discovered and documented from real-world
   practice.

2. **Program to an interface, not an implementation.** This principle
   underlies most patterns and enables flexibility, testability, and
   loose coupling.

3. **Favor object composition over class inheritance.** Composition
   provides more flexibility by changing behavior at runtime.
   Inheritance binds you to compile-time decisions.

4. **Patterns are a vocabulary, not a recipe book.** The real value of
   patterns is in communication. Saying "use a Strategy pattern"
   conveys design intent instantly.

5. **The catalog is not exhaustive.** The 23 patterns are a starting
   point. Many more patterns have been documented since (Enterprise,
   concurrency, architectural).

---

## The Three Categories

### Creational Patterns
Abstract the instantiation process. They make a system independent of
how its objects are created, composed, and represented.

### Structural Patterns
Concerned with how classes and objects are composed to form larger
structures. Structural class patterns use inheritance; structural
object patterns use composition.

### Behavioral Patterns
Concerned with algorithms and the assignment of responsibilities
between objects. They describe not just patterns of objects or classes
but also the patterns of communication between them.

## Who Should Read

| Reader Type | Why |
|---|---|
| Junior developers | Foundational knowledge for understanding professional codebases |
| Senior engineers | Shared vocabulary for design discussions and code reviews |
| Architects | Patterns form the building blocks of system design |
| Anyone maintaining OOP code | Understanding patterns helps recognize design intent |

---

## Who Should Skip

- Developers working exclusively in non-OOP paradigms
- Beginners who have not yet learned basic OOP concepts
- Those looking for implementation-specific examples — the book uses
  C++ and Smalltalk

---

## Why This Book Matters

Design Patterns changed how we talk about software. Before the GoF
book, every developer invented their own names for common solutions.
After it, we had a shared vocabulary. The book's influence extends
beyond its 23 patterns — it established the pattern community, the
pattern format, and the discipline of pattern-oriented design.

## Final Verdict

*Design Patterns* is a foundational work that every software developer
should read. Its flaws — dated examples, C++ focus, lack of functional
programming — are real but do not diminish its value. The catalog
remains relevant because it captures fundamental truths about object-
oriented design that transcend languages.

**Rating: 8/10** — Essential for understanding professional software
design. The examples are dated but the concepts are timeless.
