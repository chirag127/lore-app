## The Disk Drive Industry Study

Christensen built his theory on an exhaustive study of the disk drive industry, which experienced eight distinct generations of technological change between 1950 and 1990. Disk drives were an ideal test case: a well-documented, fast-moving industry where leadership changed hands repeatedly. Every time a disruptive technology emerged — moving from 14-inch to 8-inch to 5.25-inch to 3.5-inch drives — the established leaders were the first to develop working prototypes, yet every time they ceded the market to entrants.

The pattern was consistent. Incumbents listened to their existing customers — mainframe manufacturers who wanted larger capacity, not smaller form factors. The disruptive drives offered less capacity initially but were smaller, cheaper, and opened new markets (minicomputers, then desktops, then portables). By the time the disruptive technology improved enough to serve the mainstream, the entrants had captured the market and the incumbents had missed their window.

## Sustaining vs. Disruptive Innovation

| Type | Sustaining Innovation | Disruptive Innovation |
|---|---|---|
| **Target market** | Existing customers in mainstream markets | Emerging or overlooked market segments |
| **Performance trajectory** | Improves performance along dimensions that mainstream customers value | Initially underperforms on those dimensions but offers different benefits (cheaper, smaller, simpler) |
| **Incumbent response** | Incumbents typically lead in sustaining innovation | Incumbents almost always ignore or dismiss disruptive innovations |
| **Outcome** | Reinforces leadership | Topples leadership when performance catches up |

Sustaining innovations improve existing products along dimensions that existing customers already value — faster processors, brighter displays, longer battery life. They are incremental or even radical, but they serve the needs of established markets. Incumbents consistently win at sustaining innovation because their resources, processes, and incentives are aligned to deliver precisely these improvements.

Disruptive innovations introduce a different value proposition. They are typically simpler, cheaper, smaller, and more convenient. They initially perform worse on the attributes that mainstream customers care about. But they create new markets — or they take root at the low end of an existing market that incumbents are happy to abandon because the profit margins are too thin.

## The Resource Dependence Theory

Firms are constrained by the needs of their existing customers. Christensen draws on resource dependence theory, which holds that organizations act based on where their resources come from. If a firm's revenue depends on a set of customers, its resource allocation processes will naturally prioritize innovations that those customers want. This is not a failure of management — it is rational behavior.

The paradox: the processes that make firms responsive to their best customers also make them blind to disruptive threats. Middle managers, knowing that their careers depend on meeting quarterly revenue targets from existing customers, kill disruptive projects that lack a clear market. Senior executives, receiving filtered information from below, never see the disruptive threat until it is too late.

## Value Networks

Every firm operates within a value network — the context of customers, suppliers, competitors, and cost structures that defines what is profitable. Firms in different value networks evaluate opportunities differently because their cost structures and margin requirements differ.

A mainframe computer manufacturer in the 1970s had a cost structure built around 50% gross margins. The 8-inch disk drive — which sold for a fraction of the price of 14-inch drives — simply could not generate the margins the mainframe maker needed. The opportunity was invisible not because engineers did not see it, but because the firm's financial filters rejected it. Entrants with lower cost structures, serving the minicomputer value network, found the 8-inch drive highly attractive.

## Case Studies

### Mechanical Excavators

The transition from cable-actuated to hydraulic excavators followed the disruption pattern exactly. Hydraulic technology was initially too weak for the heavy digging that cable excavators handled. It was deployed in applications where its unique attributes — smoother operation, lower cost, smaller size — mattered. Cable excavator leaders dismissed hydraulics as a toy. Over two decades, hydraulic technology improved until it could handle the mainstream, and the cable excavator leaders had been left behind.

### Steel Minimills

Minimills began by making low-quality rebar — the least profitable segment of the steel market, which integrated mills were happy to abandon. From rebar, minimills moved upmarket into angles, rods, and beams, each time perfecting their process to serve the next tier. By the 1990s, minimills (led by Nucor) had captured most of the steel market, and the integrated mills had been pushed into a shrinking premium segment.

### Honda vs. Harley

Christensen uses the motorcycle industry to illustrate how a disruptive entrant (Honda) overtook an established leader (Harley-Davidson). Honda entered the US market with small, cheap motorcycles — the Super Cub — that were derided by Harley as toys. Harley's customers wanted larger, louder bikes. Honda built volume and capability in the small-bike segment, then moved relentlessly upmarket. Harley, protected by its value network and customer base, could not respond effectively until the disruption had transformed the industry.

## The Five Principles of Disruptive Innovation

1. **Companies depend on customers and investors for resources.** Firms allocate resources where the most profitable customers demand them, making it structurally difficult to invest in disruptive technologies that initially serve smaller, less profitable markets.

2. **Small markets don't solve the growth needs of large companies.** A disruptive innovation that might generate $50 million in revenue is irrelevant to a firm that needs $500 million in growth. Large companies rationally ignore the very opportunities that could eventually replace them.

3. **Markets that don't exist cannot be analyzed.** When faced with a disruptive technology, managers demand market research. But if the market does not yet exist, there is no data to analyze. Entrants succeed by discovery-driven planning and learning through experimentation.

4. **An organization's capabilities define its disabilities.** The very processes and values that make an organization excellent at sustaining innovation make it incapable of pursuing disruptive innovation within the same organizational structure.

5. **Technology supply may not equal market demand.** Disruptive technologies often overshoot the performance that mainstream customers can actually use. This creates a vacuum at the bottom of the market that entrants fill with "good enough" products at lower prices.

## Why Established Firms Fail Despite Good Management

The central thesis of the book is counterintuitive: **good management causes failure**. Firms that do everything right — listen to customers, invest in the most profitable businesses, allocate resources efficiently — are the most likely to be disrupted. Failures of innovation in established companies are not failures of competence or leadership. They are failures of process and structure — the very things that made the firm successful in the first place.

The innovator's dilemma is this: the practices that maximize a firm's current performance are precisely the practices that leave it vulnerable to disruption.

## How Entrants Succeed by Targeting Overshot Customers

Markets have a performance trajectory — the rate at which customers can absorb improved performance — and technologies have a separate trajectory — the rate at which they improve. Christensen found that technology almost always improves faster than customers can use it. This means that a technology that is "not good enough" for mainstream customers today will eventually overshoot their needs.

When this happens, the basis of competition shifts. In the early stages, customers choose the product with the best performance. But once performance exceeds what they can use, they begin choosing on other dimensions — convenience, customization, reliability, and price. Entrants that have built low-cost platforms in underserved segments are perfectly positioned to win when this shift occurs, while incumbents are stuck with high-cost structures designed to deliver ever-increasing performance that customers no longer value.
