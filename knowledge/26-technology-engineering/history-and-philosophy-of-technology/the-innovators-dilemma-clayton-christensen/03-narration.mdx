title: Narration
subtitle: Audio Script — The Innovator's Dilemma
---

Imagine you run a company that makes 14-inch disk drives for mainframe computers. You have the best engineering team in the industry. Your customers are the largest computer manufacturers in the world. Your profit margins are healthy, and your market share is dominant. When a new technology emerges — an 8-inch drive — your engineers build a working prototype. It is not as good as your 14-inch drive, but it is smaller and cheaper. Your customers look at it and say: we do not need this. Our mainframe customers want more capacity, not less.

So you do what any good manager would do. You put your best engineers on the sustaining project — the next-generation 14-inch drive that your customers actually want. You allocate your sales and marketing budget to the products that generate the most revenue. You set performance targets that your competitors cannot match. This is not bad management. This is excellent management.

And this is exactly why, five years later, your company is in decline. The 8-inch drive found a market you did not see — the minicomputer industry, which was too small to interest you. By the time the 8-inch drive improved enough to meet your mainframe customers' needs, the minicomputer manufacturers were already buying from a new set of suppliers. Your 14-inch drive was still the best product in the world, but your customers no longer needed it. You had been disrupted.

This is the innovator's dilemma.

The book is built on a single question that Clayton Christensen posed as a doctoral student at Harvard Business School: why do great companies fail? Not troubled companies, not poorly managed companies, but the best companies — the ones admired by executives and studied in business schools. The answer, Christensen discovered, is that they fail precisely because they are well managed.

The structure of the book is itself a case study in Christensen's method. He opens with the puzzle. He examines the disk drive industry, where the pattern is so clean it reads like a designed experiment — eight generations of technology, eight transfers of leadership from incumbents to entrants. He then tests the pattern across other industries: mechanical excavators, steel minimills, motorcycles, retail. Each case confirms the pattern. Then he builds a theory to explain it.

The theory works through several interlocking ideas. The first is the distinction between sustaining and disruptive technologies. Sustaining technologies improve products along dimensions that existing customers value. They are what good companies do naturally. Disruptive technologies initially underperform on those dimensions but offer something else — simplicity, affordability, accessibility — that opens new markets. The critical insight: incumbents almost always lead in sustaining innovation. They almost always lose at disruptive innovation.

The second idea is resource dependence. Firms cannot freely choose their strategy. They are constrained by the needs of their existing customers and investors. A firm's resource allocation process — the system that decides which projects get funded and which are killed — is wired to serve the most profitable customers. A disruptive project that promises $20 million in revenue will be starved of resources in a company that needs $200 million to grow. This is not a decision any executive makes consciously. It is embedded in the firm's processes and incentives.

The third idea is value networks. Every firm operates within a set of relationships that define what is profitable. A mainframe manufacturer has a cost structure that requires 50% gross margins. The 8-inch drive, selling for a fraction of the price, could not support that cost structure. The opportunity was invisible not because engineers did not see the technology, but because the firm's financial model could not see the market. Entrants with lower cost structures, operating in different value networks, saw a highly attractive opportunity.

The fourth idea is the performance oversupply phenomenon. Christensen measured something that seems obvious in retrospect but was not obvious at the time: technology improves faster than customers can absorb it. By the time a product has exceeded what customers can actually use, the basis of competition shifts. Customers stop caring about more performance and start caring about price, convenience, and reliability. This is exactly the moment when the disruptor's product, built on a simpler and cheaper platform, becomes good enough for the mainstream.

The fifth idea is that organizations have capabilities and disabilities that are not the same as those of the people who work in them. A firm's processes — the way it makes decisions, evaluates projects, and allocates resources — are designed for a particular kind of work. Those same processes make a different kind of work impossible. The values of the organization — what it considers profitable, what it considers significant — filter out the very opportunities that could save it.

Christensen's solution is not to stop being well managed. The solution is to recognize that disruptive innovations require different organizational structures. The disk drive incumbents that successfully navigated disruption were those that created independent organizations, with their own processes and values, to commercialize the disruptive technology. The parent company provided resources; the spin-off provided the right environment.

The book's prose is plain and direct, a reflection of Christensen's HBS training and his background as a Mormon missionary. He writes as a teacher explaining a concept rather than a prophet announcing a revelation. The case studies are told in detail but without drama — the evidence speaks for itself. The tone is measured, almost clinical, which makes the argument more persuasive. Christensen does not need to raise his voice because the data is doing the work.

The disk drive industry was the ideal test case because it moved fast and kept records. Christensen had access to detailed engineering data, market share numbers, and corporate histories. He interviewed executives at the companies that succeeded and the companies that failed. The pattern was so consistent that the theory almost wrote itself. Christensen once said that the disk drive industry was "the fruit fly of innovation studies" — a laboratory where generational change happened quickly enough to observe clearly.

The book's enduring place in business literature is secure not because every prediction it enables is correct, but because it identified a real phenomenon that was systematically overlooked. Before Christensen, the conventional explanation for why companies fail was hubris, complacency, or strategic error. After Christensen, the conversation shifted. Failure could be caused by the very practices of success. This insight — that the same behaviors that produce excellence can produce vulnerability — has a tragic quality that gives the book its lasting power.

Christensen died in January 2020 at the age of 67. By that time, "disruption" had become one of the most used and abused words in business. The term had been stretched to cover everything from a new restaurant to the collapse of industries. Christensen spent his final years trying to reclaim the term and restore its precision. He argued that not every change is disruption, not every new technology is disruptive, and not every innovator is a disruptor. The careless use of the word, he said, had done real damage to the theory he had built.

But the theory itself endures. Every generation of entrepreneurs reads The Innovator's Dilemma and recognizes the pattern in their own industry. Every executive sees their own company in the disk drive manufacturers who did everything right and still lost. Every startup founder tells a story that Christensen's framework made possible. The innovator's dilemma is not a problem that can be solved once and forgotten. It is a permanent feature of competitive markets — the shadow that success casts.

The disk drive industry that Christensen studied no longer exists in the form he analyzed. But the pattern he identified in it has been observed in industries he never studied: software, retail, transportation, media, healthcare, education. The form changes. The mechanism remains. And the question Christensen posed — what do you do when doing the right thing leads to failure? — is more relevant than ever.
