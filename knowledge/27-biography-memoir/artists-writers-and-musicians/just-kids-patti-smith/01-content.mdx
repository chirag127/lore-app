# Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

*Just Kids* is divided into five main sections plus a Foreword, following a loose chronological arc from Smith's childhood through Mapplethorpe's death. The first edition (Ecco, 2010) runs 278 pages. What follows covers every named segment of the narrative.

---

## Foreword

The book opens *in medias res*: Smith is asleep when Mapplethorpe dies. She had called the hospital to say goodnight, but he was already under, "beneath layers of morphine." She holds the receiver and listens to his labored breathing. The next morning she awakens with the knowledge — she does not know how — that he is gone. His younger brother's phone call confirms it.

The Foreword establishes the book's governing tension: this is a story told by a survivor who promised to tell it. "I didn't write it to be cathartic," Smith has said. "I wrote it because Robert asked me to." The reader knows from the first page where the story ends. The question becomes not *what* happens but *how* it mattered.

---

## Monday's Children

Smith begins with her childhood in rural South Jersey, the eldest of four children in a working-class Jehovah's Witness family. Her mother, Beverly, was a jazz singer turned waitress; her father, Grant, a machinist at a Honeywell plant. The family moved from Chicago to Philadelphia to Woodbury, New Jersey, always a step behind economic security.

Young Patti is a daydreamer, reprimanded by teachers for inattention. She discovers art on a school trip to the Philadelphia Museum of Art, standing before a Picasso and feeling "transformed, moved by the revelation that human beings create art." She begins writing poems, drawing, and reading voraciously — Arthur Rimbaud becomes her guiding spirit, alongside Bob Dylan and the Beat poets.

At nineteen, working in a factory, Smith becomes pregnant after a brief relationship. She gives birth to a daughter and places her for adoption, a loss she carries silently through the rest of the memoir. In July 1967, with a suitcase and a copy of Rimbaud's *Illuminations*, she boards a bus to New York City.

The chapter introduces her early New York years: sleeping in doorways and Central Park, working at Scribner's bookstore on Fifth Avenue, living on day-old bread and the 15-cent specials at the Automat. She is poor but not desperate — she has come to New York to become an artist, and poverty is the entrance fee. She meets Robert Mapplethorpe by chance in Brooklyn while escaping a bad date. He is a student at Pratt Institute from Floral Park, Long Island, raised in a devout Catholic family, a former altar boy who has begun experimenting with LSD and drawing obsessively. She notes his "mass of dark curls," his "pale and slim" frame, his beaded necklaces. They recognize something in each other immediately — a shared seriousness about art that the world around them does not seem to share. Within weeks they are inseparable, sharing a bed in his small Brooklyn apartment.

Smith's early jobs provide a vivid cross-section of New York's literary scene. At Scribner's she works alongside the poet Janet Hamill, who becomes a lifelong friend. She later moves to Brentano's and then the Strand, the legendary used bookstore where she meets Lenny Kaye for the first time — though it will be years before they collaborate. The bookstore jobs also give her access to the writers she worships: she steals a copy of Rimbaud's *A Season in Hell* from a display table, justifying the theft as a kind of tribute.

## Just Kids

This section covers the core of their shared life from 1967 to 1969. Smith and Mapplethorpe move into a series of progressively worse apartments: a small room at the Allerton Hotel, a loft on Hall Street near Pratt, a cold-water flat with no heat. They are spectacularly poor. The daily decision is "grilled-cheese sandwiches or art supplies." They shoplift food, steal milk from doorsteps, and rely on the kindness of acquaintances.

But poverty, in Smith's telling, is not misery — it is the crucible of their vocation. "We were living out our own bohemian fairy tale," she writes. They make art from everything: Robert assembles collages from magazine clippings, religious cards, and found objects; Patti writes poems on scrap paper and sketches in the margins of books. They study art books obsessively, adopting William Blake's palette as their own — "shades of rose, cadmium, and moss."

Robert begins to explore his sexuality in private. The physical relationship between them fades, but the emotional bond intensifies. Smith writes with remarkable delicacy about his Catholic guilt, his attraction to men, and his fear of what that attraction means. She does not claim to understand it — "I knew nothing of the reality of homosexuality," she admits — but she does not turn away.

The chapter features encounters with the era's luminaries. They see Jimi Hendrix perform at the Scene, meet Janis Joplin at a party, and observe Andy Warhol's Factory crowd from a cautious distance. Smith is alienated by Warhol's ironic detachment — "I hated the soup, and felt little for the can" — while Robert is drawn to the glamour. The tension between their tastes becomes a quiet motif: she is a romantic, he is an arriviste.

One of the most striking passages describes their visit to the Metropolitan Museum of Art, where they study the paintings for hours, arguing about which are worthy. They adopt William Blake's palette as their own — "shades of rose, cadmium, and moss" — and begin making works that directly reference his illuminated manuscripts. Mapplethorpe creates a series of mixed-media assemblages that incorporate religious imagery, found objects, and his own drawings. Smith writes poems on brown paper bags and glues them into handmade books. They consider their poverty not as deprivation but as purification: "We were living out our own bohemian fairy tale."

The chapter also documents the darker edges of their world. The Summer of Love, 1967, gives way to the political violence of 1968 — the assassinations of Martin Luther King Jr. and Robert Kennedy, the riots at the Democratic National Convention, the escalating war in Vietnam. Smith and Mapplethorpe are aware of these events but strangely detached; they are focused on their work. Smith's distance from politics is one of the book's more controversial aspects — she seems uninterested in the liberation movements that were transforming the world around her, preferring the solitary, apolitical tradition of the romantic artist.

Perhaps the most important development in this section is Smith's gift of a Polaroid SX-70 camera to Robert. It is a practical act of love: she knows he needs a medium, and photography allows him to make images instantly, without the months of labor his assemblages required. He begins photographing everything — her, their room, the streets, the detritus of their daily life. The Polaroid becomes the seed of his future career. Smith later reflects that she gave him the camera "knowing he would create things I could never imagine, which he did."

The section ends with a note of prophecy. Smith has a dream in which she sees Robert "crumbling into dust" — a premonition of his death that she keeps to herself but that haunts her for the rest of their relationship.

## Hotel Chelsea

By 1970, they have moved into Room 1017 of the Hotel Chelsea, the legendary artist residence on West 23rd Street. The Chelsea is a character as much as a setting: a shabbily elegant warren of long-term residents, famous and obscure, where rent is paid in art and the management has a reputation for accepting "difficult" tenants.

The Chelsea years are the most eventful of the book. Smith and Mapplethorpe befriend a rotating cast of the period's most interesting figures. Allen Ginsberg takes Smith to the Automat for lunch (mistaking her for "a very pretty boy" and trying to pick her up). William S. Burroughs holds court in the lobby. Gregory Corso recites poetry in the hallway. Grace Slick, Harry Smith, and members of the Warhol Factory drift through.

Smith begins performing her poetry at St. Mark's Church in the East Village, part of the Poetry Project. She is nervous, self-conscious — until she discovers a "submerged arrogance" that surfaces in performance, a "whole other side" of herself that behaves like "a young cock." It is the first glimpse of the performer she will become.

She also meets the playwright Sam Shepard, with whom she collaborates on the one-act play *Cowboy Mouth*. They have a brief, intense romance; Shepard becomes the subject of several poems. Robert is jealous, and the tension strains their living arrangement.

In 1973, Smith meets Lenny Kaye, a guitar-playing rock historian who works at a record store. She asks him to accompany her poetry reading at St. Mark's with electric guitar. The pairing is electrifying. Kaye's guitar provides the rhythmic backbone that her words had been missing, and the crowd responds with an energy Smith has never felt before. It is the birth of what will become the Patti Smith Group.

Robert, meanwhile, is struggling. He is still making art — increasingly sophisticated collages and assemblages — but he has not yet found his medium. Smith watches him flounder and urges him toward photography. When he finally picks up the Polaroid seriously, the results are immediate and assured. "It was as if he had been waiting his whole life for this instrument," she writes.

The Chelsea years are also when the couple mark their first significant milestones in the art world. Smith's poetry reading at St. Mark's Church on February 10, 1971, with Lenny Kaye on electric guitar, is a turning point. The crowd — a mix of poets, musicians, and hangers-on — responds with an intensity she has never felt. She describes the discovery of her "submerged arrogance": a stage persona that was bolder, sexier, more aggressive than her everyday self. After the reading, she is approached by Danny Fields, the legendary talent scout who had worked with the Velvet Underground and the Stooges. He offers encouragement. The path is beginning to open.

Mapplethorpe's own breakthrough comes through Sam Wagstaff, a wealthy curator and art collector twenty years his senior who becomes his patron and lover. Wagstaff is refined, connected, and deeply supportive. He buys Robert a proper Hasselblad camera, introduces him to the gallerists and collectors who will define his career, and gives him the financial freedom to work without distraction. Smith describes Wagstaff with careful generosity — she is grateful for his support of Robert but aware that his entry into their lives marks the beginning of their separation. Wagstaff takes Robert to Europe, introduces him to high society, and provides the material base for his transformation from starving artist to international figure. Smith watches this happen from a distance, ambivalent but not resentful.

## Separate Ways Together

This section, the longest, traces their diverging but still intertwined paths from 1974 through the early 1980s.

Smith's music career accelerates. The Patti Smith Group — Smith on vocals, Kaye on guitar, Richard Sohl on piano, Ivan Kral on bass, Jay Dee Daugherty on drums — becomes a fixture at CBGB, the Bowery club that is incubating the punk scene. They open for the New York Dolls, the Ramones, and Television. Smith's performances are raw, poetic, and confrontational. She spits, howls, and stalks the stage like a shaman.

In 1974, they record a single: "Hey Joe / Piss Factory." The A-side is a radical reinterpretation of the Jimi Hendrix classic, prefaced by a spoken-word meditation on Patty Hearst. The B-side recounts Smith's factory work in South Jersey. It is widely considered the first punk rock single. The record gets radio play, builds a following, and attracts the attention of Clive Davis at Arista Records.

The debut album *Horses* is recorded in 1975 at Electric Lady Studios with producer John Cale (ex-Velvet Underground). The sessions are tense. Cale pushes Smith to her limits, and the band struggles with his exacting methods. But the result is a masterpiece: an album that fuses punk energy with Beat poetry, surrealist imagery with three-chord rock and roll. It opens with Smith's iconic declaration: "Jesus died for somebody's sins but not mine." The cover photograph, taken by Mapplethorpe, shows Smith in a white shirt, a jacket slung over her shoulder, her gaze direct and unapologetic. It becomes one of rock's most famous images.

*Horses* is not an immediate commercial success — it peaks at number 47 on the Billboard charts — but it is a critical triumph. Reviewers praise its fusion of punk energy with Beat poetry, its raw production, its refusal to conform to any existing genre. Smith is hailed as the godmother of punk, the poet laureate of the downtown scene. She tours Europe, appears on British television, and becomes a cultural figure almost against her will. The album's influence far exceeds its sales: it will eventually appear on Rolling Stone's list of the 500 greatest albums of all time, and its cover — Smith in a white shirt, jacket slung over shoulder, photographed by Mapplethorpe — becomes one of rock's most iconic images.

Meanwhile, Mapplethorpe's career is also ascending. With Wagstaff's backing, he produces a series of photographs that are technically exquisite and deliberately provocative: male nudes, BDSM scenes, floral still lifes, celebrity portraits. His work is shown at major galleries and attracts both acclaim and scandal. Smith attends his openings, watches him navigate the high society that has embraced him, and feels both pride and distance.

In 1980, Smith marries Fred "Sonic" Smith, the guitarist of the MC5, and moves to Detroit. She has two children, Jackson and Jesse. Her music career goes into hiatus; she releases only one album in the 1980s (*Dream of Life*, 1988). The section captures the strange bittersweetness of success achieved separately: they got what they wanted, but not together.

Yet they never lose contact. Robert sends letters, photographs, and gifts. Smith calls him from Detroit. When she returns to New York, she visits his studio. Their bond remains intact, transformed but undiminished. "He was still my twin flame," she writes.

## Holding Hands with God

The final section is the briefest and the most devastating.

In 1986, Robert is diagnosed with AIDS. Smith learns the news on the same day she discovers she is pregnant with her second child. The juxtaposition — death entering her life just as new life does — is handled without sentimentality. She simply reports it.

She begins traveling to New York regularly to care for him. Robert, astonishingly, continues working. He photographs until his body will not cooperate, producing some of his most powerful images during these final years. His exhibition *The Perfect Moment* — a retrospective that includes his most controversial work — opens in 1988 and solidifies his reputation even as it reignites culture-war debates about public funding of the arts.

Smith writes about the final months with extraordinary restraint. She reads to him, holds his hand, and prays — she, who had long since left the Jehovah's Witness faith of her childhood. They talk about their lives, their work, and the vow she made him: that she would tell their story. She brings her children to visit, and Mapplethorpe, weakened but still mischievous, makes faces at them. His apartment is filled with flowers and his own photographs, stacked against the walls.

In his last conscious moments, he asks her: "Patti, did art get us?" It is the question the entire book has been leading toward — a summing-up of two lives lived in service of the irrational, impractical, all-consuming drive to make things that did not exist before. She answers: "Perhaps it did. But only a fool would regret being had by art; or a saint."

He dies on March 9, 1989, at age 42. Smith is not in the room; she had gone home to say goodnight over the phone, and he slipped away while she slept. The symmetry of beginning and ending the book with this phone call gives the narrative its emotional architecture.

The book ends not with his death but with Smith walking on a beach, alone, in a long dark coat. "Why can't I write something that would awake the dead?" she asks. The question is the book's final sentence — and also its answer. *Just Kids* is that attempt.

## Reading Guide

*Just Kids* is a complete, self-contained narrative that does not require supplementary reading. However, readers who want the full Patti Smith trilogy of memoirs should continue with *M Train* (2015), which covers her later life and widowhood, and *Year of the Monkey* (2019), a dreamlike account of her drifting in the late 2010s.

For readers interested specifically in Mapplethorpe, Patricia Morrisroe's *Mapplethorpe: A Biography* (1995) provides a more comprehensive, less reverent account of his life. Smith has been critical of Morrisroe's book, but it is the standard biography.

The book can be read in a single sitting (approximately six hours) or savored one section at a time. The chapters are short and the prose is fluid. The photographs interspersed throughout — many by Mapplethorpe — add a visual dimension that rewards slow attention.

**Recommended path:** Read straight through. The Foreword and "Holding Hands with God" form a frame that gives the central chapters their emotional weight. Skipping ahead would diminish the cumulative effect of Smith's careful pacing.
