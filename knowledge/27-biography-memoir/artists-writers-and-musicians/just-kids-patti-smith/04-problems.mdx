# Problems, Solutions, and Open Questions

## Key Problem

*Just Kids* does not present itself as a problem-solving book. It is a memoir, not a manifesto. But it addresses, across its five sections, a set of interconnected problems that define the artist's life: **how to sustain a creative vocation in conditions of poverty and uncertainty; how to love someone whose identity is transforming in ways you cannot follow; how to bear witness to a life without betraying its complexity; and how to go on living after the person who made your world meaningful is gone.**

These problems are not solved in the book — they are held up for contemplation, lived through rather than resolved. Smith's contribution is not to offer answers but to demonstrate, through the concrete example of her own experience, that these problems are survivable and that surviving them with one's integrity intact is a form of success.

---

## THE PROBLEM OF POVERTY: How to make art when you cannot afford art supplies

### Proposed Solution

Smith's answer is pragmatic: use what you have. Mapplethorpe makes collages from found objects and magazine clippings. Smith writes on scrap paper. They steal milk from doorsteps and shoplift food, not out of desperation but as a strategic allocation of limited resources toward the only thing that matters. Poverty, in this framework, is not an obstacle to art but a filter: those who cannot tolerate it were never serious enough to begin with.

### Effectiveness

This romantic framing of poverty has been criticized as both naive and privileged — naive because it assumes that poverty is a choice, privileged because Smith and Mapplethorpe had the safety net of whiteness, education (Mapplethorpe had Pratt), and social connections that many poor artists lack. Yet the book's actual argument is more nuanced: Smith does not celebrate poverty but describes a specific historical moment when New York's cheap rents and tolerant culture made it possible for penniless artists to survive. The problem she addresses is not poverty itself but how to maintain creative integrity within it.

## THE PROBLEM OF TRANSFORMING LOVE: How to stay bonded when the sexual basis of a relationship dissolves

### Proposed Solution

Smith and Mapplethorpe's relationship evolved from romance to friendship without losing its intensity. Smith's solution was to redefine the bond in terms of mutual creative support rather than sexual exclusivity. They became each other's ideal audience, harshest critic, and most loyal champion. The transformation required both of them to tolerate ambiguity: she accepted his homosexuality without fully understanding it; he accepted her subsequent relationships with other men.

### Unresolved Questions

Smith does not fully explore what this transformation cost her. She admits to crying when Mapplethorpe first went out to hustle, but she does not examine the jealousy, the loneliness, or the sense of rejection that must have accompanied the end of their physical relationship. The book's discretion, while artistically effective, leaves open the question of whether the smooth transition she describes was really as smooth as she remembers it.

## THE PROBLEM OF THE ARTIST-MUSE DYNAMIC: Can two artists be each other's muses without one subsuming the other?

### Proposed Solution

The traditional artist-muse relationship is hierarchical: the male artist creates, the female muse inspires. Smith subverts this by making both parties artists and both parties muses. She inspires Mapplethorpe's photography; he inspires her poetry. The relationship is reciprocal, and both credit the other with making their work possible. Mapplethorpe tells her: "Nobody sees as we do, Patti."

### Criticisms

Some feminist critics (notably those cited by Sarah Mesle's essay in MQR) have questioned whether the relationship was as reciprocal as Smith claims. Mapplethorpe's fame and market success outstripped Smith's during his lifetime; his reputation has arguably grown more since his death. Smith's framing of herself as his "guardian" and caretaker — especially in the final years — risks reinscribing the very gender dynamics she seeks to escape: the woman sacrificing her own career to tend to the man's legacy.

## THE PROBLEM OF GRIEF: How to go on living after the death of a soulmate

### Proposed Solution

Smith's answer is the book itself. She wrote *Just Kids* because Mapplethorpe asked her to. The act of writing became the act of grieving. The book transforms loss into literature without pretending that literature compensates for loss. The final line — "Why can't I write something that would awake the dead?" — acknowledges the inadequacy of art even as it demonstrates its necessity.

### Practical Applications

Smith's model of grief as creative obligation has been adopted by other writers who have lost partners to AIDS, cancer, and other illnesses. The model is simple: make something that the dead person would have been proud of. It does not require the bereaved to "move on" or "find closure"; it requires them to keep working.

## THE PROBLEM OF MEMORY AND TRUTH: How to write a memoir when the person you are writing about cannot verify your account

### Proposed Solution

Smith solves this problem by limiting her ambition. She does not claim to be writing an objective biography; she is writing her version of the story, the one Mapplethorpe asked her to tell. The book is openly, even aggressively, partial. Smith acknowledges that others remember Mapplethorpe differently — Patricia Morrisroe's biography is the implied antagonist throughout — but she does not pretend to adjudicate between competing accounts.

### Alternative Solutions

Morrisroe's approach in *Mapplethorpe: A Biography* was the opposite: extensive interviews with friends, lovers, family, and enemies, triangulating competing accounts, producing a portrait that Mapplethorpe's mother said was accurate but that Smith said was unrecognizable. The contrast between the two books raises a fundamental question about biographical method: is the subject's own version of his life more authoritative than the accounts of those who observed it? Smith clearly believes yes; Morrisroe clearly believes no.

## THE PROBLEM OF THE HISTORICAL RECORD: What happens when the city that made you no longer exists?

The New York of *Just Kids* — cheap rent, tolerant margins, a critical mass of young artists — was already disappearing when Smith wrote the book. Gentrification, the internet, and the financialization of real estate have made it nearly impossible for penniless artists to replicate Smith and Mapplethorpe's trajectory. The book is itself a solution to the problem of vanished conditions: it preserves the memory of a city that no longer exists and, in doing so, provides a template that young artists can adapt even if they cannot directly reproduce it.

Smith has acknowledged this explicitly. Speaking at Cooper Union in 2010, she warned young artists: "New York has closed itself off to the young and the struggling. Find a new city." The book, then, is not a prescription but a record — an act of historical preservation that also functions as a challenge to find new frontiers.
