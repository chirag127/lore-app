Published in 2010, *Just Kids* won the National Book Award for Nonfiction and established Patti Smith — already legendary as the godmother of punk — as a literary voice of the first rank. The memoir traces Smith's relationship with the photographer Robert Mapplethorpe from their first meeting in Brooklyn in 1967 through his death from AIDS in 1989. But it is not a conventional biography. Smith calls it a "love story" and an "elegy" — a book whose real subject is the mystery of creative partnership: how two people who were neither lovers for long nor family by blood became each other's most important witness.

The book's power lies in its refusal to be cynical. Smith writes about poverty, hustling, and the grimy glamour of the Chelsea Hotel with the earnest wonder of someone who never stopped believing that art was the only thing worth living for. The title comes from a moment in Washington Square Park when an older couple, observing Smith and Mapplethorpe, dismisses them as "just kids." The irony, of course, is that those kids would grow up to define an era.

<BookHeader />

The title *Just Kids* comes from an incident at Washington Square Park. Smith and Mapplethorpe, already living as artists in poverty, are observed by an older couple. "Oh, take their picture," says the woman. "I think they're artists." Her husband shrugs: "Oh, go on — they're just kids." The book is Smith's long answer to that dismissal: a demonstration that being "just kids" — innocent, earnest, unabashed in one's devotion to art — was precisely what made them extraordinary.
