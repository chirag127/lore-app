# Problems, Solutions, and Open Questions

## Key Problem

In 1916, at the conclusion of *Relativity: The Special and the General Theory*, Einstein posed the problem that would fuel both his greatest public triumph and his deepest private regrets: **how does a scientist trained in universal physical laws respond when their work becomes entangled with the violent machinery of state power?** The problem has two faces. First, theoretical: Einstein's search for a unified field theory — an equation from which all of physics would be derivable — was a scientific problem that lasted almost forty years and consumed nearly all his working life from 1925 onward, fruitlessly. Second, moral: Einstein's signature on the 1939 letter to Franklin D. Roosevelt was a political act that launched the Manhattan Project and made thermonuclear war literally thinkable. Both problems are live in the biography and both are partially unresolved at Einstein's death on April 18, 1955.

---

## THEORETICAL PROBLEM: The Unified Field Theory

### Proposed Solution
Einstein's proposed solution was elegant in principle: if gravity could be described by the geometry of curved spacetime (general relativity), and electromagnetism by field equations in flat spacetime (Maxwell), perhaps a higher-dimensional or higher-symmetric geometry could unify both forces into a single framework. Over four decades he worked on reformulations using Ricci calculus, projective geometry, and later symmetric tensor fields, hoping their equations would reduce smoothly to both general relativity and Maxwell's electrodynamics under appropriate limits.

### Evidence and Argumentation
Isaacson shows Einstein pursuing the problem with monkish discipline, spending hours each day at the blackboard in his Princeton study. He collaborated with Wolfgang Pauli, Ernst Straus, and Peter Bergmann, all younger physicists who increasingly regarded the project with polite scepticism. Einstein's approach was rooted in aesthetic conviction — he believed a unified equation must exist because the world is mathematically harmonious — rather than experimental guidance. This aesthetic reasoning, while intellectually honest, became its own blind spot: the quantum forces (strong and weak nuclear forces) that would emerge in the 1940s had no place in Einstein's geometric program and no mathematical tools that could bridge them.

### Unresolved Questions
Unification remains arguably physics' central open problem. Einstein died without solving it; string theory and loop quantum gravity, conceived decades later, are attempts to answer the same motivation — a single, coherent physical law — with radically different mathematics. Einstein's program was not wrong in spirit; it was simply, Isaacson shows, ahead of the physics needed to answer it.

## MORAL PROBLEM: Science and State Power

### The 1939 Letter to Roosevelt
Einstein's co-authorship of the Szilard-Wigner letter to President Roosevelt was a response to concrete intelligence: Leo Szilard had realised that uranium fission could produce a chain reaction, and that German scientists working under Heisenberg were pursuing it. Einstein's voice was the moral weight that persuaded Roosevelt to fund the Manhattan Project. The letter's famous closing lines attributed urgency: "This new phenomenon would also lead to the construction of bombs, and it is conceivable — though much less certain — that extremely powerful bombs of a new type may thus be constructed."

### Proposed Solution
The letter was the solution to the Nazi-threat problem of its moment. In retrospect, Isaacson argues, Einstein recognised that the solution generated a larger problem: an arms race with Stalin's USSR that made the very existence of civilisation precarious. From 1945 to his death Einstein funded the Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists, campaigned for international control of nuclear weapons, and wrote extensively on the need for a world federation strong enough to prevent nuclear war.

### Evidence and Argumentation
The evidence for Einstein's regret is unambiguous. In a 1955 television interview he said: "Had I known that the Germans would not succeed in producing the atomic bomb, I would not have lifted a finger." Letters to colleagues and the Russell-Einstein Manifesto (1955, issued weeks before his death) document sustained moral engagement with the very problem his letter had created. Isaacson presents this not as a moral failure but as a moral complexity: a principled act for which the full consequences could not have been foreseen.

### Unresolved Questions
Nuclear weapons have not been eliminated; the world remains in the structural condition Einstein feared in 1946. Whether the lesson of his regret is personal (never advise statesmen) or collective (abolish weapons science entirely) is a question the book does not resolve. Isaacson's position, implied rather than declared, leans toward collective institutional reform.

## THEORETICAL PROBLEM: Quantum Indeterminacy

### The Bohr-Einstein Debates
At the 1927, 1930, and 1935 Solvay Conferences Einstein proposed thought experiments designed to expose contradictions in the Copenhagen interpretation of quantum mechanics. His most famous challenge, the EPR paradox (with Boris Podolsky and Nathan Rosen, 1935), attempted to show that quantum mechanics must be incomplete if it allowed simultaneous definite values of non-commuting observables (position and momentum). Bohr replied with characteristic tetchiness. Einstein's position: a complete physical theory must describe physical reality independent of observation. Bohr's position: the act of measurement participates in defining reality.

### Evidence and Argumentation
Isaacson presents both sides with fairness. He traces Einstein's realism back to his earliest training in classical field theory and his philosophical reading of Spinoza and Ernst Mach. The debate, Isaacson shows convincingly, was not about experimental results but about metaphysical commitments: did the universe have an observer-independent character, or was the observer constitutive of it? Neither participant changed the other's mind.

### Unresolved Questions
Bell's theorem (1964) and subsequent experiments (Alain Aspect, 1982) progressively foreclosed Einstein's realist position, suggesting that quantum non-locality is an actual feature of the physical world. Yet foundational debates continue: Everett's many-worlds interpretation, Bohmian mechanics, and QBism all offer realist alternatives that Einstein, had he lived, might have found congenial. Isaacson correctly notes the debate is philosophical, not merely technical, and materials for continuing it have not exhausted themselves.

## Criticisms

**E. L. Schucking (Physics Today):** Schucking complained that Isaacson's simplifying treatment of physics deprived the reader of the actual mathematical content — equations — required to evaluate Einstein's arguments. Schucking is right that a reader relying on this biography alone cannot reconstruct E=mc² or the Einstein field equations. The tradeoff, Isaacson might reply, is accessibility.

**Matthew Stanley (Historical Studies in the Natural Sciences):** Stanley criticised Isaacson for overstating the individual genius thesis at the expense of collective scientific culture. He is partly right that Isaacson foregrounds Einstein at the expense of the Brillouin, Lorentz, and Grossmann network that made him possible. The criticism is less damning than Stanley implies because Isaacson gives enough institutional context to satisfy a moderate reader.

**Some Goodreads reviewers (anecdotal):** Several readers on Goodreads charged Isaacson with dedicating excessive space to Einstein's love life with Mileva Marić and later Elsa, arguing it intruded into scientific content. The counterargument is that the love letters illuminate Einstein's working habits, his emotional economy, and his sense of betrayal — all relevant to understanding what drove him intellectually.

## Alternative Solutions and What Other Thinkers Have Said

**On unification:** Niels Bohr argued that Einstein's mistake was seeking a complete classical description where quantum mechanics required probabilistic complementarity. Richard Feynman, more generously, treated Einstein's geometric program as laying the intellectual infrastructure for quantum field theory, even if it did not succeed as Einstein intended.

**On nuclear weapons:** The Russell-Einstein Manifesto proposed conflict resolution through world government, an institutional solution. Hans Bethe, who ran the theoretical division at Los Alamos, argued after the war for a technical arms-control solution — verification mechanisms — rather than institutional reform. Einstein favoured institutional reform; history has generally validated Bethe's more practical approach while accepting Einstein's diagnosis of the underlying problem.

**On quantum foundations:** David Bohm proposed in 1952 a deterministic hidden-variable theory — a fully realist quantum mechanics — that preserves Einstein's philosophical commitments while rejecting EPR's assumption of locality. John Bell would later show Bohm's theory was non-local but entirely consistent; it remains a live alternative to Copenhagen.

## Conclusion of the Analytical Case

Einstein: His Life and Universe documents a life in which the key problems of twentieth-century physics and politics converged upon a single individual. Isaacson's biography treats these convergences faithfully, if at uneven scientific depth. The open questions — what is a complete physical theory, how can scientists discharge moral responsibility to the public — remain with us, sharpened by climate science, AI, and nuclear proliferation. Einstein's life, as Isaacson renders it, remains an argument rather than a conclusion.
