Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman! is not a conventional autobiography. Assembled from tape-recorded conversations between Richard Feynman and his friend Ralph Leighton, it reads less like a life story and more like a series of campfire tales told by a man who found the entire universe — from quantum electrodynamics to the inside of a safe at Los Alamos — equally fascinating. The anecdotes jump from fixing radios by pure reasoning as a teenager to crashing a Nobel ceremony, from picking up women in Brazilian bars to testifying before the Rogers Commission on the Challenger disaster. What holds them together is not chronology but character: Feynman's refusal to accept anything on authority, his compulsion to understand things from first principles, and his evident delight in the sheer strangeness of being alive.

Published in 1985 when Feynman was already a cultural icon, the book became an unlikely bestseller, introducing a generation of readers to a physicist who played the bongo drums, painted nudes, and insisted that "the first principle is that you must not fool yourself — and you are the easiest person to fool." Its continued popularity reflects a hunger for a model of intellectual life that is rigorous without being solemn, joyful without being shallow.

The title comes from a moment at Princeton when the dean's wife, meeting Feynman at a tea party, asked whether he wanted cream or lemon in his tea, and then, noticing his fidgeting, asked what he did. "I'm a physicist," Feynman replied. She sighed: "Oh. That's a social problem. Surely you're joking, Mr. Feynman." He was not joking — he was simply incapable of being the kind of scientist she expected.
