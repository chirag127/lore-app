## Key Problem

The central problem *The Lord of the Rings* addresses is the nature of power and its relationship to moral agency. How can a free people resist an overwhelmingly powerful evil without becoming evil themselves? The problem is both political and spiritual: Sauron cannot be defeated by conventional means because any army that challenges him directly is destroyed, and any weapon powerful enough to defeat him would corrupt its user. The Ring cannot be used — it can only be renounced or destroyed. This creates a dilemma that runs through the entire work: the tools of resistance are themselves instruments of corruption.

## Proposed Solution

Tolkien's answer is that evil is defeated not by strength but by renunciation, not by power but by humility. The Ring is destroyed not by a heroic act of will but by a chain of seemingly weak choices: Bilbo's pity for Gollum, Frodo's mercy, Gollum's obsessive greed. The solution is a kind of negative capability — the ability to act without power, to resist without dominating, to suffer without retaliating. This is given Christian theological resonance in Tolkien's concept of eucatastrophe: the good outcome is not earned by the hero but granted as a grace, though the hero's choices have prepared the way. At the political level, the solution is the restoration of legitimate monarchy (Aragorn's coronation) and the rejection of industrial despotism (the Scouring of the Shire).

## Evidence & Argumentation

The argument is made narratively across six books. The failure of every attempt to use the Ring (Isildur, Gollum, Boromir, Frodo at the last moment) constitutes the negative evidence; the success of renunciation (Sam's return of the Ring, Frodo's mercy toward Gollum, Galadriel's refusal) constitutes the positive evidence. The most powerful piece of evidence is Frodo's failure at Mount Doom — even the most virtuous character, after a year of bearing the Ring, cannot resist its final temptation. The Ring is destroyed only because an external force (Gollum's obsession) intervenes. This suggests that Tolkien does not believe human will alone is sufficient to overcome evil's attraction.

## Unresolved Questions

The novel leaves several questions open. What happens to the elves after they depart Middle-earth? Tolkien's later writings suggest they go to a kind of purgatorial dwelling, but the details are sketchy. More significantly, the novel does not fully resolve what happens to the political order after Aragorn's death — his descendants are named, but the durability of his restoration is not tested. The fate of the hobbits who can never fully return to innocence (Frodo's departure, Sam's eventual departure implied in the appendices) suggests that some wounds are permanent, but the nature of Frodo's healing in the West is left mysterious. The origin of orcs — an intractable problem in Tolkien's legendarium — troubled him until his death, and the published text deliberately leaves it vague.

## Criticisms

The most persistent criticism of the novel's problem-solution structure concerns the orcs. Tolkien wrestled with the question of whether orcs are inherently evil or corrupted beings, and the novel never resolves it. If orcs are irredeemably evil, the novel's moral framework becomes a racial one, uncomfortably close to the ideologies Tolkien opposed. If orcs can be redeemed, why are they slaughtered without mercy? This is the problem Michael Moorcock identified as political conservatism: the novel's moral geography is too neat, with the good peoples in the west and the evil forces in the east echoing colonial cartography. China Miéville's charge of racial essentialism — that the "good" peoples are fair-skinned and the "evil" ones are dark-skinned, slant-eyed, and described in terms that evoke racist caricatures — has been debated extensively by Tolkien scholars. John Garth and Dimitra Fimi have argued that Tolkien's racial categories are linguistic rather than biological, grounded in his philological interests rather than any deliberate racial hierarchy. They point to the diversity within the Free Peoples (the swarthy Haradrim are men, not orcs) and Tolkien's own condemnation of racial prejudice in his letters. Nevertheless, the text's surface-level racial coding — black Numenoreans, swarthy Easterlings, pale good elves, dark evil orcs — remains a liability for many modern readers.

A separate line of criticism concerns the novel's treatment of women, which goes beyond mere underrepresentation. Catherine Stimpson notes that the female characters who wield agency — Galadriel and Eowyn — ultimately submit to male authority: Galadriel passes her test and retreats into the West; Eowyn finds glory in battle only to fall into a healing domestic love with Faramir. Arwen exists almost entirely offstage, a reward for Aragorn rather than a person in her own right.

## Alternative Solutions

Other fantasy works have approached the problem of power differently. Le Guin's Earthsea operates on a Taoist framework: power is balance, not domination, and the wizard's task is to maintain equilibrium rather than defeat evil. Martin's A Song of Ice and Fire rejects Tolkien's moral clarity entirely, presenting power as a game in which good and evil are matters of perspective and survival matters more than principle. Pullman's His Dark Materials directly challenges Tolkien's Christian framework, offering a liberating rebellion against divine authority rather than humble submission to providence. Each alternative represents a fundamentally different answer to the problem of how power should be understood and resisted.

## Practical Applications

*The Lord of the Rings* offers a framework for thinking about real-world political and ethical problems. Its critique of industrial despoliation — Saruman's factories, the Shire under occupation — is directly applicable to environmentalism. Its anti-totalitarian theme — the rejection of a single power that attempts to dominate all others — resonates with critiques of authoritarianism and surveillance states. The insight that mercy is strategic, not merely sentimental, has applications in conflict resolution and restorative justice. The novel's insistence that small acts of resistance by ordinary people matter — that Samwise Gamgee, a gardener, is the true hero of the story — has been cited by activists and soldiers alike. American soldiers in Iraq and Afghanistan frequently reported reading Tolkien for its depiction of asymmetric warfare and the burden of carrying a dangerous responsibility through hostile territory.
