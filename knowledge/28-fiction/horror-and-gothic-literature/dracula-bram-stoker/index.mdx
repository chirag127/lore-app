Bram Stoker's *Dracula* is the definitive vampire novel, the cornerstone of modern horror, and one of the most adapted works in literary history. Published in 1897, the novel uses an epistolary structure — diaries, letters, ships' logs, newspaper clippings, and phonograph recordings — to tell the story of Count Dracula's journey from his ancient Transylvanian castle to Victorian England, and the desperate campaign to destroy him. More than a century after its publication, *Dracula* has never been out of print and has shaped the popular conception of vampires more than any other single work. The novel functions on multiple levels simultaneously: as a gripping horror narrative, as a document of Victorian sexual and racial anxiety, as an exploration of the limits of scientific rationalism, and as a drama of communal resistance against an ancient and seductive evil.
