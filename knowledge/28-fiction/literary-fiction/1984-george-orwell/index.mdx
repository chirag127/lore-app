Few novels have imprinted themselves on the language and political imagination of the twentieth century as deeply as George Orwell's *Nineteen Eighty-Four*. Published in 1949, the novel gives us the permanent vocabulary — Big Brother, thoughtcrime, doublethink, Newspeak, Room 101 — through which we still process the dangers of state overreach. But the book is not merely a prophecy about Soviet-style tyranny; it is a surgical examination of how power operates when it no longer pretends to legitimacy. Orwell shows us a regime that has abandoned even the rhetoric of liberation, one that openly declares its purpose: to hold power for its own sake.
