Few novels have reshaped science fiction as thoroughly as Frank Herbert's *Dune*. Published in 1965, it won both the Hugo and Nebula awards and inaugurated a new kind of speculative fiction — one in which ecology, politics, religion, and human potential are braided into a single narrative as dense as the spice melange that drives its plot. Set 20,000 years in the future, the novel follows young Paul Atreides as his noble family is drawn into a trap on the desert planet Arrakis, the sole source of the universe's most precious resource. What begins as a story of dynastic betrayal becomes a meditation on power, prophecy, and the terrible cost of leadership. Herbert built a world so complete — with its own languages, religions, economic systems, and ecology — that readers have been returning to Arrakis for over sixty years, finding new layers each time.
