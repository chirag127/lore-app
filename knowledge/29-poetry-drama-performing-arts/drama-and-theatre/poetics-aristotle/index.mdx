No work of literary criticism has shaped Western thinking about literature and drama more profoundly than Aristotle's *Poetics*, composed around 335 BCE. In its surviving twenty-six chapters, Aristotle lays out the first systematic account of what poetry is, how it works, and why it matters — introducing concepts such as mimesis, catharsis, hamartia, peripeteia, and anagnorisis that remain central to how we analyse stories today. Written partly as a response to Plato's condemnation of poetry in the *Republic*, the *Poetics* defends art by demonstrating its cognitive and emotional power.
