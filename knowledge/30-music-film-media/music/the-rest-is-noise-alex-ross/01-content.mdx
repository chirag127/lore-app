## Part I: The Last of the Romantics

### Chapter 1: The Golden Age

Ross opens with a portrait of fin-de-siecle Vienna and the premiere of Strauss's opera *Salome* in 1905. He evokes the opulent, overheated atmosphere of prewar Europe, where tonality was reaching its limits and the old certainties were crumbling. Strauss represents the end of the Romantic tradition -- music of extreme emotional expression and orchestral opulence.

### Chapter 2: Doctor Faustus

The story of Arnold Schoenberg and the birth of atonality. Ross traces Schoenberg's journey from late-Romantic composer to inventor of the twelve-tone method. He argues that atonality was not an arbitrary rejection of tradition but a logical response to the exhaustion of the tonal system. Schoenberg emerges as a tragic figure -- a conservative revolutionary who wanted nothing more than to continue the German tradition.

### Chapter 3: The Great War

World War I shattered the old world and with it the old music. Ross shows how composers responded to the trauma of war: some retreated into neoclassicism (Stravinsky), others into mysticism (Sibelius), others into increasingly radical experimentation (Schoenberg, Berg, Webern). The war broke the continuity of the German-Austrian tradition that had dominated Western music for two centuries.

## Part II: The Twentieth Century

### Chapter 4: The Cult of the Composer

Ross examines the relationship between music and politics in the 1920s and 1930s. In the Soviet Union, Shostakovich navigated the treacherous waters of Stalinist cultural policy. In Germany, the Nazis condemned modernist music as degenerate while promoting a sanitized Romanticism. In America, Copland created a populist, accessible modernist style.

### Chapter 5: The Synthetic Age

The postwar avant-garde: Stockhausen, Boulez, Cage, and others. Ross traces the radical experiments of the 1950s and 1960s -- total serialism, indeterminacy, electronic music, chance operations. He is sympathetic to the avant-garde's ambitions but critical of its dogmatism and isolation from audiences.

### Chapter 6: The Rise of Minimalism

Minimalism emerged as a reaction against the complexity and obscurity of the postwar avant-garde. Ross profiles La Monte Young, Terry Riley, Steve Reich, and Philip Glass, showing how their radical simplification of musical language created a new classical music that was both intellectually serious and accessible.

### Chapter 7: The End of the Century

Ross concludes with the pluralism of the late twentieth century: the return to tonality, the blending of classical and popular traditions, the globalization of classical music, and the role of new technology. He profiles composers like John Adams, Kaija Saariaho, and Thomas Ades, showing how the twenty-first century inherits a tradition that is no longer unified by any single style or ideology.

## Reading Guide

### Sufficiency Assessment
This summary captures the major narrative arcs and key figures. What is compressed is the richness of Ross's musical analysis and the depth of his historical contextualization.

### Recommended Reading Path
| Reader Type | Time | What to Read |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | ~15 min | This summary |
| Interested | ~8 hr | Chapters 1, 2, 3, 4, 6 |
| Scholar/Practitioner | ~20 hr | Full book with listening |

### Chapters to Read in Full
- **Chapter 2 (Doctor Faustus)** -- The heart of the book, on Schoenberg and atonality
- **Chapter 4 (The Cult of the Composer)** -- Music and politics in the age of dictators

### Chapters to Skim or Skip
- **Chapter 5 (The Synthetic Age)** -- Dense and less narratively compelling

### What You'll Miss by Not Reading the Full Book
- Ross's brilliant musical descriptions that make you hear the music on the page
- The political and cultural context for each composer
- The listening guide and recommended recordings
