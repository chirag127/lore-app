## How People See

Weinschenk opens with the visual system, because perception is the foundation of design. She covers key findings: peripheral vision is used more than foveal vision for getting the gist of a scene; people see patterns and gestalts before details; faces attract immediate attention; color influences perception differently across cultures. Designers can use these insights to direct attention, create visual hierarchy, and ensure key information is perceived accurately.

## How People Read

People do not read letter by letter — they recognize whole word shapes. This is why ALL CAPS text is harder to read (it removes word shape cues). People scan in an F-pattern (on text-heavy Western pages), reading the first line fully, the second line less, and the rest in a vertical scan. Designers should structure content to support scanning: meaningful headings, bullet points, highlighted keywords.

## How People Remember

Memory operates in three stages: sensory memory (very brief), working memory (limited capacity, about 4 items), and long-term memory (vast but fallible). Working memory limitations mean users can hold only about four pieces of information at once — a key constraint for interface design. Recognition is much more reliable than recall, supporting the design principle of making options visible rather than requiring users to remember commands.

## How People Think

Thinking is mostly unconscious. The conscious mind handles about 40 bits per second while the unconscious processes 11 million. Most decisions are made unconsciously, then rationalized consciously. This has profound implications for design: visual appeal, first impressions, and emotional responses matter more than rational arguments. Weinschenk reviews Kahneman's System 1/System 2 framework and explains its design implications.

## How People Decide

People are not rational decision-makers. They are influenced by framing effects (how choices are presented), loss aversion (losses loom larger than gains), anchoring (first information encountered sets reference point), and social proof (what others do guides decisions). Designers can structure choices, defaults, and information presentation to help users make better decisions.

## How People Get Motivated

Motivation depends on autonomy (the desire for self-direction), mastery (the desire to get better at something), and purpose (the desire to do something meaningful). Weinschenk covers intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation, the power of progress (a sense of forward momentum is highly motivating), and the role of rewards in shaping behavior.

## Social Psychology and Design

People are deeply social creatures influenced by social norms, reciprocity, and authority. Weinschenk covers the influence of social validation (reviews, ratings, testimonials), the power of personal stories over statistics, and how the mere presence of other people (even virtual) changes behavior.
