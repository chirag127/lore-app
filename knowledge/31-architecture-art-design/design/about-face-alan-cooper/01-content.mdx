## Goal-Directed Design

Cooper's foundational methodology: design starts with understanding user goals at a deep level. Goals are not tasks — a task is "transfer funds between accounts" while the underlying goal is "feel in control of my finances." Goal-directed design uses ethnographic research to uncover these fundamental human motivations and then designs digital products that serve them. Cooper distinguishes three types of goals: life goals (who the user wants to be), experience goals (how the user wants to feel), and end goals (what the user wants to accomplish).

## Personas: The Foundation of Design

Personas are detailed, fictional user archetypes based on research. Each persona has a name, demographic details, goals, behaviors, skills, and frustrations. Cooper argues that personas are the single most powerful design tool because they make abstract user research concrete and personal. When a team debates design decisions, asking "What would Sarah do?" focuses the discussion. Primary personas (the primary target user) define the interface; secondary personas may have additional needs but should not drive core design.

## Scenarios and Requirements

Scenarios are stories about how personas interact with a product to achieve their goals. They are written before any design work begins, describing ideal experiences without technical constraints. From scenarios, the team derives functional requirements — what the product must do. This process ensures requirements serve user goals rather than technical convenience or feature checklists.

## Design Principles

Cooper provides extensive design principles for digital products: reduce cognitive load, design for the likely case first, provide clear navigation paths, use appropriate interface controls, provide feedback for every action, and design for forgiveness. He distinguishes between postures — how a product behaves in relation to the user. Productivity applications should be sovereign (fill the screen, demand attention). Utility applications should be transient (appear when needed, disappear when done).

## Interaction Patterns and Controls

The book catalogs interaction patterns for common design challenges: data entry, navigation, search, forms, notifications. Cooper provides guidelines for selecting appropriate controls (buttons, menus, dialogs, palettes), organizing information, and managing user attention. He also discusses visual design considerations like layout, typography, and color from an interaction perspective.

## Testing and Iteration

Unlike Cooper's earlier *Inmates*, which focused on organizational change, *About Face* provides detailed guidance on design validation. Cooper advocates for formative usability testing throughout the design process, not just at the end. He emphasizes that design is iterative — personas, scenarios, and designs must be refined based on user feedback.
