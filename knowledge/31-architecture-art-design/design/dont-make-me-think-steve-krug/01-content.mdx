## Don't Make Me Think

Krug's first and most important principle is the title itself. A web page should be self-evident — the user should be able to understand what it is, how it works, and where to go without a moment of thought. Every question mark — "Is this clickable?" "Where am I?" "What does this do?" — represents a failure of design. Krug argues that users do not leave sites because they are hard to use; they leave because they feel stupid. Self-evident pages make users feel smart.

## How We Really Use the Web: Scanning, Satisficing, and Mudding Through

Krug debunks the myth that users carefully read and evaluate websites before making decisions. In reality, users scan pages (looking for words or phrases that match their goal), satisfice (choose the first reasonable option rather than the optimal one), and muddle through (figure things out just enough to accomplish the task). These behaviors are not laziness — they are efficient strategies for dealing with information overload. Good design accommodates these behaviors rather than fighting them. Krug introduces "billboard design" — making pages scannable through clear visual hierarchies, effective headings, and concise content.

## Navigation Design

Navigation is the most critical element of any website. Krug devotes several chapters to navigation principles: tell users where they are, where they can go, and how to get back. He advocates for persistent navigation (consistent across the site), clear section labels, and visible indicators of current location. The "trunk test" is his memorable method for testing navigation: if someone drops into any page on your site, can they figure out within seconds what site it is, what page they are on, and what the major sections are?

## The Home Page and Content Design

Krug discusses the unique role of the home page — it must convey the site's purpose, identity, and personality while providing access to everything. He recommends that home pages avoid carousels, minimize promotional content, and prioritize the most important user tasks. On content pages, Krug's rule is ruthless: get rid of half the words, then get rid of half of what remains. Happy talk (marketing fluff) is the first thing to eliminate.

## Usability Testing on a Budget

Krug makes a compelling case that usability testing does not need to be expensive or elaborate. Testing with three users early in the design process reveals more problems than testing with dozens of users late in the process. He provides a practical guide for low-budget usability testing: recruit representative users, give them specific tasks, watch where they struggle, and fix the problems. The key insight is that any testing is better than no testing.
