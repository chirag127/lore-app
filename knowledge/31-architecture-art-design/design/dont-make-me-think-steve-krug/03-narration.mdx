## Writing Style & Voice

Krug writes like a friendly consultant explaining things over coffee. The voice is direct, humorous, and self-deprecating. He uses cartoons, jokes, and personal anecdotes to keep the tone light. The book famously opens: "If I had a nickel for every time I've heard the phrase 'Don't make me think'..." The writing is deliberately non-academic — no footnotes, no citations, no jargon.

## Narrative Structure

Each chapter is short (5-10 pages) and self-contained, making the book easy to read in short sessions. The first half introduces principles; the second half provides practical methods. Chapters end with "Krug's first law of usability" or similar takeaways that are easy to remember.

## Rhetorical Techniques

Krug uses humor effectively to lower resistance. He also uses vivid contrasts (designer fantasy vs. user reality) to make points memorable. His most powerful technique is the confession of his own mistakes — "I once designed a site where..." — which makes his expertise feel earned rather than assumed.

## Readability & Accessibility

The most accessible book on the list. Written at a popular reading level, with short sentences, plenty of white space, and supporting illustrations. Can comfortably be read in 2-3 hours. No technical background required.

## Comparative Context

Krug followed this with *Rocket Surgery Made Easy* (2010), an expanded guide to usability testing. His work sits in the popular practitioner tradition alongside Jared Spool's writings and Luke Wroblewski's *Mobile First*. Among the books in this directory, *Don't Make Me Think* is the most accessible and the most limited in scope.
