Don Norman's *The Design of Everyday Things* is the rare book that permanently changes how you see the world. First published in 1988 as *The Psychology of Everyday Things*, it introduced concepts like affordances, signifiers, and the seven stages of action that have become the lingua franca of design thinking.

The book's central insight is that poorly designed objects are not the user's fault. When doors confuse, faucets baffle, and stoves mystify, the failure lies in design that ignores the cognitive and perceptual capabilities of the people who use them.
