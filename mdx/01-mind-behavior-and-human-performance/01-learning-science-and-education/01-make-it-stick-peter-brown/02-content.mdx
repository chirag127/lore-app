## Core Concepts

### Retrieval Practice

The most important finding in the book: **pulling information out of memory is more effective for learning than putting it in**. When you try to recall a fact, concept, or procedure, you strengthen the neural pathways that lead to it. This is why self-testing with flashcards, practice questions, or free recall is dramatically more effective than re-reading notes.

### Spaced Repetition

Cramming creates short-term fluency that vanishes quickly. Spacing study sessions over time—hours, days, or weeks apart—forces the brain to work harder to retrieve the information, which strengthens memory. Each time you revisit material just before you're about to forget it, you rebuild the memory trace more strongly.

### Interleaving

Rather than studying one topic completely before moving to the next (blocking), interleaving mixes different topics or problem types together. This makes learning feel harder because you have to discriminate between approaches, but it produces much better ability to apply the right solution to the right problem.

### Desirable Difficulties

Difficulties that require effort to overcome—like generating answers rather than recognizing them, or solving problems without examples—are "desirable" because they trigger deeper processing. The key insight: if learning feels easy, you're probably not learning much.

### Elaboration and Generation

Explaining new material in your own words (elaboration) and trying to solve problems before being shown the solution (generation) both create richer mental models that are more accessible later.

## Practical Applications

- Replace re-reading with active recall: close the book and summarize what you remember
- Use spaced repetition systems like Anki or manual schedules (1 day, 7 days, 30 days)
- Mix up practice problems from different chapters rather than doing all of one type
- Teach what you're learning to someone else to force elaboration
- Test yourself frequently even before you feel ready

## Key Questions

1. How often do you re-read notes versus testing yourself?
2. Are you spacing your review sessions or cramming?
3. Could you replace blocked practice with interleaved practice?
4. Are you embracing the feeling of difficulty as a sign of learning?
