## Analysis

### Historical Context

Published in 2014, *Make It Stick* arrived during a growing movement to bridge cognitive psychology research and classroom practice. The "learning styles" myth was still dominant in education, and evidence-based study skills were not widely taught. The book was part of a wave that included Benedict Carey's *How We Learn* (same year) and the growing popularity of spaced repetition systems like Anki.

### Author Background

Peter C. Brown is a writer, not a psychologist, which gives the book strong narrative clarity. Henry Roediger and Mark McDaniel are prominent cognitive psychologists at Washington University in St. Louis, both specializing in memory research. Roediger is particularly known for his work on retrieval-induced forgetting and the testing effect. The combination of journalist and researchers produces both rigor and readability.

### Key Arguments

The book's central argument—that common study strategies are ineffective—is well-supported by decades of research. The testing effect (retrieval practice improves retention) is one of the most robust findings in all of cognitive psychology. Spacing effects have been replicated across hundreds of studies. The concept of desirable difficulties has strong theoretical grounding.

### Strengths

Exceptionally well-grounded in research. Each principle is supported by multiple studies, clearly explained, and illustrated with concrete examples. The book directly challenges deeply held intuitions about learning, which makes it genuinely transformative for many readers.

### Weaknesses

The book sometimes overstates the generalizability of laboratory findings to real-world learning environments. Some critics argue that retrieval practice works better for factual knowledge than for complex conceptual understanding. The writing can be repetitive, and the examples occasionally feel forced.

### Reception

*Make It Stick* has been widely praised by cognitive scientists and educators. It has influenced curriculum design at medical schools and universities. Some criticism has come from educators who argue that the book's prescriptions are difficult to implement in traditional classroom settings with fixed curricula and assessment schedules.
