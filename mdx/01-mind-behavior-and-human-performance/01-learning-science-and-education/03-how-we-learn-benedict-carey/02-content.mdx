## Core Concepts

### The Forgetting Curve

Carey explains Ebbinghaus's classic finding that memory decays exponentially over time unless reinforced. The counterintuitive insight: forgetting is not a bug but a feature. The act of forgetting and re-learning strengthens memory more than perfect initial retention would.

### The Spacing Effect

One of the most robust findings in all of psychology: information learned in multiple, spaced sessions is retained far longer than the same information learned in a single session. Carey provides the research background and practical guidance for implementing spaced study.

### The Testing Effect

Retrieving information from memory changes the memory itself, making it stronger and more accessible. Carey explains why self-testing is not just assessment but the very mechanism of learning.

### Sleep and Learning

Sleep actively consolidates memories, transferring them from temporary hippocampal storage to more permanent cortical storage. Carey reviews the research showing that sleep after learning dramatically improves retention and that sleep before learning prepares the brain to absorb new information.

### Perceptual Learning

The brain can learn to make subtle perceptual discriminations through exposure alone, without explicit instruction. This has implications for developing expertise in fields ranging from radiology to wine tasting.

## Practical Applications

- Use recall practice: close the book and write down what you remember
- Study in multiple locations—contextual variation strengthens memory
- Take breaks and sleep between study sessions
- Don't re-read immediately; allow yourself to forget first
- Test yourself before you feel ready
