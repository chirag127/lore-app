## Core Concepts

### The Illusion of Attention

We believe we notice most of what happens in our field of vision. In reality, attention is highly selective. When focused on a task—counting basketball passes—we can miss a gorilla walking through the scene. This isn't a failure of vision but a feature of how attention works.

### The Illusion of Memory

Memory is not a recording. It is reconstructed each time we recall it, and it changes in the process. The authors show how false memories can be implanted, how confident eyewitnesses can be completely wrong, and why we should never trust our memory for details.

### The Illusion of Confidence

Confidence is not correlated with accuracy. The most confident eyewitness is no more likely to be correct than the hesitant one. The book shows why we should ignore confidence when evaluating testimony.

### The Illusion of Knowledge

We think we understand how things work far more than we actually do. The "illusion of explanatory depth" means we can't explain even everyday objects (toilets, zippers) in detail, yet we feel we understand them.

### The Illusion of Cause

We are pattern-seeking creatures who see causal relationships where none exist. The book explores superstitious thinking, conspiracy theories, and the human tendency to find meaning in random patterns.

### The Illusion of Potential

Brain training, IQ enhancement, and "10% of your brain" myths fall under this illusion. The authors debunk claims about cognitive enhancement and show why most brain-training products don't deliver on their promises.
