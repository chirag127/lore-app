## Core Concepts

### The Central Bottleneck of Attention

The brain can only process a limited amount of information at once. Multitasking is a myth—what we actually do is task-switch rapidly, which incurs a switching cost. Levitin explains the neuroscience of attention and why focused work produces better results than constant context-switching.

### The Four-Category Sorting Method

For any decision or information management task, Levitin recommends sorting items into four categories: keep (requires action), archive (reference for later), delegate (someone else should handle), or delete (no value). This framework reduces decision fatigue and simplifies complex situations.

### Memory Systems

The book explains the difference between working memory (limited, easily overwhelmed) and long-term memory (vast but slow). Levitin shows how understanding these systems allows you to design better organizational strategies.

### The Organizing Principles of the Brain

The brain organizes information by category, by salience, and by narrative. Effective external organization systems should mirror these principles. Levitin applies this to organizing physical spaces, digital files, email, time, and social obligations.

## Practical Applications

- Use the four-category method for email, tasks, and physical clutter
- Block time for focused work and batch routine tasks
- Create decision routines to avoid decision fatigue
- Keep a "maybe later" list separate from active tasks
- Review and archive regularly to prevent accumulation
