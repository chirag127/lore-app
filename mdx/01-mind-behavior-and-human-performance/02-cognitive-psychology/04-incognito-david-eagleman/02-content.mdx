## Core Concepts

### The Unconscious Brain

Most neural activity is unconscious. The conscious mind is aware of only a tiny fraction of what the brain is doing. Eagleman estimates that conscious processing accounts for perhaps 1% of brain activity—the other 99% runs on autopilot.

### Neural Competition

The brain is not a unified system but a collection of competing neural networks that vie for control of behavior. Different networks handle different tasks, and they don't always agree. This explains internal conflict and the feeling of being "of two minds."

### Perception as Controlled Hallucination

The brain doesn't passively receive sensory information—it actively constructs a model of reality and checks it against sensory input. What we perceive is the brain's best guess, not objective reality. This explains optical illusions, phantom limbs, and why different people can perceive the same event differently.

### The Neuroscience of Blame

If behavior is determined by neural activity we don't control, what happens to personal responsibility? Eagleman explores the legal and ethical implications, arguing that understanding neural determinism should make us more compassionate and change how we think about punishment.

## Key Experiments

- Split-brain patients showing that the left brain creates narratives to explain actions the right brain took independently
- Libet's experiments showing neural activity precedes conscious decision by milliseconds
- Cases of Capgras syndrome where people recognize faces but lack emotional response
