## Core Concepts

### The Collaboration

Kahneman and Tversky met in the 1960s at Hebrew University in Jerusalem and began a collaboration that would last until Tversky's death in 1996. Their partnership was remarkably productive and intensely personal—they would spend hours in conversation, often finishing each other's sentences.

### Heuristics and Biases

Their framework showed that people rely on mental shortcuts (heuristics) that usually work but lead to systematic errors (biases). Key heuristics include representativeness (judging by similarity), availability (judging by ease of recall), and anchoring (fixing on initial information).

### Prospect Theory

Their most famous contribution, for which Kahneman won the Nobel Prize. Prospect theory showed that people evaluate gains and losses asymmetrically—losses hurt more than equivalent gains feel good (loss aversion). This contradicted the rational-actor model of economics.

### The Replication Crisis

Lewis touches on the early signs of psychology's replication crisis, noting that some of Kahneman and Tversky's findings have been difficult to replicate. Kahneman himself acknowledged this and encouraged the replication efforts.
