## Core Concepts

### Visual Perception as Construction

Sacks shows that vision is not a passive process of recording the world but an active construction. The brain takes incomplete sensory data and builds a coherent picture using memory, expectation, and inference. When parts of the visual system are damaged, the construction can break down in revealing ways.

### Neuroplasticity

The brain's ability to reorganize itself is a central theme. Sacks describes musicians who lose language but not musical ability, people who become blind and develop enhanced tactile perception, and individuals who learn to navigate the world without stereoscopic vision.

### Face Blindness

Prosopagnosia—the inability to recognize faces—reveals how specialized facial recognition is in the brain. Sacks discusses his own mild prosopagnosia and the cases of people who cannot recognize their own children.

### The Varieties of Visual Experience

Sacks explores the distinction between visual perception (seeing the world) and visual imagery (seeing in the mind's eye). Some people who become blind develop vivid visual imagery; others lose both. This dissociation reveals that these abilities use overlapping but distinct neural systems.
