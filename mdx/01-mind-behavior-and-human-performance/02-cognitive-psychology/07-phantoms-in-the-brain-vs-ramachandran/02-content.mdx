## Core Concepts

### Phantom Limbs and the Body Image

Ramachandran's most famous work: patients who have lost an arm still feel it—sometimes painfully. He developed the mirror box therapy, where reflecting the intact limb tricks the brain into seeing the phantom move, relieving paralysis and pain. This reveals that the body image is a dynamic construct in the brain.

### Mirror Neurons

Ramachandran's work on mirror neurons—cells that fire both when we perform an action and when we observe someone else performing it—leads to speculations about the evolution of language, culture, and empathy.

### Denial and Anosognosia

Patients with right-hemisphere damage may deny that their left arm is paralyzed, even when confronted with evidence. This reveals that the brain has a "left hemisphere interpreter" that constructs coherent narratives, sometimes at the expense of accuracy.

### Capgras Syndrome

Patients recognize faces but lack the emotional response that normally accompanies recognition, leading them to believe loved ones have been replaced by impostors. This reveals that face recognition has both cognitive and emotional components.

### Synesthesia

Some people experience cross-sensory perception—seeing sounds or tasting shapes. Ramachandran argues this may result from cross-activation between adjacent brain areas and may be more common than previously thought.
