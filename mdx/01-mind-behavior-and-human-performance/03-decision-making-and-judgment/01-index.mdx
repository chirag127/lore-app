## Overview

Decision Making and Judgment addresses perhaps the most practically important question in all of cognitive science: given the limitations and biases of the human mind, how can we make better decisions? This shelf covers the tools, frameworks, and habits of thought that improve judgment—from probabilistic forecasting to mental models to the systematic reduction of error.

It sits third in the category because it builds on cognitive psychology's understanding of how the mind works and applies it to the practical challenge of making good choices. Understanding cognitive biases (from subcategory 02) is necessary but not sufficient; you also need positive frameworks for better thinking.

## What's In This Shelf

- **Judgment under uncertainty**: how to make decisions when outcomes are uncertain
- **Forecasting and prediction**: the science and practice of accurate probability estimation
- **Mental models**: multidisciplinary frameworks for understanding complex systems
- **Decision hygiene**: systematic methods for reducing bias and noise in judgment
- **Rationality**: the tools of logic, probability, and critical thinking
- **Wisdom**: the synthesis of knowledge, experience, and good judgment

## What's Not In This Shelf

Books about specific **financial decision-making** (investing, portfolio management) belong in Money, Markets and Wealth. Books about **systems thinking** or **game theory** as standalone disciplines may belong elsewhere. The focus here is on individual judgment and decision processes.

## Reading Order

1. **Superforecasting** — The most practical entry point. Tetlock shows that accurate forecasting is a learnable skill built on specific habits of thinking, starting with probabilistic reasoning and humility.
2. **Noise** — The systematic companion to Thinking, Fast and Slow. Kahneman turns from bias to the equally damaging problem of inconsistency in judgment and how to fix it.
3. **Decisive** — The most actionable decision-making framework. The Heath brothers provide a step-by-step process (WRAP) for making better choices by overcoming the four villains of decision-making.
4. **Algorithms to Live By** — Applies computer science principles to everyday decisions. Christian and Griffiths show how optimal stopping, explore/exploit tradeoffs, and other algorithms map to human problems.
5. **Rationality** — Pinker's sweeping defense of reason and its tools. This provides the philosophical and cognitive foundations for why rationality matters and how to cultivate it.
6. **Seeking Wisdom** — A practical compendium of mental models organized as a decision-making manual. Bevelin draws on Darwin, Munger, and Einstein to show how clear thinking works.
7. **Poor Charlie's Almanack** — The culminating work on worldly wisdom. Munger's latticework of mental models represents the mature integration of multidisciplinary thinking for real-world judgment.

## Contribution to the Knowledge Tree

This shelf is the **practical application** of cognitive psychology to life's most consequential activity: making decisions. It bridges the gap between understanding how the mind works (subcategory 02) and actually thinking better. The mental models from this shelf reappear across investing, business, strategy, and every domain where judgment matters.
