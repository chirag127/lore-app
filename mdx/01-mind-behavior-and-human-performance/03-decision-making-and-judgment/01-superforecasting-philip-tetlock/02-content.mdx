## Core Concepts

### Probabilistic Thinking

Superforecasters don't predict that something will or won't happen; they assign probabilities. "There's a 70% chance X will happen" replaces "X will happen." This forces precision and makes it possible to learn from being wrong.

### The Fox and the Hedgehog

Drawing on Isaiah Berlin's essay, Tetlock distinguishes between hedgehogs (who know one big thing and apply it everywhere) and foxes (who know many small things and adapt). Foxes make better forecasts because they are more open to contradictory evidence and more likely to synthesize multiple perspectives.

### Bayesian Updating

Superforecasters update their beliefs incrementally as new evidence arrives, rather than making dramatic reversals or ignoring contradictory information. The book provides a practical introduction to Bayesian reasoning without the math.

### The Dragonfly Eye

Superforecasters view problems from multiple perspectives, just as a dragonfly's compound eye views the world from thousands of angles simultaneously. They synthesize information from diverse sources.

## Practical Applications

- Convert all predictions into probability ranges
- Break large questions into smaller, more tractable sub-questions
- Keep a forecasting diary to track accuracy and learn from mistakes
- Actively seek out information that could prove you wrong
- Update beliefs incrementally, not dramatically
