## Introduction: The Four Villains

The Heath brothers open by identifying four cognitive villains that undermine every decision: narrow framing (considering too few options), confirmation bias (seeking evidence that supports your preferred choice), short-term emotion (letting temporary feelings drive lasting decisions), and overconfidence (assuming you know how the future will unfold).

These villains are not exotic — they are wired into how humans naturally think. The WRAP process is a set of countermeasures, not a cure.

## Part I: Widen Your Options

The first villain is narrow framing — the tendency to see decisions as binary choices (do it or don't) when more options exist. The countermeasure is to actively seek alternatives.

**Multitracking**: Never consider fewer than two options. Even adding a terrible option (the "vanishing options test" — what would you do if neither current option were available?) forces a broader search.

**Opportunity Cost**: Every decision implicitly rejects alternatives. Making opportunity costs explicit reveals options that narrow framing hides.

**Ladder of Inference**: Climb down from conclusions to examine the data and assumptions beneath them.

## Part II: Reality-Test Your Assumptions

The second villain is confirmation bias — seeking evidence that supports our preferred choice and ignoring contradictory data. The countermeasure is to actively seek disconfirming evidence.

**Ooching**: Run small experiments to test assumptions before committing. Instead of deciding between two careers, do informational interviews. Instead of building a full product, run a pilot.

**The Pre-Mortem**: Imagine it's a year from now and your decision failed disastrously. Why? This technique surfaces risks that optimistic planning overlooks.

**Consider the Opposite**: Actively ask what would have to be true for the alternative option to be correct.

## Part III: Attain Distance Before Deciding

The third villain is short-term emotion — making decisions based on transient feelings. The countermeasure is to create psychological distance.

**10/10/10 Rule**: Ask how you'll feel about this decision in 10 minutes, 10 months, and 10 years.

**The "Best Friend" Test**: What advice would you give your best friend in this situation? This creates emotional distance by shifting perspective.

**Core Priorities**: Identify your deepest values and test decisions against them. Short-term emotions fade; core priorities persist.

## Part IV: Prepare to Be Wrong

The fourth villain is overconfidence — assuming we know how the future will unfold. The countermeasure is to prepare for a range of outcomes.

**Bookending**: Estimate a best case and worst case for any forecast. This reveals the range of uncertainty.

**Tripwires**: Set specific, measurable conditions that trigger a reconsideration of a decision. Tripwires prevent automatic persistence in failing courses of action.

**Safety Factor**: Build redundancy into plans to absorb negative surprises. This is cost-effective for high-consequence decisions.
