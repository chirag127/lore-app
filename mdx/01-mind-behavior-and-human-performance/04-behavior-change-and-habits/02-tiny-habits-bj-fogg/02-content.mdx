## The Fogg Behavior Model

BJ Fogg opens with his core formula: Behavior = Motivation x Ability x Prompt (B=MAP). All three elements must converge at the same moment for a behavior to occur. If any element is zero, the behavior will not happen. This model applies to every human behavior — brushing teeth, checking Instagram, writing a report.

**Motivation** fluctuates wildly. High motivation is unreliable for building habits. **Ability** is more stable and more leverageable. **Prompt** is the trigger — the most overlooked but most critical element.

The key insight: when a behavior does not happen, the solution is almost never to increase motivation. The solution is to make the behavior easier (increase ability) or to improve the prompt. This insight reverses the entire self-help paradigm.

## Tiny Habits: The Method

Fogg's method has three steps:

1. **Anchor** the new habit to an existing routine: "After I [existing habit], I will [new tiny behavior]."
2. **Make it tiny**: The behavior must take less than 30 seconds and require virtually no effort. Walking one block. Flossing one tooth. Writing one sentence.
3. **Celebrate immediately**: A genuine feeling of celebration — a fist pump, a "Yes!", a smile — releases dopamine and rewires the brain to associate the behavior with positive emotion.

The celebration is the most critical and most counterintuitive element. Fogg presents brain scan evidence showing that emotion accelerates habit formation more than repetition. A habit formed with celebration will stick faster than one performed dozens of times without emotional reward.

## The Magic of Baby Steps

The tiny behavior must feel ridiculous. Flossing one tooth. Doing one pushup. Reading one sentence. The reason is motivational physics: at this size, the behavior requires virtually no motivation, which means it will happen even on your worst days. And once started, the hardest part is over — the one pushup often becomes ten.

Fogg tells the story of a woman who wanted to start flossing. She began by flossing one tooth. Within weeks, she was flossing all her teeth, and the habit was automatic. The behavior did not grow because she increased motivation; it grew because the tiny behavior removed the barrier to starting.

## Ability Chain

When a behavior is not happening, trace the ability chain. Fogg identifies five factors that affect ability: time, money, physical effort, mental effort, and routine. The solution to any behavior problem is to reduce one of these barriers. Want to exercise more? Reduce physical effort (park farther away and walk). Want to cook more? Reduce time effort (use pre-cut vegetables).

## Prompt Design

Fogg distinguishes three types of prompts: person prompts (internal cues, least reliable), context prompts (external cues like alarms, moderately reliable), and action prompts (existing behaviors as anchors, most reliable). The Tiny Habits method uses action prompts exclusively — the "After I" formula.

## Breaking Bad Habits

To stop a bad behavior, Fogg recommends removing the prompt, making the behavior harder, or reducing your exposure to the motivation behind it. The same model applies in reverse — attack Ability and Prompt rather than trying to suppress motivation through willpower.

## The Role of Environment

Fogg devotes significant attention to environment design, arguing that small changes in your surroundings produce more behavior change than large commitments to yourself. Put the floss next to your toothbrush. Keep the running shoes by the door. The environment is the most permanent behavior modification tool.
