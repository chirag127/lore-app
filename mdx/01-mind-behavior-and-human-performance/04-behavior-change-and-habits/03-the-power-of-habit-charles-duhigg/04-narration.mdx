## Writing Style & Voice

Duhigg writes with the authority and clarity of an investigative journalist. His prose is confident, narrative-driven, and meticulously researched — each chapter reads like a long-form New Yorker article. He uses short, punchy sentences to deliver key insights and long, detailed paragraphs to build narrative momentum. The vocabulary is accessible (no technical jargon without explanation), and the tone is neutral rather than inspirational. Duhigg does not tell you to change your habits; he shows you how habits work and leaves the application to you.

His signature technique is the narrative hook — each chapter opens with a compelling story (a brain-damaged man who cannot form memories, a CEO who makes safety the only metric that matters) that frames the scientific content that follows. This structure journals non-fiction readers instantly recognize.

## Narrative Structure

The book is organized in three parts of increasing scope: the individual, the organization, and society. Each chapter within these parts follows a consistent template: story opening, scientific explanation, case study, and practical implication. The structure creates a satisfying intellectual progression from the micro (neurons) to the macro (social movements).

The narrative tension comes from the conflict between automaticity and choice. Duhigg sets up habits as powerful, invisible forces that control our lives, then provides the tools for reclaiming agency. The climax is the free will discussion in Chapter 9, where the philosophical stakes are made explicit.

## Rhetorical Techniques

Duhigg relies heavily on **ethos** — his Pulitzer Prize and New York Times affiliation establish credibility. He uses **pathos** through emotionally resonant case studies: the frustration of the Alabama bus boycott organizers, the tragedy of the Rhode Island Hospital patients, the transformation of Alcoholic Anonymous members. The **logos** comes from the neurological research, which Duhigg presents as more settled and definitive than it actually is.

His most effective device is the **repeated framework**. He introduces the habit loop in Chapter 1 and returns to it in every subsequent chapter, reinforcing the model until it becomes intuitive. The three-part repetition (cue, routine, reward) is mnemonic brilliance — readers cannot forget it.

## Readability & Accessibility

The book targets a general audience with a reading level appropriate for high school graduates and above. Technical terms (basal ganglia, dopamine, chunking) are defined on first use and then repeated. The diagrams are simple and clarifying. Each chapter ends with a summary paragraph that reinforces key points. The main barrier to accessibility is length (roughly 90,000 words) rather than complexity — the density of case studies can be overwhelming in a single sitting.

## Comparative Context

Among Duhigg's own works, The Power of Habit is the most culturally influential, though Smarter Faster Better is a stronger application guide. Compared to the genre, Duhigg is more journalistic than Clear (who writes as a teacher), less academic than Baumeister (who is writing about his own research), and more cautious than Gladwell (who makes bigger claims with less evidence). The Power of Habit sits at the sweet spot between popular science and practical guide, which explains its enduring appeal.
