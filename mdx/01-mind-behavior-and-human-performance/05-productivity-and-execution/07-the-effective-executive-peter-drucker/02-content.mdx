## Core Concepts

### Know Thy Time

Drucker's first practice: track where your time actually goes, identify time-wasting activities, and consolidate discretionary time into meaningful blocks. He argues that most executives have no idea how they spend their time and that the process of tracking and analysis is transformative.

### Focus on Contribution

Ask "What can I contribute that will significantly affect my organization's performance and results?" This shifts focus from effort and activity to output and results. Drucker argues that the effective executive asks this question constantly.

### Make Strengths Productive

Effective executives build on strengths—their own, their superiors', their colleagues', and their organization's. They don't spend time trying to fix weaknesses. This requires a systematic approach to identifying and deploying strengths.

### First Things First

Concentration is the key to results. Drucker advises focusing on a few major priorities and completing them one at a time. "If there is any one 'secret' of effectiveness," he writes, "it is concentration."

### Effective Decisions

Drucker's approach to decision-making is counterintuitive: start with opinions, not facts (facts come later); insist on disagreement as a tool for testing assumptions; focus on what is right before worrying about acceptability; and build implementation into the decision.

## Practical Applications

- Keep a time log for two weeks and analyze where time actually goes
- Ask at the start of each project: what contribution can I make here?
- Identify your key strengths and those of your team members
- Choose one priority and protect time for it
- Structure decisions around disagreement and alternative hypotheses
