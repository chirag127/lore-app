## Strengths

- **Extremely practical.** Unlike most memory books that spend too much time on history and theory, Dellis focuses on step-by-step instruction. Each technique comes with exercises and practice routines.
- **Everyday focus.** The book addresses things people actually want to remember — names, passwords, errands — rather than competition feats.
- **Credible author.** Dellis is a four-time USA Memory Champion. He has tested every technique he teaches.
- **Well-organized.** The structure by use case (names, passwords, speeches) makes it easy to find relevant content.

## Weaknesses

- **Light on science.** Readers wanting the neuroscience behind the techniques should look to *Moonwalking with Einstein* or academic sources.
- **Repetitive.** The same core techniques (visualization, association, memory palace) are applied across chapters, which can feel redundant.
- **Minimal coverage of forgetting.** The book focuses on encoding but says little about the science of forgetting or optimal review schedules (spaced repetition).
- **Associations feel forced.** Some of Dellis's ready-made name associations are culturally specific and may not work for all readers.

## Comparison with Moonwalking with Einstein

| Dimension | Moonwalking with Einstein | Remember It! |
|---|---|---|
| Genre | Narrative nonfiction | Instructional manual |
| Best for | Understanding and motivation | Learning the techniques |
| Science coverage | Good | Minimal |
| Practical exercises | Few | Many |
| Reading pleasure | High | Moderate |

## Overall Assessment

*Remember It!* is best read *after* *Moonwalking with Einstein* — the narrative provides motivation and context, while Dellis provides the instruction manual. Together they form a complete introduction to memory techniques.
