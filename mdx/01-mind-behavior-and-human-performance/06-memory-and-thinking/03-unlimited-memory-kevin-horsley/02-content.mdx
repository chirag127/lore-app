## Part 1: Concentration

Horsley opens with a provocative claim: most people do not have a memory problem — they have a concentration problem. We forget names because we were not truly listening when we heard them. We forget where we put our keys because we were on autopilot when we set them down.

Concentration exercises:
- **The one-minute attention exercise**: Focus on your breath for one minute. Every time your mind wanders, bring it back. This builds the mental muscle for sustained attention.
- **Observation practice**: Spend 30 seconds observing a room, then close your eyes and describe it in detail.
- **Active listening**: When someone speaks, repeat their words internally. Ask yourself: what are they really saying?

## Part 2: Visualization and Association

Horsley teaches a systematic approach to creating memorable images:

1. **Substitution**: Replace abstract concepts with concrete images. "Courage" becomes a lion. "Democracy" becomes a voting booth.
2. **Exaggeration**: Make images larger than life. A giant key for "important."
3. **Movement**: Add action to images. A dancing key is more memorable than a static one.
4. **Senses**: Engage all five senses. What does it look, sound, smell, feel, taste like?
5. **Humor**: Funny images stick better than serious ones.
6. **Vivid colors**: Rainbows are more memorable than grayscale.

## Part 3: The Link Method

To memorize a list, create a story linking each item to the next. For a shopping list (milk, bread, eggs, apples): Imagine a cow (milk) juggling loaves of bread, which are actually eggshells cracking open to reveal apple seeds. The more absurd the story, the better.

## Part 4: The PEG System

Pre-memorize "pegs" for numbers 1-10 using rhymes:
1 = bun, 2 = shoe, 3 = tree, 4 = door, 5 = hive, 6 = sticks, 7 = heaven, 8 = gate, 9 = vine, 10 = hen.

To memorize a numbered list, associate each item with its peg image. For a list where item 3 is "carrots," imagine a tree growing carrots instead of leaves.

## Part 5: The Memory Palace

Horsley's approach emphasizes speed. He recommends using locations you know intimately (your childhood home, your daily commute) and populating them with vivid images. The key to speed is practice — the first palace takes an hour to build; the tenth takes five minutes.
