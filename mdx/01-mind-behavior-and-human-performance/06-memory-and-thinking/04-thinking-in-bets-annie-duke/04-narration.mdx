## Writing Style & Voice

Duke writes in a conversational, accessible register that mirrors her public speaking style. Sentences are short and direct; vocabulary avoids jargon. Despite her academic background, she deliberately suppresses scholarly affectations. The tone is that of a wise, slightly irreverent mentor — someone who has seen it all at the poker table and is willing to share the lessons. She uses poker terms extensively (resulting, tilt, bad beat, fold) but defines them clearly on first use.

## Narrative Structure

The book follows a logical arc: diagnose the problem (resulting), introduce the solution (bet-framing), build the toolkit (journal, pod, pre-mortem), and reinforce through repetition. Each chapter opens with a poker anecdote or thought experiment, develops the conceptual framework, and closes with practical exercises. The structure is effective for learning but can feel formulaic by the later chapters. Duke relies heavily on the Pete Carroll example, returning to it multiple times — a technique that reinforces the concept but risks overuse.

## Rhetorical Techniques

Duke's primary rhetorical strategy is recalibration through metaphor. By persistently reframing decisions as bets, she rewires the reader's default cognitive framing. She uses what cognitive linguists call "conceptual metaphor" — mapping a concrete domain (poker) onto an abstract one (life decisions) to enable new reasoning. Her ethos rests on her dual credentials: PhD in cognitive psychology and WSOP bracelet. She occasionally deploys pathos through stories of poker losses and personal vulnerability.

## Readability & Accessibility

The book targets a general audience with no background in poker or decision science. The reading level is approximately 9th grade. Technical terms are introduced slowly and supported by examples. The chapters are short (15-20 pages), making the book easy to digest in short sessions. Duke includes summary boxes and bullet-point exercises at the end of each chapter.

## Comparative Context

*Thinking in Bets* is Duke's first solo book and remains her most influential. It established the voice she uses in *How to Decide* and *Quit* — direct, practical, poker-inflected. Compared to other decision science popularizers, Duke is less academic than Kahneman, less experimental than Ariely, less policy-oriented than Thaler, and more personal than Tetlock. Her poker background gives her a distinctive angle that no other writer in the space can claim.
