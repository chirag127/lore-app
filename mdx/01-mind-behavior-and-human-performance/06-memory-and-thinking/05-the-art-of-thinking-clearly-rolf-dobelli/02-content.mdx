## Selected Bias Summaries

### Confirmation Bias (Chapter 2)

We search for, interpret, and remember information that confirms our existing beliefs while ignoring contradictory evidence. Dobelli traces this to the brain's efficiency drive: confirming is easier than challenging. The fix: actively seek disconfirming evidence. Make a habit of asking "What evidence would change my mind?"

### The Sunk Cost Fallacy (Chapter 9)

We continue investing in a losing course of action because of what we have already invested. The past is gone — only future costs and benefits should matter. The fix: separate ego from investment. Ask: "If I had not already invested time/money, would I start now?"

### The Availability Heuristic (Chapter 14)

We overestimate the likelihood of events that come easily to mind. Plane crashes feel more common than car crashes because they are more vivid in memory. The fix: base probability estimates on statistics rather than memory.

### The Halo Effect (Chapter 22)

Our overall impression of a person influences our assessment of their specific traits. A physically attractive person is assumed to be smarter, kinder, more competent. The fix: evaluate individual traits independently — do not let a single positive quality color your entire assessment.

### Overconfidence Effect (Chapter 37)

We systematically overestimate our knowledge, abilities, and the accuracy of our predictions. 93% of American drivers consider themselves above average. The fix: calibrate confidence by asking "What is the probability that I am wrong?" and tracking prediction accuracy.

### The Spotlight Effect (Chapter 44)

We overestimate how much others notice and remember about us. That embarrassing moment? Most people did not see it, and those who did have already forgotten. The fix: realize that you are not the center of anyone else's narrative.

### Regression to the Mean (Chapter 53)

After an extreme event, the next event is likely to be less extreme. The best-performing salesperson one quarter almost always performs worse the next. The fix: do not mistake statistical noise for cause and effect.

## Practical Applications

| Domain | Key Biases to Watch For |
|---|---|
| Investing | Overconfidence, confirmation bias, sunk cost, availability |
| Relationships | Halo effect, self-serving bias, fundamental attribution error |
| Business decisions | Anchoring, groupthink, planning fallacy, survivorship bias |
| Hiring | Halo effect, similarity bias, contrast effect |
| Personal finance | Mental accounting, endowment effect, hyperbolic discounting |
