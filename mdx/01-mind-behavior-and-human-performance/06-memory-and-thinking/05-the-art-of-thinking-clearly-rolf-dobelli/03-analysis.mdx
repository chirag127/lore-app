## Strengths

- **Exceptionally accessible.** Each bias is explained in 3-4 pages with vivid examples. Perfect for browsing.
- **Broad coverage.** 99 biases is comprehensive without being overwhelming.
- **Practical format.** The short-chapter structure makes it easy to read one bias at a time.
- **Well-referenced.** Dobelli draws on legitimate research and cites sources at the end.

## Weaknesses

- **Lacks depth.** Each bias gets only 3-4 pages — enough for awareness but not deep understanding.
- **Prescriptions are thin.** The advice for avoiding each bias is often just "be aware of it" or "think differently."
- **No unifying framework.** Unlike Kahneman (System 1 vs System 2), Dobelli offers no overarching theory that connects the biases.
- **Pop-science gloss.** Some chapters oversimplify complex phenomena.
- **No systematic bias interaction.** In real life, biases compound — Dobelli treats them in isolation.

## Comparison with Thinking, Fast and Slow

| Dimension | The Art of Thinking Clearly | Thinking, Fast and Slow |
|---|---|---|
| Depth | Shallow (3-4 pages per bias) | Deep (full chapters per concept) |
| Coverage | 99 biases | ~20 key concepts |
| Readability | Very easy | Moderately dense |
| Framework | None (alphabetical) | System 1 vs System 2 |
| Best use | Reference and awareness | Deep understanding |

## Overall Assessment

Best used as a reference after reading *Thinking in Bets*. Dobelli's catalog helps you spot biases in the moment; Duke's framework helps you build systems to counteract them.
