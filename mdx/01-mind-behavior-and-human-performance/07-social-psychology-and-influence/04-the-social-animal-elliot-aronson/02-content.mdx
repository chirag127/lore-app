## Chapter 1: What Is Social Psychology?

Aronson defines social psychology as the scientific study of how people's thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are influenced by the actual, imagined, or implied presence of others. He distinguishes it from personality psychology (which studies individual differences) and sociology (which studies group-level phenomena). The interactionist perspective is central: behavior is a function of person and situation.

## Chapter 2: Conformity

**Asch's Line Judgment Experiments**: When subjects were asked to match line lengths in a group where confederates gave obviously wrong answers, 76% conformed at least once. Even when they knew the answer was wrong, they doubted their own perception.

**Milgram's Obedience Experiments**: 65% of subjects delivered what they believed were lethal shocks to a stranger when ordered by an authority figure. The most disturbing finding: subjects were not sadistic — they were ordinary people who could not bring themselves to defy authority.

**When do people resist?** The presence of a dissenter — even one who gives a different wrong answer — dramatically reduces conformity.

## Chapter 3: Mass Communication and Persuasion

The Yale Communication Research Program (Hovland et al.) established the key factors:
- **Source**: Credibility (expertise + trustworthiness) and attractiveness
- **Message**: Fear appeals work if they include a solution; one-sided vs two-sided arguments depend on audience education
- **Audience**: Intelligence, self-esteem, and involvement moderate persuasion effects
- **Channel**: Face-to-face is most persuasive; mass media works best for agenda-setting

## Chapter 4: Social Cognition

We are cognitive misers — we use mental shortcuts (heuristics) to conserve mental energy. The availability heuristic, representativeness heuristic, and affect heuristic all lead to systematic errors. Schemas (mental frameworks) shape how we interpret new information: we see what we expect to see.

## Chapter 5: Self-Justification (Cognitive Dissonance)

This is Aronson's specialty and the book's most detailed chapter. When behavior contradicts self-concept, dissonance arises — and people change their attitudes to reduce it. Key experiments:
- **$1/$20 Study**: Subjects paid $1 to lie rated the task as more enjoyable than those paid $20. The $20 subjects had sufficient justification; the $1 subjects reduced dissonance by changing their attitude.
- **Forbidden Toy Paradigm**: Children who were mildly threatened not to play with an attractive toy later devalued it more than children who were severely threatened.
- **Effort Justification**: The more effort you put into something, the more you value it.

## Chapter 6-9: Aggression, Prejudice, Attraction, Prosocial Behavior

Aronson covers the remaining topics with the same blend of classic experiments and real-world application. The overarching theme: social context shapes even our most personal experiences.
