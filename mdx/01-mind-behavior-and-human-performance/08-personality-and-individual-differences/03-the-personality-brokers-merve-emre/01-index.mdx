## Overview

*The Personality Brokers* is not a guide to personality types — it is a biography of the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator and the two remarkable women who created it. Katharine Briggs and her daughter Isabel Myers were passionate amateur psychologists with no formal training in psychometrics. Over decades, they built an instrument that would be taken by millions — despite nearly unanimous rejection by academic psychologists.

Merve Emre, a literary scholar at Oxford, recovered the untold story through archives, letters, and interviews. The result is a fascinating account of ambition, obsession, and the strange appeal of being told who you are.

## Executive Summary

**Part 1: The Mother** — Katharine Briggs, a brilliant autodidact, developed her own system of personality types before discovering Carl Jung. Her encounter with Jung's work was a revelation — she believed she had found the scientific validation she was seeking.

**Part 2: The Daughter** — Isabel Myers inherited her mother's obsession. During World War II, she saw an opportunity: personality typing could help Americans find the jobs where they would be most productive and happy.

**Part 3: The Instrument** — The long, strange journey of the MBTI from a parlor game to a corporate behemoth. Emre traces its adoption by businesses, universities, and the military.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The MBTI was created by amateurs.** Neither Briggs nor Myers had any training in statistics, psychometrics, or experimental psychology.

2. **The MBTI has no scientific validity.** Academic psychologists have consistently rejected it. The test does not predict job performance or any meaningful outcome.

3. **Its success is a story about culture, not science.** The MBTI thrives because people want simple, flattering categories for themselves.

4. **Personality testing raises ethical questions.** When tests are used for hiring, the line between self-understanding and social control blurs.

## Final Verdict

_**Rating: 8.5 / 10**_

*The Personality Brokers* is a brilliant, deeply researched, and surprisingly moving history. It is not an attack on the MBTI — it is a nuanced exploration of why we are so drawn to being categorized, and what that says about us. Essential reading alongside *Quiet*.
