## Part 1: The Mother

Katharine Briggs was a brilliant, eccentric woman who homeschooled her daughter Isabel and developed an elaborate system of personality types based on close observation of people. She classified people into types like "The Meditator," "The Spontaneous," "The Executor" — all before reading Jung. When she discovered Jung's *Psychological Types*, she believed she had found a kindred spirit and spent years corresponding with him.

## Part 2: The Daughter

Isabel Myers was more practical and ambitious than her mother. During World War II, she saw a national need: millions of women entering the workforce needed guidance on what jobs suited them. She created the MBTI as a tool for matching people to occupations. She spent decades collecting data, refining questions, and lobbying organizations to adopt her test.

## Part 3: The Controversy

Academic psychologists were nearly unanimous in their rejection. The MBTI fails the basic psychometric criteria: test-retest reliability (people get different results on retesting up to 50% of the time), predictive validity (it does not predict job performance), and construct validity (the four dimensions are not independent). The Big Five, by contrast, has strong evidence for all three.

## Part 4: The Cultural Impact

Despite the scientific rejection, the MBTI became a global phenomenon. Emre argues that its appeal lies in its flattering, binary categories (no one is "bad" in the MBTI — every type has strengths) and the human desire for a clear answer to "Who am I?"

## Why the MBTI Works Culturally but Not Scientifically

| Dimension | MBTI | Big Five |
|---|---|---|
| Scientific validity | Near zero | Strong |
| Test-retest reliability | Poor (50% change) | Good (90%+ stable) |
| Predictive power | Weak | Moderate |
| Self-flattery | High | Low |
| Simplicity | Very high | Moderate |
| Cultural appeal | Massive | Academic only |
