## Strengths

- **Meticulously researched.** Emre spent years in archives reading Briggs and Myers's private letters and unpublished manuscripts.
- **Fair and balanced.** She does not mock the creators or the test. She treats Briggs and Myers with respect while evaluating their work critically.
- **Excellent cultural analysis.** The final third of the book, on why the MBTI succeeded despite scientific rejection, is brilliant.
- **Beautifully written.** Emre is a literary scholar, and it shows in the quality of her prose.

## Weaknesses

- **Limited practical value.** This book will not help you understand your own personality better.
- **Too much biography, not enough psychometrics.** Some readers will want more detail on the scientific case against the MBTI.
- **Omits some competitors.** The history of the Big Five and other validated instruments is only briefly mentioned.

## Comparison with Quiet

| Dimension | The Personality Brokers | Quiet |
|---|---|---|
| Genre | History / Biography | Psychology / Self-help |
| Focus | The MBTI as cultural artifact | Introversion as a trait |
| Scientific relevance | Low (about the test) | High (about the trait) |
| Emotional tone | Wry, analytical | Warm, personal |

## Overall

Read this after *Quiet* to understand the cultural history of personality typing. It provides essential context for why we are so fascinated by personality categories — and why we should be skeptical of them.
