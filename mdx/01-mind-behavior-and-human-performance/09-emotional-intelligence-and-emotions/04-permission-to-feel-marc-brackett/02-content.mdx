## The RULER Framework

### Recognize

The first step is noticing emotions — in yourself and others. Brackett introduces the **Mood Meter**, a 2x2 grid with axes of pleasantness (unpleasant to pleasant) and energy (low to high):

| Quadrant | Energy | Pleasantness | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Red | High | Unpleasant | Angry, anxious, frustrated |
| Yellow | High | Pleasant | Excited, joyful, hopeful |
| Blue | Low | Unpleasant | Sad, disappointed, lonely |
| Green | Low | Pleasant | Calm, content, relaxed |

### Understand

Emotions have causes and consequences. Brackett teaches readers to ask: "What caused this emotion? What is it telling me? What do I need?"

### Label

Precise labeling reduces ambiguity and gives you control. Instead of "I feel bad," name the emotion: "I feel disappointed because my presentation did not go well."

### Express

Emotions must be expressed appropriately. Context matters: the same emotion may need different expression with a partner versus a colleague.

### Regulate

Brackett distinguishes five regulation strategies:
1. **Mindful breathing** — Calms the nervous system
2. **Cognitive reappraisal** — Reframe the situation
3. **Forward-looking** — Ask "Will this matter in a year?"
4. **Problem-solving** — Address the source
5. **Healthy distraction** — Temporary break, not avoidance

## The Mood Meter in Practice

The Mood Meter is the most practical tool in the book. Brackett recommends checking in with it multiple times per day. The simple act of plotting your emotional state on the grid builds emotional awareness. Over time, you can track patterns and identify what shifts your emotional state.
