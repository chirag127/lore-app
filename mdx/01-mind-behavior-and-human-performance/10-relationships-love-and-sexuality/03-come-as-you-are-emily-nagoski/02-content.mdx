## The Dual Control Model

Everyone has two systems governing sexual response:
- **Sexual Excitation System (SES)**: The accelerator. Responds to sexual cues and turns on desire.
- **Sexual Inhibition System (SIS)**: The brakes. Responds to threats, stress, and reasons not to be sexual.

People vary in the sensitivity of both systems. High SES sensitivity = easily aroused. High SIS sensitivity = easily inhibited. Neither is better — they are different wiring.

## Spontaneous vs. Responsive Desire

The biggest myth Nagoski debunks: that desire must precede arousal. For many women, desire follows arousal. This is called "responsive desire" — you get turned on during sex, not before it.

The problem: couples expect desire to be spontaneous. When it is not, women feel broken and partners feel rejected. Understanding responsive desire transforms this dynamic.

## Arousal Non-Concordance

Arousal (genital response) does not always match subjective desire (what you feel). This is normal. Women's genitals can respond to sexual stimuli without conscious desire. The opposite can also happen: you want sex but your body does not respond.

Key statistic: The correlation between genital response and subjective arousal is about 0.2 for women (vs. 0.5 for men). This is normal variation, not a defect.

## The Orgasm Gap

In heterosexual encounters, men orgasm 95% of the time; women orgasm 65% of the time. Nagoski traces this to:
1. Lack of clitoral stimulation (the clitoris has 8,000 nerve endings — more than the penis)
2. The orgasm-as-goal mindset that creates performance anxiety
3. Insufficient arousal before genital touch

## Context Is Everything

The single most important factor in female sexuality: context. Stress, sleep, body image, relationship satisfaction, and emotional safety all affect the sensitivity of the SES and SIS. The "brakes" are especially sensitive to stress. Nagoski's prescription: remove the brakes (reduce stress) before trying to step on the accelerator.
