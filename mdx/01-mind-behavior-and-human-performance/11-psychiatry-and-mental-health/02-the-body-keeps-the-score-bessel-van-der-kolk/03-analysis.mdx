## Strengths

- **Comprehensive synthesis.** Van der Kolk integrates neuroscience, attachment theory, developmental psychology, and clinical practice into a coherent framework. No other book covers the same breadth.
- **Scientific grounding.** Every claim is supported by research, much of it from van der Kolk's own lab. The science is presented accurately and accessibly.
- **Clinical wisdom.** Decades of clinical experience inform every page. This is not an academic treatise — it is a practical guide written by someone who has treated thousands of patients.
- **Humanity.** Van der Kolk writes with deep empathy. Patients' stories are told with dignity and respect. The book never feels cold or clinical.
- **Practical value.** The treatment chapters are genuinely useful. Readers can identify approaches that might work for them and advocate for appropriate care.
- **Public health message.** The ACE study findings and the argument for trauma-informed care are urgent and important.

## Weaknesses

- **Length.** The book is dense and detailed. Some readers may find it overwhelming, especially if they are trauma survivors themselves.
- **Polarizing treatment claims.** Van der Kolk's advocacy for EMDR, neurofeedback, and MDMA therapy has been criticized by some as overstating the evidence for these approaches.
- **Occasional self-promotion.** The book features van der Kolk's own Trauma Center extensively. While the research is legitimate, the self-reference can feel promotional.
- **Limited attention to systemic factors.** The book focuses on individual treatment. Broader social and political dimensions of trauma — poverty, racism, inequality — receive less attention than some readers would like.
- **Can be triggering.** The detailed descriptions of traumatic experiences are difficult to read, especially for trauma survivors.

## Criticism

### Evidence for EMDR

EMDR has been criticized as theoretically dubious (why would eye movements help process trauma?) and as no better than exposure therapy in controlled trials. Proponents argue the bilateral stimulation component does matter, but the evidence is mixed.

### The Trauma Industry

Critics argue that the concept of trauma has expanded to include almost any unpleasant experience, diluting its meaning. Van der Kolk is sometimes blamed for contributing to this "trauma culture" — though he himself advocates for precise diagnosis.

### Overemphasis on the Body

Some critics argue that van der Kolk overcorrects against talk therapy. Cognitive and behavioral approaches have strong evidence for trauma treatment. The claim that trauma cannot be addressed through talking alone is overstated.

## Counterarguments

| Criticism | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| "EMDR is just exposure therapy with extra steps" | Even if true, the structure of EMDR may make exposure more tolerable for patients who cannot engage with traditional exposure therapy. |
| "Trauma is overdiagnosed" | Van der Kolk's point is the opposite: trauma is underrecognized, especially in patients presenting with physical symptoms. |
| "The body focus is unscientific" | The physiological evidence for trauma's embodiment is strong. Interoceptive deficits in trauma survivors are measurable. |
