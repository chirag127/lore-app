## Overview

*Feeling Good: The New Mood Therapy* (1980) by David D. Burns is the classic popularization of Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT). Burns, a psychiatrist at Stanford, trained under Aaron Beck (the founder of CBT) and adapted Beck's clinical methods into a practical self-help program.

The central idea: your feelings are not caused by events, but by your *thoughts* about events. When you are depressed, your thinking becomes systematically distorted. These distortions (which Burns calls "cognitive distortions") create and maintain negative moods. By learning to identify and correct these distortions, you can change how you feel.

The book is structured as a workbook: it teaches specific techniques, provides exercises, and includes self-assessment tools (the Burns Depression Checklist, the Beck Depression Inventory). It has been used by millions of readers and by therapists as a supplement to therapy.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Thoughts cause feelings.** The CBT model: Event → Thought → Feeling. Change the thought, change the feeling. This is not positive thinking — it is *accurate* thinking.

2. **Ten cognitive distortions.** Burns identifies ten systematic thinking errors that fuel depression, including all-or-nothing thinking, overgeneralization, mental filtering, and personalization.

3. **CBT works.** Clinical trials show CBT is as effective as antidepressant medication for mild to moderate depression, with lower relapse rates.

4. **Specific techniques for specific problems.** The book provides tools for different situations: the triple-column technique for automatic thoughts, cost-benefit analysis for motivational problems, the perfectionism scale for setting realistic standards.

5. **Self-help can be effective.** Studies show that using the book alone (without a therapist) produces significant improvement for many people.

## Who Should Read

| Reader Type | Why |
|---|---|
| Anyone experiencing depression or anxiety | Clinically proven techniques you can use immediately |
| Therapy clients | Supplement your therapy with structured homework |
| People who want to understand their thinking patterns | The cognitive distortions framework is eye-opening |
| Clinicians and trainees | The clearest popular introduction to CBT methods |

## Who Should Skip

- Those with severe or suicidal depression (the book is not a substitute for professional help)
- Readers seeking insight-oriented therapy (CBT focuses on symptoms, not underlying causes)
- People who dislike structured self-help programs

## Core Themes

| Theme | Description |
|---|---|
| Cognitive Model | Thoughts mediate between events and feelings; changing thoughts changes feelings |
| Cognitive Distortions | Ten systematic thinking errors that maintain depression |
| Behavioral Activation | Action precedes motivation — doing things changes how you feel |
| Verbal Techniques | Specific language-based tools for countering distorted thinking |
| Self-Assessment | Quantitative tools for tracking mood and progress |

## Why This Book Matters

*Feeling Good* is one of the most important mental health books ever written. It brought CBT — one of the most empirically validated forms of psychotherapy — to a mass audience. Its practical, structured approach has helped millions of people manage depression and anxiety without medication. The cognitive distortions framework has entered the mainstream: concepts like "all-or-nothing thinking" and "catastrophizing" are now part of everyday language.

**Rating: 8/10** — A practical, evidence-based classic. Somewhat dated in presentation but the core techniques are timeless.
