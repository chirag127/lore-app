## The Ten Cognitive Distortions

1. **All-or-Nothing Thinking:** Seeing things in black-and-white categories. If your performance falls short of perfect, you see yourself as a total failure. ("I got a B+ on that exam — I might as well have flunked.")

2. **Overgeneralization:** Seeing a single negative event as a never-ending pattern of defeat. ("I didn't get the job. I'll never get any job.")

3. **Mental Filter:** Picking out a single negative detail and dwelling on it exclusively, so your vision of all reality becomes darkened. ("The presentation went well, but one person looked bored. It was a disaster.")

4. **Disqualifying the Positive:** Rejecting positive experiences by insisting they "don't count." ("Anyone could have done that.")

5. **Jumping to Conclusions:** Making negative interpretations even though there are no definite facts. Two subtypes:
   - **Mind Reading:** Assuming others are reacting negatively to you without evidence.
   - **The Fortune Teller Error:** Anticipating that things will turn out badly.

6. **Magnification (Catastrophizing) or Minimization:** Exaggerating the importance of problems or shrinking your positive qualities.

7. **Emotional Reasoning:** Taking your emotions as evidence of truth. ("I feel guilty, so I must have done something wrong.")

8. **Should Statements:** Trying to motivate yourself with "shoulds," "musts," and "oughts." The emotional consequence is guilt. When directed at others: anger and frustration.

9. **Labeling:** An extreme form of overgeneralization. Instead of "I made a mistake," you say "I am a loser."

10. **Personalization and Blame:** Seeing yourself as the cause of external negative events you were not primarily responsible for.

## The Triple-Column Technique

Burns's signature technique for challenging automatic thoughts:

1. **Column 1 — Automatic Thought:** Write down the negative thought that went through your mind.
   - "I'll never get this project done. I'm such a failure."

2. **Column 2 — Cognitive Distortion:** Identify which distortions are present.
   - Fortune teller error, labeling, all-or-nothing thinking.

3. **Column 3 — Rational Response:** Write a more realistic, balanced thought.
   - "I feel overwhelmed right now, but I have completed difficult projects before. Let me break this down into steps."

## Daily Mood Log

Burns recommends keeping a daily log of automatic thoughts and practicing the triple-column technique. Over time, the rational responses become automatic — the cognitive equivalent of building a muscle.

## Behavioral Techniques

Sometimes you cannot think your way out of depression because the thinking part of your brain is too depressed. In these cases, Burns recommends behavioral activation:

- **The Antiprocrastination Sheet:** Break tasks into tiny steps. Record your predicted satisfaction and actual satisfaction. The gap between prediction and reality is often dramatic — depressed people consistently underestimate how much they will enjoy activities.
- **The Little Steps Approach:** Do not wait for motivation. Start with the smallest possible action. Motivation follows action, not the other way around.
- **Pleasure-Predicting Schedule:** Schedule activities and rate your predicted and actual pleasure. This provides evidence that you can still enjoy things.

## Verbal Techniques

Burns provides specific verbal techniques for different cognitive distortions:

- **Examine the Evidence:** Instead of assuming your thought is true, ask: what is the evidence for and against this thought?
- **The Survey Method:** Ask other people what they think. Your distorted perception is not reality.
- **The Double-Standard Technique:** Talk to yourself the way you would talk to a friend in the same situation.
- **The Pleasure-Predicting Sheet:** Predict how much you will enjoy an activity, then compare with actual enjoyment. This counters the "nothing will make me feel better" distortion.
- **The Acceptance Paradox:** Sometimes the best way to change a feeling is to stop trying to change it. Accept the feeling, and it loses its power.

## Practical Applications

- Keep a thought record for one week. Identify your most common cognitive distortions.
- When you feel depressed, ask: "What was I just thinking?" Write down the automatic thought and identify the distortion.
- Practice the triple-column technique daily, even when you feel fine. It builds the skill for when you need it.
- If you are stuck in inaction, use behavioral activation. Commit to a 5-minute task. Motion creates motivation.
- For perfectionism: use the "let's test it" approach. Do something imperfectly and see what actually happens.
- If you use "should" statements frequently, reframe them as preferences: "I would like to do X" instead of "I should do X."
