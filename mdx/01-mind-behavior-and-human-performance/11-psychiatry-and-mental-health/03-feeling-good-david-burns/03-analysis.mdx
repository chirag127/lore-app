## Strengths

- **Evidence-based foundation.** CBT has more empirical support than any other psychotherapeutic approach for depression and anxiety. Burns translates this evidence into accessible practices.
- **Practical and structured.** The exercises are concrete and actionable. Readers who do the work see results.
- **Demystifies therapy.** Explains exactly how CBT works, making the therapeutic process transparent and collaborative.
- **Self-assessment tools.** The mood scales allow objective tracking of progress — rare in self-help books.
- **Broad applicability.** The cognitive distortions framework applies to many problems beyond depression: anxiety, anger, relationship issues, procrastination.

## Weaknesses

- **Can feel oversimplified.** Depression is not *only* distorted thinking. For some people, the causes are primarily biological, social, or existential. CBT alone may be insufficient.
- **Dated presentation.** The writing style and examples feel like the 1980s. Some readers find the tone too prescriptive.
- **Overpromising.** The subtitle "The New Mood Therapy" and the claims about cure rates set expectations that may not be realistic for severe depression.
- **Minimal attention to trauma.** The book does not address trauma-related depression, where the cognitive model may need modification.
- **Excessive length.** At 700+ pages, the book could be half the length. Repetition is a common criticism.

## Criticism

### The CBT Debate

CBT has been criticized for being surface-level — it changes symptoms but not underlying causes. Psychodynamic therapists argue that the cognitive distortions are themselves symptoms of deeper conflicts. Burns would respond: symptom relief is valuable in itself, and for many people, it is sufficient.

### Efficacy Questions

Recent meta-analyses suggest CBT's efficacy has been overstated due to publication bias. The Dodo Bird Verdict — that all therapies are roughly equally effective — applies to CBT as much as to other approaches. Burns's claims about superiority may be inflated.

### The Blame-the-Victim Problem

Some critics argue that CBT implies the patient is responsible for their depression — if you just think differently, you would feel better. Burns explicitly addresses this: the goal is not blame but empowerment. You have more control over your thinking than you realize.

## Counterarguments

| Criticism | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| "CBT is just positive thinking" | CBT is about *accurate* thinking, not positive thinking. The goal is realism, not optimism. |
| "CBT does not address root causes" | For some people, distorted thinking *is* the root cause. For others, CBT provides tools to cope while deeper work proceeds. |
| "The techniques are too mechanical" | The structure is a scaffold. As skills become automatic, the mechanical quality fades. |
| "Self-help cannot replace therapy" | Burns agrees — the book is for mild to moderate depression. He advises seeking professional help for severe cases. |

## Rating: 8/10

*Feeling Good* is not the last word on depression, but it is one of the best first steps. The cognitive distortions framework alone is worth the price — it changes how you hear your own inner voice. Use it as a tool, not a cure.
