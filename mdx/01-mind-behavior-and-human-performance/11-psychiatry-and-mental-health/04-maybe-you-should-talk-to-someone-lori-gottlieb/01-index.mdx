## Overview

*Maybe You Should Talk to Someone* (2019) by Lori Gottlieb is both a memoir and a window into the therapy process. Gottlieb, a psychotherapist and writer, interweaves the stories of several patients with the story of her own therapy after a sudden breakup. The result is a book that demystifies therapy while telling a deeply human story.

Gottlieb's patients include a self-destructive young woman, a wealthy elderly man contemplating suicide, a young newlywed diagnosed with a terminal illness, and Gottlieb herself — a therapist who suddenly needs therapy when her life falls apart. Each story illuminates different aspects of the therapeutic process: resistance, transference, the fear of being known, the courage to change.

The book's central message: therapists are human. They struggle. They avoid. They grow. And the process of therapy — which seems mysterious from the outside — is really just two humans sitting in a room, trying to figure out what it means to be alive.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Therapists go to therapy too.** Being a therapist does not make you immune to being human. Gottlieb's own therapy is as central to the book as her patients' stories.

2. **Therapy is not about answers — it is about questions.** A good therapist does not tell you what to do. They help you ask better questions of yourself.

3. **Resistance is normal.** The urge to avoid, to change the subject, to quit therapy — this is not failure. It is part of the process.

4. **We all tell stories about our lives.** Therapy helps us examine our stories and see where they are not serving us.

5. **Change is hard, even when we want it.** Knowing what you need to do and doing it are different things.

6. **Connection heals.** The therapeutic relationship itself — feeling truly seen and heard — is the most powerful factor in therapy.

| Storytelling | The narratives we construct about our lives — and how to revise them |
| Universal Struggles | Loss, loneliness, mortality, meaning, love — the human condition |

## Why This Book Matters

This book is arguably the best popular introduction to what therapy actually *looks and feels like*. It is not a textbook or a self-help manual. It is a story — and through story, it teaches more about the therapeutic process than many academic texts. It has been credited with convincing people to try therapy for the first time.

**Rating: 9/10** — Beautifully written, deeply humane, and genuinely educational about the therapy process.
