## Introduction

Welcome to BookAtlas. Today: *Maybe You Should Talk to Someone* by Lori Gottlieb. Published 2019. A book that is part memoir, part therapy primer, and part love letter to the messy, beautiful, terrifying process of being human.

Gottlieb is a therapist in Los Angeles. She writes an advice column for The Atlantic. She had her life together. And then her boyfriend broke up with her suddenly, and she found herself doing everything her patients do: obsessing over text messages, catastrophizing, stalking his social media at 2 AM.

To cope, she did what she advises her patients to do: she found a therapist. His name is Wendell. And he turns out to be the best character in the book.

---

## The Patients

Gottlieb weaves together four patient stories.

John is not his real name. She is a young woman who keeps making choices that hurt her. Bad relationships. Self-sabotage at work. She wants to change, but change is terrifying because if she succeeds, she will be expected to keep succeeding. It is safer to stay small. Her story is about the fear of potential.

Rita is in her 70s. She has had three disastrous marriages. Her children will not speak to her. She is thinking about ending her life. Her question: is it too late to change? At 70, can you still become the person you were meant to be? Spoiler: yes. But it is hard.

Julie is a young woman who just married the love of her life when she is diagnosed with a terminal illness. Her therapy is not about change — it is about facing death with open eyes. She has the hardest job in the book, and she does it with breathtaking courage.

And then there is Lori — the therapist who needs a therapist. She is smart, she is trained, she knows all the techniques. And yet here she is, lying on a couch, crying about a man.

---

## Wendell

Wendell is the therapist every patient dreams of having. He is calm, direct, and completely unfooled. When Gottlieb uses therapeutic language to describe her own situation, he cuts through it:

"Stop talking like a therapist and talk like a person."

He does not let her hide behind her training. He calls her on her avoidance, her intellectualizing, her need to be the smartest person in the room. He is kind, but he is not soft.

This is the model of good therapy: someone who sees through your defenses without punishing you for having them.

---

## What Therapy Actually Is

The book's greatest contribution is demystifying therapy. If you have never been, you probably imagine something from movies: the patient lies on a couch, the therapist nods sagely, and suddenly everything makes sense.

The reality is messier. Therapy is two people in a room, one of them trained to ask the right questions. The patient talks. The therapist listens — really listens — and reflects back what they hear. Slowly, patterns emerge. The patient starts to see the story they have been telling themselves, and realizes it is not the only story they could tell.

Therapy is not about getting answers. It is about learning to ask better questions.

---

## The Universal Human Condition

Gottlieb's patients have different problems, but they are all struggling with the same things: loneliness, the fear of being known, the desire for connection, the terror of mortality, the pain of not being who you want to be.

These are not mental illnesses. They are the human condition.

Gottlieb's point: we all need someone to talk to. Not because we are broken, but because we are human. We are wired for connection. And sometimes, the most radical thing you can do is sit down with another person and tell them the truth about your life.

---

## The Verdict

*Maybe You Should Talk to Someone* is not a self-help book. It will not give you techniques or exercises. But it might do something more valuable: it might make you feel less alone.

If you are in therapy, this book will validate your experience. If you are considering therapy, it will reduce your fear. If you are neither, it will still teach you something about being human.
