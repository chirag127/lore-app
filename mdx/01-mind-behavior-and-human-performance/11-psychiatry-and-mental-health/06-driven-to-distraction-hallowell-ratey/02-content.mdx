## What ADD Is — and Is Not

Hallowell and Ratey define ADD as a neurological syndrome characterized by:

- **Difficulty sustaining attention** on tasks that are not intrinsically interesting
- **Impulsivity** — acting without thinking, difficulty delaying gratification
- **Hyperactivity** — physical restlessness (in some types) or internal mental restlessness
- **Difficulty with organization** and planning
- **Poor time perception** — time blindness, chronic lateness
- **Emotional dysregulation** — low frustration tolerance, mood lability
- **Difficulty with working memory** — forgetting what you were about to do

The key: ADD is a disorder of *inconsistency*. On a good day, with the right task, the right stimulation, and the right environment, a person with ADD can perform as well as anyone. On a bad day, they cannot do the simplest things.

## The Three Subtypes

1. **Predominantly Inattentive Type:** Daydreamy, spacey, easily overwhelmed. These people are often overlooked because they are not disruptive. They are more common in girls and women.

2. **Predominantly Hyperactive-Impulsive Type:** Always moving, always talking, always interrupting. This is the stereotype — more common in boys.

3. **Combined Type:** Both inattentive and hyperactive-impulsive symptoms.

## ADD Across the Lifespan

### Childhood

ADD in children is usually caught early if it includes hyperactivity. But the inattentive type is often missed. These children are labeled as lazy, unmotivated, or "not living up to their potential." They are told to try harder — when trying harder is precisely what they cannot do.

### Adolescence

The hormonal changes of adolescence often worsen ADD symptoms. Risk-taking behavior increases. Academic demands exceed the student's organizational capacity. This is a common time for diagnosis — or crisis.

### Adulthood

Adult ADD looks different. Hyperactivity becomes internal (mental restlessness, racing thoughts). Impulsivity shows up in relationships and spending. Disorganization affects work and family. Many adults are diagnosed only after their children are diagnosed — they see themselves in their child's symptoms.

## The Gift of ADD

The authors are careful not to pathologize ADD entirely. They document the positive traits:

- **Creativity:** People with ADD think divergently. They make novel connections.
- **Hyperfocus:** When interested, they can focus more intensely than anyone else.
- **Energy:** Physical and mental energy that can be channeled productively.
- **Intuition:** Heightened sensitivity to others and to the environment.
- **Resilience:** Having struggled with failure, they often develop tolerance for setbacks.
- **Entrepreneurial Drive:** Many successful entrepreneurs have ADD — the willingness to take risks, the ability to think fast, the comfort with chaos.

## Treatment

### Medication

Stimulants (Ritalin, Adderall, Vyvanse) are the most effective treatment. They increase dopamine and norepinephrine in the prefrontal cortex, improving attention and impulse control. Non-stimulants (Strattera, Intuniv) are alternatives.

The authors caution: medication is not a cure. It is a tool. It makes other interventions possible.

### Coaching

ADD coaching is different from therapy. Coaches help with practical skills: organization, time management, prioritization, follow-through. They provide external structure that the ADD brain cannot generate internally.

### Environmental Modifications

- Remove distractions at work
- Use external memory systems (lists, alarms, calendars)
- Break tasks into tiny steps
- Use body doubling (working alongside someone else)
- Build in accountability

### Lifestyle

- Exercise is as effective as medication for some people
- Sleep is non-negotiable — sleep deprivation mimics ADD
- Diet matters — stable blood sugar helps
- Structure and routine are protective

## Practical Applications

- If you suspect you have ADD, get evaluated. Self-diagnosis is not reliable, but self-suspicion is often accurate.
- Read the diagnostic criteria in the book. If they resonate, seek a professional evaluation.
- Do not rely on medication alone. Build systems. Find a coach. Exercise. Sleep.
- Find work that matches your brain. Novel, urgent, interesting, high-stimulation environments are ADD-friendly.
- Stop trying to be "normal." Your brain works differently. That is not a moral failing.
- For parents: your child is not choosing to be difficult. Their brain is wired differently. Punishment does not fix neurological problems.
- For teachers: students with ADD need structure, movement, and engagement — not lectures and punishment.
