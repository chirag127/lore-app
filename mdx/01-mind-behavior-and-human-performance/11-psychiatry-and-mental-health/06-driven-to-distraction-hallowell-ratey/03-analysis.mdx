## Strengths

- **Humanizing case studies.** The patient stories bring ADD to life. Readers see themselves in the pages.
- **Dual perspective.** Both authors have ADD and treat ADD. The combination of personal and professional authority is powerful.
- **Non-pathologizing.** The "gifts of ADD" framing was ahead of its time. It anticipates the neurodiversity movement.
- **Practical.** The book is full of concrete suggestions for managing ADD.
- **Broad audience.** Written for patients, families, and clinicians alike.

## Weaknesses

- **Dated diagnostic terminology.** The book uses ADD (without the H) which is no longer the official DSM term. The diagnostic criteria have evolved.
- **Overly broad criteria.** The authors have been criticized for casting too wide a net — for including traits that are common in the general population.
- **Overemphasis on the "gifts."** Critics argue that romanticizing ADD may discourage treatment. The "gifts" are real but the impairments are more consequential.
- **Limited evidence base for some claims.** Some of the treatment recommendations (diet, supplements) are not strongly evidence-based.
- **Underrepresentation of inattentive type.** The book focuses more on the hyperactive-impulsive presentation.

## Criticism

### The "ADD Epidemic"

Critics argue that ADD is overdiagnosed — that normal childhood behavior and common adult distractibility are being medicalized. Hallowell and Ratey respond that ADD is underdiagnosed, especially in adults and women.

### Medication Concerns

The book's positive stance on stimulant medication has been criticized as promoting drug dependence. The authors argue that stimulants, when properly prescribed and monitored, are safe and effective.

### The Neurodiversity Response

Some neurodiversity advocates argue that the "disorder" framing is itself problematic — that ADD is a difference, not a defect. Hallowell and Ratey's "gifts of ADD" framing anticipates this but still positions ADD as a condition to be treated.

## Counterarguments

| Criticism | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| "ADD is overdiagnosed" | Underdiagnosed in adults, especially women. The majority of people with ADD do not know they have it. |
| "Stimulants are overprescribed" | When properly prescribed, they are safe and effective. The problem is misuse, not legitimate use. |
| "Everyone has some ADD symptoms" | Yes — but the diagnosis requires impairment. The difference between a trait and a disorder is functional impact. |
| "ADD is an excuse for laziness" | ADD is not a lack of effort. It is a neurological inability to direct effort consistently. |

*Driven to Distraction* changed how ADD is understood and talked about. It is not the most current book on the subject, but it remains an essential starting point. If you suspect you might have ADD, start here.
