## Strengths

- **Intellectual depth with readability.** Wright manages to explain complex theories (inclusive fitness, reciprocal altruism, parental investment) with exceptional clarity.
- **Darwin as narrative device.** Using Darwin's life as a case study is brilliant and effective. It humanizes the theory.
- **Balanced tone.** Wright is enthusiastic about evolutionary psychology but not dogmatic. He acknowledges limitations and criticisms.
- **Broad scope.** Covers morality, sex, family, status, self-deception — the full range of human experience.
- **Intellectual honesty.** Wright does not flinch from the unsettling implications of the theory. He confronts them directly.

## Weaknesses

- **Overreach.** Critics argue that Wright, like many early EP advocates, overstates the degree to which specific behaviors are direct adaptations.
- **Dated.** The book was published in 1994. Some of the science has moved on. The debate between evolutionary psychology, human behavioral ecology, and cultural evolution has become more nuanced.
- **Sex difference claims are controversial.** The strong claims about evolved sex differences have been challenged by many feminist and evolutionary critics. Some of the empirical support is weaker than Wright suggests.
- **Just-so stories.** The adaptationist approach — telling stories about why a trait must have been selected for — is vulnerable to the Gould-Lewontin critique of adaptationism.
- **Limited cross-cultural evidence.** Wright relies heavily on Western data. The universality of the patterns he describes is contested.

## Criticism

### The Standard Social Science Model Debate

Wright positions evolutionary psychology in opposition to the "Standard Social Science Model" — the idea that the mind is a blank slate shaped entirely by culture. This framing, popularized by Pinker and Tooby, has been criticized as a straw man. Most social scientists never believed in a completely blank slate.

### Feminist Criticism

Feminist critics argue that EP's claims about evolved sex differences are used to justify gender inequality. Wright explicitly argues against this — understanding evolved differences does not justify them — but the concern is persistent.

### Adaptationism

Gould and Lewontin's critique of adaptationism applies to Wright: not every trait is an adaptation. Some are byproducts of other adaptations (spandrels). Some are neutral with respect to selection. Wright is more careful than many EP writers, but the book still leans heavily on adaptive storytelling.

## Counterarguments

| Criticism | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| "EP is just storytelling" | All science involves storytelling. The question is whether the stories generate testable predictions. EP has generated thousands of studies. |
| "Sex differences are culturally imposed" | Cross-cultural universals and cross-species parallels suggest biological foundations. Culture amplifies or attenuates, but does not create, sex differences. |
| "The theory justifies the status quo" | Understanding evolved tendencies does not justify them. Wright explicitly argues for using this understanding to *transcend* our nature. |
| "The mind is not a collection of modules" | The modularity claim is debated. But some form of domain-specificity is widely accepted. Even critics agree the mind has specialized systems. |

*The Moral Animal* remains the best popular introduction to evolutionary psychology. Read it critically, read it with an awareness of the debates, but read it. It will change how you see yourself.
