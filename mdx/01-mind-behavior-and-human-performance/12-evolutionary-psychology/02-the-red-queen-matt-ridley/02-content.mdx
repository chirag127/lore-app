## The Puzzle of Sex

Sex is expensive. Asexual organisms produce twice as many offspring (everyone reproduces). Sexual organisms produce half as many (only females reproduce). Given this "twofold cost of sex," asexual reproduction should dominate. But it does not. Most complex life reproduces sexually.

Why? The Red Queen provides the leading answer: parasites. Asexual reproduction produces clones — genetically identical offspring. If a parasite evolves to exploit one clone, it can exploit all of them. Sexual reproduction shuffles the genetic deck, producing diverse offspring that parasites cannot predict.

The Red Queen hypothesis: organisms must constantly evolve just to keep up with their evolving parasites. Sex is the strategy that makes this possible.

## Sexual Selection

Darwin identified sexual selection — competition for mates — as a distinct evolutionary force. Traits that would be maladaptive for survival (the peacock's tail, the stag's antlers) could evolve if they helped in competition for mates.

Two forms:

1. **Male-male competition:** Males compete directly for access to females. This favors larger size, weapons (antlers, horns), and aggressive tendencies.

2. **Female choice:** Females choose among competing males. This favors elaborate displays (peacock's tail, bird song, bowerbird structures) and traits that signal genetic quality.

The logic: females invest more in each offspring (eggs, gestation, lactation), so they are the limiting resource. Males compete for access to them. Females choose the best males they can get.

## Human Mating Strategies

Ridley applies this framework to humans with nuance and balance:

**Male strategies:** Men compete for status and resources, which are cues to their ability to provide for offspring. They are attracted to youth and physical attractiveness — cues to fertility. They are more eager for casual sex than women are, on average.

**Female strategies:** Women prioritize resources and commitment in a partner — cues to a man's ability and willingness to invest in offspring. They are more selective than men. They are attracted to status, intelligence, and humor — cues to a man's ability to compete successfully.

**Long-term vs. short-term:** Both sexes have dual mating strategies. Long-term: find a reliable partner for parenting. Short-term: seek genetic quality or resources. The balance differs by sex and circumstance.

## Beauty

Ridley argues that standards of beauty are not arbitrary cultural constructs. They reflect evolved preferences:

- **Symmetry:** A cue to developmental stability — the ability to resist disease and stress during development.
- **Clear skin:** A cue to health and absence of parasites.
- **Waist-to-hip ratio:** A cue to female fertility and hormonal health.
- **Facial masculinity/femininity:** Cues to hormone levels and genetic quality.

Culture shapes how these preferences are expressed, but the underlying preferences are universal.

## The Human Brain as a Sexual Ornament

Ridley's most provocative argument: intelligence, creativity, humor, and language may have evolved as much for courtship as for survival.

The human brain is metabolically expensive — it consumes 20% of our energy while comprising 2% of our body weight. Natural selection alone may not explain its size. Sexual selection may have been a driving force: the brain as a peacock's tail, a costly signal of genetic quality.

This would explain:
- Why humans invest so heavily in art, music, and storytelling
- Why humor is so important in attraction
- Why intelligence is universally valued across cultures
- Why male creativity peaks during mating years

## Practical Applications

- The Red Queen reminds us that conflict — with parasites, competitors, even partners — is not a bug in the system. It is the engine of evolution.
- Understanding the evolutionary logic of attraction can help you see through your own preferences. Not to override them, but to understand them.
- In relationships, recognize that male and female strategies evolved in different contexts. Awareness reduces conflict.
- The brain as a sexual ornament: develop your mind not just for survival, but for its own sake. That is what it evolved for.
