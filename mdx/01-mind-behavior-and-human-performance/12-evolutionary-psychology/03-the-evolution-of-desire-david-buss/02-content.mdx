## The Cross-Cultural Evidence

Buss's landmark 1989 study surveyed over 10,000 people across 37 cultures on six continents. Participants rated the importance of various characteristics in a mate. The results revealed striking universals:

**What both sexes want:**
- Kindness and understanding
- Intelligence
- Emotional stability
- Dependability
- Good health

**Sex differences:**
- Women valued "good financial prospects" more than men (across all 37 cultures)
- Women valued "ambition and industriousness" more than men
- Men valued "physical attractiveness" and "youth" more than women
- These differences were consistent across cultures, economic systems, and political ideologies

The magnitude of the sex differences varied by culture (smaller in gender-egalitarian societies) but the direction was universal.

## Short-Term vs. Long-Term Mating

**Male short-term strategy:**
- Lower standards for casual sex partners
- Desire for variety — multiple partners
- Sensitivity to cues of sexual availability and low commitment cost
- More regret over missed sexual opportunities than over past casual sex

**Female short-term strategy:**
- Higher standards — more selective
- Focus on immediate resource access or genetic quality
- Preference for masculine features (symmetry, deep voice, muscular build) in short-term contexts
- Use short-term mating to evaluate potential long-term partners

**Long-term strategy (both sexes):**
- Emphasis on compatibility, dependability, and shared values
- Importance of parental investment and commitment
- Preference for partner characteristics that predict long-term investment

## Jealousy: The Sex Difference

Buss's most famous research: men and women experience jealousy differently.

**Men:** More distressed by sexual infidelity. The evolutionary logic: a man can never be 100% certain of paternity. Sexual infidelity threatens his genetic fatherhood — the worst possible evolutionary outcome.

**Women:** More distressed by emotional infidelity. The evolutionary logic: a woman is always 100% certain of maternity. The threat is not paternity but investment — if her partner falls in love with another woman, he may divert resources away from her offspring.

Buss tested this using physiological measures (heart rate, skin conductance, EMG) and self-report. The sex difference is robust and replicable across cultures. It appears even in young children in anticipated scenarios.

## Mate Retention Tactics

Buss catalogues the strategies people use to keep their partners:

| Tactic | Examples |
|--------|----------|
| Vigilance | Spying, checking up, monitoring |
| Concealment | Hiding partner from rivals |
| Monopolization | Dominating partner's time |
| Intrasexual threats | Warning off rivals |
| Resource display | Flashing wealth or status |
| Submission and appeasement | Giving in, being extra nice |
| Derogation of competitors | Putting down rivals |
| Emotional manipulation | Guilt trips, threats of leaving |
| Enhancement of appearance | Dressing up, getting fit |
| Physical possession signals | Public displays of affection |

Men and women differ in which tactics they use, consistent with evolutionary predictions. Men are more likely to use resource display and concealment. Women are more likely to use appearance enhancement and emotional manipulation.

## Mate Poaching

Buss devotes a chapter to mate poaching — the deliberate seduction of someone who is already in a relationship.

- Approximately 10-15% of marriages and long-term relationships begin with poaching
- Poached relationships tend to form faster and end faster, on average
- Men are more likely to poach and be poached
- The most common reason for poaching: the poacher is more attractive or has more resources than the current partner

## Practical Applications

- Recognize that mate preferences are not arbitrary. Understanding their evolutionary logic can help you be more intentional about your choices.
- Jealousy is a natural emotion with evolutionary roots. The goal is not to eliminate it but to manage it.
- Relationship conflict often stems from divergent evolutionary interests. Awareness can reduce blame and increase understanding.
- Short-term preferences and long-term preferences are different. Be clear about what you are looking for.
- Mate value is not static. Invest in yourself — health, status, skills, emotional stability.
- Love is real. It is an evolved commitment device, but that does not make it less meaningful.
