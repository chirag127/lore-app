## Overview

*Livewired: The Inside Story of the Ever-Changing Brain* (2020) by David Eagleman is the most up-to-date popular account of neuroplasticity. Eagleman, a neuroscientist at Stanford, presents the brain as a dynamic, adaptive system that is never finished — constantly rewiring itself in response to experience.

Where Doidge's *The Brain That Changes Itself* introduced plasticity through case studies, Eagleman explains the *mechanisms* and *principles* of brain plasticity, from the molecular level to the systems level. He also explores the frontiers: sensory substitution, brain-machine interfaces, and the possibility of expanding human experience through technology.

The title captures the core idea. The brain is not "hardwired" — a metaphor from computing that suggests fixed circuits. It is **livewired** — constantly adjusting its own circuitry based on what it encounters.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The brain is not a finished product.** It is a dynamic, ever-changing system. Plasticity is not a special feature — it is the fundamental operating principle.

2. **Information determines structure.** The brain's circuitry is shaped by the information it receives. The same starting brain can develop into radically different adult brains depending on input.

3. **The brain is a prediction machine.** It constantly models the world, adjusts its predictions based on error signals, and updates its circuitry accordingly.

4. **Sensory substitution is real.** The brain can learn to "see" through sound, "hear" through touch, and "feel" through vision. The brain does not care which sensory channel provides the input.

5. **Plasticity has a cost.** The same mechanisms that allow learning also allow the entrenchment of bad patterns. The brain's plasticity means it can be shaped for better or worse.

6. **Technology is extending the brain.** Brain-machine interfaces, sensory augmentation, and neurofeedback are expanding what the brain can do.
