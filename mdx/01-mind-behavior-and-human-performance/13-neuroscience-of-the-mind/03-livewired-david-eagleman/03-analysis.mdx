## Strengths

- **Most up-to-date account.** The book reflects current research, including Eagleman's own lab's work on sensory substitution.
- **Clear framework.** The "livewired" concept is memorable and captures the essence of plasticity.
- **Exciting frontiers.** The discussions of sensory augmentation and brain-machine interfaces are genuinely exciting.
- **Accessible writing.** Eagleman is a skilled communicator. Complex ideas are presented clearly.
- **Good balance.** Covers both the promise and the limitations of plasticity.

## Weaknesses

- **Shorter than the topic deserves.** At 320 pages, some topics are covered briefly. Readers wanting depth may prefer Sapolsky.
- **Can feel like a TED talk.** The book is engaging but sometimes lacks depth. Some topics are introduced but not fully explored.
- **Overly optimistic about technology.** The brain-machine interface sections are exciting but speculative. The technical challenges are understated.
- **Less practical than expected.** Despite the emphasis on plasticity, concrete guidance for harnessing it is limited.
- **Limited engagement with criticism.** The book presents plasticity as an unqualified good. The trade-offs and limitations get less attention.

## Criticism

### The "Anything Is Possible" Framing

Critics argue that Eagleman sometimes gives the impression that the brain can adapt to anything. In reality, plasticity has limits. Critical periods exist. Some functions cannot be fully restored.

### The Sensory Substitution Claims

Some researchers question whether sensory substitution truly creates the *experience* of the replaced sense. Is a blind person using a visual-to-tactile device really "seeing"? The claim is controversial.

### The Tech Optimism

Eagleman's enthusiasm for brain-machine interfaces and sensory augmentation is not shared by all neuroscientists. The engineering challenges are enormous. The ethical implications are barely addressed.

*Livewired* is the best current introduction to neuroplasticity. It is not the deepest book on the subject, but it is the most up-to-date and one of the most engaging. Read it after Doidge for the latest science.
