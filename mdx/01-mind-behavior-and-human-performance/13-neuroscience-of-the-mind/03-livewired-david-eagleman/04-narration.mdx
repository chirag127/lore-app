## Introduction

Welcome to BookAtlas. Today: *Livewired* by David Eagleman. Published 2020. A book about the most remarkable property of the human brain: its ability to change itself.

For most of history, people thought the brain was fixed. You get what you get in childhood, and after that, it only declines. This is wrong. The brain is not wired at all — it is *livewired*. It is constantly adjusting its own circuitry, every moment of every day, in response to what it encounters.

Eagleman is a neuroscientist at Stanford, and he has spent his career studying how the brain adapts. This book is the most current popular account of that research.

---

## The Principle

The brain starts with a basic blueprint — broad regions for vision, hearing, touch, movement. But the detailed wiring is not specified in advance. It is determined by what the brain encounters.

Here is the evidence: people born blind use their visual cortex to process sound and touch. The brain area that was supposed to handle vision is repurposed for other senses. People born deaf use their auditory cortex for vision.

The brain does not care which sensory organ provides the information. It cares about the information itself. Any brain area can learn to process any type of input, as long as the input has a consistent structure.

---

## Sensory Substitution

Eagleman's lab developed a vest that translates sound into vibration patterns on the skin. Deaf people wearing the vest can learn to "hear." They report that the vibration feels like sound — it comes from the direction of the source, it has texture and rhythm.

Even more remarkable: blind people using a device that translates visual input into tactile patterns report seeing. The input comes through the skin, but the brain treats it as vision because it carries spatial information about the world.

This tells us something profound about the brain. It is not a collection of specialized modules. It is a general-purpose learning machine that allocates its resources based on what input it receives.

---

## The Prediction Machine

Eagleman explains the brain as a prediction engine. It is constantly building models of the world and using them to predict what will happen next.

When predictions match reality, the brain does nothing. When predictions fail — prediction error — the brain updates its models. This is learning. This is plasticity.

This explains why we learn from surprise, why habits are hard to break, and why the world feels predictable even though it is not.

---

## The Future

Eagleman looks forward to a world where we can expand the brain beyond its natural limits. Cochlear implants already restore hearing. Retinal implants restore sight. Motor prosthetics controlled by thought are becoming practical.

The next step: new senses. What if you could feel the stock market as a vibration? What if you could hear radio frequencies? What if you could see in infrared?

If the brain can learn to process any input, there may be no limit to what we can experience.

---

## Final Thoughts

*Livewired* is a book about possibility. The brain is not fixed. It is not limited. It is the most flexible, adaptive, creative system in the known universe. And it never stops changing.
