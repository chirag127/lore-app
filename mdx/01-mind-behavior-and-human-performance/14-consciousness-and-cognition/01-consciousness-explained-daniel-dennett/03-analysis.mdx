## Strengths

- **Brilliant and provocative.** Dennett's arguments are original, powerful, and challenging. Even his critics acknowledge the force of his thinking.
- **Clear framework.** The Multiple Drafts model and the critique of the Cartesian Theater are genuinely useful frameworks.
- **Comprehensive.** Covers perception, memory, language, self, and qualia. The scope is impressive.
- **Engaging voice.** Dennett is witty, polemical, and entertaining. The book is a pleasure to read even when you disagree.
- **Scientific grounding.** Dennett engages seriously with neuroscience and cognitive science. This is not armchair philosophy.

## Weaknesses

- **The title is misleading.** The book does not really explain consciousness — it explains why Dennett thinks consciousness is not what you think it is. Many readers feel the title overpromises.
- **Dismissive tone.** Dennett is famously dismissive of views he disagrees with. Critics (especially Chalmers, Searle, Nagel) are treated with less respect than they deserve.
- **The qualia argument is controversial.** Many philosophers find Dennett's dismissal of qualia unconvincing. The "intuition pumps" (thought experiments) are powerful but not decisive.
- **Dated neuroscience.** The book is from 1991. Some of the neuroscience is outdated. The computational model of mind is less dominant than it was.
- **Does not address the hard problem.** Chalmers's hard problem — why is there experience at all? — is not really addressed. Dennett essentially denies that there is a hard problem.

## Criticism

### The Hard Problem

Chalmers argues that Dennett "explains" consciousness by explaining something else — explaining the functions of consciousness (attention, memory, reportability) while ignoring the subjective experience itself. Dennett responds: there is nothing else TO explain. Once you have explained the functions, you have explained consciousness.

### The Qualia Denial

Critics like Nagel and Searle argue that Dennett's denial of qualia is simply a refusal to take first-person experience seriously. They claim he is not explaining consciousness — he is explaining it away.

*Consciousness Explained* is essential reading for anyone who takes consciousness seriously. You may not agree with Dennett — most people do not — but you will think more clearly after reading him.
