## Prenatal Development and Infancy

Development begins before birth. The prenatal period is one of the most rapid and vulnerable phases: the brain forms, the heart begins beating, genetic and environmental factors interact in ways that shape the entire lifespan.

At birth, infants are not blank slates. They arrive with preferences, temperaments, and learning capacities. They prefer faces, can imitate expressions, and are primed to learn language.

## Cognitive Development: The Major Theories

### Piaget's Stages
- **Sensorimotor (0-2 years):** Knowledge through senses and actions. Object permanence develops.
- **Preoperational (2-7):** Symbolic thinking, but egocentric and not yet logical.
- **Concrete Operational (7-11):** Logical thinking about concrete events. Conservation develops.
- **Formal Operational (12+):** Abstract reasoning. Hypothetical and systematic thinking.

### Vygotsky's Sociocultural Theory
- Development is socially mediated
- The zone of proximal development (ZPD): the gap between what a child can do alone and with help
- Scaffolding: providing support that is gradually removed as competence increases

### Information Processing
- Development as improvement in basic cognitive capacities: attention, memory, processing speed
- Executive function (inhibition, working memory, cognitive flexibility) develops through childhood and adolescence
- Metacognition — thinking about thinking — improves with age

## Social and Emotional Development

### Attachment Theory

Bowlby's theory: infants are biologically predisposed to form attachments to caregivers for survival. Ainsworth's Strange Situation procedure identified patterns:

- **Secure attachment:** Infant uses caregiver as a secure base, shows distress at separation, is comforted on reunion.
- **Insecure-avoidant:** Infant avoids caregiver, shows little distress at separation, ignores caregiver on reunion.
- **Insecure-resistant:** Infant clings to caregiver, is extremely distressed at separation, both seeks and resists comfort on reunion.
- **Disorganized:** Infant shows confused, contradictory behaviors — associated with abuse or trauma.

Secure attachment predicts better social and emotional outcomes across the lifespan.

### Erikson's Psychosocial Stages

| Stage | Age | Crisis | Virtue |
|-------|-----|--------|--------|
| Infancy | 0-1 | Trust vs. Mistrust | Hope |
| Toddlerhood | 1-3 | Autonomy vs. Shame | Will |
| Preschool | 3-6 | Initiative vs. Guilt | Purpose |
| School Age | 6-12 | Industry vs. Inferiority | Competence |
| Adolescence | 12-20 | Identity vs. Role Confusion | Fidelity |
| Young Adulthood | 20-40 | Intimacy vs. Isolation | Love |
| Middle Adulthood | 40-65 | Generativity vs. Stagnation | Care |
| Late Adulthood | 65+ | Integrity vs. Despair | Wisdom |

## Adolescent Development

Adolescence is a period of profound transformation: puberty changes the body, the brain undergoes major remodeling (especially the prefrontal cortex), and identity becomes the central task.

The adolescent brain is not a "broken" adult brain. It is a brain optimized for learning, social engagement, and exploration — which sometimes leads to risk-taking.

## Adult Development and Aging

Development continues across adulthood:

- **Young adulthood:** Intimacy, career establishment, perhaps parenthood
- **Middle adulthood:** Generativity — contributing to the next generation
- **Late adulthood:** Reflection, wisdom, coming to terms with life

Cognitive aging is selective. Processing speed declines, but crystallized intelligence (knowledge, vocabulary, wisdom) can increase. The brain remains plastic, and cognitive engagement can slow decline.

## Practical Applications

- Early relationships matter. Secure attachment provides a foundation for healthy development.
- The zone of proximal development is a powerful teaching tool. Work at the edge of competence with support.
- Development does not stop in childhood. You can continue to grow, learn, and change at any age.
- Culture shapes development. Be aware of your own cultural assumptions about what "normal" development looks like.
- Sensitive periods exist, but plasticity is lifelong. It is never too late to learn, but some things are easier to learn earlier.
- Adolescent risk-taking is partly biological. Structure the environment to provide safe opportunities for exploration.
