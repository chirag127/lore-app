## The Two Mindsets

### Fixed Mindset
- Intelligence is static
- Effort is a sign of inadequacy — if you are smart, you should not need to try
- Challenges are threatening — you might fail and reveal your limits
- Failure is a judgment — you are a failure
- Others' success is threatening
- Feedback is criticism

### Growth Mindset
- Intelligence can be developed
- Effort is the path to mastery
- Challenges are opportunities to grow
- Failure is information — what can I learn?
- Others' success is inspiring — I can learn from them
- Feedback is helpful

## In Education

Dweck's original research: children praised for intelligence ("You are so smart") chose easier tasks and gave up when things got hard. Children praised for effort ("You worked so hard") chose harder tasks and persisted.

The mechanism: praise for intelligence teaches children that performance reveals ability. When they encounter difficulty, they infer they must not be smart. Praise for effort teaches that effort leads to improvement. Difficulty is a sign to try harder.

## In Business

Growth mindset organizations outperform fixed ones. Leaders with growth mindsets are more likely to mentor, to listen to feedback, and to build collaborative cultures. Fixed mindset leaders are threatened by talented subordinates and create cultures of secrecy and blame.

## In Relationships

Fixed mindset in relationships: the perfect match exists. If we have to work at it, we are not meant to be. Conflict means character flaws.

Growth mindset in relationships: relationships require effort. Conflict is an opportunity to understand each other better. The ideal is not a perfect match but a growing partnership.

## In Sports

Dweck analyzes athletes: Michael Jordan had a growth mindset — he was cut from his high school team and used it as motivation. He was the hardest worker in the NBA. Athletes with fixed mindsets blame others for their failures and crumble under pressure.

## Changing Your Mindset

Mindset is not permanent. It can be changed:

1. **Learn about the two mindsets.** Awareness is the first step.
2. **Notice your fixed mindset triggers.** What situations make you feel threatened?
3. **Name your fixed mindset voice.** "Oh, there is my fixed mindset telling me this is too hard."
4. **Talk back in a growth mindset voice.** "This is hard — that means I am learning."
5. **Take growth mindset action.** Try a new approach. Ask for help. Practice.

## Practical Applications

- Pay attention to how you praise children. Praise effort, strategy, and persistence — not intelligence or talent.
- When you face a challenge, notice whether your mindset feels fixed or growth. If fixed, ask: "What would I do if I believed I could improve?"
- In relationships, replace "we are not compatible" with "we need to work on this together."
- In work, seek feedback — it is information, not judgment.
- Celebrate effort, not just outcomes. In yourself and others.
- Remember: mindset is not binary. You have both fixed and growth mindsets in different areas. The goal is to expand the growth territory.
