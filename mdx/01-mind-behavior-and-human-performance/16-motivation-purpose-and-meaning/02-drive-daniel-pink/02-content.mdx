## Motivation 2.0 vs Motivation 3.0

Pink organizes the book around a metaphor: human motivation has evolved
through operating systems.

**Motivation 1.0** — biological drive (hunger, thirst, sex).

**Motivation 2.0** — external rewards and punishments. This served the
industrial economy well: routine tasks respond to carrots and sticks.
If you pay people more to produce more widgets, they produce more
widgets.

**Motivation 3.0** — intrinsic drive. For creative, conceptual,
non-routine work, Motivation 2.0 breaks down. The science shows that
extrinsic rewards can actually *reduce* performance on tasks requiring
cognitive flexibility.

## The Candle Problem (The Key Experiment)

Karl Duncker's 1945 candle problem: participants are given a candle,
a box of tacks, and matches, and must attach the candle to the wall
so it doesn't drip wax on the table. The solution requires seeing the
tack box as a platform, not just a container.

Sam Glucksberg (1962) replicated the experiment with a twist: one
group was told the study was about how long people typically take;
the other was offered money for finishing in the top 25% (fastest time).
Result: the incentivized group took **three and a half minutes longer**
on average. The reward narrowed focus and blocked insight.

This is the central finding that Pink builds the book around: for
purely mechanical tasks, rewards work; for tasks requiring even
rudimentary cognitive skill, rewards degrade performance.

## The Seven Deadly Flaws of Carrots and Sticks

1. **They extinguish intrinsic motivation** — the Sawyer Effect: pay
   people to do what they already enjoy, and they enjoy it less.
2. **They diminish performance** — as the candle problem demonstrates.
3. **They crush creativity** — rewards encourage tunnel vision.
4. **They crowd out good behavior** — people do only what is rewarded.
5. **They encourage cheating, shortcuts, and unethical behavior** —
   when the reward is the goal, the means become flexible.
6. **They become addictive** — once offered, the reward must escalate.
7. **They foster short-term thinking** — immediate rewards kill
   long-term perspective.

## Type I vs Type X

| Dimension | Type X (Extrinsic) | Type I (Intrinsic) |
|---|---|---|
| Primary drive | External rewards | Autonomy, mastery, purpose |
| Work orientation | Job (what you do for pay) | Calling (what you do for its own sake) |
| Goal focus | Outcome (grades, bonuses) | Process (learning, improving) |
| Response to failure | Blame, give up | Learn, adjust |
| Sustainable? | Burns out over time | Self-reinforcing |

Pink emphasizes that Type I is not about money — it's about how money
is used. Pay people enough to take money off the table, then focus on
intrinsic factors.

## Autonomy

Pink describes four dimensions of autonomy:

- **Task** — what people do (Google's 20% time)
- **Time** — when they do it (ROWE — Results-Only Work Environment)
- **Technique** — how they do it (let people choose their methods)
- **Team** — who they do it with (self-organizing teams)

The key insight: autonomy is not the same as independence. It's about
self-direction — having choice within a framework of accountability.

## Mastery

Mastery requires three conditions:

1. **Goldilocks tasks** — challenges that are neither too hard (anxiety)
   nor too easy (boredom), but just right for the current skill level.
   This is the same condition that produces flow (Csikszentmihalyi).
2. **Immediate feedback** — you need to know how you're doing in
   real time to adjust.
3. **An asymptote you never reach** — mastery is an infinite game.
   The journey is the reward.

Pink introduces the "mastery spiral": you improve → you get feedback →
you improve some more → the challenge increases → you improve further.
This loop is intrinsically motivating because it satisfies the need
for competence.

## Purpose

Purpose answers the question: *why am I doing this?* Pink argues that
the most motivated people and organizations have a purpose that
transcends profit or personal gain.

The book introduces three levels of purpose:
- As a **goal** — what you're working toward
- As a **words** — the stated mission
- As a **policies** — how the organization operates day to day

Purpose transforms work from a job (I do this for pay) into a calling
(I do this because it matters).

## Practical Applications

### For Individuals

- Audit your work: are you motivated by Type X or Type I factors?
- Ask yourself: if there were no external rewards, would I still do
  this work?
- Design your own goldilocks tasks — seek challenges at the edge of
  your ability
- Find the purpose in any task by asking who benefits

### For Organizations

- Consider ROWE (Results-Only Work Environment)
- Implement 20% time or FedEx days (autonomous project time)
- Pay enough to make money a non-issue, then focus on autonomy,
  mastery, and purpose
- Remove unnecessary rules and bureaucracy
- Allow self-organizing teams

### For Educators

- Reduce emphasis on grades (which are extrinsic rewards)
- Provide meaningful feedback instead of evaluative judgment
- Allow choice in assignments and learning methods
- Connect learning to real-world purpose
