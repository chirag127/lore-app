## The Grit Equation

Duckworth's core formula:

**Talent × Effort = Skill**
**Skill × Effort = Achievement**

Effort counts twice. Talent is real — some people learn faster or have
natural aptitudes — but without effort, talent is unrealized potential.
Effort builds skill from talent, and effort turns skill into
achievement.

This means the grittier person with less talent will outperform the
more talented but less gritty person over time.

## The Two Components of Grit

### Passion (Consistency of Interest)

Passion is not intensity but endurance. Duckworth distinguishes between
having many passionate interests (which is scattered) and maintaining
one top-level goal over years. The grittiest people have a "hierarchy
of goals" — a single ultimate concern that organizes all lower-level
goals.

### Perseverance of Effort

This is the more intuitive component: working hard through adversity.
But Duckworth emphasizes that perseverance is not just about dramatic
setbacks — it's about showing up on ordinary days, when you're bored,
tired, or uninspired.

## The Grit Scale

The Grit Scale is a 12-item questionnaire. Sample items:

- "I finish whatever I begin."
- "Setbacks don't discourage me."
- "I have difficulty maintaining my focus on projects that take more
  than a few months to complete." (reverse-scored)
- "My interests change from year to year." (reverse-scored)

The scale predicts:
- Retention at West Point (better than the Army's own measure)
- Performance at the National Spelling Bee
- College GPA (better than SAT scores)
- Teacher effectiveness in low-income schools
- Sales retention rates

## The Four Paragons of Grit

### 1. Interest

Grit requires passion, and passion starts with interest. Duckworth
argues that interests are not discovered fully-formed — they are
developed through exposure, curiosity, and repeated engagement. The
key is to nurture early interests by giving them time and space to
deepen.

### 2. Practice

This is deliberate practice (Ericsson): setting specific stretch goals,
seeking immediate feedback, and concentrating with full effort.
Duckworth adds that the key is to turn deliberate practice into a
habit — making it automatic reduces the willpower cost.

### 3. Purpose

Grit is stronger when it serves others. Duckworth found that the
grittiest people see their work as a calling, not just a career. The
purpose can start small — helping a team, serving a customer — and
grow over time.

### 4. Hope

Hope is the belief that your efforts can improve your future. This
connects to Carol Dweck's growth mindset: if you believe you can grow,
you keep trying. If you believe your abilities are fixed, you give up
at the first sign of difficulty.

## The Hard Thing Rule

Duckworth's family rule for developing grit:

1. Everyone in the family (including parents) must do one hard thing —
   something that requires deliberate practice every day.
2. You can quit — but only when the season or commitment period is
   over, not in the middle of a difficulty.
3. You get to choose your hard thing — no one imposes it on you.

This rule teaches three things: follow-through, the value of effort,
and autonomy in commitment.

## Grit and Talent

The book devotes significant attention to the relationship between
grit and talent. Key points:

- Talent and grit are not opposites — many talented people are also
  gritty
- Talent can be a liability — if things come easily, you may not
  develop the habit of effort
- Grit is not a substitute for talent; it's what you do with whatever
  talent you have
- The "naturalness bias" — we prefer people who seem effortlessly
  talented over those who work hard — is a cultural prejudice that
  hurts both individuals and organizations

## Grit vs Self-Control

Duckworth distinguishes grit (long-term passion and perseverance) from
self-control (short-term impulse regulation). They are correlated but
not identical:

- Self-control is about resisting temptation *now*
- Grit is about staying committed *over years*
- Some gritty people have poor self-control in daily life
- The two traits predict different outcomes

## Growing Grit

Duckworth is cautious about grit development:

- Grit increases with age — older people are grittier than younger
- Life experiences (parenting, career challenges) build grit
- Grit can be cultivated through the four paragons (interest, practice,
  purpose, hope)
- The "hard thing rule" is the most direct method
- Culture matters — being part of a gritty group makes individuals
  grittier

The most important thing: **grit is not about never quitting. It's
about staying committed to your top-level goal while adjusting the
lower-level goals that serve it.**
