## Strengths

- **Unparalleled science communication.** Sapolsky makes complex
  endocrinology accessible, funny, and memorable. The zebra metaphor
  is perfect.
- **Comprehensive coverage.** Every major body system is covered in
  appropriate detail.
- **Intellectually honest.** Sapolsky consistently separates what we
  know from what we think we know, and flags weak evidence.
- **Updated editions.** The 2004 edition incorporates new research on
  things like the *H. pylori* / ulcer connection and telomere biology.

## Weaknesses

- **Light on solutions.** The book is 90% problem description, 10%
  solution. Sapolsky is a biologist, not a clinician. He explains what
  goes wrong but offers limited guidance on how to fix it.
- **Dense.** At ~500 pages with serious physiology, this is not a quick
  read. Some readers may find the level of detail overwhelming.
- **Dated in places.** Some specific findings (particularly around
  stress and cancer progression) have been refined since 2004.
- **Limited on psychological interventions.** Cognitive reframing,
  mindfulness, and other stress reduction strategies are mentioned in
  passing rather than explored.

## Criticisms

**The zebra comparison is oversimplified.** Acute stress in humans also
causes damage — heart attacks from sudden emotional shocks are real.
The dichotomy (zebras = good, humans = bad) is rhetorically effective
but physiologically imprecise.

**Individual differences under-explored.** Sapolsky acknowledges that
some people thrive under pressure, but doesn't deeply explore why. The
psychology of stress mindset (which McGonigal covers) is mostly absent.

**Animal research extrapolation.** Much of the evidence is from
laboratory rats and wild baboons (Sapolsky's own research). The
applicability to humans is plausible but not always directly tested.

## Evidence Summary

| Finding | Evidence Strength |
|---|---|
| Chronic glucocorticoid elevation damages the hippocampus | Strong — replicated in animals and humans |
| Stress suppresses immune function | Strong — many replications, moderate effect sizes |
| Social support buffers stress | Strong — one of the most robust findings in all of health psychology |
| Predictability and control reduce stress | Strong — animal and human studies agree |
| Stress accelerates telomere shortening | Moderate to strong — growing evidence base |
| Type A personality causes heart disease | Mixed — hostility component is robust, other components are not |

## Enduring Influence

*Why Zebras Don't Get Ulcers* remains the go-to book for the biology
of stress 30 years after its first publication. It has educated an
entire generation of medical and psychology students. The allostatic
load framework is now standard in stress research. The book's greatest
contribution is making endocrinology relevant to everyday life.
