## The 10 Principles

### 1. Steal Like an Artist

Nothing is original. Kleon quotes T. S. Eliot: "Immature poets imitate;
mature poets steal." The distinction: bad artists copy what they see;
good artists steal what they *don't* see — the ideas, the methods, the
sensibility behind the work. The goal is not to avoid influence but to
curate your influences carefully.

Practical advice:
- Keep a swipe file of things that inspire you
- Study your heroes deeply — not just their work, but their process
- Ask: what did they steal? Who influenced them?

### 2. Don't Wait Until You Know Who You Are to Get Started

Identity follows action, not the reverse. Kleon argues that you
discover who you are by making things, not by introspection. Start
making before you feel ready. The work itself will teach you what you
need to know.

### 3. Write the Book You Want to Read

The best creative projects fill a gap you feel personally. Don't ask
"What would sell?" or "What's trendy?" Ask: "What do I wish existed?"
That gap is your opportunity.

### 4. Use Your Hands

Digital tools make it too easy to erase mistakes. Hand-drawing,
physical collage, and analog processes force you to commit, make
mistakes, and live with them. The friction of analog tools produces
different kinds of thinking.

### 5. Side Projects and Hobbies Are Important

The most interesting creative work often comes from the margins — the
project you're working on when you're supposed to be doing something
else. Side projects keep you fresh, create cross-pollination, and
produce your most distinctive work.

### 6. Do Good Work and Share It with People

The internet has eliminated the gatekeepers. You can publish your work
directly. The key is to share your process, not just your finished
products — this builds an audience and creates accountability.

### 7. Geography Is No Longer Our Master

You don't need to be in a creative capital to be creative. The internet
connects you to the people and resources you need. Your physical
location matters far less than your curiosity and discipline.

### 8. Be Nice (The World Is a Small Town)

The creative world runs on relationships. Be generous, share credit,
connect people, be a good collaborator. Burning bridges is stupid.
In a connected world, reputation is everything.

### 9. Be Boring (The Routine Is the Key)

The romantic image of the tortured artist is a myth. The most
productive creators have boring routines — they show up every day at
the same time and do the work. Creativity is not a lightning strike;
it's a habit.

### 10. Creativity Is Subtraction

Constraints are liberating. The most creative work often comes from
severe limitations — a tight budget, a small canvas, a short deadline.
Choosing what to leave out is as important as choosing what to include.
