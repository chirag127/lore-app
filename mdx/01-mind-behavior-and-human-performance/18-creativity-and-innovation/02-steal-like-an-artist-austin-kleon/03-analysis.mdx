## Strengths

- **Permission-giving.** The book's greatest value is psychological:
  it gives people permission to start making things without waiting
  for originality or inspiration.
- **Accessible format.** Short, illustrated, quotable. Designed to be
  consumed in one sitting and revisited.
- **Memorable framework.** The 10 principles are simple but sticky.
  Readers actually remember and apply them.
- **Intellectually honest.** Kleon's "nothing is original" thesis is
  better supported by art history than the romantic myth of genius.

## Weaknesses

- **Lacks depth.** The book is a manifesto, not a theory. Readers who
  want rigorous research or detailed methods will find it thin.
- **Selective evidence.** Kleon selects examples that support his
  thesis and ignores the genuinely original — some works really are
  breakthroughs, not remixes.
- **Over-simplifies originality.** The "nothing is original" claim is
  rhetorically effective but philosophically slippery. Some works
  involve a level of synthesis that transcends their sources.

## Criticisms

**The "stealing" metaphor is legally and ethically problematic.**
Kleon distinguishes between stealing (transforming) and plagiarism
(copying), but the metaphor encourages boundary-testing. In an era of
copyright litigation, the "steal like an artist" frame could be
misleading to naive creators.

**Does this apply to all creative domains?** The book is rooted in
visual art and writing. It's less clear how the principles apply to
scientific discovery, mathematical invention, or technological
innovation — where genuine novelty does occur.

**Individual differences.** Some creators are naturally more influenced
by their predecessors; others are more divergent. Kleon treats his
model as universal, but personality and cognitive style likely shape
how much "stealing" is involved.

## Enduring Influence

*Steal Like an Artist* has become a modern classic of creativity
literature. It is assigned in art schools, recommended by established
artists, and quoted endlessly on social media. The phrase "steal like
an artist" has entered the cultural lexicon. The book's influence
extends far beyond its modest length — it has genuinely changed how
a generation of creators thinks about originality.
