## The 10 Ways

### 1. You Don't Have to Be a Genius

The romantic image of the lone genius who produces masterpieces from
solitude is a myth. Most creative work is collaborative, iterative,
and built on the work of others. You don't need to be a genius — you
need to be present, generous, and engaged.

**The "scenius"** — the idea that creativity emerges from scenes and
networks, not isolated individuals. Be part of a scene. Contribute
generously.

### 2. Think Process, Not Product

Share your work in progress — sketches, drafts, false starts, behind-
the-scenes. People are fascinated by how things are made. The process
itself is content.

Kleon distinguishes:

- **Flow** — the daily, moment-to-moment sharing of your process
- **Stock** — the curated, finished work that represents your best

Share from both. The flow builds the relationship; the stock builds
the reputation.

### 3. Share Something Small Every Day

Consistency matters more than volume. Share something — a photo, a
thought, a sketch, a link — every day. This builds the habit of sharing
and creates a body of work that accumulates.

### 4. Open Up Your Cabinet of Curiosities

Share your influences. The things that inspire you are interesting to
others. A reading list, a playlist, a collection of reference images —
these are content. They also help people understand where your work
comes from.

### 5. Tell Good Stories

Work doesn't speak for itself. You need to frame it with narrative —
where the idea came from, what you were trying to solve, what you
learned. A good story makes the work meaningful.

### 6. Teach What You Know

Teaching is the best way to learn and the most effective way to build
authority. Share what you've figured out. Write tutorials, make videos,
give talks. When you teach, you attract people who want to learn from
you.

### 7. Don't Turn into a Human Spam

The line between sharing and spamming: are you giving value, or just
demanding attention? Share things because they're useful or interesting,
not because you want something in return. Generosity builds trust;
self-promotion erodes it.

### 8. Learn to Take a Punch

Criticism is inevitable. The bigger your audience, the more criticism
you'll face. Kleon's advice: don't feed the trolls, learn from
constructive criticism, and keep going. The worst thing you can do is
stop sharing.

### 9. Sell Out

"Keeping your day job" is romanticized. Kleon argues that it's okay to
make money from your work. The goal is to find a way to support yourself
doing what you love — and sharing your work is how you get there.

### 10. Stick Around

The most important rule. The people who succeed are not necessarily the
most talented; they're the ones who stay in the game. Keep showing up,
keep sharing, keep improving. Time is the ultimate competitive
advantage.
