## Strengths

- **Reframes self-promotion as generosity.** This is the book's lasting
  contribution. It solves the discomfort that many creatives feel about
  marketing by showing that sharing process and teaching what you know
  is not selling out — it's contributing.
- **Actionable and immediate.** The advice is simple and can be applied
  today. Share one thing. Start a blog. Document your process.
- **Format matches function.** Like *Steal Like an Artist*, the book is
  short, illustrated, and quotable. It practices what it preaches.
- **Ethical.** Kleon's framework is sustainable and relationship-
  focused, unlike the growth-hacking approaches that burn people out.

## Weaknesses

- **Internet-centric.** The book assumes an audience of digital
  creators. The advice applies less directly to fields where work
  cannot be shared publicly (classified work, trade secrets, client
  confidentiality).
- **Assumes meritocracy.** Sharing your work helps only if the
  conditions for being discovered are fair. Structural barriers
  (discrimination, network inequality) limit who gets noticed.
- **Light on the difficult parts.** What happens when you share and
  no one cares? What about the long period of obscurity before anyone
  notices? The book acknowledges this but offers limited comfort.

## Criticisms

**"Building in public" is a privilege.** Not everyone can share their
work process. Some people work in environments where sharing would be
prohibited. Others do work that is not visually interesting. Others
face harassment when they put themselves online. Kleon acknowledges
some of this but doesn't fully address the access issue.

**The audience-as-community model requires constant labor.**
Responding to comments, maintaining a presence, and building
relationships takes significant time. For someone who already has
limited creative energy, this can be a burden.

## Enduring Influence

*Show Your Work!* has become foundational for the "building in public"
movement. The phrase "show your work" is now standard advice for
creators. The book's approach — share process, teach, be generous —
has influenced how independent creators think about audience building.
It remains one of the most recommended books for anyone starting a
creative career.
