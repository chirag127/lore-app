## Introduction

**Narrator:** Welcome to BookAtlas. Today: *Show Your Work! 10 Ways
to Share Your Creativity and Get Discovered*, by Austin Kleon.
Published 2014. Workman Publishing. 224 pages.

The book that solved the creative's dilemma: how do I get noticed
without being obnoxious? We're joined by Diana Chen, a freelance
illustrator who built her career using Kleon's methods, and Alex Turner,
who runs a popular YouTube channel about creative work.

---

## The Book in One Sentence

**Narrator:** Diana, the core idea?

**Diana:** Don't wait until your work is perfect to share it. Share
your process — the sketches, the false starts, the behind-the-scenes.
People connect with process more than product. When you share
generously, you attract an audience that cares about how you think,
not just what you produce.

**Alex:** And the key reframe: this is not self-promotion. It's
generosity. You're giving people value — insight into how creative work
happens. The audience follows because they're learning, not because
you're selling.

---

## Process Over Product

**Narrator:** The central principle.

**Diana:** Before reading this book, I only shared finished work. One
illustration a week. Kleon taught me to share the process — the
research, the rough sketches, the color tests. My engagement went up
dramatically. People love seeing how things are made.

**Alex:** On YouTube, the most popular videos in my niche are not the
finished products. They're the "how I made this" videos, the process
vlogs, the tutorials. Process is inherently interesting.

---

## Teach What You Know

**Narrator:** Teaching as audience building.

**Alex:** This was the biggest shift for me. I started making tutorials
about my process, and my channel grew faster than when I was just
showing finished work. Teaching attracts people who want to learn from
you. It positions you as an authority.

**Diana:** And teaching forces you to understand your own process
better. You can't teach what you don't understand. The act of
explaining clarifies your own thinking.

---

## Share Something Every Day

**Narrator:** The consistency principle.

**Diana:** This is harder than it sounds. But it doesn't have to be big.
A photo of your workspace, a quote you're thinking about, a sketch,
a link to someone else's work. The goal is presence — being consistently
visible so people remember you.

**Alex:** Consistency compounds. One post a day is 365 touchpoints a
year. Even if each one reaches a small number of people, the
accumulation is significant.

---

## Limitations

**Narrator:** What are the book's blind spots?

**Diana:** It assumes the internet is a safe place to share. For women,
people of color, and other marginalized groups, public sharing comes
with real risks — harassment, doxxing, theft. Kleon acknowledges this
briefly but doesn't fully address it.

**Alex:** And it assumes everyone has something visually interesting to
share. Not all work is photogenic. If your creative output is strategy
documents or code, "show your work" is harder.

---

## Final Verdict

**Narrator:** Does it hold up?

**Diana:** It's the most useful book I've read on building a creative
career. The reframing of promotion as generosity changed everything
for me.

**Alex:** It's idealistic, but that's its strength. It offers a vision
of creative career-building that is sustainable, ethical, and
relationship-focused.

**Narrator:** *Show Your Work!* — the book that made "building in
public" a mainstream creative practice.
