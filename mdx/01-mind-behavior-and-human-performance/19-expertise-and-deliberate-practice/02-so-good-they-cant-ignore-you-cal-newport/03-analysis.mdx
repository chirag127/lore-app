## Strengths

- **Necessary corrective.** The "follow your passion" advice has done
  real damage — encouraging people to quit stable jobs, take on debt
  for degrees in unmarketable fields, and feel inadequate when their
  passion doesn't materialize. Newport's counter-argument is both
  evidence-based and humane.
- **Clear framework.** Career capital, craftsman mindset, the law of
  financial viability — these concepts are memorable and practically
  useful.
- **Good case studies.** Newport profiles real people (Steve Jobs,
  Alex Berger, the MacArthur fellows) who illustrate the framework.
- **Connects to deliberate practice.** The link to Ericsson's research
  gives the argument scientific grounding.

## Weaknesses

- **Overstates against passion.** Newport's argument works better as
  a corrective than as a complete theory. Some people *do* have genuine,
  lasting passions that should guide their career. The "passion
  hypothesis" is more nuanced than Newport admits.
- **Limited evidence for the framework itself.** The career capital
  model is persuasive but Newport provides no systematic evidence for
  it. The case studies are illustrative, not proof.
- **Assumes a functioning market.** The law of financial viability works
  only in fields where market value accurately reflects contribution.
  Many valuable contributions are not well-compensated (teaching,
  caregiving, art).
- **Privilege blind spot.** Building career capital requires the
  luxury of time, stability, and access. Someone working multiple jobs
  to survive does not have the bandwidth for deliberate practice.

## Criticisms

**The "passion hypothesis" is a straw man.** Most career advisors do
not advocate quitting your job to chase a vague passion. The more
common advice is to align your career with your values and strengths —
which is closer to Newport's model than he admits.

**Confirmation bias in case study selection.** Newport selects
examples that support his thesis. The counter-examples — people who
found fulfilling work by following a clear passion — are not explored.

## Evidence Summary

| Finding | Evidence Strength |
|---|---|
| Pre-existing passion does not predict career satisfaction | Moderate — supported by some studies, but not definitive |
| Skill and autonomy predict work engagement | Strong — well-established in job design research |
| Deliberate practice builds expertise | Strong — the Ericsson research is robust |
| Career capital framework is useful | Conceptual — not empirically tested as a whole |

## Enduring Influence

*So Good They Can't Ignore You* has become a standard reference in
career development. The "craftsman mindset" vs. "passion mindset"
distinction is widely used. The book influenced a generation of
professionals to focus on skill building rather than soul-searching.
It remains the best counter-argument to the follow-your-passion
narrative.
