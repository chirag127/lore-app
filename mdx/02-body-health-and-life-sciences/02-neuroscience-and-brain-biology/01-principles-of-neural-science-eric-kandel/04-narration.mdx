Principles of Neural Science, now in its sixth edition, is widely regarded as the definitive textbook in the field of neuroscience, a monumental work that traces the functioning of the nervous system from individual molecules to the emergence of conscious thought. Edited by Nobel laureate Eric R. Kandel together with John D. Koester, Sarah H. Mack, and Steven A. Siegelbaum, this volume represents the accumulated knowledge of a field that has undergone explosive growth since the first edition appeared in 1981. At over sixteen hundred pages, it is simultaneously a reference work, a textbook, and a testament to the reductionist program in biology — the conviction that the most complex phenomena of mind can be understood through the lens of cellular and molecular mechanisms. The book's central argument is that the nervous system is comprehensible through systematic analysis across five levels of organization: molecular, cellular, circuit, systems, and behavioral. At the molecular level, ion channels, receptors, and signaling cascades govern neuronal excitability. At the cellular level, individual neurons integrate inputs and generate outputs through action potentials and synaptic release. At the circuit level, ensembles of interconnected neurons perform computations such as detecting edges in visual scenes and encoding spatial locations. At the systems level, distributed networks of circuits give rise to perception, emotion, memory, and motor coordination. At the behavioral level, these neural processes manifest as the full repertoire of animal and human action, from simple reflexes to abstract reasoning.

Kandel's own Nobel Prize-winning work on the sea slug Aplysia californica — demonstrating that learning produces measurable changes in synaptic strength — serves as a unifying thread throughout the text. His laboratory established that the molecular cascade from short-term to long-term memory involves cyclic AMP, protein kinase A, MAP kinase, the transcription factor CREB, and ultimately the growth of new synaptic connections. This account of memory storage is one of neuroscience's great success stories: a complete causal chain linking behavior to molecules and back again. The sixth edition incorporates major advances in connectomics, optogenetics, chemogenetics, and human brain imaging, reflecting a field transformed by new tools. It also expands coverage of neural computation, decision-making, and the circuit basis of psychiatric illness, bridging basic science and clinical practice in ways that earlier editions could not.

The book is organized into nine parts spanning sixty-seven chapters, each written by leading researchers in the relevant specialty. The opening sections cover cellular and molecular neuroscience in extraordinary depth: the structure and function of ion channels, the biophysics of the action potential, the mechanisms of neurotransmitter release, and the diverse families of neurotransmitter receptors. These chapters are among the most detailed treatments available anywhere. The sensory systems section surveys vision, audition, somatosensation, olfaction, and taste, each with detailed accounts of transduction, pathway organization, and cortical processing. The visual system, from retina to inferotemporal cortex, serves as the prototypical example of how neural circuits extract and represent information about the external world. The motor systems chapters follow the neural control of movement from spinal cord to cortex, including posture, locomotion, eye movements, reaching, and grasping, with particular attention to the cerebellum and basal ganglia — structures whose dysfunction underlies devastating movement disorders. The development section covers neural induction, patterning, axon guidance, synapse formation, and critical periods, explaining how a fertilized egg gives rise to the most complex structure in the known universe.

The intellectual heart of the volume is the section on learning and memory, which presents the molecular and cellular mechanisms of synaptic plasticity in unparalleled detail. Long-term potentiation, long-term depression, and other forms of plasticity are dissected at every level: the role of NMDA receptors as coincidence detectors, the autophosphorylation of CaMKII as a molecular memory switch, the trafficking of AMPA receptors to and from the synapse, the protein synthesis-dependent consolidation of long-term memories, and the structural changes — dendritic spine growth and remodeling — that accompany enduring memories. This section also reviews the multiple memory systems of the brain: declarative memory dependent on the medial temporal lobe, procedural memory involving the striatum and cerebellum, emotional memory engaging the amygdala, and priming reflecting changes in neocortical processing.

The final part of the book translates basic neuroscience into clinical context, covering stroke, epilepsy, neurodegenerative diseases, psychiatric disorders, and spinal cord injury. Each disorder is presented as a disruption of specific neural mechanisms. Alzheimer's disease involves synaptic loss driven by amyloid-beta and tau pathology. Parkinson's disease reflects degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra. Schizophrenia is associated with alterations in prefrontal cortical circuitry and dopamine signaling. This clinical framing gives the fundamental science immediate practical significance and makes the book essential reading for medical students, neurologists, and psychiatrists.

Principles of Neural Science is not an easy book. It assumes substantial background in molecular biology, biochemistry, and neuroanatomy. Its prose is dense and technical, its chapters are long, and its sixteen hundred-plus pages require a serious commitment. But for its intended audience of advanced students and professionals, it is irreplaceable. The book's influence on neuroscience education cannot be overstated: its multilevel framework has become the standard organizing principle for curricula worldwide, and generations of neuroscientists have cut their teeth on its pages. No other single volume approaches its combination of breadth, depth, and authority. The sixth edition ensures that this remarkable resource remains current for another generation of researchers and clinicians, carrying forward the vision that the biology of mind is not only comprehensible but one of the great intellectual adventures of our time.
