## Washington's Prose

Harriet A. Washington writes with the controlled intensity of a prosecutor building a case. Her sentences are precise and accumulative: each paragraph adds a documented fact, a corroborated detail, a quote from a primary source, until the weight of evidence becomes overwhelming. She does not rely on emotional appeals; the horror emerges naturally from the documentation itself. When she describes Dr. H. H. Turner operating on the open brain of an anesthetized Black infant to test his theory of skull development in 1895, she does not condemn Turner in those terms — she lets the facts condemn him: "He determined through his research that the cause of tetany in the babies was a result of the movement of skull bones during birth. To test his theory, he took a Black baby and, using a shoemaker's tools, opened the baby's brain."

This is not literary flourish serving emotional effect. It is an argumentative technique: Washington wants the reader to understand not just that things were bad, but that they were bad in specific, known, documented ways. The prose style is the argumentative strategy. She refuses sentimentality precisely because sentimentality would allow the reader to dismiss the book as overwrought. The cumulative effect of her calm, documented account is more devastating than any rhetorical exaggeration could be.

## The Structure of Accumulation

The book is organized in three parts, each roughly chronological but organized around a thematic claim rather than a strict timeline. Part 1 covers slavery through the 19th century and establishes the pattern. Part 2 covers the 20th century and demonstrates its persistence. Part 3 covers contemporary consequences. The structure is cumulative: readers who understand the colonial background will respond to 20th-century incidents with greater urgency; readers who have absorbed the 20th century will understand Part 3's recommendations as necessary rather than theoretical.

## Primary Sources as Voice

One of the book's most distinctive stylistic features is Washington's reliance on primary documentary quotation. She lets physicians describe their own experiments in their own published words, letting the gap between stated intent and actual practice do the rhetorical work. When she quotes J. Marion Sims's published vaginal fistula paper — in which Sims describes operating on Lucy until "her countenance indicated intense suffering" — the moral condemnation emerges without Washington needing to construct it explicitly. The reader experience is sometimes similar to reading a trial transcript in which the prosecution's evidence is the defendant's own contemporaneous record.

## Ethical Distance and Moral Clarity

Washington maintains a careful ethical distance from her subjects. She does not anthropomorphize the victims — she does not invent dialogue, fill emotional gaps, or romanticize suffering. At the same time, she does not treat Black subjects as research objects either, which would reproduce the very dynamic the book condemns. The balance is difficult to sustain but Washington accomplishes it: Black patients are presented as complex human beings whose experiences she documents from surviving records, family accounts, and institutional reports, but whose inner lives she acknowledges cannot be fully recovered from the record.

This restraint gives the book its moral authority. Critics who have accused Washington of "anti-science" sentiment misunderstand the book's argument: she is not arguing against medical research but for research conducted under conditions of genuine consent and equity. That argument is more effective precisely because Washington does not adopt the tone of an ideologue but of a careful historian demonstrating the consequences of failed ethical guardrails.

## The Epilogue as Argument

The epilogue shifts from history to contemporary prescription. The prose becomes more direct, more urgent. Washington moves from documenting what happened to proposing how to ensure it does not happen again: independent IRBs, ending informed-consent exceptions, mandatory bioethics training for researchers, and community partnership in research design. The shift in tone is deliberate and effective: the reader who has finished the historical sections arrives at the epilogue not as a blank slate but as someone who understands the gravity of failures in the system Washington proposes to reform.
