The writing style is authoritative and deliberate, reflecting Marmot's dual identity as a research scientist and a public intellectual. The prose is clear and direct, avoiding unnecessary jargon, but it is also measured and careful—every claim is hedged with appropriate caveats and supported by specific evidence. The tone is that of a clinician presenting findings to a colleague: serious, respectful of the reader's intelligence, and confident in the evidence.

The narrative structure follows a logical progression from problem to explanation to solution. The opening chapters diagnose the health gap using international comparisons and the Whitehall data. The middle chapters trace the causal pathways from social position to biology through stress, early childhood, education, work, and income. The closing chapters present the policy agenda of the Marmot Review and argue for health equity as a social justice imperative. This three-part architecture is transparent and easy to follow.

The rhetorical approach combines epidemiological evidence with ethical argument. Marmot uses data as a form of moral persuasion: the numbers showing that a Glaswegian deprived-area resident lives 28 years less than a neighbor in an affluent area are not just statistics but injustices made visible. He frequently invokes the concept of fairness—the health gradient is "unnecessary, avoidable, and unjust"—to move from scientific description to social prescription.

Readability is high for a book rich in statistical findings. Marmot avoids tables of complex numbers, instead presenting key comparisons in natural language. Technical terms like "allostatic load" and "social gradient" are explained on first use and consistently reinforced. The chapters are organized with clear subheadings that make the book easy to navigate for reference.

This book fits within Marmot's career-long project of documenting and addressing health inequalities, including his earlier books *The Status Syndrome* and the edited volume *Social Determinants of Health*. Compared to other books on inequality, *The Health Gap* stands out for its grounding in specific epidemiological evidence and its policy focus. The unique contribution is the synthesis of the Whitehall findings with the WHO Commission's global perspective into a coherent framework for action.
