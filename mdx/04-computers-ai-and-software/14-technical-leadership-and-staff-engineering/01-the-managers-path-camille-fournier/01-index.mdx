## Overview

*The Manager's Path* (2017) by Camille Fournier is one of the most
highly regarded books on engineering management. Fournier, formerly
CTO of Rent the Runway and a managing director at Goldman Sachs, draws
on her extensive experience to provide a structured guide through the
engineering management career path.

The book follows the arc of an engineering leader's career: from being
mentored, to mentoring, to managing a team, managing multiple teams,
managing managers, and finally becoming a CTO. Each chapter addresses
the distinct challenges of that stage.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Management is a craft, not a promotion.** The skills that made you
   a good engineer are not the same skills that make you a good manager.

2. **1-on-1 meetings are sacred.** They are the primary mechanism for
   building trust, providing feedback, and understanding your team.

3. **Feedback is a gift.** Deliver it promptly, specifically, and
   constructively. Avoid the feedback sandwich.

4. **Technical debt is a management problem.** Prioritize paying it
   down as part of your roadmap, not as an afterthought.

5. **Hire for strengths, not lack of weaknesses.** Build complementary
   teams rather than looking for unicorns.

6. **Project management is part of your job.** Whether you use Agile,
   Scrum, or Kanban, you need a system for tracking work.

7. **You are responsible for your team's career growth.** Create
   opportunities for learning, challenging assignments, and visibility.

8. **Managing skip-level reports requires intention.** Don't
   micromanage your managers, but stay connected to their teams.

9. **Organizational design affects everything.** Team size, reporting
   structure, and communication patterns determine velocity.

10. **Take care of yourself first.** Burned-out managers create burned-
    out teams. Model sustainable work habits.

## Final Verdict

The best introductory book on engineering management. Fournier's
advice is practical, candid, and grounded in real experience. A must-
read for anyone considering or starting the management path.

**Rating: 9/10** — The definitive starting point for engineering
managers. Required reading for new managers.
