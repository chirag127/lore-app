keyIdeas:
  - "Game theory is the science of interactive decision-making — every situation where the outcome depends on the choices of multiple parties is a game"
  - "The Nash equilibrium describes a stable state where no player can benefit by changing strategy alone; it is the single most important concept in game theory"
  - "The prisoner's dilemma reveals why individually rational choices can produce collectively irrational outcomes — the foundational paradox of strategic interaction"
  - "Commitment strategies — burning bridges, making threats credible, tying one's hands — can paradoxically strengthen your bargaining position"
  - "Mixed strategies (randomizing your choices) are essential in zero-sum games where predictability is deadly"
keyTakeaways:
  - "In any strategic situation, put yourself in the other player's shoes and reason recursively about what they think you will do"
  - "The true value of game theory is not in predicting behavior but in providing a framework for clear strategic thinking"
  - "Cooperation can emerge from repeated play even when defection is the dominant short-term strategy — tit-for-tat is remarkably effective"
  - "Credible commitments and reputation matter because they change the beliefs and expectations of other players"
  - "Information asymmetry — when one party knows more than another — is the basis for much of business strategy and negotiation"
