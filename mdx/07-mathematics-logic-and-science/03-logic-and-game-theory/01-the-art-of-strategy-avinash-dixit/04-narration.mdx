The Art of Strategy by Avinash Dixit and Barry Nalebuff is a guide to
thinking strategically — which means thinking about situations where
the outcome depends on what other people do, and they are thinking
about what you will do.

The central concept is the Nash equilibrium. John Nash, the Nobel
Prize-winning mathematician portrayed in A Beautiful Mind, proved
that in any finite game, there exists at least one set of strategies
where no player can improve their outcome by changing alone. This
sounds technical, but it is profoundly practical. When two drivers
approach an intersection, each deciding whether to stop or go, the
stable outcome is that one stops and the other goes — and that is
a Nash equilibrium.

The prisoner's dilemma is the most famous game in the theory. Two
suspects are arrested. Each is offered a deal: testify against the
other and go free. If both testify, both get five years. If neither
testifies, both get one year. From each individual perspective,
testifying is always better. But when both pursue their individual
interest, the result — five years for each — is worse for both
than the cooperative outcome. This is the paradox at the heart of
strategic interaction: individually rational choices produce
collectively irrational results. It explains price wars, arms races,
and why international cooperation on climate change is so difficult.

But there is good news. When the same players interact repeatedly,
cooperation can emerge. The most famous successful strategy is
tit-for-tat: cooperate on the first move, then mirror whatever your
opponent does. It is nice, provocable, forgiving, and clear —
and it consistently wins tournaments of computer programs playing
the prisoner's dilemma.

The authors introduce the concept of the BATNA — Best Alternative
To a Negotiated Agreement. Your bargaining power depends on how good
your alternatives are. The negotiator who can walk away has the
power. The one who cannot, does not. Before any negotiation,
identify your BATNA and, crucially, improve it.

Credible commitments are another key idea. Burning bridges — making
it impossible to back down — can paradoxically strengthen your
position. Cortes burned his ships upon arrival in Mexico. His men
had no choice but to fight, and the Aztecs knew it. In business,
building a reputation for always retaliating against price cuts
serves the same function: you do not need to retaliate every time
because the reputation itself deters.

Brinkmanship — creating a shared risk of disaster to force
concessions — was analyzed by Thomas Schelling and applied during
the Cuban Missile Crisis. Kennedy established a blockade, creating
a real risk of escalation to nuclear war. Khrushchev blinked. The
art is to calibrate the risk precisely: enough to unnerve the
opponent, not so much that the situation spirals out of control.

The Art of Strategy will not make you infallible. But it will change
how you see every interaction where the outcome depends on someone
else's choices. Once you learn to see games, you see them everywhere.
