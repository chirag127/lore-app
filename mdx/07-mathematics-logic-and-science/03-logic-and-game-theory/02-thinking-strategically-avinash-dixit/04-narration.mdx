Thinking Strategically by Avinash Dixit and Barry Nalebuff was the
first book to bring game theory to a general audience. It remains
one of the best.

The central principle is simple: look forward and reason backward.
Before making a move, think about where the game will end. What
will the last player do? Then work backward to determine what you
should do now. This is backward induction. It sounds obvious, but
most people do not do it — they react to the present instead of
anticipating the future.

The prisoner's dilemma is the foundational paradox. Two suspects
are offered a deal. Testify and go free while the other gets ten
years. But if both testify, both get five years. If neither
testifies, both get one year. Each individual sees that testifying
is always better. Yet when both testify, they get five years each —
worse than the one year they would get if both kept silent.
Individually rational choices produce a collectively irrational
outcome. This explains why price wars happen, why arms races
accelerate, and why international cooperation is so difficult.

The authors introduce the Nash equilibrium: a set of strategies
where no one can improve their outcome by changing alone. In
prisoner's dilemma, the equilibrium is both defect — even though
both would prefer to cooperate.

Credible commitments are the most powerful strategic move. By
making it impossible or costly to change course, you can transform
your strategic position. Cortes burned his ships in Mexico. His men
could not retreat, so they fought harder, and the Aztecs knew it.
In business, building a reputation for retaliating against price
cuts serves the same purpose.

Mixed strategies involve deliberate randomness. In poker, you
cannot be predictable about when you bluff. In soccer penalty kicks,
the kicker must randomize which direction to shoot. The mathematics
is simple: choose probabilities that make your opponent indifferent,
so they cannot exploit any pattern.

In repeated games, cooperation can emerge from self-interest.
Robert Axelrod's computer tournament showed that the most effective
strategy for the repeated prisoner's dilemma was tit-for-tat:
cooperate on the first move, then mirror your opponent's previous
move. It is nice, provocable, forgiving, and clear.

Your bargaining power depends on your alternatives. Before any
negotiation, identify your best alternative to a negotiated
agreement — your BATNA — and improve it. The party with the better
BATNA has the stronger position.

Thinking Strategically will not help you win every game. But it
will help you see the game you are actually playing — and that is
the first step to playing it well.
