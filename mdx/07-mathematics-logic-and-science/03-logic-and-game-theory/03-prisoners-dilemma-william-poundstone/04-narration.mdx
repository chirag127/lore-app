Prisoner's Dilemma by William Poundstone is the story of an idea
that nearly destroyed the world. It is also the story of the man
who invented it.

John von Neumann was perhaps the most brilliant mathematician of
the twentieth century. By age six, he could divide eight-digit
numbers in his head. By his twenties, he had founded game theory,
contributed to quantum mechanics, and begun designing the modern
computer. He was also the driving intellectual force behind the
atomic bomb, the hydrogen bomb, and the Cold War strategy that
threatened to use them.

The idea is deceptively simple. Two prisoners are arrested. Each
is offered a deal: testify against the other and go free while the
other gets ten years. If both testify, both get five years. If
neither testifies, both get one year. From each prisoner's
perspective, testifying is always better. But when both follow this
logic, both get five years — worse than the one year they would
get by staying silent. Individually rational choices produce a
collectively irrational outcome. That is the prisoner's dilemma.

The prisoner's dilemma was invented in 1950 at the RAND Corporation,
a think tank created by the US Air Force to apply mathematics to
nuclear strategy. The Cold War was a prisoner's dilemma on a
planetary scale. Both the United States and the Soviet Union would
have been better off disarming. But neither could trust the other.
Each side's rational choice was to build more weapons. And so both
built enough nuclear weapons to destroy human civilization many
times over.

Mutually Assured Destruction — MAD — was the equilibrium that
stabilized this deadly game. If both sides had nuclear weapons that
could survive a first strike and retaliate, then neither side could
attack without being destroyed in return. Civilization was held
hostage to the credibility of a threat that no sane leader would
want to carry out.

But there is hope. Robert Axelrod organized a computer tournament
for strategies playing the prisoner's dilemma repeatedly. The
winner was the simplest strategy: tit-for-tat. Cooperate on the
first move, then mirror whatever your opponent does. It is nice,
provocable, forgiving, and clear. And it works — not by defeating
any single opponent but by eliciting cooperation from almost
everyone over repeated interactions.

Prisoner's Dilemma is about an idea that terrifies and an idea that
offers hope. The terror is that rational self-interest can destroy
us. The hope is that cooperation can emerge, even among adversaries,
when we know we will meet again.
