keyIdeas:
  - "Mathematical models are simplified representations of biological reality that capture essential dynamics while omitting inessential detail"
  - "Differential equations are the primary mathematical language for modeling continuous biological processes — population growth, enzyme kinetics, and neural dynamics"
  - "Stability analysis determines whether a biological system returns to equilibrium after perturbation — the key to understanding resilience and oscillation"
  - "Pattern formation in biology — stripes, spots, spiral waves — can emerge from simple reaction-diffusion equations without centralized control"
  - "Discrete models (difference equations, cellular automata) are appropriate when populations are small or generations are non-overlapping"
keyTakeaways:
  - "The logistic equation is the foundation of population dynamics: growth is exponential at low density but limited by carrying capacity"
  - "Lotka-Volterra predator-prey equations produce stable oscillations that explain why predator and prey populations cycle"
  - "Reaction-diffusion systems, proposed by Alan Turing, explain how biological patterns like animal coat markings can arise from simple chemical interactions"
  - "Hopf bifurcations mark the transition from stable equilibrium to oscillation — a common transition in biological systems"
  - "Sensitivity analysis reveals which parameters most affect model behavior, guiding experimental design"
