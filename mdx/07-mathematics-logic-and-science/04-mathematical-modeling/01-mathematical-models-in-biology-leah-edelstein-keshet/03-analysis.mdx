## Strengths

**The field-defining textbook.** Mathematical Models in Biology is to mathematical biology what Goldstein is to classical mechanics or Jackson to electrodynamics. It defined the curriculum for a generation of graduate students. The combination of biological motivation, mathematical rigor, and computational practicality has not been surpassed by any single volume.

**Biological content before mathematical machinery.** Each chapter begins with a biological problem, not a mathematical technique. The mathematics emerges from the biology, which gives students a compelling reason to learn the techniques and a framework for applying them to new problems. The reverse — teaching mathematics first, then tacking on biological examples — is less effective, and Edelstein-Keshet avoids it.

**The exercises are outstanding.** The problem sets are challenging, biologically motivated, and designed to build mathematical intuition. They range from analytical exercises (derive the Michaelis-Menten equation from mass-action kinetics) to computational projects (simulate a reaction-diffusion system and analyze the resulting patterns). Students who complete the exercises gain genuine modeling competence.

**The 2005 edition adds needed updates.** The original 1988 edition was showing its age by the late 1990s. The SIAM reissue adds material on stochastic methods, spatial pattern formation, and biological oscillations that brings the book into the modern period.

---

## Weaknesses

**No coverage of network biology and genomics.** The book was written before the genomics revolution. There is no treatment of gene regulatory networks, metabolic network analysis, systems biology approaches, or bioinformatics. A modern mathematical biology curriculum must supplement Edelstein-Keshet with material on network theory, high-dimensional data analysis, and statistical inference.

**Light on computational methods.** The book discusses simulation but does not provide code or detailed algorithms. Modern students need to learn Python, MATLAB, or R alongside the mathematics. The book's approach — describe the model, analyze it mathematically, suggest that the reader simulate it — is pedagogically sound but practically incomplete without computational supplementation.

**Spatial pattern formation treatment is dated.** The reaction-diffusion chapters are excellent but do not cover more recent developments: mechanochemical models, cell-based models (vertex models, phase field), agent-based models, and the integration of gene regulatory networks with spatial dynamics. The field has advanced considerably since the 2005 edition.

## Reception and Legacy

Mathematical Models in Biology has been cited over 10,000 times and has shaped the careers of thousands of mathematical biologists. The 2005 SIAM edition was published as part of the Classics in Applied Mathematics series — a designation reserved for books that have fundamentally shaped their fields.

The book's primary limitation is its age: the revolution in molecular biology, genomics, and high-throughput data that began in the 1990s and accelerated through the 2000s is not reflected in its content. No single textbook can cover everything, and Edelstein-Keshet remains the standard for the fundamentals of ecological, evolutionary, and physiological modeling. For network biology and systems biology, it must be supplemented with more recent texts.

It is essential reading for any graduate student entering mathematical biology. It remains the gold standard for the first half of a comprehensive course in the field.
