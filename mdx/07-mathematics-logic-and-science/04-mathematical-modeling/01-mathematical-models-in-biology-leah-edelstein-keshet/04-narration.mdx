Mathematical Models in Biology by Leah Edelstein-Keshet is the
classic textbook that defined the field of mathematical biology.
It teaches you how to translate biological questions into
mathematical language.

The simplest model is exponential growth: a population grows at a
rate proportional to its size. But nothing in nature grows
exponentially forever. The logistic equation adds a carrying
capacity — the maximum population the environment can support.
The result is an S-shaped curve that fits real populations from
yeast to sheep to human cities.

Predator-prey dynamics are captured by the Lotka-Volterra equations.
Prey grow, predators eat them, predators starve without prey, prey
recover. The result is persistent oscillation — the cycle of lynx
and hare, of fish and fisherman.

The book introduces phase plane analysis, the mathematical
equivalent of a weather map for biological systems. Nullclines
divide the plane into regions of different behavior. Equilibria
are the fixed points where the system comes to rest. Stability
analysis determines whether the system returns to equilibrium
after a disturbance or diverges.

Enzyme kinetics follow the Michaelis-Menten equation: the reaction
rate increases with substrate concentration until the enzyme
becomes saturated. This simple hyperbolic relationship is one of
the most widely used equations in all of biology.

Alan Turing proposed in 1952 that animal coat patterns — stripes,
spots, spirals — could arise from the interaction of two chemicals
diffusing at different rates. An activator stimulates its own
production and the production of an inhibitor. The inhibitor
diffuses faster. The result: spontaneous pattern formation from a
uniform starting point. This mechanism may explain zebra stripes,
leopard spots, and even the arrangement of fingers on a hand.

Discrete models like the logistic map show that even the simplest
nonlinear system can produce chaos. As the growth rate increases,
the population goes from a fixed point to cycles of period two,
four, eight, and then to chaos — a sequence now recognized as a
universal route to chaos in many systems.

When populations are small, randomness matters. Stochastic models
capture the probability that a population goes extinct, that a
mutation spreads, that a chemical reaction occurs. They are the
bridge between the deterministic equations of classical biology
and the probabilistic reality of the living world.

Edelstein-Keshet teaches a way of thinking: translate the biology
into equations, analyze the equations, translate the results back
into biology. It is the fundamental skill of the mathematical
biologist.
