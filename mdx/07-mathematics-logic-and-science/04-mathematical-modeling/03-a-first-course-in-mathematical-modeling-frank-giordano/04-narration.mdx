A First Course in Mathematical Modeling by Giordano, Fox, and
Horton teaches you how to translate real-world problems into
mathematical language. It covers three modeling paradigms:
optimization, probability and simulation, and discrete models.

The modeling process has five steps. First, identify the problem:
what decision needs to be made? Second, state your assumptions:
what are you simplifying and why? Third, translate into mathematics.
Fourth, solve the mathematical problem. Fifth, validate: does the
solution make sense? If not, return to step two.

Linear programming is the most widely used optimization technique.
A factory has limited labor and materials. Each product generates
profit. How many of each should you produce? The constraints form
a polygon. The optimal solution is always at a vertex. The simplex
algorithm finds it efficiently.

When solutions must be whole numbers, we use integer programming.
You cannot build three point seven factories. Branch and bound
solves these problems by systematically exploring the space of
integer solutions.

Probability models capture uncertainty. The newsvendor model
decides how many newspapers to order when demand is uncertain.
The optimal order depends on the ratio of overage cost to underage
cost — how much you lose from ordering too many versus too few.

Monte Carlo simulation is the universal fallback. When you cannot
solve a model analytically, simulate it. Generate thousands of
random scenarios, compute the outcome for each, and analyze the
distribution. The accuracy improves as the square root of the
number of trials.

Difference equations model discrete-time dynamics. The logistic
map, x(t+1) equals r x(t) times one minus x(t), is a simple
equation that produces extraordinary complexity. As r increases,
the system goes from a fixed point to cycles of period two, four,
eight — and then to chaos.

Graph theory models networks. Dijkstra's algorithm finds the
shortest path through a network. The traveling salesman problem
finds the shortest tour visiting all cities. It is NP-hard —
for large instances, we must use heuristics that find good
solutions without guaranteeing optimality.

The book's central message is that modeling is a craft. It requires
judgment about what to include, what to simplify, and which
paradigm to use. No single model is right. The goal is to build
models that are useful — that improve decisions, reveal insights,
and deepen understanding.
