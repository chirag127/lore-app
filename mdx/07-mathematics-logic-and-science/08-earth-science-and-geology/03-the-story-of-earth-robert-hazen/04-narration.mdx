The writing style is engaging, enthusiastic, and accessible. Robert Hazen writes with the passion of a scientist who has made important discoveries and wants to share them with the world. His prose is clear and vivid, using metaphors and analogies to make complex geochemical processes understandable. He explains technical concepts without relying on jargon, and when he does use specialized terms, he defines them clearly. The tone is optimistic and wonder-filled, conveying the excitement of scientific discovery without sacrificing intellectual rigor.

The narrative structure follows Earth's chronological history from formation to the present, but each chapter is organized around a theme rather than just a time period. Hazen moves from the Hadean Earth through the origin of life, the Archean eon, the Great Oxidation, and finally to the modern Earth. Within each chapter, he alternates between narrative history, scientific explanation, and reflections on the implications of the discoveries. The pacing is varied, with dramatic events like the oxygen crisis getting extended treatment and quieter periods covered more briefly.

The rhetorical approach is argumentative and revelatory. Hazen is presenting a new way of understanding Earth history, and the book is structured to build the case for the co-evolution thesis chapter by chapter. He presents evidence, addresses counterarguments, and draws connections between apparently unrelated phenomena. The book is designed to change how readers think about the planet, and the argument accumulates force as it proceeds.

Readability is high. The prose is clear and flowing, with good use of narrative to maintain engagement. The science is presented at a level appropriate for interested general readers. The book assumes a basic curiosity about science but no specialized knowledge. Technical concepts are introduced carefully and built upon gradually.

The book fits within Hazen's broader work on mineral evolution and the co-evolution of life and Earth. He is a leading figure in this emerging field, and The Story of Earth represents the popular presentation of his research. Compared to other Earth history books, it stands out for its emphasis on the role of life in shaping geology. Traditional accounts treat life as a relatively recent phenomenon that adapts to geological conditions. Hazen reverses this perspective, showing that life has been an active geological force for most of Earth's history. This shift in perspective has implications beyond geology, affecting how we think about the search for extraterrestrial life, environmental policy, and humanity's role as a geological force.
