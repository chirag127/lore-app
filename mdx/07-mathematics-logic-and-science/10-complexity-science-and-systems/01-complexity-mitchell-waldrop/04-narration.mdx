Complexity by M. Mitchell Waldrop is the story of a scientific
revolution that began in Santa Fe, New Mexico in 1984. A group of
scientists — physicists, economists, biologists, computer scientists
— decided that the most important problems in science could not be
solved within a single discipline. They created the Santa Fe
Institute to study complex systems.

The central idea: wholes are more than the sum of their parts.
An ant colony is more than a collection of ants. The brain is more
than a collection of neurons. The economy is more than a collection
of firms. In each case, the interactions between the parts create
emergent behavior that cannot be predicted from studying the parts
alone.

Chris Langton discovered that complex systems function best at the
edge of chaos — a narrow region between rigid order and pure
randomness. In the ordered regime, nothing new happens. In the
chaotic regime, nothing meaningful happens. At the edge of chaos,
systems are stable enough to maintain structure and flexible enough
to adapt. This is where life exists, where evolution happens, where
intelligence emerges.

Stuart Kauffman asked how much of biological order comes from
natural selection and how much comes from the spontaneous
self-organization of complex systems. He studied Boolean networks
— systems of on-off switches connected by simple rules. He found
that these networks spontaneously settle into a small number of
stable states, and the number of states matches the number of cell
types in the human body. He called this order for free.

Brian Arthur studied increasing returns in the economy. Unlike the
diminishing returns of classical economics, knowledge-based
industries exhibit self-reinforcing dynamics: the more a technology
is adopted, the more valuable it becomes. This can lock the economy
into inferior standards — QWERTY keyboards, VHS tapes — that
persist not because they are best but because they got there first.

Per Bak studied self-organized criticality. A sandpile naturally
evolves to a critical state where the next grain of sand could
trigger an avalanche of any size. Earthquakes, forest fires, and
evolution all exhibit this pattern: events of all scales, with no
way to predict whether the next one will be tiny or catastrophic.

John Holland invented genetic algorithms — problem-solving systems
that evolve solutions through selection, crossover, and mutation,
mimicking natural evolution. Craig Reynolds created boids — simple
agents with three local rules that produce flocking behavior
indistinguishable from real birds.

Complexity captures the excitement of a new science being born. It
is a story about ideas, about the people who had them, and about
the Institute that brought them together.
