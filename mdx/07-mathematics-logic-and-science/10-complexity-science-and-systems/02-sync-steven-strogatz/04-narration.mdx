Sync by Steven Strogatz is about the spontaneous tendency of
systems to synchronize. It is a detective story spanning three
centuries.

It begins in 1665 with Christiaan Huygens, the great Dutch
scientist, lying sick in bed watching two pendulum clocks on his
wall. He noticed that the pendulums swung in perfect opposite
phase — mirror images of each other. No matter how often he
disturbed them, they returned to opposite phase within an hour.
The clocks were communicating through vibrations in the wall.
Huygens had discovered synchronization.

The same phenomenon occurs in Southeast Asian fireflies. Thousands
of males flash in perfect unison, lighting up entire trees as if
controlled by a single switch. Each firefly has its own internal
rhythm. But when it sees another firefly flash, it adjusts. The
collective result is global synchrony — order without a conductor.

Arthur Winfree developed the mathematical theory of biological
synchronization. He showed that the transition from random to
synchronized is a phase transition, like water freezing into ice.
Below a critical coupling strength, nothing happens. Above it,
order emerges spontaneously.

The Kuramoto model is the simplest mathematical description of
this transition. Below a critical coupling, oscillators drift
independently. At the critical point, a cluster locks into
synchrony. As coupling increases, the cluster grows. In the limit,
everyone locks together. The behavior is described by a single
equation that has become the foundation of synchronization
research.

The human body is a symphony of oscillators. Pacemaker cells in
the heart fire together to produce the heartbeat. The circadian
clock synchronizes to the light-dark cycle. Neural oscillations
coordinate brain activity. When synchronization goes wrong, we
get epilepsy — pathological synchrony of neurons — or Parkinsonian
tremor. Researchers are developing desynchronization therapies
using electrical stimulation.

At the quantum scale, synchronization produces superconductivity
— electrons flowing without resistance, locked in perfect quantum
synchrony.

Strogatz and his collaborators showed that the structure of
connections matters. Small-world networks — a mix of local
connections and long-range shortcuts — synchronize much faster
than regular networks. The brain is wired as a small-world network,
perhaps because synchronization speed matters for cognition.

The 2003 Northeast blackout was caused by oscillators in the power
grid falling out of step. Banks crash when traders synchronize
their selling. Synchrony is powerful, but not always good. The
complex region between incoherence and complete synchrony — where
partial order and partial freedom coexist — may be where the most
interesting phenomena occur.
