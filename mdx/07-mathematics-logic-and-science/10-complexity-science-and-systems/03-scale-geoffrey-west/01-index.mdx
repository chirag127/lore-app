keyIdeas:
  - "Scaling laws — power-law relationships between size and biological/structural properties — are universal across life, cities, and companies"
  - "Metabolic rate scales to the 3/4 power of body mass — Kleiber's law — a quarter-power scaling that defies simple geometric explanation"
  - "The fractal geometry of biological distribution networks (blood, plant vasculature) explains the quarter-power scaling laws"
  - "Cities scale like organisms in some ways (infrastructure sublinearly) but unlike organisms in others (social activity superlinearly)"
  - "Companies scale like organisms: as they grow, efficiency increases but innovation slows — explaining why companies eventually die"
keyTakeaways:
  - "Quarter-power scaling laws (metabolism ∝ mass^¾) are among the most universal patterns in biology, spanning 27 orders of magnitude"
  - "The same mathematics that explains metabolic scaling also explains why elephants live longer than mice and why hearts beat at characteristic rates"
  - "Cities follow superlinear scaling: social interactions per capita increase with city size, producing both innovation and crime"
  - "Companies follow sublinear scaling like organisms: as they grow, overhead increases and innovation decreases — the corporate lifecycle"
  - "The finite size of organisms is a consequence of the scaling of distribution networks — there is no such limit for cities, suggesting they could live forever"
