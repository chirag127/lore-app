Scale by Geoffrey West is about the universal mathematical laws
that govern the structure and dynamics of virtually every complex
system — cells, bodies, cities, and companies.

The starting point is Kleiber's law: metabolic rate scales to the
three-quarter power of body mass. An elephant is ten million times
heavier than a mouse, but it uses only ten thousand times more
energy. This quarter-power scaling is remarkably precise across
all of life, from mitochondria to whales.

The explanation lies in the fractal geometry of distribution
networks. Blood vessels, plant vasculature, and respiratory systems
are all fractal — they branch repeatedly, filling space efficiently.
From three simple assumptions — the network reaches every cell,
the terminal units are the same size, and the network is fractal —
the quarter-power laws follow mathematically.

The same mathematics explains why larger animals live longer, have
slower heart rates, and grow more slowly. A mouse's heart beats
six hundred times per minute and it lives two years. An elephant's
heart beats thirty times per minute and it lives seventy years.
They get roughly the same number of heartbeats in a lifetime:
one point five billion. The pace of life scales with size.

Then West makes the leap to cities. When you double a city's
population, infrastructure costs increase by only eighty-five
percent — economies of scale. But socioeconomic activity — GDP,
patents, crime — increases by one hundred fifteen percent. Larger
cities are not just scaled-up towns. They accelerate the pace of
social interaction. This is why people move to cities despite the
higher cost of living: for the innovation, the opportunity, the
energy.

Companies scale sublinearly, like organisms. As a company grows,
overhead grows faster than revenue. Bureaucracy consumes an
increasing share of output. Young companies innovate rapidly;
mature companies stagnate. The average lifespan of a publicly
traded company is about forty years. To survive, companies must
innovate — but growth itself suppresses the conditions for
innovation.

West's central message: there are universal laws governing how
complex systems grow, mature, and die. Understanding these laws
can help us design better cities, build more sustainable companies,
and perhaps even extend the lifespan of our civilization. The
challenge is that growth is constrained by scaling. The only way
to escape the limits of one scaling regime is through innovation
that creates a new one. The question is whether we can innovate
fast enough.
