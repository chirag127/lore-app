*A Short History of Nearly Everything* by Bill Bryson is, in his words,
"a book about how we went from knowing nothing to knowing quite a lot."
It is a tour of scientific discovery from the Big Bang to the first human
civilizations, written not by a scientist but by a travel writer who realized,
in mid-life, that he did not understand the most basic facts about the only
planet he was ever going to live on.

Bryson opens not with a fact but with a failure. He is on the roof of his hotel
in Nairobi, watching his fellow tourists identify exotic birds through binoculars.
He realizes he cannot name the planets in order, does not know what a proton is,
has no idea why the sea is salty, and cannot explain how anyone knows the Earth
is four and a half billion years old. Every science class he ever took taught him
definitions and formulas, not understanding. He writes the book he wishes he had
had.

The universe began, Bryson tells us, thirteen point eight billion years ago.
If the history of the whole universe were compressed into a single year,
the Big Bang happened on January first at midnight. Everything in human history
— every war, every song, every equation, every kiss — fits in the last fourteen
seconds before midnight on December thirty-first.

From the cosmic scale, Bryson pulls the camera in to our own solar system.
If the Sun were a hollow sphere, you could fit one million three hundred thousand
Earths inside it. If Earth were the size of a pea, Jupiter would be a basketball,
and Pluto would be a mile and a half away. The nearest star, Proxima Centauri,
would be thirteen thousand miles away on this scale. The universe is big in a way
the human mind is simply not equipped to comprehend.

And then to atoms. An atom is mostly empty space. If the nucleus were the size
of a fly in the center of a cathedral, the rest of the atom would be the cathedral.
The solidity of matter is an illusion created by the electromagnetic repulsion of
electron clouds. When you sit on a chair, you do not touch the chair. You hover
approximately one angstrom above it, repelled by your own electrons.

You are stardust. The carbon in your cells, the iron in your blood, the calcium
in your bones — all of it was forged inside a star billions of years before our
Sun existed. When that star exploded as a supernova, its atoms scattered across
space, eventually becoming part of the gas cloud that formed our Sun and planets.
In the most literal sense, you are stardust that has become conscious of itself.

The Earth. Four and a half billion years old. Before James Hutton proposed deep
time in 1788, educated people believed the Earth was six thousand years old.
Hutton looked at rock formations — tilted, ancient layers at Siccar Point — and
realized the Earth was incomprehensibly ancient. His ideas were ridiculed for a
generation before Charles Lyell popularized them in 1830. The concept of deep
time — that same slow processes we see today have been operating for hundreds of
millions of years — was the key that unlocked geology and gave Darwin the time
he needed for evolution.

The K-Pg asteroid struck the Yucatan sixty-six million years ago with the force
of a billion Hiroshima bombs. Dinosaurs had dominated Earth for 165 million years.
Without this accident, mammals would still be small and nocturnal. There would be
no humans. We exist because of an extinction event we did not cause.

Bryson ends not with triumph but with smallness. We share 98.8 percent of our
DNA with chimpanzees. The bacteria in your gut outnumber your own cells.
You are not an individual organism. You are a walking ecosystem.

The book's deepest gift is the conviction that the universe is not about us.
And that we are, in the end, very lucky to be here at all.
