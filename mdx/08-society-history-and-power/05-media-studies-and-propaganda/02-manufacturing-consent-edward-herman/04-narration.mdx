Welcome to BookAtlas. Today we explore Manufacturing Consent by Edward S. Herman and Noam Chomsky, first published in 1988 with an expanded edition in 2002. This is one of the most influential works of media criticism ever written, and it remains essential reading for anyone who wants to understand how news is produced in advanced capitalist societies.

The book presents what Herman and Chomsky call the propaganda model. This is not a conspiracy theory. The authors are not arguing that a small group of powerful people meets in secret to decide what the news should say. Rather, they argue that normal, structural features of the media system systematically produce news that serves elite interests. The system works without central coordination, without conscious conspiracy, and without overt censorship.

The propaganda model identifies five filters that news content must pass through. The first filter is ownership. Major media outlets are owned by large, profit-seeking corporations that have substantial holdings in many industries. These corporations are deeply embedded in the capitalist system and have little interest in producing content that threatens it. The second filter is advertising. Media companies derive most of their revenue from advertisers, not from their audiences. This makes them responsive to advertiser interests. Content that might offend big advertisers or create an unsuitable buying mood is subtly discouraged.

The third filter is sourcing. Media rely heavily on official sources, government press releases, corporate announcements, and credentialed experts. These sources provide a steady, predictable, and low-cost flow of information. But they also systematically bias coverage toward official narratives. A White House press conference is news. An activist press release is not. The fourth filter is flak, a term for organized attacks on journalists or media outlets that produce content challenging elite interests. Flak can take many forms, from letter writing campaigns to threats of libel suits to political pressure on media owners. The threat of flak creates a chilling effect that shapes what journalists are willing to pursue.

The fifth filter was originally identified as anti-communism, a controlling ideology that could be used to delegitimize any challenge to the established order. In the updated edition, the authors show how this filter has evolved into the War on Terror and the general framework of national security. The function is the same, to provide a ready-made label that can be applied to dissenters, associating them with a feared enemy.

The bulk of the book is devoted to detailed case studies that demonstrate the propaganda model in action. Herman and Chomsky compare how US media covered atrocities committed by enemy states versus atrocities committed by friendly states. They show a systematic double standard. The killing of a Polish priest by the communist regime received massive coverage, while the killing of tens of thousands of people by US backed regimes in Latin America received minimal attention. They analyze coverage of elections in Central America, showing how media accepted the legitimacy of US backed elections while questioning the legitimacy of left wing elections.

The most famous case study examines the coverage of the murder of a Catholic priest in El Salvador versus the murder of a Catholic priest in Poland. The Polish case was presented as a front page story about communist oppression. The Salvadoran case, in which the priest was killed by US backed death squads, was buried in the back pages and framed as a complex local conflict. The difference in treatment cannot be explained by the facts of the cases. It is explained by the propaganda model.

The book has been criticized on several grounds. Mainstream media professionals reject its premise entirely, pointing to adversarial journalism like Watergate or the Pentagon Papers as evidence that media do challenge power. Academics have questioned the methodology, arguing that the case studies are selected to confirm the thesis rather than tested against alternative explanations. And some critics argue that the propaganda model is outdated in the digital age, when citizen journalism, social media, and algorithmic curation have transformed the media landscape.

Herman and Chomsky would likely respond that their model is about structure, not about individual exceptions. Adversarial journalism exists, they would say, but it operates within boundaries set by the filters. The Pentagon Papers were published, but they did not lead to fundamental changes in the corporate structure of media or the ownership of the newspaper that published them. As for the digital age, the five filters have arguably intensified, platform ownership is more concentrated than old media ownership ever was, advertising dependency has shifted to even more powerful algorithmic systems, and new forms of flak, including online mobs and platform demonetization, have emerged.

Manufacturing Consent is not an easy read. It is dense, academic in style, and packed with detailed historical examples. But the effort is rewarded with a framework that changes how you see the news forever. This has been a BookAtlas narration of Manufacturing Consent by Edward Herman and Noam Chomsky.
