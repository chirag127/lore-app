The writing style is engaging, conversational, and intellectually rigorous. Mary Beard writes with a distinctive voice that combines scholarly authority with wit and warmth. Her sentences are clear and direct, avoiding academic jargon while maintaining precision. She frequently addresses the reader directly, asking questions and pointing out puzzles in the historical record. The tone is that of a brilliant teacher talking to an interested student, never condescending but always challenging.

The narrative structure is unconventional. Beard opens with the murder of Cicero in 43 BCE before looping back to Rome's foundation and then moving forward chronologically to 212 CE. This non-linear approach signals that the book is as much about how we think about Rome as about what happened. Each chapter focuses on a theme or period, with frequent digressions exploring evidence, sources, and debates among scholars. The structure rewards attentive reading but may disorient those expecting a straightforward storyline.

The rhetorical approach is argumentative and revisionist. Beard is constantly questioning received wisdom, showing readers that things they thought they knew about Rome are more complicated than they appear. She presents evidence, explains scholarly debates, and then offers her own conclusions. The book is structured as an invitation to think critically about Roman history rather than as a passive absorption of facts. Beard is scrupulously honest about what we know, what we do not know, and how we know it.

Readability is high. The prose is accessible and engaging, with no assumed knowledge of ancient history. Technical terms are explained when first used. The occasional asides and modern analogies keep the text lively. The book is long but never tedious, as Beard varies the pace between narrative, analysis, and reflection.

The book fits within Beard's broader project of making classical scholarship accessible to general readers. SPQR is her most comprehensive work, drawing together themes she explored in earlier books like The Roman Triumph and Pompeii. Its unique contribution is combining a synthesis of modern scholarship with a critical methodology that teaches readers not just facts but how to think about the past. The book has been widely praised for its success in bringing the newest academic research on Rome to a popular audience without dumbing it down.
