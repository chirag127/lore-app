# On War

## Executive Summary

*On War* (Vom Kriege) by Carl von Clausewitz is the most important philosophical work on war ever written in the Western tradition. Clausewitz, a Prussian general and military theorist who fought in the Napoleonic Wars, wrote the book between 1816 and 1830 but left it unfinished at his death. It was published posthumously by his wife. The book is notoriously difficult, incomplete, and sometimes contradictory, but its core concepts — war as the continuation of politics by other means, the remarkable trinity, friction, the fog of war, and the center of gravity — have shaped military thinking for nearly two centuries. The 1984 Princeton edition, translated by Michael Howard and Peter Paret, is the standard English translation.



## Key Takeaways

- **War is political.** Clausewitz's most famous dictum: war is a continuation of political intercourse with the admixture of other means. Military actions are always in service of political ends, not independent activities.
- **Friction is universal.** In war, everything is simple, but the simplest thing is difficult. Accumulated difficulties produce friction that makes real war fundamentally different from war on paper.
- **The trinity explains war's nature.** War is composed of three tendencies: primordial violence (the people), chance and probability (the military), and rational political purpose (the government).
- **Focus on the center of gravity.** All energy should be directed toward the enemy's center of gravity — the source of their power. This may be their army, their capital, their alliances, or their public opinion.

## Who Should Read

- **Military professionals** — the foundation of Western strategic theory.
- **Students of political science and international relations** — Clausewitz provides the classic framework for understanding the relationship between war and politics.
- **Strategic thinkers** in any domain who want a sophisticated framework for thinking about competition and conflict.

## Who Should Skip

- **Casual readers** — this is a dense, difficult, often frustrating work.
- **Those seeking tactical guidance** — Clausewitz is about the nature of war, not battlefield techniques.
- **Anyone who wants clear, definitive answers** — Clausewitz emphasizes complexity, uncertainty, and the limits of theory.

## Difficulty

**Hard** — Dense, philosophical, often abstract. Requires patience and careful reading. The unfinished nature adds to the difficulty.

## Reading Time

- **Reading**: 25–30 hours
- **Listening**: 1,500–1,800 minutes

## Related Books

- [The Art of War](the-art-of-war-sun-tzu) — The Eastern counterpart, more concise and aphoristic
- [The Face of Battle](the-face-of-battle-john-keegan) — The experience of war from below
- [Strategy by Arthur Lykke](strategy-arthur-lykke) — Modern strategic theory building on Clausewitz
- [War and Peace](war-and-peace-leo-tolstoy) — Tolstoy's novel, which grapples with many of the same questions about war

## Final Verdict

**A foundational text.** On War is difficult, incomplete, and frustrating, but it is also profound. Its concepts are essential for anyone who wants to think seriously about war, strategy, and the relationship between military force and political purpose. **Rating: 9/10**
