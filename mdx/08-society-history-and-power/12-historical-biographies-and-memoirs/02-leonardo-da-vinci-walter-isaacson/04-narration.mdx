The writing style is clear, engaging, and accessible. Walter Isaacson writes with the confidence of a journalist who has written many bestselling biographies. His prose is direct and unpretentious, avoiding academic jargon while maintaining intellectual depth. He explains complex concepts in painting technique, anatomy, and engineering with remarkable clarity. The tone is admiring but not reverential, treating Leonardo as a brilliant but flawed human being rather than a demigod.

The narrative structure is chronological but organized thematically within each period. Isaacson moves through Leonardo's life from birth to death, but within each phase he devotes chapters to specific themes — the notebooks, particular paintings, scientific investigations. This allows him to explore Leonardo's multifaceted genius without losing the narrative thread. The chapters on individual paintings are particularly effective, combining art historical analysis with biographical context.

The rhetorical approach is biographical and analytical. Isaacson presents Leonardo's life as a case study in how genius works. He frequently steps back from the narrative to draw lessons about creativity, observation, and interdisciplinary thinking. The book is both a biography and a meditation on the nature of creativity. Isaacson's own curiosity is evident throughout — he clearly enjoyed researching and writing this book, and that enthusiasm is contagious.

Readability is high. Isaacson assumes no specialized knowledge of art history or Renaissance history. Technical terms are explained when first used. The prose flows naturally, and the many illustrations of Leonardo's drawings and paintings help the reader follow the analysis. The book is long but never feels tedious because the subject is so varied and Isaacson's prose is so engaging.

The book fits within Isaacson's larger project of writing biographies of transformative figures who changed how we think and work. Alongside his biographies of Einstein, Steve Jobs, and Benjamin Franklin, this book explores a different kind of genius — not the inventor of a theory or a technology, but a mind that integrated art and science in a way that had never been done before and has never been equaled since. Leonardo da Vinci is Isaacson's most personal book, reflecting his conviction that curiosity and interdisciplinary thinking are the keys to creativity, and that these qualities can be cultivated by anyone willing to look at the world with fresh eyes.
