The writing style is elegant, detailed, and deeply researched. Ron Chernow writes with the authority of a scholar and the narrative skill of a journalist. His prose is literary without being pretentious, employing a rich vocabulary and complex sentences that remain clear and readable. The tone is sympathetic to Hamilton without being hagiographic, though Chernow clearly admires his subject. He is particularly skilled at explaining complex financial and constitutional issues in accessible terms.

The narrative structure is strictly chronological, following Hamilton from his birth in the Caribbean to his death in the duel with Burr. Within this framework, Chernow devotes extended sections to major themes: Hamilton's wartime service, his work on the Federalist Papers, his Treasury years, the Reynolds affair, and the fatal rivalry with Burr. The pacing is deliberate and thorough, with Chernow taking time to develop context and character before the major dramatic events. The book builds toward the duel with the inevitability of tragedy.

The rhetorical approach is biographical and argumentative. Chernow is not merely telling Hamilton's story; he is making a case for Hamilton's importance and rehabilitating his reputation. The book is structured to show Hamilton's achievements and to counter the criticisms that had diminished his historical standing. Chernow is particularly effective at showing how Hamilton's financial program was not just technocratic policy but a coherent vision for American greatness.

Readability is good for a book of this length and complexity. The prose flows well, and Chernow varies the pace between narrative action, analytical explanation, and character portraiture. The book assumes some familiarity with the American founding but explains necessary context clearly.

The book fits within Chernow's broader body of work, which includes definitive biographies of the House of Morgan, John D. Rockefeller, George Washington, and Ulysses S. Grant. Chernow specializes in American financial and political history, and his biographies are known for their exhaustive research, narrative power, and willingness to make strong arguments about their subjects. Alexander Hamilton is arguably his masterpiece, the book that restored a forgotten founder to prominence and inspired a cultural phenomenon in the Hamilton musical. Its unique contribution is showing that America's financial system was not an inevitable development but the creation of a single brilliant mind operating against considerable opposition.
