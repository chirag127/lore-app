The writing style is elegant, warm, and literary. Will Durant writes with a distinctive voice that combines scholarly authority with a novelist's sense of character and drama. His prose is richly textured, employing balanced sentences, vivid metaphors, and memorable epigrams. The tone is humane and philosophically reflective, with an underlying optimism about human achievement tempered by a realistic awareness of human failure. Durant writes as a humanist in the fullest sense, interested in everything human beings have done and created.

The narrative structure varies by volume but generally follows a consistent pattern. Each volume opens with a broad overview of the geographical and historical context, then proceeds chronologically through the period. Within each chronological section, Durant integrates political events, economic developments, intellectual movements, and artistic achievements. He pauses frequently for character sketches of major figures, analyses of important works, and philosophical reflections on the meaning of events. The volumes are long but carefully structured, with clear sections and headings that help readers navigate.

The rhetorical approach is narrative and celebratory. Durant is not writing to prove a thesis or advance an argument but to tell the story of human achievement. He believes that history is fundamentally interesting and that its purpose is to both inform and inspire. The book is written for the general reader, not the specialist, and Durant assumes no prior knowledge of the periods he covers. He explains context, introduces characters, and connects events in a way that makes the past feel present and relevant.

Readability is high for serious general readers. The prose is sophisticated but not academic, the narrative engaging but not simplistic. Durant's love of language and his gift for the memorable phrase make the long volumes a pleasure to read.

The Story of Civilization belongs to the tradition of narrative history that runs from Herodotus through Gibbon to Macaulay. Durant saw himself as continuing this tradition of literary history written for a general audience. His unique contribution is the sheer scope of his achievement — no single author since has attempted anything comparable. The series has introduced millions of readers to the history of civilization, and its influence on popular understanding of history is incalculable. Though academic historians have moved in very different directions, the Durants' work remains a monument to the belief that history is a story worth telling well.
