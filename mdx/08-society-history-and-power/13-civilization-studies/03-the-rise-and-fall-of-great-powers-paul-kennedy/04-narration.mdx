The writing style is clear, analytical, and accessible. Paul Kennedy writes with the authority of a professional historian who wants to reach a general audience. His prose is direct and unpretentious, avoiding academic jargon while maintaining scholarly rigor. He explains economic concepts in plain language and supports his arguments with well-chosen data. The tone is measured and judicious, presenting arguments without excessive certainty and acknowledging counter-evidence. Kennedy is not a polemicist but an analyst, and the book's power comes from the weight of evidence rather than rhetorical force.

The narrative structure is chronological, following the rise and fall of great powers from 1500 to the late twentieth century. Each chapter covers a period, examining the relative economic and military positions of the major powers within that era. The book moves from the Habsburg ascendancy through the rise of Britain, the industrial revolution, the German challenge, and the twentieth-century world wars. The final section on the United States is the most controversial and the most engaged with contemporary politics. Kennedy builds his case cumulatively, with each chapter adding evidence for his thesis about the relationship between economic and military power.

The rhetorical approach is analytical and empirical. Kennedy presents data on national income, industrial production, and military expenditure, using these to track shifts in relative power. He acknowledges the limitations of his data and the complexity of the factors involved. The book is structured as an argument, with each chapter supporting the central thesis about imperial overstretch. Kennedy is careful not to claim too much, presenting his conclusions as tendencies rather than laws.

Readability is good for a book of this intellectual ambition. The prose is clear and well-organized, with helpful summaries and transitions. The economic data is presented in accessible tables and charts. The book assumes some familiarity with modern history but explains necessary context clearly.

The book belongs to the tradition of grand historical synthesis that examines long-term patterns in international affairs. Kennedy's unique contribution is combining economic history with strategic analysis in a way that made academic research accessible to policymakers and general readers. The book sparked an important debate about American decline that has continued for decades, and its concepts have become part of the standard vocabulary of international relations. Despite the criticism that Kennedy's predictions about American decline were not fully borne out, the framework he developed remains essential for understanding the relationship between economic productivity and national power.
