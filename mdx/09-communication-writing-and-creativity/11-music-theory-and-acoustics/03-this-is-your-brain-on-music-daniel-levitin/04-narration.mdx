This Is Your Brain on Music by Daniel Levitin is a landmark work of popular science that explains the neuroscience of musical experience. Published in 2006, it was a New York Times bestseller and has been translated into twenty languages. Levitin is uniquely qualified to write this book. He is a neuroscientist who founded the Laboratory for Music Cognition at McGill University. But before becoming a scientist, he worked as a record producer and sound engineer with artists like Stevie Wonder and Santana. He understands music from the inside and the brain from the inside.

The book explains how the brain processes the basic elements of music. Pitch is the perceptual correlate of sound frequency. The brain analyzes pitch in the auditory cortex, but recognizing a melody involves memory systems that can identify familiar patterns even when the tempo or instrument changes. Rhythm engages the motor system. When you hear a beat, your brain activates the same circuits that control movement. This is why you cannot help tapping your foot. Timbre is the quality that distinguishes a guitar from a piano playing the same note. It is processed by specialized neural circuits that analyze the harmonic spectrum of the sound.

Levitin debunks the myth of a single brain center for music. Unlike language, which relies on relatively localized regions, music processing is distributed across the brain. This explains why a musician who loses the ability to speak after a stroke may still be able to play and compose. Damage to one brain region may impair some musical abilities while leaving others intact.

The most fascinating part of the book is the explanation of musical emotion. Why does music make us feel joy, sadness, or awe? Levitin's answer involves the brain's prediction and reward systems. The brain is constantly predicting what will happen next. When the music follows our expectations, we feel satisfaction. When it violates our expectations in interesting ways, we feel surprise and tension. The resolution of that tension produces a powerful emotional release. This alternation of tension and release is the foundation of musical emotion.

The dopamine system is central to this process. Music triggers dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens, the same reward center activated by food, sex, and addictive drugs. The anticipation of a musical peak can be as rewarding as the peak itself. This is why a familiar song can still give you chills even after you have heard it a hundred times. Your brain is anticipating what is coming, and the anticipation is itself pleasurable.

Levitin also explores musical memory. Musical memory is remarkably robust. Patients with amnesia who cannot remember what happened an hour ago can still recognize songs from decades earlier. This durability reflects the distributed nature of musical memory. It is encoded in multiple brain systems, so damage to any single system is unlikely to erase it completely. The earworm phenomenon, songs that get stuck in your head, reflects the brain's tendency to complete musical patterns. When a song is interrupted, the brain keeps replaying it in an attempt to achieve closure.

The final chapters examine how musical training changes the brain. Musicians have larger auditory cortices, more developed motor regions, and stronger connections between brain regions. These changes are use dependent. The more you practice, the more your brain adapts. Early training produces lasting changes, but the adult brain remains plastic enough to learn.

The book has been criticized for biological reductionism. Some argue that explaining music in terms of brain chemistry misses the cultural and historical dimensions of musical experience. Oliver Sacks, in his book Musicophilia, offers a more humanistic approach. These criticisms have merit, but they miss the point of Levitin's project. He is not claiming that neuroscience is the only way to understand music. He is showing that neuroscience can illuminate dimensions of musical experience that other approaches cannot reach. After reading this book, you will understand why certain songs give you chills and why your brain demands music every day.
