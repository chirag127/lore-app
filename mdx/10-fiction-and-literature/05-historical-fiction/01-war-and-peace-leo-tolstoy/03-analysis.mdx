## Analysis

### Strengths

**Unrivaled scope and depth.** No novel before or since has attempted such a complete portrait of a society. Tolstoy's ability to shift from a ballroom to a battlefield to a peasant hut, rendering each with equal conviction, is extraordinary.

**Radical psychological realism.** Tolstoy's characters feel more real than most actual people. Their inner lives—Pierre's wandering thoughts, Andrei's cold pride, Natasha's volatile emotions—are rendered with a precision that anticipates modern psychology.

**The philosophical argument.** The interludes on history are not digressions; they are the novel's intellectual center. Tolstoy's attack on the "great man" theory of history remains provocative and relevant.

### Weaknesses

**Structural excess.** The novel is too long by any reasonable standard. Tolstoy's second epilogue is a philosophical essay, not fiction; some readers find it tedious.

**The Natasha problem.** Natasha's transformation from a vibrant young woman to a domesticated mother who neglects her appearance has troubled feminist critics. Tolstoy seems to celebrate the erasure of her vitality.

**Class bias.** Tolstoy writes sympathetically about peasants but always from an aristocratic perspective. The novel's world is fundamentally that of the nobility.

### Criticism

**Isaiah Berlin, philosopher and historian:** "Tolstoy was by nature a fox, but believed in being a hedgehog." Berlin's famous essay, *The Hedgehog and the Fox*, argues that Tolstoy's genius lay in his multiplicity of perspectives, even as he insisted on a single unifying theory of history.

**Vladimir Nabokov, novelist and critic:** Nabokov, who taught *War and Peace* at Cornell, called it "the greatest novel ever written" but noted that Tolstoy's philosophy of history was "a muddle of brilliant sophistries."

**Henry James, novelist:** James criticized Tolstoy's novels as "loose, baggy monsters," arguing that they lacked the formal architecture of the great French novels.

### Historical Context

Published in 1869, *War and Peace* was written in the aftermath of Russia's disastrous defeat in the Crimean War (1853-1856) and during the era of Alexander II's reforms, including the emancipation of the serfs. Tolstoy, who had served in the Crimean War, was deeply disillusioned by the gap between Russia's self-image and its reality. The novel's critique of Napoleon was also a veiled critique of autocracy in Tolstoy's own time.

### Literary Significance

*War and Peace* fundamentally expanded what the novel could do. Before Tolstoy, the novel was primarily a form of entertainment or moral instruction. Tolstoy made it a vehicle for everything: psychology, philosophy, history, sociology, and spiritual autobiography. The novel's influence can be seen in everything from Proust's *In Search of Lost Time* to Gabriel García Márquez's *One Hundred Years of Solitude*.

### Similar Books

**Books This Builds On**

- *Les Misérables* by Victor Hugo — Tolstoy was influenced by Hugo's epic social vision and his use of historical fiction
- *The Iliad* by Homer — Tolstoy acknowledged Homeric epic as a model for combining individual fate with collective catastrophe

**Books That Challenge This**

- *Crime and Punishment* by Fyodor Dostoevsky — Dostoevsky's psychological intensity and urban setting offer a counterpoint to Tolstoy's domestic and rural focus
- *Ulysses* by James Joyce — Joyce's modernist fragmentation challenges Tolstoy's confidence in representing reality whole

### Long-Term Relevance

*War and Peace* has never been out of print and is consistently ranked among the greatest novels ever written. Its theory of history remains a powerful corrective to the cult of personality in politics. Its insistence on the sacredness of ordinary life feels increasingly urgent in an age of constant crisis and media spectacle. The novel's future is secure: as long as readers seek to understand the relationship between individual lives and the great events of history, *War and Peace* will be read.
