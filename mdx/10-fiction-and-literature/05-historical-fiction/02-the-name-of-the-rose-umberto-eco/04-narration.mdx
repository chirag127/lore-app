## Narration

### Writing Style

Eco writes in a carefully archaized prose that imitates medieval chronicles while remaining accessible to modern readers. The novel is supposedly Adso's memoir, written in his old age, and the voice shifts between the naive immediacy of a young monk and the weary wisdom of a survivor who has seen everything burned. The English translation by William Weaver captures this dual register beautifully, preserving Eco's long, clause-laden sentences without sacrificing clarity.

### Structure

The novel is organized into seven days, each divided by the monastic hours (Matins, Lauds, Prime, etc.). This structure imposes a rigorous order on the narrative while also evoking the regulated life of the abbey. Within each day, the investigation progresses, but so do the theological debates, the political intrigues, and the slow revelation of character. The structure is architectural, like the library itself—complex but purposeful.

### Rhetorical Techniques

Eco's most distinctive technique is the embedded text. The novel is filled with other texts: manuscripts, marginal notes, cryptic library catalogs, quotations from forbidden books. We are constantly reading about reading. This self-consciousness is the novel's postmodern signature—it is a book about books, a fiction that never lets us forget it is a fiction. Yet Eco does not sacrifice narrative engagement for metafictional play; the murder mystery keeps us turning pages even as we reflect on the nature of textuality.

### Readability

The novel's readability depends heavily on the reader's tolerance for intellectual digression. The prose itself, while ornate, is never obscure. The difficulty is entirely in the density of reference and argument. Readers who enjoy learning about medieval theology while following a detective plot will find the book absorbing. Those who want a lean, propulsive narrative will find it frustrating. The Latin quotations can be skipped without losing the story, but the untranslated passages also create a sense of authenticity, of being admitted to a world that does not cater to the modern reader.

The novel's most famous scene—the description of the library, with its hidden entrance, its labyrinthine corridors, and its sealed inner sanctum—is a triumph of atmospheric writing that has become iconic. Like the library itself, the novel rewards those who persist in its exploration, revealing new chambers of meaning with each return visit.
