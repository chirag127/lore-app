## Narration

### Writing Style

Chekhov's prose appears simple but is the product of immense labor. His sentences are clear, direct, and unpretentious. He avoids metaphor and simile in favor of concrete, sensory detail. A room is not "depressing"; it "smelled of cabbage soup and cheap tobacco." A character is not "anxious"; he "kept looking at the clock, though he had nowhere to go." This commitment to the concrete over the abstract is the foundation of Chekhov's realism. He refuses to tell us what to think, instead presenting the world in such precise detail that we cannot help but see it clearly.

### Structure

Chekhov's stories typically begin in medias res, with no exposition and no introduction of character. We are dropped into a situation and must orient ourselves. The ending is equally abrupt. There is no resolution, no summing up. The story simply stops, and we are left with the feeling that life has continued beyond the page. This open-endedness was revolutionary in its time and remains one of Chekhov's most imitated techniques.

### Rhetorical Techniques

Chekhov's most characteristic device is what might be called the "significant triviality"—a small detail that reveals a character's entire situation. In "The Lady with the Little Dog," Gurov eats a slice of watermelon in Anna's hotel room after their first night together. The detail is so precise, so physical, that it carries the entire weight of the story's themes: the banality of the affair, the deflation of romantic expectation, the way physical details persist when emotions fade.

### Readability

Chekhov is among the most accessible of the great writers. His vocabulary is not large, his sentences are not complex, and his stories are short. The difficulty lies entirely in the subtlety of his effects. A Chekhov story demands an active reader—someone willing to notice details, to infer meaning, to hold ambiguity. The rewards of this attention are enormous. No writer rewards re-reading more generously; a Chekhov story read for the fifth time yields insights that were invisible on the first reading. This is because his stories are not about what happens but about the texture of experience itself, and texture can only be felt, not summarized.
