The writing style is direct, authoritative, and economical. Strunk's sentences are short and declarative. Rules are stated as commands: "Use the active voice." "Omit needless words." "Put statements in positive form." The tone is that of a confident professor who knows what is correct and expects students to follow. White's additions are warmer and more reflective, creating a contrast between Strunk's brusque rules and White's thoughtful essays on style.

The narrative structure is simple and functional. The book is divided into five short sections, each addressing a different aspect of writing. Within each section, entries are organized as discrete rules or observations. The structure is designed for quick reference rather than linear reading, though the book rewards both approaches.

The rhetorical approach is prescriptive and pedagogical. Strunk presents himself as an authority on correct usage and expects compliance. This approach has been criticized by linguists who argue that usage cannot be neatly divided into right and wrong, but it has proven extraordinarily effective for generations of writers seeking clear guidance.

Readability is very high. The prose is simple, the rules are short, and the examples are clear. The book's brevity ensures that even reluctant readers can finish it quickly. E.B. White's chapters are more literary but equally accessible.

This book fits within the long tradition of English style guides, from Fowler's The King's English to modern works like Garner's Modern English Usage. Its unique contribution is combining genuine wisdom about writing with extraordinary compression and a voice of absolute authority.
