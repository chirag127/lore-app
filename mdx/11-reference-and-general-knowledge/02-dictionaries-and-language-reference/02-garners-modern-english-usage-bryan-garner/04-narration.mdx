The writing style is scholarly but accessible. Garner writes with authority and precision, drawing on his legal training to make careful, nuanced arguments about usage questions. His prose is itself a demonstration of the principles he advocates: clear, precise, and confident without being dogmatic. The tone is that of a knowledgeable guide who has studied the evidence and reached considered conclusions, not a schoolmaster laying down arbitrary rules.

The narrative structure is encyclopedic, organized alphabetically by usage question. Each entry follows a consistent format: the disputed usage, historical background, current evidence from corpora and usage panels, analysis, and a clear recommendation with Language-Change Index rating. This consistency makes the book easy to navigate, but the dictionary format means there is no overarching argument to follow sequentially.

The rhetorical approach is persuasive through evidence rather than authority. Garner wins readers over by presenting data from language corpora and usage panel surveys, not by asserting his personal preferences. When he recommends against a usage, he explains his reasoning and acknowledges counterarguments. This approach builds trust even with readers who may disagree with specific judgments.

Readability is good given the reference format. The prose is clear and engaging for a reference work, and the entries are written to be self-contained. The introductory essay is particularly well-written and provides context for the dictionary entries that follow.

This book fits within the tradition of single-volume English usage guides established by Fowler's Modern English Usage. Garner's unique contribution is applying modern linguistic methodology—corpus analysis, usage panel surveys, and systematic indexing—to the usage guide format, creating a work that is both more evidence-based and more practically useful than its predecessors.
