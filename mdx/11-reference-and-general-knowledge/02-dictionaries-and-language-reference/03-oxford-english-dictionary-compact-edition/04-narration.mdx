The writing style is scholarly, precise, and economical. OED entries are written in a compressed format designed to convey maximum information in minimum space. Abbreviations are extensive, and the prose follows strict conventions that take some getting used to. The definitions are models of clarity and precision, distinguishing subtle shades of meaning with careful wording. The etymological sections are particularly dense, tracing words through multiple languages with exacting attention to sound changes and borrowing patterns.

The narrative structure is purely alphabetical, following the dictionary convention. There is no overarching narrative, though the entries collectively tell the story of the English language and the culture it has served. Each entry is self-contained, designed for lookup rather than sequential reading, though browsing the OED is a rewarding experience that reveals unexpected connections between words and their histories.

The rhetorical approach is objective and descriptive. The OED records how English has been used without passing judgment on usage. This descriptive approach distinguishes it from prescriptive works like The Elements of Style. The authority of the OED comes from its comprehensiveness and accuracy, not from any claim to regulate usage.

Readability is challenging for the Compact Edition specifically. The micrographic type is extremely small, requiring a magnifying glass and good lighting. The abbreviations used to save space require familiarity. The online edition solves these problems and is significantly more accessible, but the print edition offers a unique aesthetic experience that many word lovers treasure.

The OED represents the culmination of the historical-philological tradition in lexicography, reaching back to Samuel Johnson's dictionary of 1755 and forward to the digital age. Its unique contribution is the systematic application of the historical method to the entire English vocabulary, creating a record of the language that is unmatched in scope and depth.
