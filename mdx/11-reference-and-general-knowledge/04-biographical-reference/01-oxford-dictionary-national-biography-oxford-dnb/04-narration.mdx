The writing style is scholarly, precise, and economical. DNB entries are written by specialists for an audience of fellow scholars and informed general readers. The prose is efficient and information-dense, with each sentence contributing to the biographical portrait. Judgments are carefully qualified, and claims are supported with evidence. The tone is objective and critical rather than celebratory or moralistic.

The narrative structure is biographical, following the subject's life chronologically from birth to death, with emphasis on their achievements, character, and historical significance. Within the chronological framework, writers highlight the subject's most important contributions and provide critical assessment of their legacy. The structure is standardized across entries, making comparison between subjects straightforward.

The rhetorical approach is scholarly and critical. Entries aim to present a balanced assessment of the subject's life and achievements, acknowledging both strengths and weaknesses. Unlike celebratory biography, the DNB seeks critical distance from its subjects, placing them in historical context and evaluating their significance with scholarly detachment.

Readability varies depending on the author and subject. Entries are generally well-written but assume some historical knowledge and familiarity with academic conventions. Technical terms and period-specific references are used without extensive explanation. The prose is denser and less accessible than popular biography but rewards careful reading.

The Oxford DNB represents the culmination of two centuries of biographical scholarship in Britain. Its unique contribution is combining the scale of a national biographical project with the scholarly rigor of specialist contributors, creating a resource that is both comprehensive and authoritative.
