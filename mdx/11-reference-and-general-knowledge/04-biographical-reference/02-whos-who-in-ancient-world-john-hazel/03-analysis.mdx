# Strengths

## Strength 1: Practical Concision

The book packs a remarkable amount of information into a compact format. Entries are long enough to be useful but short enough for quick reference. This balance makes it ideal for students and general readers.

## Strength 2: Myth-History Integration

The inclusion of both mythological and historical figures in a single volume is genuinely useful. Many works separate them, but Hazel recognizes that readers encountering ancient names in literature need both categories.

## Strength 3: Accessible Scholarship

Hazel draws on current scholarship but presents it in accessible language. The entries are reliable without being forbidding.

# Weaknesses

## Weakness 1: Limited Geographic Scope

The book covers only the Greco-Roman world, excluding ancient Egypt, Mesopotamia, Persia, India, and China. The title "Ancient World" is misleadingly broad.

## Weakness 2: Age

Published in 2002, the book does not incorporate the most recent archaeological discoveries and scholarly interpretations. A new edition would be welcome.

## Weakness 3: No Illustrations

The book lacks maps, photographs, and illustrations that would help readers visualize the figures and their world. Given the compact format, this is understandable but regrettable.

# Criticism

## Critic 1: *Bryn Mawr Classical Review*

"A useful reference work for students and general readers. Hazel writes clearly and the entries are well-informed. The inclusion of mythological alongside historical figures is a practical choice, though specialists might prefer a stricter separation."

## Critic 2: *Reference Reviews*

"Compact, reliable, and well-organized. Who's Who in the Ancient World fills a useful niche between children's reference books and scholarly biographical dictionaries. Recommended for school and public libraries."

# Historical Context

## The Classical Biography Tradition

Who's Who in the Ancient World belongs to a long tradition of classical biographical reference works that extends back to Plutarch's Parallel Lives and Suetonius's The Twelve Caesars. Modern biographical dictionaries serve a different purpose — not moral instruction or entertainment, but reliable information retrieval.

## Place in the Genre

The book competes with Oxford's Who's Who in the Classical World and other similar reference works. Hazel's distinguishing feature is his concise, accessible format designed for the student and general reader rather than the specialist.

# Final Assessment

**7/10** — A useful, reliable biographical reference for the Greco-Roman world. Its concision and accessibility are strengths, but the limited geographic scope and lack of recent updates are drawbacks. Recommended for students and general readers, not specialists.
