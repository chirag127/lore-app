The writing style is clear, efficient, and informative. Hazel writes for a general audience, avoiding unnecessary academic jargon while maintaining scholarly accuracy. His entries are concise, typically 200-500 words, focusing on the essential biographical facts and historical significance of each figure. The tone is objective and descriptive, presenting what is known about each figure without excessive speculation.

The narrative structure is alphabetical, following standard dictionary format. Each entry is self-contained, designed for quick lookup rather than sequential reading. Within each entry, the structure follows a consistent pattern: identification and dates, biographical narrative, historical context, and assessment of significance.

The rhetorical approach is descriptive and informative. Hazel's goal is to provide reliable information efficiently, not to argue a thesis about the ancient world or its figures. This makes the book a trustworthy reference but not a page-turner.

Readability is good for a reference work. The prose is clear and direct, and entries are short enough to digest quickly. Some background knowledge of ancient history is assumed, but the entries are accessible to motivated readers with basic familiarity.

This book fits within the Routledge Who's Who series of biographical reference works covering different historical periods. Its unique contribution is providing a concise, reliable bridge between mythological and historical figures in a single accessible volume.
