The writing style is dense, allusive, and passionately opinionated. Bloom writes in a distinctive voice that combines enormous erudition with personal conviction. His sentences are complex and layered, often requiring multiple readings. The prose is epigrammatic at times, with memorable formulations that stick in the mind. The tone is combative and confident — Bloom does not hedge or qualify his judgments.

The narrative structure is organized around the 26 canonical authors, with chapters devoted to each. The book opens with an introduction establishing Bloom's criteria and polemical position, then proceeds through the canon chronologically from Dante to Beckett. A concluding chapter surveys the current state of the canon and issues a warning about the threats facing literary study.

The rhetorical approach is argumentative and persuasive. Bloom is not simply describing the canon; he is defending it against what he sees as hostile forces. The book is structured to build a cumulative case for the value of aesthetic criticism and the importance of the Western tradition.

Readability is moderate to challenging. Bloom assumes substantial literary knowledge and does not explain references that would be obvious to specialists. The prose is demanding, with complex syntax and vocabulary. The book rewards careful reading but is not accessible as a casual read.

This book fits within the tradition of influential works of literary criticism that seek to define the canon, including Arnold's Culture and Anarchy, Eliot's The Sacred Wood, and Leavis's The Great Tradition. Bloom's unique contribution is combining his theory of influence with a comprehensive reading list and a passionate polemic against the politicization of literary studies.
