The writing style is clear, systematic, and pedagogical. Sister Miriam Joseph writes as a teacher explaining foundational concepts to students. Her prose is precise and methodical, building concepts step by step. She defines terms carefully before using them and provides examples to illustrate each point. The sentences are well-constructed and grammar is correct throughout, as one would expect from a book about grammar. The tone is patient and instructive, occasionally rising to moments of enthusiasm for the subject.

The narrative structure follows a logical progression from the simplest to the most complex art. The book moves from grammar, the foundation of all language use, to logic, the art of reasoning, and finally to rhetoric, the art of persuasion. Each section builds on the previous one, and the author frequently shows how concepts from earlier sections apply in later ones. The structure mirrors the subject matter itself, demonstrating the trivium through its own organization.

The rhetorical approach is expository and analytical. Sister Miriam Joseph is not arguing a controversial thesis but laying out a body of knowledge. She explains, illustrates, and reinforces. When she introduces technical terms, she defines them clearly and uses them consistently. The book is designed to be studied rather than merely read, with exercises and review questions that encourage active engagement with the material.

Readability is moderate. The concepts are abstract but explained clearly. Readers with no background in formal logic or classical rhetoric may need to read sections more than once. The terminology is technical but always defined. The prose is dense but not unnecessarily complex, and the organization helps readers track where they are in the argument.

The book fits within the broader revival of classical education that gained momentum in the late 20th century. Sister Miriam Joseph's contribution is unique in its systematic completeness. Other treatments of the trivium tend to focus on one art or to present the material less rigorously. Her book remains the standard text on the subject, cited by classical educators and homeschooling parents alike. The book's enduring value lies in its recovery of a coherent educational tradition that had been largely lost, presented in a form that modern readers can study and apply. It preserves the insight that the three arts of language and reason are not obsolete skills but permanent tools of the human mind, as relevant today as they were in the medieval university.
