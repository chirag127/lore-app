# The Great Conversation

## Executive Summary

Mortimer Adler's The Great Conversation serves as the introductory volume to the Great Books of the Western World series (1952). It articulates the vision behind the project: that the foundational texts of Western civilization constitute an ongoing conversation about fundamental human questions. Adler explains how to read the Great Books in dialogue with each other, how the Syntopicon indexes ideas across the set, and why this conversation remains essential for liberal education.



## Key Takeaways

- **The Great Conversation** is the dialogue among authors across centuries about fundamental ideas
- **The Great Books** are those with the most to contribute to this conversation
- **Reading in conversation** with each other deepens understanding
- **The Syntopicon** indexes ideas across all the Great Books
- **Liberal education** is incomplete without direct engagement with the Great Books

## Who Should Read

- **Great Books readers**: Start here before diving into the set
- **Liberal arts students**: Understand the vision behind Great Books education
- **How to Read a Book fans**: The next step in Adler's system
- **Education philosophers**: Adler's vision of liberal learning

## Who Should Skip

- Those who **reject the Western canon** framework
- Those seeking **non-Western** intellectual traditions
- People who prefer **specialized study** to broad reading

## Difficulty

**Medium** — Philosophical and educational arguments presented clearly. Some familiarity with Western thought helpful.

## Reading Time

- **Full Read**: 4-5 hours

## Final Verdict

**Recommended** for anyone embarking on a Great Books reading program. Adler's vision of the Great Conversation is inspiring and the reading guidance is practical. The book is short and focused, making it an ideal orientation to the Great Books project.
