# Core Concepts

The foundational ideas about common knowledge and misconceptions.

## The Myth of Common Knowledge

Lloyd and Mitchinson argue that much of what passes for "common knowledge" is actually a collection of myths, simplifications, and outright errors that are perpetuated through repetition in schools, media, and casual conversation.

## Types of Misconception

The book covers several categories: historical misconceptions (Napoleon was not short), scientific misconceptions (there is no dark side of the moon), linguistic misconceptions (the Eskimos do not have 50 words for snow), and biological misconceptions (humans only use 10% of their brains).

## The QI Approach

The book embodies the QI philosophy: "Quite Interesting" facts that are surprising but true. The tone is witty and irreverent, but the research is serious. Each entry is backed by reference to primary sources or authoritative secondary works.

# Sample Entries

## "Napoleon was short"

Actually, Napoleon was of average height for his time (5'6" or 168 cm). The myth originated from a combination of factors: French inches were longer than British inches, his elite Imperial Guard were unusually tall, and British propaganda depicted him as small to diminish him.

## "Humans only use 10% of their brains"

This is completely false. Brain imaging shows that virtually all parts of the brain are active over the course of a day. The myth likely originated from a misinterpretation of early neuroscience research or from self-help literature that used it as a metaphor for untapped potential.

## "The Great Wall of China is visible from space"

It is not visible to the naked eye from low Earth orbit, let alone from the Moon. This myth has been repeated by textbooks and even some astronauts, but it is physically impossible given the wall's dimensions and the resolution of the human eye at that distance.

## "Vikings wore horned helmets"

There is no archaeological evidence that Viking helmets had horns. The image was created by 19th-century Romantic artists, particularly for Wagner's opera cycle, and has become fixed in popular culture despite being historically inaccurate.

# Practical Applications

- **Critical thinking**: Question assumptions and verify "common knowledge"
- **Conversation**: Share surprising facts to spark discussion
- **Humility**: Recognize that we all carry misconceptions

# Actionable Lessons

1. **Question everything** — Common knowledge is often wrong
2. **Check your sources** — Facts repeated many times are not necessarily true
3. **Be humble about what you know** — Everyone has misconceptions
4. **Enjoy being wrong** — Discovering errors is an opportunity to learn
