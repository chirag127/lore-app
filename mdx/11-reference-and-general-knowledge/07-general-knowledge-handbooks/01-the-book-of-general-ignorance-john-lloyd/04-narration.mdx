The writing style is witty, conversational, and engaging. Lloyd and Mitchinson write in the voice of clever companions sharing surprising facts over drinks. The tone is playful and irreverent, with a running undercurrent of gentle mockery toward received wisdom. Sentences are lively and varied, designed to amuse while informing. The humor is distinctly British — dry, self-deprecating, and occasionally surreal.

The narrative structure is loose, organized alphabetically or thematically depending on the edition. Each entry is self-contained, allowing readers to dip in and out. Within each entry, the pattern is consistent: state the common belief, reveal that it is wrong, explain the correct version, and often add a humorous comment. This pattern creates a satisfying rhythm throughout the book.

The rhetorical approach is corrective and revelatory. The authors position themselves as knowledgeable insiders sharing secrets that most people do not know. The pleasure of the book comes from the revelation that what you thought you knew is wrong, combined with the discovery of a more interesting truth.

Readability is very high. The entries are short, the prose is lively, and the concepts are accessible. The book is designed for casual reading and can be picked up and put down easily.

This book fits within the genre of popular fact and trivia collections, from Schott's Original Miscellany to Uncle John's Bathroom Reader. Its unique contribution is the debunking angle — rather than simply presenting interesting facts, it corrects misconceptions, which adds an extra layer of engagement and memorability.
