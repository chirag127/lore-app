The writing style is witty, conversational, and self-deprecating. Jacobs writes in the voice of a neurotic, endlessly curious New York intellectual who can't resist making a joke even at his own expense. His prose is lively and varied, mixing short punchy sentences with longer reflective passages. The humor is constant but never cruel — Jacobs is always the primary target of his own jokes.

The narrative structure follows the alphabet, with each chapter covering one or more letters. Within this framework, Jacobs weaves together multiple threads: the specific facts he learns from that letter's entries, his personal life and family, his meetings with experts, and his reflections on the nature of knowledge. The structure allows for both the pleasures of trivia and the satisfactions of narrative.

The rhetorical approach is confessional and exploratory. Jacobs presents himself as a flawed everyman on an absurd quest, inviting readers to share his journey and his discoveries. The book is more about the process of learning than about the facts themselves, though the facts are entertaining.

Readability is very high. The prose is lively and engaging, chapters are short, and the narrative momentum carries the reader forward. Jacobs's humor ensures that even potentially dry subjects are entertaining.

This book fits within the genre of humorous nonfiction that combines personal narrative with intellectual exploration. Its unique contribution is the brilliant premise of reading an entire encyclopedia, which generates both humor and genuine insight into the nature of knowledge.
